lemp say:
There are several places where Windows and your computer "see" a hard drive.  The problem is different depending on where you're looking.

Do other USB devices get recognized when you connect them?  Do you hear the "connect sound"?

Open Device Manager (Start > Run > devmgmt.msc > OK)
Expand the "Disk Drives" category by clicking on the +.
Do you see an entry corresponding to your external drive? If so, is there a warning icon next to it?

Now scroll down to the "Universal Serial Bus Controllers" section and expand it
Are there any warning icons?

Log on as a user with "Computer administrator" privileges
Open Disk Management (Start > Run > diskmgmt.msc > OK)
You should see something like this:



One of the sections on the bottom part of the window should correspond to your external drive.  If it is there but doesn't have a drive letter, right click on it and select "Change Drive Letter and Paths..." and then assign a drive letter.

Reboot the computer and enter the BIOS configuration utility.  There will be a message that flashes by very quickly at boot up telling you what key to press.  It may be F1, F2, Del, or another key.  Once in the BIOS configuration utility, you'll have to poke around until you find the section that deals with storage.  Is your external drive recognized here?  When you're done looking, exit the BIOS configuration utility without saving/making changes.

lemp say:
It looks like you have a Seagate 40 GB drive as your main (only?) internal hard drive.  This drive appears to be organized with two partitions. 

The main system partition (where Windows and your programs and (some?) data are located) is the C drive and is about 33 GB.  Although this is pretty small by today's standards, you still have over 14 GB of free space (about 44% free), which is very good.

The second partition on this drive uses letter E (I don't know what happened to D) and seems to be a recovery partition of some sort.  Often, these partitions are hidden from casual view and don't have drive letters.

Your external drive appears to be a 160 GB Western Digital drive, and is recognized by your computer, but no partitions are indicated for it.  Did you not see a line for "Disk 1" at the bottom of the Disk Management screen?

Open Device Manager (Start > Run > devmgmt.msc > OK)

Do you see any warning icons in the "Disk Drives" or "Universal Serial Bus controllers" sections?  If so, right-click on any device that shows a warning icon, select Properties, and report what is shown under "Device status."

What sort of enclosure is the external drive in?  Is it a Western Digital "My Book" or "Essentials" or is it a generic enclosure?  Does it have it's own power supply?

At this point, rather than taking a chance at losing the data that's already on that drive, I suggest that you take it to a local computer repair shop (not a BigBoxStoreUSA or GeekSquad type of place) -- or even a friend with a Windows computer -- and see if the drive and its contents are visible on a known working computer.  If you know that the drive is working properly, you can concentrate on getting your computer to recognize it. 

On the other hand, if there is something wrong with the drive (such as a bad power supply or damaged electronics in the external enclosure -- both of which do happen), then no amount of messing around with your computer is going to resolve the problem.

ronnie vernon say:

Hi Bob

For the Bing Aerial theme, the images are stored in a super hidden folder.

The only way to access this folder is to:

1. Open Windows Explorer.

2. Copy the following path:

%userprofile%\AppData\Local\Microsoft\Windows\Temporary Internet Files\Enclosure

3. Paste this into the Address bar and press Enter.

4. When that folder opens, you will see a folder with a long alphanumeric number. Double click that folder to open.

The images that have been downloaded will be in that folder. You can copy the images to another location from there.

Note: You will see a security warning about a possible unsafe file. This is normal, since the source of the file is the internet.

A better idea is to simply right click that folder, select Copy and then Paste it to the Desktop. From there, you can rename the copied folder and move it wherever you wish.
