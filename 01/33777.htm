lemp say:

First of all, most ISPs have different upload and download speeds, with uploads typically being far slower.

Wi-fi "speed" depends on a number of factors.

The main determinant of the speed of your wi-fi connection is the type of wi-fi equipment you have.  This means both your Comcast gateway device and whatever wi-fi adapter is in your computer.  Even if you have the latest, greatest, fastest, Comcast gateway, because a wi-fi link will only go as fast as the slowest component, I'll focus below only on your computer.

"HP TrueVision HD" is your webcam, not your computer.  Find your HP computer model information ...

  • for HP laptops https://support.hp.com/us-en/document/c00033108
  • for HP desktops  https://support.hp.com/us-en/document/bph07555

In general, starting in about 1999, home computer users used "wireless-B" (802.11b).  In about 2003, most newer home wi-fi systems used "wireless-G" (802.11g).  In about 2010, "wireless-N" (802.11n) devices became available.  And in about 2014, "wireless-ac" (802.11ac) consumer devices were available. 

Each of these wireless standards has a theoretical maximum speed:

  • wireless-b          11 Mbits/sec
  • wireless-g          54 Mbits/sec
  • wireless-n        600 Mbits/sec
  • wireless-ac     1300 Mbits/sec

It is important to realize that none of these systems actually achieves that sort of speed in the real world.  The following analysis is typical (LINK):

Below is a breakdown of actual real-life average speeds you can expect from wireless routers within a reasonable distance, with low interference and small number of simultaneous clients:
802.11b - 2-3 Mbps downstream, up to 5-6 Mbps with some vendor-specific extensions.
802.11g - ~20 Mbps downstream
802.11n - 40-50 Mbps typical, varying greatly depending on configuration, whether it is mixed or N-only network, the number of bonded channels, etc. Specifying a channel, and using 40MHz channels can help achieve 70-80Mbps with some newer routers. Up to 100 Mbps achievable with more expensive commercial equipment with 8x8 arrays, gigabit ports, etc.
802.11ac - 70-100+ Mbps typical, higher speeds (200+ Mbps) possible over short distances without many obstacles, with newer generation 802.11ac routers, and client adapters capable of multiple streams.


Assuming that your computer really is from 2010, I suspect that you have a wireless-G wi-fi adapter.  To know for sure, open Device Manager (click Start, type Device Manager in the Search box, and press Enter).  Double-click the "Network adapters" section.  Report the complete name of your wireless network adapter.  If there is more than one item in the "Network adapters" section and you're not sure which one is which, list them all and I'll get you the information you need.

From the above table, in the real world, one might expect a wireless-g connection to deliver 20 Mbps throughput.  That compares reasonably well with your measured 15.3 Mbps.  The difference can easily be explained by the radio environment in which your router and computer are located.  Any interference from nearby devices (and there are many potential interfering devices in the wireless-g waveband) will slow the connection.

If you want a faster connection to the Internet and you want to use wi-fi, you'll need to upgrade your equipment.  First, find the specifications of your Comcast gateway.  Assuming that it's either wireless-n or wireless-ac, buy a wireless network adapter for your computer with the same specification.  These are not very expensive.  For a laptop, you can get an adapter that connects via USB.  For a desktop, you can either use a USB adapter or, if you have a spare internal slot, you can get an adapter that goes inside your computer.

canadian tech say:

Let me add a slightly different perspective to LemP's excellent advice:

Keep in mind that a wired connection using an Ethernet cable to the router will always deliver superior speed.  For example, many printers have Ethernet connections and can operate as a network printer by plugging them into your router (or the supplied modem with router).

It has been my experience that the wireless facilities in provider-supplied modems do notdo a good job of wireless.  If that is the case, you will need to get your own wireless router and get your Internet Service Provider to shut down the wireless facility in their modem.

You should not have more than one wireless router in a network or even try to connect one to another.  In some wireless routers there is the capability of shutting down the router function and having them operate as wireless access points which acts like a signal repeater.  In that case the wireless access point wirelessly connects to the main wireless router and has the network key (password) to it.

Check list & Tips

  1. Microwave ovens will interfere with your wireless signal
  2. 2.4 Ghz cordless phones will interfere with your wireless signal
  3. Large metal objects like refrigerator, freezer, cabinets, laundry will interfere with your wireless signal
  4. Metal in walls, floors or ceiling — some apartment or condo buildings are built of Concrete with reinforcing steel rods in it will interfere with your wireless signal
  5. Near large steel beams either in floor, ceiling or walls will interfere with your wireless signal
  6. Your computer itself will interfere with your wireless signal
  7. Other electronic devices such as a TV will interfere with your wireless signal
  8. Sometimes other nearby neighbours can cause interference through use of over-powered wireless systems
  9. If you are located near an airport or other place where radio signals are used extensively
  10. Most (not all) routers have an ip address of 192.168.0.1.  Entering that in the Internet Explorer address bar should bring up a window for entering user id  and password (not necessarily the same as the wireless key).  You must have this.   Once past that point, you can do all kinds of manual setup.  For example the wireless encryption key (other names too). You can reset your router back to factory defaults by using a straightened paper clip.  Press it into the tiny hole in the back that says reset while the router is powered up.  Hold that pin there for at least 15 seconds.
  11. Many routers then have a user id of admin and no password.
  12. Do not get carried away with WiFi security.  It can not be accessed from any point much further than about 50 feet.  Write the key you create on a sticker and put it on the router or modem.
  13. There is a type of technology that few will tell you about that can make a huge difference for this type of problem.  It amazes me that more people, even technical people, are not aware of it.  It is actually in some routers and notebook PC radios and the sellers don’t even know it.  It is called MIMO, which stands for Multiple In Multiple Out.  It is supported by n radios, and will be used provided both the sending and receiving devices are capable.  It works by transmitting on 2 or more closely adjacent band widths simultaneously.  If a packet of information is lost or garbled, MIMO attempts to find a better copy of the packet from the adjacent stream, and is usually successful.  Obviously 3×3 MIMO systems are better than 2×2.
  14. If you buy a new router, you should insist it has MIMO and you may have to do a lot of research to find which ones have it.  More than you might think, but their sellers don’t know about it.
