Renaming user file folder - Microsoft Community

hogenson say:

Renaming user file folder

In Vista I cannot rename a user file folder.  This computer used to be used by someone else, and I cannot rename her folder so everything I do gets tagged with her name on it.  I changed the computer name and log in name.  I can change any other user folder except this one.  Any ideas how to rename this folder?

a. user say:

This sounds like a permissions issue to me (it doesn't sound like a corrupt profile) - you are trying to use her files under your username and the system wants her username.  The solution is to give your username full rights to all folders in her profile and then to take ownership of all files in her profile - then you'll have the rights you need.

To see your permissions, right click the folder/file, click on properties, and check out the security tab.  Check to see what permissions you have by clicking on your username (or user group).  Here are the types of permissions you can have: http://windows.microsoft.com/en-US/windows-vista/What-are-permissions.  You need to be an administrator or the owner to change permissions (and sometimes being an administrator or even an owner isn't enough - there are ways to block even them from access (though a smart administrator knows these ways and can get around them - but usually shouldn't because they were denied access usually for a very good reason).  Here's how to change folder permissions in Vista: http://www.online-tech-tips.com/windows-vista/set-file-folder-permissions-vista/.  To add take and grant full right permissions and ownership to the right click menu (which will make it faster to get in once it is set up), check out the following article: http://www.mydigitallife.info/2009/05/21/take-and-grant-full-control-permissions-and-ownership-in-windows-7-or-vista-right-click-menu/.

To fix this problem with the folders, take Owner tab.  Click edit and then click the name of the person you want to give ownership to (you may need to add it if it isn't there - or it may be yourself). If you want it to apply to sub-folders and files within that folder/drive, then check the box for replace owner on sub-containers and objects and click OK.  Back out and now there's a new owner for the folders/drive and files who can change permissions as needed.  You can now change them from read-only (even if the main folder says they still are read only - you can have access yourself as the owner).  You can keep them as read-only for other users and guests and even administrators (though they can take ownership themselves and gain access if they so desire and there really isn't much you can do to stop this except protect the folder with a password using a 3rd party product).  This should also give you access to open and work with all of the files in these filders as if they were in your folder.  Here's more information on taking ownership of a file or folder: http://www.vistax64.com/tutorials/67717-take-ownership-file.html.  To add take ownership to the right click menu (which will make it faster to get in once it is set up), check out the following article: http://www.howtogeek.com/howto/windows-vista/add-take-ownership-to-explorer-right-click-menu-in-vista/.

You can also create a folder in your profile named the other person's username and move the files to this folder.  It should pick up the permissions and attributes of the new folder (YOUR user profile folder) and you won't need to go to her profile every time you want to get one of her files.  You may still need to change permissions and/or ownership if you do this - it would just make things easier and quicker to access.

Good luck and I hope this helps!

Group Policy Power Profiles Have Issues on Surface Pro 3 - Microsoft Community

truequazl say:

Group Policy Power Profiles Have Issues on Surface Pro 3

Setting the "Sleep after -  Plugged in" in a GPO to anything will not change the sleep settings in a power plan on the Surface Pro 3.

You have to change the "Turn off Display after" setting to affect the Sleep settings. This is backwards.

To recreate the issue:

  1. Create a new Power Plan in a group policy and apply it to an OU with a Surface 3 Pro in it
  2. In Sleep - Sleep After - Plugged in (minutes): Set the time to "0"
  3. Force a Gpupdate on the Surface Pro 3
  4. Goto your newly created Power Plan and "Change Plan Settings"
  5. The "Put the Computer to sleep" for the plugged in profile will not have changed.
  6. Go back to your GPO
  7. In Display - Turn off display after - Plugged in (minutes): Set it to "0"
  8. Force a Gpupdate on the Surface Pro 3
  9. Goto your newly created Power Plan and "Change Plan Settings"
  10. The "Put the Computer to sleep" for the plugged in profile will have changed.

I have tried this on 3 Surface Pros and they all act the same.
