canadian tech say:

Hi!  I have some strongly held views on this issue myself.  I recognize that many may disagree with my conclusions, but here goes.  

First understand that I am a long-term computer professional with close to 60 years in the field.  In my retirement, I look after 130 client Win7 machines.

Second understand that I do not recommend SSD and very few of my clients' computers have them.

SSD technology itself is self destructing.  This is physics, not opinion.  Each time a storage cell is written to, it wears.  It will eventually wear out and no longer store anything.  SSDs come with "spare" cells that are "substituted" when this happens.  Over time, an SSD will degrade.  This is not a matter opinion but a matter of fact.  It is not a question of whether this will happen, but how long before it becomes an unusable device.  That could  be a few months, a few years or a lot longer.  Very little is published.  Some (with significant financial incentives) will tell you that they will outlast a rotating hard drive.

Now lets look at what advantages an SSD  has over a rotating disk:

  • It is much more expensive (well I guess that is not an advantage after all)
  • It is faster because it does not have to wait for a rotation of a disk to find the data
  • It uses less power than a rotating drive
  • It runs cooler than a rotating drive

Some untruths:

  • It is not lighter (weight)
  • In a typical modern PC, SSDs are not perceptibly faster in normal operation once startup is complete

When a rotating drive begins to fail, it will give lots of warning signs to a vigilant user.  Providing the time needed to backup the data on another device.  When SSDs fail, they tend to fail suddenly and without warning.  If you use an SSD, you had better be vigilant about your backup habit.

Without question, startup time on a PC that is equipped with an SSD is significantly faster.  On a modern PC with an uncorrupted system, startup time will be less than half a minute.  The same PC with a rotating drive will be about a minute and a quarter.  If you startup your computer many times a day, that could add up to a few minutes, but the average user, this is not that significant.  In fact, in most cases SSDs are in laptops and people who use them seldom if ever shut their computers down.

By the way, I insist my clients' computers are equipped with 7200 rpm drives, not the slower 5400 rpm ones.  That makes  a significant difference.

However, once the computer is started, on a modern day computer there is no perceptible difference in responsiveness.  It is faster, but you'd have to  have a really good stop watch to be able to measure it.

The worst possible scenario would be using an SSD as a backup.  Backup requires writing and deleting massive amounts of data.  That means you are writing to a massive number of cells quite often.  That will lead to failure much quicker.

You could use a design in which the SSD is used only for programs and the OS itself, which would minimize write activity.  That would be the only way I would consider using an SSD.  But, that means the user must be aware of the two kinds of devices and ensure they do not start to inadvertently store their data on the SSD.  Most users are not that capable of doing that.
