nick_loss say:

Hi Rosie,

To sum it up, it is most definitely possible. Not that I am meaning to tout my audio engineering skills, nor my experience from the ripe age of 16 y/o back in 1995 DJ'ing in dark unlicensed venues, abandoned factories, underground parking garages, outdoor festivals etc....(yeah the early rave scene afforded me with the unexpected life skill of some technical knowledge!) The first time I tried this was in 1999 i think. I used WinXP and the standard Sound Recorder application that came with Windows. It was a decent enough quality to record my step father's odd collection of 1980's new-wave records and burn them to CD! 

To answer your question:
It all depends on your turntable. The signal from traditional vinyl decks (ie: the ones my parents used) are considerably weaker than the output from say, a CD player, or iPod. What we would do in this case would be to connect the turntable (using RCA cables) to a receiver of some sort (ie: my parent's stereo system). That receiver *must* have a specific input meant for "phono." Those phono ports on the receiver basically boosts the signal strength from the turntable to the level that you would get from a CD player/iPod. That's called *Line-Level*

Line-Level is the proper signal you would need to connect to your computer's Line-In. Connecting the phono-out from the turntable directly into the line-in on the computer would result is a horribly thin, weak recording. So, in this case, you would have to plug the turntable into your receiver (as described above); then you would need to take the stereo-out from the receiver and plug that in to you computer's line-in port. (Some receivers actually have an output specifically designed for this. It might be called a "Tape-Out" or "Record-Out."

*IF* your turntable was purchased in this century (kidding!), you may have a much simpler way to do this. If your turntable has a USB or Firewire port, then more than likely it has a built-in amplifier that will bring the phone signal up to line-level. Simply plug the usb/firewire in to you computer. Your computer should recognize it as a audio device (ie: audio interface)

*IF* your turntable has a "Line-out" port on the back, then it means it also has a built-in amplifier. Simply plug the line-out into the computer's line-in, and wham-bam-thank-you-ma'am, you've got a line-level signal going into your computer,

There are a number of alternative methods to connect a phono source to your computer...but these usually tend to be used by serious audiophiles.. This entails having separate devices such as phone-adapters, and/or audio interfaces to handle the analog2digital conversion.... Your computer's soundcard would be sufficient for most user's needs...  Here's a link to a helpful PDF. They have very helpful diagrams. Ignore most of the sales-pitch from the source; after-all they are attempting to get sales for their 3rd party software. And most definitely ignore their statements about MAC vs PC audio... They're heavily biased by a lack of understanding of PC components and Windows OS.
The diagrams are pretty accurate tho!

http://www.channld.com/Connecting%20a%20Turntable%20R1.pdf

 

good luck!
