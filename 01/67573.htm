peterleonard1 say:

The Shader model will depend on your video hardware.

If you press the Windows key + R, type DXDIAG and press enter, wait a few seconds for the utility to scan, then click the Display tab it will show your graphics hardware.

Or press Windows key + pause/break to bring up system info and click Device manager, click on video adapter.

Then simply look it up online to find shader model support.

As far as changing the graphics card is concerned it depends on your system, you might have onboard graphics (on the motherboard) or onchip graphics (built into the CPU) or dedicated graphics (an add in board), your system may or may not have an expansion slot for an add in card.

If you currently have an add in card have you checked it is being used by default? And are the graphics card drivers up to date?

Most systems with add in cards come shipped with any integrated (chip/motherboard) graphics disabled in the bios since the add in card is more powerful and generally has a better feature set.

There are some systems that have both types of graphics hardware enabled and switch between the 2 depending on which type of application is run, desktop and web for example uses the integrated, then if you start a game it switches to the dedicated graphics.

Giving your system specs here (Make, model, Windows version) would help someone give you more detailed advice.
