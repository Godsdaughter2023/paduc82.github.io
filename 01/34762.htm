Hi,

 

Welcome to Microsoft Community forums and thank you for posting the question.

 

As per the description, you need remove the boot entry from the startup. Perform the steps provided and check.

 

Method 1:

 

The Boot.ini file might still have an unnecessary Windows entry. To remove the unused entry, perform the following steps:

  1. Click Start.
  2. Right-click My Computer, and then left-click Properties.
  3. In the System Properties window, click the Advanced tab.
  4. Click Settings in the Startup & Recovery section.
  5. Click Edit next to To edit the startup options file manually, click Edit.
    The BOOT.INI-Notepad file opens.
  6. Locate the [boot loader] section and write down the entire default= line.
  7. Delete any entries under the [operating systems] section that do not exactly match the entry after default= in the [boot loader] section.
  8. Ensure that the default= line has not been changed and that there is at least one entry in the[operating systems] section that matches the default= line item.
  9. Click File, and then click Save.
  10. Click File, and then click Exit.
  11. Reboot the computer.  

 

Method 2:

 

If the issue still persists, the easiest way to remove an entry in the Windows 7 Boot Configuration is to use msconfig.

To launch msconfig:

1. Click Start
2. In the Search text box, type msconfig
3. Launch msconfig by selecting it from the search results above the search text box.

After msconfig is launched:

lemp say:
This is the most important part of your question:

There are 2 of these entries which do not seem to cause any problems that I am aware of.

You do not (necessarily) have a problem.

The messages that are reported in the "Windows Error Reporting" section of System Information appear to be selected entries from the application and system event logs (viewable by Start > Run > eventvwr > OK). 

I have no idea how these entries and not others are selected to be shown in "Windows Error Reporting."  On my system, they seem to be associated with reboots, but they are far from all the events associated with a reboot.

It would have been easier to explain if you had included the IP address, but assuming that you have a router, the way things work is that your router has a "DHCP server" that is responsible for assigning each device on your local network (i.e., on your side of the router as opposed to the Internet side) an IP address.  When the router assigns an IP address, it assigns it for a specified period of time, called a "lease."  The default typically is 24 hours.

For example, if you open a Command Prompt window (Start > Run > cmd > OK) and type ipconfig /all and then press Enter, you will see something similar to the following.  Note the last two lines (in bold)

Ethernet adapter Local Area Connection:

        Connection-specific DNS Suffix  . :
        Description . . . . . . . . . . . : Intel(R) PRO/100 Network Connection
        Physical Address. . . . . . . . . : 00-07-E9-ED-0C-47
        Dhcp Enabled. . . . . . . . . . . : Yes
        Autoconfiguration Enabled . . . . : Yes
        IP Address. . . . . . . . . . . . : 192.168.1.101
        Subnet Mask . . . . . . . . . . . : 255.255.255.0
        Default Gateway . . . . . . . . . : 192.168.1.1
        DHCP Server . . . . . . . . . . . : 192.168.1.1
        DNS Servers . . . . . . . . . . . : 4.2.2.3
                                                         205.171.3.65
        Lease Obtained. . . . . . . . . . : Sunday, May 06, 2012 5:14:14 PM

Normally, the client (your computer) re-negotiates the lease with the DHCP server before the expiration time and the expiration time is then re-set for another 24 hours.  See http://www.tcpipguide.com/free/t_DHCPLeaseLifeCycleOverviewAllocationReallocationRe.htm

If for some reason the computer can't communicate with the router towards the end of the lease period, the lease may expire.  This usually is not a big deal, because the next time you turn things on, the computer will again negotiate for an IP address and get a new lease.

If you're concerned -- and particularly if you have noticed any connection problems -- open the Event Viewer as described above and double-click on the "System" log.  Click on the header of the "Source" column to sort the entries by "Source."  Look for entries from "Dhcp." 

Double-click on a "Dhcp" entry to open its Properties page and confirm it is one of the errors relating to a lost lease.  Close the Properties page and make a note of the date and time of the entry.  Now click on the header of the "Date" column so that the entries are sorted by date.  Scroll down to the date and time that you noted and find the Dhcp error or warning that you found earlier.  Examine the entries around the same time to see what else might have been going on to cause the problem.  You can also look in the "Application" log at the same date and time.

If you find one or more events that seem too obscure for you, open the Properties page and click on the "Copy" icon just below the up/down arrows.  You can then paste the data into a Reply here.

lemp say:
None of those three events is particularly unusual in themselves.  As I said, you need to look in the event logs at around the time those events occurred to see what might have been happening.  You probably won't see very much.

I note that all of those entries are far in the past.  If that is all you have, you don't have anything to worry about.

The explanatory material at the tcpipguide.com site that I linked earlier is helpful in understanding what is going on.

A likely explanation for the first event (Event ID 1002) is the following:

  1. The computer is turned on, negotiates with the router's DHCP server, and is assigned IP address 192.168.1.33.
  2. Sometime later, the computer is turned off.
  3. The lease on 192.168.1.33 times out and expires, making this address available for assignment.
  4. While the first computer is off, another computer (or other network device, such as a wireless network printer) is turned on, negotiates with the router's DHCP server, and is assigned IP address 192.168.1.33.
  5. Now the first computer is turned back on.  It "remembers" its previous IP address of 192.168.1.33 and attempts a "reallocation" of that address.
  6. The DHCP server knows that the address has been assigned to another device and so responds DHCPNACK -- sorry, you can't have it because someone else is already using it.

A similar scenario can result in the Event ID 1000 "lost lease" warning.


In either case, if things are working correctly -- and you have given no indication that they are not -- your computer simply requests another IP address from the router, it gets assigned, and things proceed.


If your event log is still showing events from last December, you don't have much going on.  You really don't need to worry about these DHCP events.
