 (If you follow these forums you'll find widespread confusion and misunderstanding of this infection.  The usual advice is to run sfc or a firewall related Microsoft fixit.  These actions don't hurt but will not fix the problem of missing or damaged services.)

Firstly, you need to make sure active infections are removed.  Be sure to run the following tool in addition to the usual tools like Malwarebytes and SuperAntiSpyware.
TDSS Killer
http://support.kaspersky.com/5353?el=88446

Then, download the Seven.zip set of registry fixes from
http://www.smartestcomputing.us.com/files/download/9-registry-network-keys/
Run bfe.reg and see if the service is back after a reboot.  If so, see if it will start (often not, due to other damaged dependencies).  There are usually multiple damaged keys and you will likely to need to run others of the included fixes as well.  Look for a missing Security Center and Windows Firewall service.

The damage from Sirefef can be extremely difficult to repair. There are usually multiple registry keys involved. Sometimes it is necessary to manually fix permission in the registry.  A repair reinstallation (an update installation type in Windows 7) may (emphasize may) work.  If all else fails, you will end up having to do a clean Windows reinstallation.

Keeping a battery from burning out - Microsoft Community

nboscaino say:

Keeping a battery from burning out

I was told that these lithium Ion Batteries basically have a type of memory where they will sort of store how many "starts" they go through and then eventually will not charge anymore.  My experience in the past almost legitimizes this claim because my past puter battery dramatically died on me.  Conversely, the person who recommended me not start my computer or run it with it's battery unless I needed to says that he has never had a battery fail on him in 8 years of using a laptop.  Question:  Should I resist keeping my battery in when there is a power source from the wall available. 

Related Question:  Wanting to take advantage of the sleep mode; would I have to leave the battery in to allow the computer to go to "sleep" or "hibernate?"

chris cupler say: The bigger issues with LiIon batteries are:
- Heat.  Computers make a lot of it, and the battery bay is nearly always located in the hottest area of a laptop.  Heat reduces the battery life.
- "charge holding."  LiIon batteries simply do not like being held at 100% charge indefinitely. 

Your person is right, to a point.  The battery will still fail, given time - every time you run on battery, you take away just a little bit of its capacity.  There's no way that his battery still lasts just as long now as it did 8 years ago (assuming that he's talking about using the same laptop for the last 8 years, rather than using several different laptops over the last 8 years. ;)  )

Sleep/hibernation is dependent on your power plan settings, not on the battery being there or not. 
To wit:  my laptop only has its battery installed when it's in transit or actually needs to be used sans AC cord.  95% of the time, it's on AC, with the battery removed.  It sleeps just fine.  :)

HTH,
Chris
