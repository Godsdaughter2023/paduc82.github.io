arminatorx say:

and what does the connect button do on controller?

The connect button starts the pairing process with a Xbox 360 console or the Wireless Gaming Receiver for Windows. 

The Xbox 360 controller uses a proprietary wireless protocol, and NOT Bluetooth. So the standard wireless devices in your computer can't work with the Xbox 360 wireless controller. 

You absolutely definitely need the Wireless Gaming Receiver for Windows if you wish to use your Xbox 360 controller on your PC. 

Make sure you do NOT get the Wireless Gaming Receiver for Xbox One controllers, as they won't work with Xbox 360 controllers. 

Also if you try to use a Play & Charge USB cable on your Xbox 360 controller, this will also not work, as the Play & Charge cable ONLY carries power, but does NOT transmit controller data. 

So if you don't want to buy a wireless receiver, your only options are to buy a dedicated wired Xbox 360 controller (that has a non-removable USB cord attached), or get a Xbox One controller with Bluetooth functionality. 

arminatorx say:

It's a proprietary (in laymen's terms: "secret") protocol that Microsoft specifically developed for the Xbox 360 wireless communication between console and accessories. 

There is no "generic" hardware like a Bluetooth adapter or anything that can communicate with the Xbox 360, so either you get the "official" Xbox 360 Wireless Gaming Receiver for Windows, or you can't connect the Xbox 360 controller wirelessly to your computer.

Well, my daughter doesn't experience any slowness at boot-up or during normal operations.  All works well.  I believe the green, as opposed to blue, screen means something but I can't remember what that is.  I think it's a Microsoft thing.

Though she isn't having any trouble, if your computer is booting up slowly after installing Windows 10, I would look at your devices.  During boot-up if a device (especially a CD-DVD ROM drive) does not function correctly, Windows will take it's time trying to communicate with it, and so, start very slowly.  If boot-up takes a long time, then look at the CD-ROM drive, but if it's just a bit slow compared to Win 7 & 8, then look for a device that needs a Windows 10 driver. To find the problem device you will need to go into the Device Manager.  Just type that into Cortana's search box and you will be able to get to it.  If a device is not functioning properly you will notice that it is marked as such (don't remember the exact marking but you will know by the look of it).  You can double-click on the specific device topic and then right click on the specific device with the problem.  Your first choice in the list will be to update the driver software.  If that doesn't work, you may need to go to the manufacturer's website to get the best driver for Windows 10 and install it through their installer software. 

Windows 10 normally boots faster than Win 7 or 8.  So, either a device does not have a proper Windows 10 driver or a CD-DVD ROM drive is not working correctly.  That's assuming that your actual start-up drive is functioning properly - if mechanical, they do start to have problems with age, and can mimic Windows problems.  Mine did before it finally died and I had to replace it, as did my daughter's desktop some year earlier.

Anyway, just my thought here.  May help; may not.
