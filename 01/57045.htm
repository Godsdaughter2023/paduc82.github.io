A couple of observations (which may not solve your problem, but it's late)

1.  The "Teredo Tunneling Pseudo-Interface" is part of IPv6 (the newer version of TCP/IP).  This is almost never needed in home computers, especially XP computers.  (BTW, what version of XP is installed, including service pack?)

  • Open Network Connections (Start > Run > ncpa.cpl > OK)
  • Right-click on the icon for your network adapter (you should do this on both the Local Area Connection (wired) and the Wireless adapter) and select Properties.
  • On the General tab, look in the list of "This connection uses the following items"
  • Most Windows XP computers should have at most only the following 4 items

    • Client for Microsoft Networks
    • QoS Packet Scheduler
    • File and Printer Sharing for Microsoft Networks
    • Internet Protocol (TCP/IP)

  • If you see an item for IPv6, select it and click the Uninstall button
  • OK your way out

2.  I'm a bit skeptical of that KB article.  I would have expected the Registry key to refer to "CurrentControlSet" rather than "ControlSet001" 

No matter, however.  Use the Services applet to confirm the proper dependency:

  • Click Start > Run > services.msc > OK
  • Scroll down to DHCP Client and double-click on it
  • Click the Dependencies tab and wait until it is populated.  It should show

    • AFD Network Support Environment
    • NetBT
    • TCP/IP Protocol Driver

      If you expand (by clicking the +), TCP/IP Protocol Driver will also be under NetBT and IPSEC driver will be under TCP/IP Protocol Driver (both places)


Assuming that everything is there (and it probably is), the next thing to check is if all of those "Dependencies" are up and running.

  • Open a Command Prompt window (Start > Run > cmd > OK)
  • Type the following, pressing Enter after each line

    sc query afd
    sc query netbt
    sc query tcpip
    sc query ipsec

    chinchanchu say:

    Hi Pedro,

    You can perform a rollback for the driver that is affected, in your case that is your display driver.

    Here's how you can do it:

    1. Open runbox by pressing and holding Windows key + R
    2. Type in "devmgmt.msc" without the quotation marks
    3. Look for display driver, double click it and the model of your videocard should be appearing.
    4. Right click it, it's either AMD/NVIDEA/Intel, choose properties.
    5. Click on Roll Back Driver, once done you will be prompted to restart the computer.

    If the Roll Back Driver button is grayed out, manually update the driver for your videocard, here's how you can do it:

    1. Open runbox by pressing and holding Windows key + R
    2. Type in "devmgmt.msc" without the quotation marks
    3. Look for display driver, double click it and the model of your videocard should be appearing.
    4. Right click it, it's either AMD/NVIDEA/Intel, choose properties.
    5. Click on Update Driver.
    6. You will see two options, click Browse my computer for driver software.
    7. Two options will be showing again, this time, choose Let me pick from a list of device drivers on my computer.
    8. You will see a list of the drivers that are previously installed on your machine with the date, try to choose the oldest date.

    Let me know if this will work on your computer.
