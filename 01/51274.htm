adamshields say:

I had a similar problem on my Xbox. While I cannot directly say it's the same thing, you can be the judge. The difference between your two device (Apple and Xbox) would be your Xbox is decoding by your choice into 5.1. This means that when you hit Netflix episodes which are not transmitting in 5.1, they are getting null data on those channels. This means they do not transmit sound. On a forum dealing with this very topic, one person suggested that the output on the Xbox be handled in "Stereo" and allow your receiver to encode the output in a 5.1 method.

My solution has been different however.

My receiver has an "All Channel Stereo" button. When watching shows where the 5.1 is not an option, I simply turn on this feature for those sounds and receive the equivalent to 2.0 audio that is loud and crystal clear.

The short answer: Your Xbox is doing what you have requested, but that means that when 2.1 sound gets transmitted, the last speakers are not given any audio. It's still "technically" transmitting 5.1. There's just nothing on the channel.  

It's best to remember that the receiver is meant to decode this information. You are like me, you thought the Xbox should handle it, and it should... if we plugged it directly into a speaker system. We fronted the money for good audio systems, we should let them do that job.

tron nz say:

Hi Darkstar,

The Xbox is working 100% as it should be. If the Xbox source signal is 5.1 i.e a game, then the audio stream to the receiver is 5.1 and the receiver will output 5.1 audio to the speakers. However if the Xbox audio source signal is 2.0 i.e music, then the stream to the receiver is 2.0 and the receiver will not magically output 5.1 audio to the speakers.

This situation will happen for HDMI or optical using DTS or Dolby decoding etc. The Xbox will not simulate 2.0 up to 5.1. You will need to use one of the surround effects (simulation) on your receiver to scale the 2.0 audio  to 5.1 audio. Sorry its not what you want to hear, but this is how it works :)

andre da costa say:

Press Windows key + X

Click Disk Management

To the right of the System Partition where Windows 10 is installed, you should see the other partitions, right click each and click Delete. If you have any important data on them, make sure it is backed up.

Do not SYSTEM and C:\ partitions where Recovery and Windows files are located.

After you have deleted all those partitions, there should be a single volume next to the C:\ that will now appear as Unallocated. Our next step is to merge back that unallocated free space with the system partition. Right click the System volume and click the ‘Extend Volume’ option on the contextual menu, click ‘Yes’ when the warning appears.

A wizard will now begin that will guide through the steps to merge back the unallocated space with your system partition.

The wizard provides a simple procedure to merge back the unallocated space with the system drive. Once you have selected the space, click Next, at the end of the wizard, you will see the amount allocated.
