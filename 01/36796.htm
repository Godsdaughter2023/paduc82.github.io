Hello, 

I had a similar problem. Thankfully, I was able to find the solution. It is a specific driver. If you are having the same problems please follow these steps: 

1) Go to Device Manager (Windows Key+X, select it) 
2) Under the "Mice and other pointing devices" list find "USB Touchscreen controller", or the driver that's not your mouse/touchpad. 
3) Right click to select "Properties", click the "Driver" tab, and click "Roll back". 
4) After confirming, this will remove the driver from that list but bring up a bunch of new ones under "Human Interface Devices", where the touchscreen stuff logically should live. 
5) Make sure that all the other drivers are updated EXCEPT "USB input device". This is the one that moves it all to the mouse drivers. 

After a restart the driver didn't automatically reinstall like they usually do. If it does, run the "Show or hide updates troubleshooter" found here.  

I would like to think this is an issue from eGalax Touch, which is responsible for the driver, but if you do a search for "egalax touch acts like mouse" this is an issue dating back to Windows 7. Either a) Microsoft should stop doing business with them, b) Microsoft should provide a fix for how the driver interacts with the OS, or c) let the consumer know that there is an issue and how to fix it. Because it's the SAME ISSUE.

    Hello,

    OK, that frame rate will simply cost you $75 - $125 more. But that's not your biggest problem. When you start going up in video card quality you find that your power supply requirements also go up as well. At the frame rate you'er talking about, you're going to need a 500 watt power supply unit (PSU), at a minimum, with 700 - 800 watt PSU preferred. Unless you had an unusual custom system built, you probably have a 180 - 250 watt PSU. That'll add another $75 onto the cost (plus install if needed).

     

    In addition to the PSU upgrade, an upgraded video may well require an upgraded motherboard for two reasons:  1) the connector adapter that's on your current motherboard may not be advanced enough for the type of video card you want now (likely);  and 2) there may be a space issue as some of these cards take up two spaces on the motherboard.

     

    And now you're talking real money. Once you upgrade your video card, the quality that you see it on, e.g., your monitor, will also come into question. So you have the cost of the video card, possibly a new motherboard, and a power supply unit, and you are talking a major re-build of your system, probably costing close to $600 in parts alone, which is far more than the $100 video upgrade we originally talked about.

    Hello,

     

    Thank you for visiting Microsoft Community.

     

    As per the description, I understand that you’re getting a message saying battery is not charging. I will certainly help you with the issue.

     

    Sometimes it is difficult to determine if a problem is caused by the hardware failing to function according to manufacturer specifications. If a battery is not charging/charging, it is either the cable is faulty or the charger component on the motherboard that is not working properly.

    Please try these basic steps and check.

    • I would suggest you to remove the battery and reinsert it, to make sure it’s connected firmly.

    • Make sure to use the original adapter that came with your Windows 7 Pc.

    • Check for any damage or loose connection on the charging port of the tablet.

    • Connect the charger to the wall socket directly instead of a surge protector while charging.

     

    Follow the methods if above steps doesn't help.

     

    Method 1: Use the hardware troubleshooter.

    The Hardware Troubleshooter is an automated tool which will check the hardware connected to the computer for any known issues with it and provides the details and on how to fix them. Follow these steps and run the troubleshooter.

    1. Connect the charger to the computer.

    2. Click on Start.

    3. Go to Control Panel.

    4. Type troubleshooting in the search tab and press Enter.

    5. Under Hardware and Sound.

    6. Click Configure a device.

    7. Follow the on screen instructions.

     

    If the issue persist, follow method 2.

     

    Method 2: Disable USB selective suspend setting.

    This method prevents your USB device from powering down.

    1. Click the Start button, type power plan in the Search box, then click Choose a power plan.

    2. Next to your currently selected plan, click Change Plan Settings.

    3. Click Change advanced power settings.

    4. Click the box to expand USB Settings, then expand USB selective suspend settings.

    5. Click plugged in, then click the drop down menu and select disabled.

    6. If you're using a laptop, click Battery, then click the drop down menu and select disabled. 

    7. Click Apply, then click OK.

     

    Hope this information is helps.
