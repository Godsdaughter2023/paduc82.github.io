a. user say:

Windows Mail is not an email client like Microsoft Office Outlook or Mozilla Thunderbird. A 'client' in this instance means a program that you install on your computer. Email clients like Outlook and Thunderbird have spam filters that you can customize to your liking, or you can dispense with the spam filter and instead decide on your own what is and isn't spam, which seems to be what you prefer.

Windows Mail is really just an interface for your webmail accounts like GMail or Outlook.com or Yahoo!, etc., which do their own spam filtering. There's very little that you can do to control a webmail provider's spam filter. If there's spam that the webmail provider's own filter isn't stopping you can create a custom filter for your account, and if the webmail provider filtered a message that isn't spam you can mark it 'not spam,' but that's as far as you can go.

This is just my opinion, and you're welcome to disagree, but I never understood why someone would want to use Windows Mail when it's so easy to go straight to GMail, Outlook.com, etc., with one click on a bookmark. What does Windows Mail add that you can't do with any of the webmail providers? Additional software is one more potential point of failure.

Leaving aside the question of how to find your file, let me make the
following point:

The recovery drive (partition) was put there by your computer's
manufacturer to enable you to reinstall (recover) Windows should it be
necessary. It is provided instead of a Windows DVD.

You should never do anything to that drive. Do not write files to
it, do not delete files from it, do not do anything else.

The only thing you should do is follow the manufacturer's instructions
to burn the contents to a DVD so you can reinstall Windows from the
DVD if the drive should fail. That should be the first thing you do
when you get the computer, and if you haven't done it yet, you should
do it ASAP.
