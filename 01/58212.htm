a. user say:

Looking back at my previous reply I noted:

SAS will probably just find a bunch of tracking cookies that you can delete.

I believe that there is no antivirus/antimalware tool yet that knows about everything so I think it is a good idea to use a few of the ones that seem to have the highest esteem.

Some folks will say "I know my system does not have a virus because I ran Norton (or some other program) and it came back clean".

That does not necessarily mean your system is not infected.  What it means to me is that Norton didn't find anything that is knows about and no AV program seems to know about everything.  And new threats are discovered every day so being one day behind in your AV definitions could allow something to be missed.

I think if you look in other Windows help communities where infected systems are cleaned up most of them start with at least the free version of Malwarebytes.   The free version does not provide realtime protection so you have to run it manually once in a while.

SUPERAntiSpyware has been known to pick up a few things that MBAM might miss (I haven't seen that in a while though) and is especially good at picking up tracking cookies which may be harmless to system behavior but some folks just don't like them.  You may have hundreds/thousands accumulate after a while - just whack them all.

Both will have shorter scan times if you clean your system of temporary Internet files first (much less to scan).

For free things those two coupled with MSE (which does provide realtime protection) are adequate for folks that don't do much unsafe Internet surfing.

I would not use or recommend any programs that scan your system for outdated drivers (or something like that) as you should only get drivers from your hardware manufacturers WWW site and only when you know that an update will fix a specific problem - not because some program told you it was needed.

Some of those sites have a free scan feature that will report lots of issues and "critical" errors and then want to charge you to unlock or obtain the solution and most will say that falls into the "scam" category targeted at naive users.

I laugh thinking about automobiles...  "Come in for a free AC check" or "Free check engine light analysis".  Sure...  the check up is free but they are bound to tell you that there is a critical problem that needs immediate attention and it probably isn't going to be cheap.  And you didn't even know you had a problem...

Entering PPPoE - Microsoft Community

xb1r athena say:

Entering PPPoE

Login Information

  • Gateway – 192.168.1.1
  • Username - admin
  • Password - admin

Entering PPPoE

  1. Open a web browser.
  2. Log into the router by entering the default gateway into the address bar of the internet browser. (Example 192.168.1.1)
  3. The router will then prompt for a user name and password. The default is that user name is “admin” and the password is “admin”.
  4. Select Setup from the top menu.
  5. Select Basic setup from the sub menu.
  6. From the Internet Connection Type dropdown select PPPoE.
  7. Enter your PPPoE username and password.
  8. Click Save Settings.
