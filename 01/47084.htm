tryx3 say:

Caritas,

You need to turn on the metered connection to gain control over when you download Windows updates.  You will need to do updates at some stage [you cannot just ignore them].  I often use my local library's free WiFi network for this.

1  The large updates you've just had are known as "Cumulative updates" [CU].  For 64 bit Windows they are generally almost 1GB whilst for 32 bit Windows they are generally about 500MB.  This month, Microsoft issued a CU a week or so ago {that is possibly the one you mentioned in your original post} that proved to be faulty so they had to issue a replacement CU on 13th December.  Each of these would have used up about 1GB of your allowance.  CUs can always be downloaded separately and installed later on so you do not need to use up your allowance for them if you sometimes have access to an unlimited data connection.

2  I have found that other Windows updates generally take up to a hundred MBs a month but can sometimes use up more.

3  There are also the installed Windows "Modern" Apps to consider [Mail, Calendar, Maps, Edge, ...].  Their updates can also be several hundred MBs each month.  If you are logged on to your computer with an MSAccount [rather than a "Local user account"] then App updates will happen automatically despite the metered setting unless you turn them off in your Windows Store online settings [you cannot turn them off on the computer itself].  I sign on to my computer with a Local user account in order to avoid these updates.  I always wait until my next library visit to update them because I regard them as mostly useless and I'm only updating them at all because I want to avoid leaving potential security vulnerabilities on my computers.  I have an MSAccount that I log on to just for this purpose

4  Your other applications will also have updates issued for them if they are still supported by their publishers. 

  • My MS Office 2007 gets small security-related updates that are generally much less than a hundred MB a month but later versions of MS Office get much larger updates. 
  • MS Office 2016 apparently gets several hundred MBs of updates each month, so others have stated in this forum, and its updates are not always managed by Windows update so they occur irrespective of the metered setting.  They have to be deliberately turned off for day-to-day running and then turned back on again & run manually when you are on an unlimited connection for a while [again, a local library can be an appropriate place for doing this]. 

5  Your antimalware application also needs to be updated.  Updates can often be issued several times a day but are generally only a few MB so I never worry about them.  The one exception I have found to this is Bitdefender; its updates are upwards of 3GB a month [this is not a typo or an experimental error] because Bitdefender does not download changes to its malware definitions, it completely replaces its existing definitions with each update.

I have been keeping two Windows 10 computers updated using my 6GB a month connection and it normally uses up about 2GB a month for the pair, ignoring Windows "Modern" App updates, but any reissued CU rather wrecks this.  My local library is on my route to the supermarket so I often stop off on the way - 

  • I update the Windows "Modern" Apps,
  • I download any new CUs for installation later on unless I am in a hurry & decide to use my own connection for them, and
  • I also check for updates to my other applications.

My advice on this subject is in Manage data usage and Windows updates in Windows 10 - WiKi  I am currently working on a significantly improved scheme for managing updates.  I had hoped to publish this a month ago but I have had to concentrate on some high priority tasks instead.  Now that I have seen your question I will make sure I post back here when the improvements are published but do make use of the existing guidance now as it remains valid [the improvements are additions to the guidance rather than replacements].

All the best,

Denis

Caritas,

You need to find out what is using your internet connection.

1  Check for Windows updates

1.1  Look  in Event viewer [it's in the Start menu within the group Windows administrative tools]

1.4  On the right-hand side, click on Filter current log

1.5  In the middle of the dialog you'll see <All event IDs>, click on that to overtype it with 31, 41 then OK

1.6  On the right-hand side, click on Save filter to Custom view, call it Windows updates - Windows log and enter a description Windows updates, WinDefr updates, App updates - also shows failures then click on OK. 

1.7  Saving this as a Custom view will allow you to see the latest results in the future by selecting this filter on the left-hand side from within the Custom views group.

1.8  So, has Windows updated or tried to update?  If so, what?

2  Check Windows apps

2.1  Right-click on the Taskbar and select Task manager.

2.2  If its bottom-left shows More details then click on it to show them

2.3  Select the App history tab

2.4  Click on the column header Metered network to see its usage by Windows apps in descending order.

2.5  Have Windows apps been using your network?

2.6  If so, you can control them to some extent at Settings, Privacy, Background apps.  If you allow them to operate in the background at all then your permission extends to letting them update in the background as well.  Personally, I just turned them all off a year ago to give me time to find out more about their background operations but I have never been able to find out anything useful about the subject so they are still all off.

2.7  I also prevent them updating by logging in to a Local user account not an MSAccount-linked user account. 

2.8  I do not know how Background apps operations relate to Live tile operations [para 3].

2.9  You will also find some entries for Windows apps in the internet data usage records for the last 30 days - Settings, Network & internet, Data usage, Usage details.

3  Check Live tiles

3.1  In your Start screen [the right-hand side of your Start menu], you probably have Tiles.  I have some Dell ones & some Office ones in this illustration.

3.2  Right click on every Tile in turn, hover your cursor over More then check if the entry Turn Live tile off appears. 

3.3  If so, consider what you want that application or app to do and decide if you want to let it operate automatically.  Just by way of example, if you use the Mail app to check your email then you might want to let that operate.  Personally, I just turned them all off a year ago to give me time to find out more about Live tile operations but I have never been able to find out anything useful about the subject.  So I just tidied them up a bit using Unpin from Start.

3.4  I do not know how Live tile operations relate to Background apps operations [para 2.6 above].

4  Check your anti-malware application. 

4.1  All decent anti-malware applications update their malware definitions regularly so they are ready to detect the latest threats.  Most of them only use a few MB a day to do so [Bitdefender uses a hundred MBs a day].

4.2  The anti-malware applications themselves can also get updated.

4.3  Many anti-malware applications also have a regular discourse with their parent website throughout the day.

4.4  They all differ.  You might need to consult their Help system, user manual, support website & user forum to find out how it uses the internet.

4.5  Windows Defender is prettyy straightforward as it is updated as part of Windows updates unless you have followed my advice about updating it using Task scheduler.

5  Check your other applications

5.1  HP might have pre-installed some "helpful" utilities.  You'll have to check each one [they are probably all in the Start menu within a group called HP stuff or similar].  Check for internet usage while it is doing its job and for any setting that allows it to update automatically.  You might find out information about them in the HP website or in the HP user forums [a link to which is given at the top of the HP support page].

5.2  You might also have installed applications.  You'll have to check the Start menu entry for each one.  Check for internet usage while it is doing its job and for any setting that allows it to update automatically.  You might find out information about them in the publisher's website or in their user forums [if they have any].

5.3  Check the internet data usage records for the last 30 days - Settings, Network & internet, Data usage, Usage details.

6  Check what level of data you are sending to Microsoft at Settings, Privacy, Feedback & diagnostics.  I have mine set to the bare minimum.

Still not working

Check your antivirus software

In your antivirus app, look for settings related to blocking access or permission to use your webcam. For help, visit your antivirus software company’s website.
Check for an outdated webcam driver

Select the Start button, type Device Manager, and then select Device Manager from the search results.
Find your webcam under Imaging devices or Sound, video and game controllers.
Press and hold (or right-click) the name of your webcam, and then select Properties.
Select the Driver tab, select the Driver Details button, and look for a file name that includes stream.sys. If it’s there, your webcam was designed before Windows 7 and you'll have to replace it with a newer webcam.
If you don't find a file name that includes stream.sys, try rolling back your webcam driver.

Roll back your webcam driver

In Device Manager, press and hold (or right-click) your webcam, and then select Properties.
Select the Driver tab, select Roll Back Driver, and then choose Yes. (Note that some drivers don’t provide a rollback option.)
After the rollback is complete, restart your PC, and then try opening the Camera app again.
If rolling back didn’t work or isn’t available, uninstall your webcam driver and scan for hardware changes.

Uninstall your webcam driver and scan for hardware changes

In Device Manager, press and hold (or right-click) your webcam, and then select Properties.
Select the Driver tab, select Uninstall > Delete the driver software for this device, and then select OK.
In Device Manager, on the Action menu, select Scan for hardware changes. Wait for it to scan and reinstall updated drivers, restart your PC, and then try opening the Camera app again.
If your webcam still doesn’t work, try looking for help on the webcam manufacturer’s website.
