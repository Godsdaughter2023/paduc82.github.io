erdeslawe say: In reply to deleted message

A very well reasoned appraisal of the true realities of life for early adopters of any major new software.  

The only thing I would add is that it's ridiculous to expect to jump past two public releases of any operating system's upgrades over a 3-year period and then expect the next release to work just like the one you just left behind, i.e. Windows 7 to Windows 10 will obviously be a steep learning curve at the start. There is absolutely no point in the "But I knew how to do this in Windows 7, so why has it so hard?" approach.

Facts of Life: Old Hardware + New Software = Problems

Also consider this:

1. It is widely known and a well publicised fact that many of the major computer manufacturers expressed a great deal concern when it became known that Windows 10 would NOT require a higher basic computer specification than Windows 7/8.1. - Why? : The negative impact on their future computer sales if hardware upgrades weren't necessary.

2. Every new Operating System Upgrade, irrespective of the Developer (Microsoft, Apple, Linux)) requires some updates to hardware 'Drivers', user software etc. - What's the impact on computer and peripheral manufacturer's? - The costs associated with providing those updates without the offset of new sales.

3. Manufacturer's responses: Largely to limit those essential software updates to supporting only those PC's models and peripherals and software releases still being sold; or those likely to be within the standard warranty/support contract period.  Why? The expectation that (a) the public will be forced to purchase new hardware if/when they want to run Window's 10 when their 'old' system can't be successfully upgraded; and (b) the expectation that public will mostly blame Microsoft because it has offered a 'free' upgrade entitlement that for some doesn't prove to be easy to 'cash in'; or is even sometimes impossible to achieve. 

4. Has Microsoft made mistakes? Obviously it has, even though it has millions of volunteer Testers in it's Insider Programme; but so have others, including many Windows computer users who failed to do their homework before rushing down the upgrade path.
