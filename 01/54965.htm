spiritx say:

Hi,

If someone is Spoofing emails that appear to be from you then they probably never
had access to your email account. Instead they either have a copy of your contacts
list or just an email from you or one to you where you were one of the recipients. Those
could have been gleaned from either your computer, a friend's computer, or by hacking
any site or intercepting an email from you or to you (you may not have been the main
recipient).

Once they have that information there is nothing you can do to prevent them from
sending out the Spoofed emails. Warn your friends that it is happening and they
might be able to setup rules such that the spoofs are handled like SPAM or JUNK
mail.

I have even seen Spoofed email that appears to be from a valid user name however
that name does not have an email account on the email system used in the Spoof.
Such as SpiritX - AT - realmail.com might be a valid account however the Spoof used
SpiritX - AT - wrongmail.com. That latter might still be thought a valid email from
SpiritX if it was allowed through JUNK and SPAM filtering. (The - AT - was used
in place of @ so that those addresses would not be filtered out by Answers auto-
magically.)

============================================================

Do an extremely thorough check for malware and then change your email password.

The reason you want to do this is to ensure no further hacking of your computer is
still in effect (if it was hacked). You changed the password once however I would
change it again after these checks.

Be aware that it is possible for someone to send email that "appears" to be from your
account but that actually isn't - called "Spoofing". Which means its "Possible" the emails
were not actually from you at all, though they may have your contact list. Have someone
that is receiving the emails to check the header to see where it is actually sent from and
NOT just the reply to address.

    Hi Vikram,

    Thank you for posting in Community.

    We understand your concern and we will provide the assistance required to help you resolve the issue.

    I would like to inform that under Windows Reset you will find three options, ie, Keep my Files, Remove Everything and Factory Settings.

    1. Keep my Files- This option has the same functions as Replace Inplace Upgrade in Windows 7. This option will repair all the defects or flaws that your OS has attracted and replace the OS without harming or deleting your personal files and folders. Even your personal settings will remain the same.

    2. Remove Everything- As the name suggest this option will remove all the settings, files and folders that were stored in your PC. Thus making your PC behave like you have performed a Clean Install.

    3. Factory Settings- This reset will return/restore your PC to the original state, that is, it will install the OS that came originally with your PC at the time of purchase.

    If you want to create a back up of your computer along with the settings, I would suggest that you perform System Image as this will enable you to create a copy of your OS and restore it in a no boot scenario.

    Depends on your needs - in general the wisest choice is to buy retail if purchasing just the OS.  It can follow you from computer-to-computer (uninstall on old, put on new) where the OEM is a one-shot deal.

    Windows XP Professional does not come with both the 32-bit and 64-bit editions - those were separate products.  Windows Vista and Windows 7 were the first to offer the 32-bit or 64-bit in a single purchase.

    Technically speaking - if you change the motherboard in your OEM licensed Windows system for a reason other than authorized repair of defective motherboard by the OEM (even if it is you) then you have to buy a new license of Windows.

    From any point of view other than licensing - there is no difference between a generic OEM and retail edition of Windows.
