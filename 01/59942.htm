Both routers are issuing addresses on the same subnet and have the same LAN side address

Routers distinguish traffic which need to be routed by the separating the LAN side and WAN side into two separate LANs through the subnets each LAN is on. Your Bridging router should have its WAN port on the ISP side an address in the 192.168.1.X subnet. It is then issuing addresses also in the same range so LAN and WAN are on the same subnet and it cannot work out what traffic to route.

Modify the settings on your bridging router.

Set its LAN side IP as 192.168.2.1 and change the 3rd octet value in its DHCP range from .1 to .2 (192.168.2.Y to 192.168.2.Z)

That then means its LAN side is in the new range and its WAN side is in the ISP router range and it can route traffic. ipconfig /release and ipconfig /renew should give the PC a new address and it should work.

There is another change to optimise performance. The bridging router requests an IP address and DNS server address from the ISP router. It then acts as a DNS relay for your PC sending any requests for addresses to the ISP router which is itself a DNS relay. Slightly better performance can be achieved by setting a fixed address on the WAN side and setting the DNS server directly to your ISP's ones. It then cuts out the lag generated in the ISP router as a relay device.
