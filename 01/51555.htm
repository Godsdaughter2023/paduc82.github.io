a. user say:

I understand what you're saying. I don't mean to argue, just want to explain the way it is:

Microsoft doesn't choose whether to support old hardware. Hardware manufacturers choose whether to support a new operating system. It's not in Microsoft's best interests to give people reasons not to upgrade to Windows 10.  If it were up to Microsoft, all hardware would run on Windows 10.

But it's not up to Microsoft. In the first place, Microsoft doesn't have detailed knowledge about how every hardware device works. In the second place, Microsoft has no legal right to modify another company's intellectual property. For its part, Microsoft makes detailed information about new versions of Windows available to hardware manufacturers. Then it's up to the manufacturers to go along or not.

It's no simple task to make old hardware work on a new operating system. You don't just throw in a few new lines of code and call it a day. All that re-coding and testing and re-re-coding and re-testing costs a hardware manufacturer money. Meanwhile, the manufacturers are doing the math about how much money they stand to make for their troubles. It's the bottom line that drives their decision.
