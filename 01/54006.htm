theofficemaven say: Actually, what you're seeing there is completely normal behavior for a "rapid hybrid drive". Once you've filled up your machine with more than 128 GB (119 GB actual) of data, then the Intel Smart Response Technology has to start making (somewhat educated) guesses as to what to cache onto the fast SSD, and what to leave over on the slow HDD. Your two benchmarks are simply showing the cases where the cache either hits its mark, or misses it terribly. That's the bummer about using a caching setup (and why it would be better for you to upgrade your storage to full SSD and get rid of the "rapid hybrid drive" completely). Obviously, the caching will get better over time as it starts to understand what files you use the most (i.e. what to keep cached on the fast SSD), and what files you use infrequently (i.e. what to keep stored over on the slow SSD). In "real world" use, I'm sure that Microsoft found the "rapid hybrid drive" to have acceptable performance for the "average" user. However, benchmarks are never gonna show that.

I completely understand why Microsoft went with the "rapid hybrid drive" setup on the Surface Studio though. Basically, it's already a really expensive machine, and they most likely didn't want to further increase the cost of it by installing expensive 1 TB or 2 TB SSDs into it (for which the cost would ultimately need to be passed along to the end user). That puts them in a bit of a dilemma seeing as a desktop PC isn't like a laptop/tablet where you can get away with smaller amounts of storage space (i.e. most users expect a modern day desktop PC to have at least 1 TB of storage I would imagine). Thus, Microsoft took a "best of both worlds" approach and went with using Intel's Smart Response Technology in order to speed up the storage with a small SSD while still allowing them to use a big (and less expensive) HDD. You certainly can't fault them for doing that. And to Microsoft's credit, they made the storage in the Surface Studio fully upgradable (instead of soldering the SSD to the motherboard as other companies would do). The storage upgrade is certainly not easy, but it's definitely doable.

If you can't get to the desktop and your device has restarted several times, it will begin automatic repair. After automatic repair, on the Choose an option screen, select Troubleshoot > Advanced options > System Restore. This option reverts your device to an earlier point, called a system restore point. Restore points are created when you install a new app, driver, update, or when you create a system restore point manually. Choose a restore point created before the error occurred. Restoring your PC to an earlier point will remove updates and drivers installed after the restore point was made. It won't affect your personal files. If restoring your PC to an earlier point fixes the stop error, temporarily block the update from automatically installing again.

 

You can also start Windows in safe mode to uninstall recent updates. After automatic repair, on the Choose an option screen, select Troubleshoot > Advanced options > Startup Settings > Restart. After it restarts, you'll see a list of options. Press the 4 or F4 key for Safe Mode. To access the Internet, press the 5 or F5 key for Safe Mode with Networking. In safe mode, select the Start button > Settings  > Update & security  > Windows update. Once there, identify the problem update, and remove it.

In some cases, this is due to a recently added new hardware. Try shutting down the device, unplugging the new hardware, and restarting as this is often caused by the connected hardware devices and their associated drivers.

Windows XP Home Edition or Windows XP Professional Edition

To log on to a computer as the administrator to reset a password, try the following steps.
  1. Log on as an administrator.
    1. Restart the computer.
    2. Press CTRL+ALT+DELETE two times, and then type the name of the administrator user account.

      Note If you do not know any other administrator user account, try to type Adminstrator in the User name box.
    3. In the Password box, type the administrator password, and then click OK.

      Note If you do not know the administrator password, leave it blank, and then click OK.

    1. Click Start, and then click Run.
    2. Type control userpasswords2, and then click OK.
    3. On the Users tab, click the name of the user account that you want to reset the password for, and then click Reset Password.
    4. Type a new password in the New password box, and then type the password again in the Confirm new password box, and then click OK.
    5. Restart the computer, and then try to log on to Windows XP again.
