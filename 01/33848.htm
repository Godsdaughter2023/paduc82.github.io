cmdrkeene say:

This takes some work - you'll need to know what your home's IP address is (the one assigned by your internet service company).  Then you'll need to set your home router to watch for any incoming remote desktop requests and forward them to the proper IP address inside your internal network.  This is known as "port forwarding" and http://portforward.com/ has a lot information about it, including how to setup most home routers.

The reason you need to do this:  When you're inside your home network, you can just connect to the other computer by name or IP (such as "Dell-LivingRoom" or "192.168.1.12").  But when you are outside your home, those addresses lose their meaning.

Another example: say you are at a hotel saying in room 213.  You can call the room next to you by dialing 215 on  your phone.  But if you drove across town and picked up a phone and called "215" it wouldn't do anything anymore. It needs more information to route the call properly (notably it needs to know which hotel to call before trying that extension).  Remote Desktop is exactly the same.

If you don't want to set all this up, let me suggest a better option.  TeamViewer is a free program that does it for you.

If you don't already know about the dread TDR, it is an error system introduced into Windows
with Vista and it can create havoc for gamers. My understanding is it was meant to prevent
premature BSOD. but it can be just as big a problem troubleshooting as BSOD because, while
the error message always points to the graphics driver, it's often only because the graphics driver is active  when something else causes  lag in the system. This could be almost anything. Hardware or software.

As you are getting BSOD the following registry entry may not help, but it's good to create one
anyway as MS has suggested using a time before the TDR of 8 sec rather than the default Windows
setting of only 2 sec. To create the new registry key -
Open regedit then -
Navigate to -
H_KEY_LOCAL_MACHINE \ System \ CurrentControlSet \ Control \ Graphics Drivers.
Right click on the open field and click on New / DWORD (32bit) Value,
Then name it:  TdrDelay -> double click it -> Set the value to 8.


With the psu test - Did he verify the stability of the +12V rail under load over a period of say 30 min.
or so ? It does not take much of a drop in voltage (I don't know how small, but not much) to cause a system lag, crash or lock-up when the system is under load.
- Without wishing to sound alarmist - It's a good thing you have the 730W Raidmax  as it provides
approx 576W on the +12V rails (24 Amps - 288W per rail )  which is adequate for the 450W minimum recommended by Nvidia for a GTX660ti. The GTX660ti can draw up to 150W.
However, most quality psu's would provide closer to  the 730W listed on the label on the
+12V rails alone.
For example, even the lower end Corsair CX600M has  552W (46 amps) on the +12V rail.

Good quality psu's are the best (safest) option on gaming machines as gaming stresses the system
a lot more than a general purpose, or common business machine.
Cheap psu's are cheap because they have low quality capacitors, which when stressed for long periods, such as with gaming, too often burn out or just don't provide consistent power. The cheap capacitors also age faster, lowering the capacity of the psu quicker than with a quality unit. The circuit protection
units are also known for either shutting the unit down too early, or too late.
I'm not suggesting you run out and buy a better quality psu.  Just that it is something to keep
and eye on.

rdrewd2 say:

Peggy,

A couple more things to try:  first go to settings, system, display, and click "Detect".  In the "multiple displays" box the setting "duplicate" should result in showing a single rectangle marked "1|2".  If you're set to "extend", then the box should show two rectangles, "1" and "2".  If you're connected via hdmi and it's still only showing a single rectangle marked "1" (and already made sure your "multiple displays" setting isn't set to "pc screen only"), then go to control panel, Hardware and Sound, Devices and Printers, and see if your TV is shown under devices; it may be labeled as "Samsung TV" or may just say "generic PnP".  No?  Then go back to settings, system, display, Advanced display settings,  Display adapter properties, click the "monitor" tab at the top, and lower the screen refresh rate down to 59 Hertz or lower.  Click "Apply", then go back to display settings and click "Detect" again.  If you've dropped your refresh rate, re-detected, and still don't see your TV listed in either settings/display or in control panel/devices, then look to see if you have a graphics icon in your notifications area.  If you do, right-click it, select properties, and see if there's a "wizard mode" that could guide you through troubleshooting your settings.  Lastly, right-click your start button and click Device Manager.  Open any "Display Adapters" or "Monitors" in that list, double click the individual device name, then click the "driver" tab, then click "update driver". If all those fail,  then yes, it may just be your socket.  At the risk of sounding like a complete idiot: "Did you give the plug a wiggle?  Sometimes you just have to give it a wiggle."  As dumb as that sounds, it's worked for me before...  Sorry I couldn't be more diagnostic for you, but hope something in here either helps out or at least identifies where the problem is.  Good luck.

rdrewd2 say:

Peggy,

It seems that one of three solutions has remedied this problem for most people: Turning everything off and turning everything back on again, replacing a faulty HDMI cable with a good one, or replacing the display driver.  Since you don't seem to be a simpleton, I'm going to rule out the first two, which leaves the display driver as the main suspect.  You said you updated the display driver from the device manager with no results, but maybe performing a "download-and-replace" from HP will help where Windows didn't; it definitely won't hurt.  You mentioned it was an HP Envy x2; turns out there are 121 different versions of that model, so here's a link to the list.  Find your build model, click it, and first I'd run the driver checker (orange box, "check now") and if that fails you, open the driver-graphics-intel section and download the new driver straight from there (this link is all Windows 10-specific drivers).  If it's a .zip file, extract it and then head back to Device Manager, Display Adapters, intel device name, right-click and "update driver software" again and let it find and install the new driver you downloaded.  I do hope that method works for you.  If not, then your laptop and TV hate each other and should be relegated to separate corners of the room.
