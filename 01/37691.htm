bstuartabq say:

Here's a follow up, since I've been doing some additional investigating and troubleshooting:

I've found three serious problems that compound on each other to help create the "Sleep of Death" problem when the following condition is true:  The Surface Pro 3 or Pro 4 is connected to a Surface Dock and attempts to enter connected standby.  I say "attempts" because if you look at the sleep study report of a CS session that resulted in the machine being unable to wake, you'll usually see that 100% of the session was spent in the No CS Phase state (under PDC Phases), specifically the NoCsPhase.WaitingForSleepTimeout state.  The time spent in a low-power state will be 0%.  Not good.

Problems #1 and #3 may also affect devices when they are not docked, but I haven't tested that scenario yet, as the SP4 in our office tends to enter a low-power state as intended when not docked.

Problem #1:

Hardware/driver problem:  Marvell AVASTAR Wireless-AC Network Adapter

(Compounds when the Surface Ethernet Adapter that resides in the Surface Dock is also connected to a network)

Symptoms:  Constant CPU usage and spurious wakes when in CS session, may also be accompanied by constant DNS registration errors in Event Viewer

Identification:  Can be identified by running the powercfg utility with the /sleepstudy switch and viewing the report.  Under Fx Devices, it is listed as either Marvell AVASTAR Wireless-AC Network Adapter \_SB.PCI0.RP09.PXSX or Unregistered Device \_SB.PCI0.RP09.PXSX, and under Networking as Marvell AVASTAR Wireless-AC Network Adapter.  It is a problem if it is listed with red text/highlighting and high active time.

Also, under Fx Devices, expand the trees of the USB xHCI Compliant Host Controller (\_SB.PCI0.XHC) to locate Surface Ethernet Adapter (USB\VID_045E&PID_07C6&REV_3000), and under Networking, find Surface Ethernet Adapter.  Those entries may also be highlighted in red and show a high active time.

Workaround:  Go into System -> Power & sleep.  Under Wi-Fi, uncheck When plugged in, stay connected to Wi-Fi while asleep.  Optionally, if it's a problem when on battery power, also uncheck On battery power, stay connected to Wi-Fi while asleep.

Problem #2:

Hardware/driver problem:  Surface Dock Audio over USB

Symptoms:  When speakers are connected to the Surface Dock (powered or not) - Very high CPU usage, overheating and thermal-related shutdowns when in CS session

Identification:  Can be identified by running the powercfg utility with the /sleepstudy switch and viewing the report.  Under Fx Devices, expand the trees of the USB xHCI Compliant Host Controller (\_SB.PCI0.XHC) to locate USB Composite Device (USB\VID_045E&PID_0905&REV_0001).  It is a problem if it is listed with red text/highlighting and very high active time.

Workaround:  Disconnect speakers from Surface Dock OR always un-dock the Surface before letting it enter a low-power state OR connect speakers to the headphone jack on the Surface when it's docked OR disable the Surface Dock Audio

To disable the Surface Dock Audio - With the Surface docked, go to Device Manager, then expand Universal Serial Bus controllers, then use the Details page of each USB Composite Device to locate the device with a HardwareID of USB\VID_045E&PID_0905&REV_0001.  Once you've found it, go to the Driver page and click on the Disable button.

Problem #3:

OS/software problem:  LockAppHost.exe

Symptoms:  (Usually occurs intermittently) Constant, but moderate CPU usage and moderate to high thermal activity when in CS session, low memory warnings upon wake, system restarts due to BugChecks

Identification:  Can be identified by running the powercfg utility with the /sleepstudy switch and viewing the report.  Look for entries with high CPU C0 Time and high CPU C0 Time.Non-attributed Time.  Then, under that entry's Srum Data section, expand the Energy Estimation section.  If the top entry is \Device\HarddiskVolume3\Windows\System32\LockAppHost.exe [Username] and the total energy consumed by that process vastly outweighs all other processes, this is a problem.

Workaround:  To minimize the damage and frustration caused by this problem, go into the Power settings and set the machine to hibernate after 1-2 hours when plugged in and asleep.  If it's also a problem when undocked, do the same for battery power.  Additionally, disabling Windows Hello may alleviate the problem entirely (as it is related to the lock screen), but you'll lose that functionality, of course.

Also, this may be fixed in the Windows 10 Anniversary Update, but I haven't put any Insider builds on this machine, as it's in a production environment.

Lesser power-suckers:
Cortana
Certain UWP apps:  Photos, Messaging, Mail & Calendar

So, I hope that helps anyone who is suffering through these problems.  Personally, given the other option of overheating and horrible system problems, I prefer having a slightly gimped Surface Pro 4 that will actually reliably wake up when docked.  :)
