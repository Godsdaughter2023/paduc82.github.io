andre da costa say:
What is the make and model of your computer?

1. Reset Facial Recognition and/or Fingerprint Options

Press Windows key + I to open Settings.
Click Accounts and select Sign-In options.
Locate Fingerprint or Facial Recognition options and click Remove under them.
After that, click Get Started and follow the on-screen instructions to reset Fingerprint and Facial Recognition.
Reboot your PC if needed.

2. Modify Group Policy Settings

Creators Update might have modified some of the crucial settings of Group Policy. Therefore, we would recommend to launch the Group Policy Editor and check/edit the settings related to Windows Hello functionality.

Pres Windows key, type gpedit, and press Enter.
Click on Computer Configuration and open Administrative Templates.
Select Windows Components and open Biometrics.
Click on Facial Features and right-click on Configure enhanced anti-spoofing option.
Select Edit and disable the Configure enhanced anti-spoofing feature.
Reboot your PC.

3. Update drivers

If some of the system drivers, most probably webcam and fingerprint reader drivers, were corrupted during the upgrade process, Windows Hello may fail to perform. To fix that, try to update the corresponding drivers:

Right-click on Windows key and select Device Manager.
Locate the Hello, webcam, and fingerprint drivers individually and right-click on each of them.
Select Remove driver software and wait for the removal to be finished.
Repeat the removal with all Windows Hello related drivers and then reboot your PC.
Once done, open Device Manager, click Action -> Scan for hardware changes.
The system should automatically detect and reinstall the drivers.

dpaz_tech say:

I know this thread is a bit old, but I wanted to share my findings regarding windows 10 mobile and default gateway / split tunneling (i.e., not sending traffic through the VPN when connected).

In our case. the correct protocol to choose when setting up any VPN manually through the Windows 10 Mobile interface is "Automatic." That is the only time when Windows will disable split tunneling, the feature that sends traffic through the VPN only when it is otherwise inaccessible. The split tunneling feature is useful in most corporate situations as you do not need all internet traffic going through your network, just traffic going behind the company firewall. Also, this is usually a faster option since you can get to most sites faster through normal WiFi or LTE than you could through your VPN. Again, some folks like us would rather all traffic go through the VPN.

Yes, it seems like a major VPN bug, or at least a poor analysis of the use cases for VPN that there is no explicit option to force traffic through it. That option existed in Windows Phone 8.1 and was very helpful so why it's missing is anyone's guess. However, you can get this to work like you want.

For those concerned about the protocol used when connecting with "Automatic," the strategy Microsoft uses to connect is first IKEv2, then SSTP, then L2TP, and finally PPTP. This has been the case since Windows 7 with the "Automatic" VPN protocol type. That is from most secure to least secure.

Now, in my testing I found that the connection must be setup from scratch as Automatic to disable split tunneling. Once it is changed to a specific protocol and saved, split tunneling for that connection is enabled, which is the opposite of what you want. So please keep that in mind. You can edit other items like the connection name, username, and password, but keep the protocol as Automatic always.

Also, I have not yet found any way to manually fix the DNS leak which occurs when using VPN on Windows 10 Mobile. You can do so on Windows 10 desktop, just not mobile. So even though you can disable split tunneling, the leak will exist and is just something to be aware of. I have no issues with that since my use for VPN is to secure connections over public WiFi, not completely hide which sites I visit.

I hope someone finds this helpful.  Good luck with your VPN's everyone!
