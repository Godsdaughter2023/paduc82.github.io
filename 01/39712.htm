joanna 777 say:

Hi,

The "COM Surrogate has stopped working" issue can be because of quite a lot of things. It could be drivers, devices connected to your system, or other installed apps. Follow the steps below to try and see if these will resolve the issue:

  1. Click Start
  2. Right click on Computer, and select Properties.
  3. Click the Advanced System Settings, then click Advanced Tab
  4. In the Performance section, click Settings
  5. Select the “Data Execution Protection” tab, and then click the ‘Turn on DEP for all programs and services except those I select’ option.
  6. Click the Add button, and then browse to C:\Windows\System32\dllhost.exe
  7. Click open, click OK on the warning message.
  8. Click Apply/OK.

Here's one thread from the community that have talked about the same issue. You might find it very helpful to read through them and learn some tricks the community have suggested.

Hello Char, I’mm Be answering your question.

a. user say:

The best way to do this is with a third-party image backup application.

Backing up is a very big topic, with lots of details and nuances, but the most important thing is to get started. You can learn the finer details later.

An image backup is a copy of an entire drive letter. For example, you can backup everything on C: with an image backup, including the metadata that is a technical description of C: and isn't contained in a file.

A third-party image backup application will make a copy of C: in a matter of minutes, and it takes only minutes to restore the image. The image is compressed, so it occupies much less space than the C: drive.

Windows 10 has its own image backup application, called System Image Backup, but Microsoft has stopped developing it and recommends instead the use of a third-party application.

By contrast, a file and folder backup copies individual files and folders. You can't use it to backup C: because it doesn't include the metadata needed to make C: bootable, but it's great for backing up your personal files.

There are many more details that are beyond the scope of a community forum post, but they're out there on the internet for you to discover. Run a web search for:

best image backup software

and you'll find plenty of reviews. Pick one or two applications and read the descriptions of how they work in their technical support websites. For example, the image backup application I use is Macrium Reflect and you can find a great description of how it works here.

There are also plenty of good file and folder backup applications to discover with a web search. An example is Backup4All.

It's very important to store your backups outside your computer, but you already know that ;-)

ken blake say:

Regarding the "how," see the other replies for suggestions. Almost any third-party program is better than what comes with Windows.

I also wanted to my thought on backup in general:

First of all, almost everyone should be backing up to external media regularly. It is always possible that a hard drive crash, user error, nearby lightning strike, virus attack, even theft of the computer, can cause the loss of everything on your drive. As has often been said, it's not a matter of whether you will have such a problem, but when.

Essentially you should back up what you can't afford to lose--what you can't readily recreate. What that is depends on how you use your computer and what you use it for.
It takes time and effort to backup, but it also takes time and effort to recreate lost data. If you back up daily, you should never have to recreate more than one day's worth of lost data. If weekly, there's potentially a lot more to recreate. You should assess how much pain and trouble you would have if you lost x days of data, and then choose a backup frequency that doesn't involve more pain and trouble than that you would have if you had to recreate what was lost.

Some things (photographs, for instance) can never be recreated, and more frequent backup may be wanted for them.
At one extreme is the professional user who would likely go out of business if his data was lost. He probably needs to back up at least daily. At the other extreme is the kid who doesn't use his computer except to play games. He probably needs no backup at all, since worst case he can easily reinstall his games.
Most of us fall somewhere between those extremes, but nobody can tell you where you fall; you need to determine that for yourself.

Should you back up Windows? Should you back up your applications? Most people will tell you no, since you can always reinstall these easily from the original media. But I don't think the answer is so clear-cut. Many people have substantial time and effort invested in customizing Windows and configuring their apps to work the way they want to. Putting all of that back the way it was can be a difficult, time-consuming effort. Whether you should backup up Windows and apps depends, once again, on you.

How to backup? What software to use? There are many choices, including the Windows-supplied backup program. Which choice is best for you depends at least in part on the answers to some of the questions above.

Finally what backup media should you choose, and how should it be stored? There are many choices, including  CDs, DVDs, thumb drives, second hard drives, and online backup services.

I don't recommend backup to a second non-removable hard drive because it leaves you susceptible to simultaneous loss of the original and backup to many of the most common dangers: severe power glitches, nearby lightning strikes, virus attacks, even theft of the computer.

In my view, secure backup needs to be on removable media, and not kept in the computer. For really secure backup (needed, for example, if the life of your business depends on your data) you should have multiple generations of backup, and at least one of those generations should be stored off-site.

My computer isn't used for business, but my personal backup scheme uses two identical removable hard drives, I alternate between the two, and make a complete copy of the primary drive.

I also use a pair of 16GB thumb drives for making more frequent backups of my most critical data (like financial information). For that I just drag and drop.

How to set up 2 Chrome icons on taskbar?

I use Chrome both for general internet browsing, but also to access my bespoke work diary on a remote server. I would like to set up 2 chrome icons on the taskbar: one for personal browsing the other for my work diary (only). At the moment, each time I create a new tab it collates them both on the one chrome taskbar icon, whereas I would like to keep them separate for ease. 

Is there a way that I can have 2 chrome icons on the taskbar and keep them separate, without using the 'never combine' option?

Thanks in advance.

a. user say:

Right click on the google Chrome icon>Properties>copy the target line command.

On the Destop right click>new>shortcut>paste the target line command>add name for example Google Chrome1>Finish.

Right click on the new Google Chrome1 icon>Pin to Taskbar.

Regards.

s.gregory say:

Hello,

I had this same issue when I moved to windows 10, here is a solution:

-> Right click on the taskbar

-> Click on "Taskbar Settings"

-> Scroll down to "Combine taskbar buttons"

-> Select "Never" from the drop down menu

This worked for me.
