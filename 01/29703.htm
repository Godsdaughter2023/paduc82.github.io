bossdweeb say: - If your games are running on the integrated graphics and assuming the driver install is not corrupt, it does sound like

either your graphics processor or psu are faulting.

The best way to test your gpu is to try using another card on your machine (this will also help determine if there is a problem with

the PCIe slot/circuit)

Or, try your card in another machine.

(btw - EVGA were very popular, and may still be often recommended. They do have fast RMA process, but I had bad luck with an

EVGA GTX770 SC. Both the original and the RMA, which is quick because they don't send back your card. So in my case I got

someone else's problem. They obviously didn't test the one they sent back for long periods at idle, because I had random crashes

to various coloured screens for over two years before the card finally gave up altogether. It ran games without any problems though,

so I think the cards power regulator was dropping too low at idle. I didn't bother RMA again because it cost me $45-AUD to post it

to EVGA Taiwan).

- Your psu is the minimum recommended by Nvidia for stock clocked GTX1080ti. The EVGA SC would draw a bit more power.

While your psu should still be adequate most gamers prefer 50-100 W more than Nvidia's recommendation due to long periods

of load on the psu.

However, your games are crashing before there is full load on the gpu,  so the psu is less likely to be the problem.

- I do have a problem with - 12GB RAM - though. You should always use factory matched pairs of RAM if you want guaranteed stability.

Using an extra single stick, even of the same brand and model will force the RAM to run in the less efficient single channel mode vs

dual channel. There is also potential mis-matched RAM could cause games to crash.

 Also note that if you have changed RAM speeds in BIOS that this is very likely to cause games to crash, often seemingly at random.

The speed on the RAM's label is the maximum over-clock speed (only use in conjunction with an overclocked cpu).

Set the BIOS to default, and if the games run well try using the XMP option (your mobo may have an auto XMP option).

You need to replace your hardware, if it's replaceable. Otherwise, you simply can't run Windows 10 on that computer.

If your problem(s) are caused by software - some of your software is incompatible with Windows 10 - you have to upgrade to the Windows 10 version or remove the incompatible software.

If your problem(s) are caused by misunderstanding how to setup Windows or work with it - welcome to the club! - you might be able to undo your mistake, or you might have to re-install, this time the right way. You can do it.
