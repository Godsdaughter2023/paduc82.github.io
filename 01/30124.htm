An anchor is always needed.

The best tested drivers for the computer's configuration are always from the computer or motherboard manufacturer.

Windows will update Microsoft and non-Microsoft drivers.

So the drivers that are displayed on the the computer's or motherboard's website are then updated.

The drivers on the component manufacturer websites are for many computer and motherboard manufacturer's.

The component manufacturer drivers may also be updated.

When you view the information within device manager or attempt to update drivers you may see information on dates.

The most important thing to remember is that there is no information provided on whether the driver is corrupt or not.

The most up to date version of Windows can become corrupt yet it remains the most up to date version of Windows.

For Windows you can run sfc /scannow to check the operating system for corruption.

For drivers there is no check.  They may BSOD.

So when installing drivers the best anchor is to install the best tested drivers.

They are always from the computer or motherboard manufacturer.

There are other methods to find drivers where you can directly install the most up to date driver from the start.

This method is not recommended as if you do not have experience you can attempt to install an up to date incorrect driver.

In summary the best method is to clean install the operating system and use the website drivers offered by the computer or motherboard manufacturer.

For any drivers needed that are not displayed the next best option is from the component manufacturer's.

Websites that check the computer for drivers should not be used as they can install unknown files.

Software that modifies drivers should not be used.

Overclocking drivers is a common cause of BSOD.

The latest drivers sometimes are bad drivers and cause BSOD. 

At times the latest drivers have to be rolled back for stability. 

The Windows environment is a moving target and the latest manufactured drivers may need to be modified as Windows is modified.

So you cannot rely on the latest drivers as always being the best drivers.

The best drivers are the drivers that function with both stability and performance.

And the best anchor are the drivers from the computer or motherboard manufacturer.

Choose what closing the lid does is greyed out. - Microsoft Community

michaelbrossart say:

Choose what closing the lid does is greyed out.

I am running Windows 10 on a Surface Pro 3.  I would like it to sleep when I close the cover, but when I go to Power settings in the Control Panel and select Choose what closing the lid does, both options (for battery and plugged in) are grayed out.  The only reference I could find for a fix was to turn off Fast Startup by going to Power Options, choosing what the lid does, select Change settings that are currently unavailable, scrolling down and unchecking the box that reads Turn on fast startup.  No joy.

While I am a member of a domain, I have verified that I am not subject to GPOs.  Any other suggestions of where to look?

michaelbrossart say:

Problem is now resolved for me.  Not sure if this will work for you, but based on a few posts and a hunch, I deleted all folders in the following registries...

  • HKEY_LOCAL_MACHINE|SOFTWARE|Microsoft|Windows|CurrentVersion|Group Policy|History
  • HKEY_CURRENT_USER|SOFTWARE|Microsoft|Windows|CurrentVersion|Group Policy|History

Note that I didn't delete the keys in these folders themselves, just the subfolders.  Unfortunately, I didn't record the names of the folders.  They were some sort of key value, not a recognizable name.  I do know that I had four folders under the HKLM tree and two folders under the HKCU tree.  After rebooting, I still have no folders under HKCU but two folders were recreated under HKLM.

As they say, it's always dicey editing the registry, but it worked for me.  Good luck.
