myronh say:

"All icons on desktop changed to that icon and I can't open anything." I presume it's the icon for windows photo viewer?

I believe what happened is you used "open with" on a shortcut. You can confirm this by going to the control panel > programs > Associate a file type or protocol with a program, scroll down to LNK for the associated program it should say Unknown. I suspect is says photo viewer now however.

If so, there are two ways this may be fixed. The easier way requires a registry change though so you might not want to go that route.

1.    Click Start Menu, and then type regedit in the Start Search and press ENTER. Right click on regedit from the search results and run as administrator.

2.    Navigate to the following registry branch:

HKEY_CURRENT_USER\Software\Microsoft\Windows\Curre ntVersion\Explorer\FileExts\[ext]

Replace the [ext] with the actual file extension that you want to restore its file type association to revert back to original Windows 7 default (probably .lnk). If you unsure, simply browse through all the sub-key under FileExts.

      3.    Delete the sub-key named UserChoice.

           4.    Exit from Registry Editor.

The second method is quite a bit more work, may not fix it, but doesn't involve the registry.

Since the default action is caused by a user choice (inadvertant as it was), you may be able to restore simply by creating a new user account. See if the icons return to their natural state and now work. If so, just move all of your files and folders over and delete the old account. If you use Outlook you may also have to reset where it looks for the Outlook files, but it might be automatic (don't try starting Outlook until everything is copied over though). If you use this method, don't forget to copy the hidden appdata folder. Just unhide everything before you start the copy.

adorable cyborg say:

If support says it's your ISP, it's likely your ISP. However, it could be wireless interference. You could have a mirror or a fish tank or even a kitchen or bathroom between the Xbox and the router that will cause problems. Wireless headsets like Turtlebeach headsets are commonly the reason why you get some slow speeds. If you are wired directly into the modem, it would definitely be your ISP. Also, those slower speeds aren't going to prove there's a problem with the Xbox. The network card in it isn't as good as the one in your computer. Comparatively, it's not going to perform as well with tests. And a speed test isn't going to show you all of your connection. It's going to show you what speeds you get. There are also pings that are a part of your connection, too, and you might be getting packet loss when it comes to the Xbox. It requires much larger packets of information than what your computer requires. If it can't get that, then you're going to run into problems. And then it's still your ISP.
