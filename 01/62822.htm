Here you go. The process is involved, it requires some very careful typing but it works.

 1. Set your BIOS to use the DVD drive as the primary boot device.

 4. Select a language, a time and currency, and a keyboard or input method, and then click Next. You are now in Repair Mode.
 5. Select an Operating System, then click Next.

    dir C:\Win* {Enter}
    dir D:\Win* {Enter}
    dir E:\Win* {Enter}
    etc.
    until you find the Windows folder.
    I will call that drive "Q:".
 8. Type these commands:
    cd  /d  Q:\windows\System32 {Enter}
    ren  sethc.exe  sethc.org  {Enter}
    copy  cmd.exe  sethc.exe  {Enter}
 9. Reboot normally.
10. When challenged for a password, tap the Shift key five times. You will see a small black screen.

    net user
    (To see a list of all accounts that exist on your machine)
    net user  "Kelli"  123456
    (to set your password to 123456)
    net user Spare abcdef  /add
    (to create the badly needed spare account)
    net localgroup administrators spare  /add

    exit

OK, this is not really good when this happens, let us see if we can reset your CMOS chip to the previous version . . .

  1. Turn off your PC and disconnect the power cable from the electrical outlet
  2. Remove the PC cover
  3. Locate the 2-pin password connector (PSWD) on the system board (you might need a magnifying glass to locate it on your motherboard)
  4. Remove the 2-pin jumper plug
  5. Locate the 2-pin CMOS jumper (RTCRST) on the system board
  6. Move the 2-pin jumper plug from the password jumper to pins into the CMOS jumper
  7. Plug in AC power to the system and wait ten seconds for the CMOS to clear
  8. Move the 2-pin jumper plug back to the password jumper
  9. Replace the PC cover
  10. Plug back in your PC and boot . . . 

If you cannot find these jumpers, please post the exact model of your system and I will show you where on your motherboard these jumpers are located . . .

a. user say:

Hi Michael,

Thank you for posting your query in Microsoft Community.

This behavior occurs if you map a network drive to the first available drive letter after the drive letters for the local volumes and CD-ROM drives. When you install a new device or volume, Mount Manager, which assigns drive letters to volumes, does not recognize the mapped network drive and assigns the next available drive letter to the new device or volume. This causes a collision with the existing mapped network drive.

To prevent this issue from occurring, when you map a network drive, assign the highest available drive letter to the mapped network drive.

To work around this issue if the new volume appears in Disk Management, specify a different drive letter for the new device or volume. To do this, follow these steps:

a. Press Windows key + X and click Disk Management

b. In the list of drives in the right pane, right-click the new drive and then click Change Drive Letter and Path(s).

c. Click Change, and in the drop-down box, select a drive letter for the new drive that is not assigned to a mapped network drive.

d. Click OK, and then click OK again.

Both the mapped network drives and the recently installed drive appear in Windows Explorer.
