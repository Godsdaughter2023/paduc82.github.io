poppybill say:

The advice about parallax error of the pen in the replies here really helped me calibrate my pen better.  To repeat the principal idea: the accuracy of the calibration depends on how much, and in what direction, you tilt your pen when you use it.  Note especially, if you tilt the pen differently while calibrating than you do in use, your calibration will be off.  For example, if to try to be more accurate in calibration by holding the pen vertically, and then you tilt it in use, the contact spot will no longer be under the pen tip. 

Even more, you may find that the best calibration is obtained by purposely missing the crosshairs with the pen tip when you calibrate.  For example, I am left handed, and I found that if I calibrated "normally", not only was the contact spot off, but also it was hidden under the pen tip where I could not see it. When I calibrated by purposely missing the crosshairs to the left side, the spot was "spot-on" when I  used the pen with my normal tilt. 

petersteier say:

I have done some research on my surface pro 3. All answers given so far bear some truth, but all are also wrong. Thus, they are of little help (sorry).

Some background: There are two problems with digitizers, and thus two kinds of calibration in Windows. The first one is non-linearity: the location of the pen is not detected exactly, but there is a position dependent offset. This deviations were awful with some devices I had in the past, the NTRIG in my Surface Pro 3 is in fact much better: on the left side of the screen, the deviation is about 0.3 mm (~1/100") horizontally, to the right no deviation is detectable. Some people may call this "calibration not recommended", but in High School in technical drawing a precision of 0.1 mm was expected (with a sharp pencil, I'm 50 years old).

Secondly, the digitizer does not detect the contact point of the pen with the glass, but some point 1 to 2 mm (1/16") from the tip. Thus, if you tilt the pen, the detected spot will move. I will call this effect "parallax".

These two effects are completely independent and have no connection with each other.

The Non-Linearity is corrected by Windows by the LinearityData (stored in the Registry). To change it, you first have to Clear the Calibration data in the Tablet PC dialogue, and the click "Calibrate". You get a 16 point calibration grid; as this is the Linearity correction, you should click hit these points with the Pen held exactly perpenticular to the screen.

If you now hit the "calibrate" button in the TabletPC settings again, you see the 4-point calibration. In fact, this is the parallax correction! Hold the pen at your comfortable writing angle, and hit the four points.

Naturally, the Linearity Correction is independent of screen rotation, thus there is only one data set (in the registry).

There are, however, four different parallax correction data sets; you can produce them all by repeating the 4-point calibration in all four screen rotations. You now have a perfectly calibrated screen in all screen rotations.

There is useful information on techniques for a "better" non-linearity correction at http://www.surfaceforums.net/threads/properly-calibrating-the-pen.5157/ However, it does not seem necessary at my Surface Pro 3.

I would like to be able to perform the 4-point parallax calibration without the 16-point linearity calibration, however, you have to go through the first to be able to do the second. You should be very precise when doing the linearity calibration, or you will actually degrade the linearity: drink no coffee before, and hold year breath.

BTW, I could not locate where Windows 10 stores the parallax correction for NTRIG (Wacom tablets store it at C:\Users[your user name]\AppData\Roaming\WTablet\ISD_Tablet.dat).
