a. user say:

I think I know what you're referring to. When you insert a removable drive into a computer, Windows gives the drive the next available drive letter, whatever it is. If you insert a USB drive or an SD card into a computer, and Windows gives that drive the letter F, that doesn't mean it's going to get the letter F next time. It might. Or it might not. It might be G the next time, or M. Or it might be F for months and months, and the next time, G.

If your removable drive has always been F, and you remove it and insert a new drive in its place, that new drive might be F. And if that new drive is still plugged in when you insert your old drive, you might see that the letter F now belongs to the new drive, which is formatted with the FAT32 file system, and your old drive is now G.

If it were not this way, Windows would eventually run out of drive letters, because over the course of your computer's life, you might attach dozens of different USB drives, SD cards, phones, MP3 players, CDs and DVDs (they also get a drive letter) and who knows what else now that we have the internet of things.
