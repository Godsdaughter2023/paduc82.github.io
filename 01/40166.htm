verdyp say:

This is clearly a bug of the SFC tool which does not properly check the ownership, or attempts to get ownership info using the wrong privileges (it cannot determine who is the owner because SFC does not seem to use administration elevation.

This occurs even if we run SFC from an elevated admin command line (so the UAC protection was already granted to the parent command line process used to launch SFC).

This could even be an issue in the UAC protection system.

For now when I look at ownership of these directories, they are properly owned, but NOT by "TrustedInstaller", instead they are owned by "System", and there's absolutely no problem in ACL lists (no duplication at all), as confirmed by inpection with ICACLS, or even with the Explorer.

Apparently SFC runs by impersonating itself to the user "TrustedInstaller", that actually does not have FULL control on all the system files (unlike the "System" account). As a "TrustedInsaller" (which is part of group "Administrators"), SFC gets limited privileges to repair files whose FULL control is only granted to "System" but limtied to "Administrators".

There's no wya a user can restore privileges to "TrustedInstaller", as there's no way to impersonate ourself to this user (owned only by Microsoft, that created this account and registered it as a member of the builtin "Administrators" group (but made invisible in the list of users).

This may also explain why SFC cannot fix/repair all files on the system. Not that when installing windows from the DVD or ISO, the Windows Install PE image (loaded in read-only mode from the WIM/ESD file on the ISO or DVD and numerically signed) was created by Microsoft and can do everything as the user "TrustedInstaller" and then grant limited privileges to the target system. But we cannot modify its content without killing the digital signature of this image.

verdyp say:

Apparently SFC attempts to access the drives uising the "Administrative shares" (alias "C$") in order to bypass other sandboxing layers in the normal user environment.

The C: drive may be shared with full control to the local "Administrators" group, or just to the local "System" user. If the PC was installed in a domain, you may grant full access also to the domain's administrator via this adminisrative share.

DON'T share this drive to everyone !

Note that you cannot explicitly grant full control to the "TrustedInstaller" user (this user account does not exist on the system, it is only known by its UID, but it is the "owner" of original/unmodified files/folders of the system, until those files/folders need other permissions (in which case they may be given ownership to another account such as the builtin "System" account, and then by granting permissions for the local "Administrator" (name may vary by language), or the local group "Administrators".
