a. user say:

This has always been the case with Windows, but it's not the fault of Windows per se.

As you correctly observed, this behavior happens when the screen resolution changes. When the screen switches to a lower resolution, there's often not enough room to maintain the icons in the arrangement you setup. Instead, the icons switch to their default location, which is not as capricious as you might think.

When a program creates a new file on the desktop, its icon will usually appear on the leftmost available space on the screen, because that's the default location for new icons. When the resolution of your monitor decreases, Windows does some fancy math to figure out where the leftmost location for your icons is in relation to where they are now.

Windows doesn't keep track of where your icons were before the screen resolution changed, so when the resolution changes back to what it was before, Windows can only leave the icons where they are, all piled up against the left side of your monitor.

You might ask, "why can't Windows remember where my icons are?" After all, that's what Fences by Stardock does, which is what I'm going to recommend to you. I don't exactly know why Windows doesn't remember, although I have some semi-sane ideas that I can share if you're interested. Basically, I don't think it's always within Windows' ability to remember.

In any case, we are actually not supposed to have fancy arrangements of icons on our desktops, even though everybody does it. The desktop isn't supposed to be where we store stuff - that's what our user folders are for. The desktop is supposed to be for shortcuts. Well, so much for 'supposed to be.'
