All 32-bit client versions of Windows (not just Vista/XP/7) have a 4GB
address space (64-bit versions can use much more). That's the
theoretical upper limit beyond which you can not go.

You can't use the entire 4GB of address space. Even though you
have a 4GB address space, you can only use *around* 3.1GB of RAM.
That's because some of that space is used by hardware and is not
available to the operating system and applications. The amount you can
use varies, depending on what hardware you have installed, but can
range from as little as 2GB to as much as 3.5GB. It's usually around

Windows 8 Back up. - Microsoft Community

gary 881 say:

Windows 8 Back up.

I have just purchased a brand new computer with Windows 8. Is it advisable to create back-up discs in case of future problems ? And if so, how do I do it ?

ken blake - microsoft mvp say:

On Sun, 13 Jan 2013 13:04:35 +0000, Gary 881 wrote:

I have just purchased a brand new computer with Windows 8. Is it advisable to create back-up discs in case of future problems ? And if so, how do I do it ?

Regardless of what operating system you are running, you should
always have backups on external media of all the files important to
you. There is always a risk of losing everything. As has often been
said, it's not a matter of whether you will have such a problem, but
when.

Essentially you should back up what you can't afford to lose--what you
can't readily recreate. What that is depends on how you use your
computer and what you use it for.

It takes time and effort to backup, but it also takes time and effort
to recreate lost data. If you back up daily, you should never have to
recreate more than one day's worth of last data. If weekly, there's
potentially a lot more to recreate. Y
