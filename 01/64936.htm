icestorm iii say:

XBOX 360 considers 1GB to be 1,073,741,824 bytes.



The drive manufacturers consider 1GB to be 1,000,000,000 bytes.



The 360 reserves about 5,000,000,000 bytes for system usage.



Between the reserved space and the unit discrepancy, you end up with a drive that has "only" 229GB free on it for user usage.

lemp say:

I'm not sure what you mean by "I want to reset my computer because I purchased the computer without an OEM."

My guess is that you want to restore your computer to its "factory condition" (i.e., the state it was in when it left the factory).  I further guess that the COA sticker has been removed from the computer or is so worn that it is no longer legible.

Still guessing, it sounds as if the 3 files you have were supplied by Lenovo as its "restore media set" or some similar description.

Using Lenovo "restore media" should obviate the need for a product key -- assuming that you have the restore media set corresponding to whatever edition of Windows originally came with the computer.

And yet further guesses are that the file named 0A44006_1 is Windows, one of the other files is a Lenovo "driver disk," and the third file is some collection of Lenovo utility software.

Do you have any description of these files other than their cryptic names?

How large are the files (each)?

Does the computer that you want to "reset" have an optical disk drive?  If so, can it read DVDs?  If not, have you confirmed that the computer is capable of booting from USB (the answer is probably but not certainly yes, depending on the system's age)?

Aside from that computer, do you have access to a computer with an optical disk drive that is capable of writing DVDs?

Based on a very brief Google search, it seems that the Lenovo T420 has difficulty in booting from a USB device.  It might work if you have the most recent BIOS or it might work if you "reset" the BIOS as described here (https://forums.lenovo.com/t5/ThinkPad-T400-T500-and-newer-T/T420s-will-not-boot-to-USB-jump-drive/m-p/1272091#M72362), or it might not work at all.

If the T420 has a working DVD drive and you have a computer with which you can write DVDs, what I suggest is that you create 3 separate DVDs.  If one of the ISO files is indeed intended to install some edition of Windows 7, it's going to be about 3 GB or so, and you'll need to "burn" (i.e., write) a DVD rather than a CD.  And if the combined size of all 3 ISO files is around 10 GB, you have 3 DVDs.  An ISO file for a CD (such as a Windows XP install) can be no larger than about 650 MB.

You can save all 3 ISO files on your 1TB external hard drive, but for use, it will be simpler to create 3 disks.

Use ImgBurn to create the DVDs using the ISO file. 

http://www.imgburn.com/

http://forum.imgburn.com/index.php?/topic/61-how-to-write-an-image-file-to-a-disc-using-imgburn/

Put the DVD in the T420's DVD drive and either set the BIOS to boot from CD/DVD first or use the "boot menu" (which you probably get to by pressing F12 just as the Lenovo logo is displaying on startup).  Then boot from the DVD.

You should be able to put the other two DVDs in any computer, look at what's there, and decide what to do with them after Windows has been installed on the T420.

angelcarreno say: Hi,

My name is Angel. I am an Independent Advisor. Thank you for posting in Microsoft Community.


The primary email address for your Microsoft account is what you use to sign into Windows and other Microsoft services. If you’d prefer to use a different address than the one you signed up with–even a non-Microsoft address–it’s an easy change to make.

If you’re using a Microsoft account to sign into Windows, you’re not stuck using the account you signed up with. Maybe you originally created an Outlook.com address, but prefer to use your Gmail address instead. You could always revert to a local account on your PC and then change back to a Microsoft account using a new address, but that takes a bit of time, and you’d need to do it on each PC where you sign in with that account. Fortunately, there’s an easier way. Microsoft lets you create aliases, which are really just additional email addresses you can use to sign in to the same account. Then, you can change any of those aliases to be your primary address.

To start, head on over to the Microsoft account page and sign in using whatever email address you’ve already got set up.


On the main account page, click the “Your info” link on the navigation bar at the top.



On that page, click the “Manage how you sign in to Microsoft” link.



The next page shows the email addresses you have set up. If you already have an alias created that you’d like to make the primary account, you can skip the next few steps. Otherwise, click the “Add email” link to create a new alias.

map_4

The “Add an alias” page gives you two options. You can create a new new email address on the outlook.com service or you can add an existing email from another site as your new alias. Choose the option you want, fill in the details, and then click the “Add alias” button.



Your new account should now appear on the list of aliases. If you added an existing email address, you’ll also get a message letting you know that a verification email was sent to that address. Go find it and verify the email address for your Microsoft account before continuing.

You can actually sign on to Microsoft services–including Windows–using any of your aliases. So if that’s all you wanted to achieve, you’re good to go.

But, you can also make your new alias your primary account if you want. The advantage of doing this is that emails from Microsoft regarding your account, payments you make for services, and so on will go to that account. It’s also a great way to just consolidate everything under your preferred email address. Also, if you want to remove an address that is already your primary alias, you need to make another address your primary before doing that.

If you do want to make your new address your primary alias, click the “Make primary” link under your new email address. Just note that you must have verified the new email address before you can do it.


On the confirmation page, click the “Yes” button to make the email address your primary alias. If you don’t want promotional offers from Microsoft at that address, be sure to turn off that option.



It can take up to 48 hours for the change of your primary alias to proliferate through all the Microsoft services. In our experience, it usually happens much faster than that. Within a couple of hours, it’s likely you’ll be able to use the primary address for logging on to Windows PCs, Xboxes, and other devices associated with your account. Other services may take a bit longer, but you can still log onto them with your old credentials just fine.



Hope this Help!

kramerllama say:

TDM - What a Fail!

I enjoy TDM, typically its fun, and a nice way to play by yourself when no ones on.  I enjoy it in COD, and Rainbow Six, so tried my hand at TDM in BF.  My God, does it not only suck, it just fails.  First problem, initial spawns.  When you first spawn, you spawn as if it were a FFA, that is, neither team has a base spawn (you know, one team spawns on this side of the map, the other on that side.)  No, its spawn, and if theres an enemy behind you, then theres an enemy behind you.  While playing, I spawned in a building directly looking at the back of an enemy, I felt bad for shooting him.  

-

Another reason is the maps.  The sections of the maps they use for TDM are very poor to have.  Areas with a ton of debris, or objects in the way, or wide open areas.  theres no balance to the TDM maps, that it results in chaos, and not the good kind.  You pretty much have to say good bye to KDR if you play TDM, because you will be negative 9/10...winning or losing (though, I dont care much for KDR myself, this is to the KDR ***)
