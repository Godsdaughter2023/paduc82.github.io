how do I send music from my computer to my phone? - Microsoft Community

chalk666 say:

how do I send music from my computer to my phone?

I have a Samsung Galaxy Ace GT-S5830i
sandeep ghatuary say:

Hi Chalk666,

 

Thanks for posting in the Microsoft Communities. Follow these steps and check.

 

Transfer Music from the Computer to a Phone via USB cable:

 

 

a)    Connect the phone to the computer using the USB cable.

b)    Wait for the computer to recognize the phone as an external drive. If a dialog box does not pop up with the name of the phone within a few minutes, open the Windows Explorer folder and navigate to the external drive in the left pane. Sometimes the name of the drive will show up as "Removable Disk."

c)    Open the music folder of the phone. In some cases, it might be under a "My Media," "My Music" or similar folder.

d)    Drag and drop the music files into their own folder under the phone's music directory. This way, it should be convenient to find the files once they're on the phone. Wait for the files to copy completely.

e)    Disconnect the phone from the USB cable. If there is a Safely Remove Hardware icon in the bottom right corner of the taskbar, use this utility to safely remove the drive.

 

Transfer Music from the Computer to a Phone Via Bluetooth:

 

a)    Go into the Bluetooth menu on the computer and set it to be "Discoverable."

b)    Go into the Bluetooth menu on the phone and have the phone "Discover new devices."

c)    Select "Set up a new Device" from the computer's Bluetooth menu and wait for the computer to find the phone.

d)    Enter the pairing code the computer provides into the phone to connect the two devices.

e)    Click on the icon that appears for the phone to see if there is a separate folder on the phone for audio files.

f)     Drag and drop audio files that you want on the phone into the "Music" folder on the phone, or if there is no music folder directly onto the phone icon. A status bar will appear for each file as it is being transferred.

 

Let us know if you need further assistance.

November Update DANGEROUS to people with Conexant 20561 (Lenovo X200, T400 & some Dell models)

The "fix" was simply to go into the driver properties (by right clicking on the Speaker icon in your tray area), picking "Playback Devices" then right clicking on the Conexant in that display that came up and disabling it. The tray icon immediately got a red X,  and clicking on THAT calls up a troubleshooter that reneabled the audio--working now--without a reboot. 

A pain in the butt, but workable.

Under the November build the Conexant has gone from inconvenient, to nightmare.

When you go into a Metro app, what happens now is instead of normal audio with the driver occasionally dying, you instead get the ENTIRE A/V of the app messed up. Not just the audio, but the video as well. Sputtering and big hesitations where the video frame rate inside the apps seems to redraw every few seconds instead of every few milliseconds.  Along with audio sputtering too, of course.  Making using anything in Metro with moving objects 100% unusable. 

Back out of Metro you also see a change--a disastrous one.  If you go into a simple web browser, go to YouTube, and use a very conservative setup---I used a system with Macromedia Flash completely uninstalled so the video was HTML5, and stream video at any higher resolution, you'll lock/crash your whole system. Consistently.  Complete freeze with no error trapping.

Now the natural suspicion with these two problems would likely be the video driver, not the audio, but knowing from previous releases how shaky the Conexant support WAS, and that the Intel Mobile Series 4 was the video (so on millions of older laptops and so well supported--and the specific GM45 derivative of the Intel chip also very popularly used) I knew the audio was more likely the issue.

Now here's the strange part. Someone who understands how the AV works better on Win10 may know why this worked, but I don't. I have proof it's the Conexant causing this, athough what I did to address this is disturbing and I think dangerously unstable as a long-term solution. And I don't know WHY it worked.

Under the properties for Speakers, in the Driver Tab, it says the driver is from Diver provider Microsoft, date 10/29/2015, driver version 10.0.10586.0. So clearly not the version Lenovo has on their site (which wasn't great, because it died a lot, but never slowed down or locked the system).  Lenovo's version also doesn't claim to support anything later than Win7.

 

Just on a hunch I disabled the Speaker driver from Device Manager. Then something very unexpected happened.

 

All the problems went away. ALL of them. But despite Device Manager reporting the driver as disabled, I'm still getting audio out of the speakers. Still have a working volume control.  There's no sputtering.  AV works just fine both in Metro and in streaming HTML5 Youtube windows.  It's like nothing bad ever happened...

I rebooted, verified that the seeming disablement inside Device Manager sticks (visually at least), but that the actual audio still works after a reboot.

 

This seems like a house of cards waiting to fall though. It makes no sense that disabling the device leaves it still working (better than ever) and I have to think that at some point some auto-update by the OS is going to zap this again. It seems like there are drivers working at two different levels too, if the Device manager doesn't "agree" with what the Playback Devices properties from the Tray show.  This seems like some deep OS conflict, and I have no idea if my workaround will stick or continue working.
