Hi Joseph,

 

Since when are you facing this issue?

 

The error CMOS Checksum Bad that appears depends on the type of BIOS used. Any of these error conditions indicate that a Basic Input/Output System (BIOS) test failed.

 

There are two main reasons why a BIOS test fails:

 

·The battery on the motherboard has lost its ability to supply power. The system time and date are also affected by a bad battery.

 

·The BIOS was updated "incorrectly". This can happen if power is lost while updating the BIOS, or when the BIOS is updated with an update that was not downloaded from HP, or when a certain virus or malicious program modifies the BIOS data.

 

To resolve the error, use one of the following sections provided by HP.

 

Note: Modifying BIOS/ complementary metal oxide semiconductor (CMOS) settings incorrectly can cause serious problems that may prevent your computer from booting properly. Microsoft cannot guarantee that any problems resulting from the configuring of BIOS/CMOS settings can be solved. Modifications of the settings are at your own risk.

 

If your PC still displays errors about the clock or CMOS when starting the PC, the CMOS battery on the motherboard probably needs to be replaced.

 

You may also contact HP for help.

I have finally resolved my issue. I’m going to put the steps down here I determined work properly in my particular case. Step one of Microsoft’s solution (disconnecting your existing account) works and was an option I wasn’t familiar with. I’ll include that. And I’ll also include and edited version of Microsoft’s step 2. 

While I am a programmer I am not a Windows anything super user. Maybe a little more intuitive than some and I don’t recommend this procedure for a complete novice. But if you follow the steps carefully you will be able to carry out the change. 

To restate the goal. To rename the windows User file path. That means that when you click on your My Documents folder those files are kept in a certain place (file path). And you may have a need for a specific file path. The user file path is built on the device name. The device name is created when you first set the machine up and give two pieces of data (that are not be confused). The Display Name (follow my example – Fred Flinstone) and the device name. I wanted Fred’s device to be named Fred. Which would make the file path name C:\users\fred\documents for My Documents folder. 

Due to poor indications or carelessness on my part at the create screen during my laptop’s initialization I got it wrong. I ended up with \fredflintsone\ in my user path. Which didn’t work with several routines I run across four machines I own and use that all run W8.1. It needed fixing. And apparently this happens occasionally and good answers are hard to find. Which is why I’m creating this. A mixture of Microsoft’s solutions and the result of several attempts and investigation of my own.

Here in this scenario, you will have to create a Local Account first and delete the old account. Once after deleting the old account, you may convert the Local Account to Microsoft Account using the same Account Name.

Step 1: Disconnect the Microsoft Account and switch to Local Account.

a) Press Windows + Q keys and search for Your account settings in the search option.

b) Click on Your Account.

c) Click on Disconnect.

d) Enter your Current Password and click Next.

e) Enter your desired password and click Next.

f) Click onSign out and finish to save the changes.

g) Reboot your machine.

 

For those of you that may not understand what local account means on a W8.1 machine means (I didn’t get it at first). That simply means the account is no longer associated with the email address you use when logging in. Through that email account your machine connects to OneDrive, your computers settings that are saved in the cloud, maybe your Office 365 account, and a host of other very useful services included in W8.1. To me this is like the ‘cloud safe mode’. It means that what you’re doing to your machine will not be saved as some permanent configuration change to your system and it frees up that email address so you can connect it to the new account.

 

Step 2: Create a new Microsoft Account.

 

We’ll skip Microsoft’s vid. It’s about creating that new connected account. But the issue I ran into is that every time I created a new connected account my new device path name came back with \fred_000\. Which was as bad as my \fredflintstone\ path name. What works is creating another non-connected account (very important).

  1. Go to PC settings and click Accounts. (You can do this from either the Control Panel or the new PC Settings app. I’ll explain the PC Settings app)

  2. Below Accounts click Other Accounts.

  3. Click + Add an account.

  4. DO NOT PROVIDE AN email address and click below, Sign in without a Microsoft account (not recommended). No, your machine won’t blow up. You’re just creating another local account that will not go to the cloud to grab any settings or check for any conflicts that it may think need to be resolved.

  5. On the next window select Local Account.

  6. Here’s where I made the mistake in my original setup. You get the Add the User information panel. First field is User name. My Windows experience goes back to 3.1 and I’ve owned and operated every Windows OpSys since. I don’t feel like pulling my last XP Pro machine out but I’m fairly certain the User Name they request is what used to be known as the device name. In that field put the name you want to appear in your \users\ file path. Not what logic tells you is either the user name associated with your email account you’ll later be connecting to this user. And not the Display Name you want to appear on your W8.1 modern interface screen. Right here is where you put the device name you want to appear in your file path.

  7. Provide the two passwords and one password hint. When you click next you’ve created the user.

  8. Click Finish and the user appears below the +Add an account.

  9. Click on the new user and select Edit.

  10. In the dropdown below the user select Administrator.

  11. Click OK.

  12. Reboot.

Step 3: Login with new user. When the login screen comes up it will default to your original Admin account. Click the arrow beside the PW capture box to see all users for this machine. Select your new local account and enter using the PW you created. Once inside go to My Documents and check your path. You’ll have to navigate to something and, as I recall, the only thing in your Documents folder is your Dowloads folder. Go there. Click in the navigation bar in File Explorer and that will bring up and highlight the real path. If it’s what you want, then on to step 4. If not then logout, reboot, go back in as your original user, delete the newly made Local user account. DO NOT DELETE THE ORIGIANL ACCOUNT THAT WAS CONNECTED TO THE CLOUD. And repeat the process until you have what you want. Actually, Step 2 is pretty simple and worked the first time for me.

Step 4: Now that you have an account with the correct users\path\ set and ready to go on local you want to grab the files and folders from your old or original account and put them into the new account. In Microsoft’s response there are detailed instructions for what they suggest. Given I’m not a Super User I decided to shy away from that. First, File Explorer did not have all the options they mentioned. Second, I have enough experience with Windows OS, users, and devices to know that everything has a fingerprint and if I don’t know exactly which files are positively unique to a user (and non-recyclable) and which ones may be copied and recycled, I could lose a lot of work and have to start again. So here’s how I did it.

  1. If not logged in as the new user, do so. You can probably do this from either user but my instructions will be based on the location – new administration user you created.

  2. Open File Explorer and in the left sidebar find This PC. > Below that you’ll see a list of places (Desktop, Documents, Downloads, etc.). At the bottom you’ll see your OS (Operating System) drive. In most cases this will be C:

  3. Click and you’ll see a list of your program, app, and Windows folders. One of those folder is named Users.

  4. Double click and you’ll see Default, Public, and a folder for each user on the machine. Find the folder for the original connected account and open that.

  5. This is the point where Microsoft instructions were to setup the File Explorer for special viewing and grab all folders (copy all) BUT a few specific files, copy, and paste directly to the new user’s user folder. You’ll find their instructions uptop and feel free. I skipped that.

  6. First, you’ll find OneDrive in that user folder. If you want to copy that you may but you can also leave it and just let the drive background download later.

  7. You’ll see the same for any Office 365 OneDrive accounts you may have connected. I have three. Same advice.

  8. Go through and grab Documents, Music, Pictures, Videos, Desktop, Favorites, any other odd folder you may have installed. Copy, paste to new user. Replace existing folders or files unless, for some reason, they come up in use.

  9. IMPORTANT – If you have any macro specific or otherwise customized Word templates, root those out of the template folder on the old user and copy them to the template folder of the new user. You may actually have to log into the old user to get those.

  10. That may or may not take a while. When finished, Step 5

Step 5: Right now it still may not look like your desktop. Now we connect the new account and let it sync. Go to PC Settings again (found off the gear settings icon). Navigate to Accounts. Your account will appear. And just below the name will be a blue link. Connect. Click that, provide the email account that you removed from the original Administrator/User account. It will want the PW, a hint, and, as I recall, will ask you to provide a Display Name. The name that appears with your photo or avatar on the W8.1 modern interface page. Complete the connect process and I recommend one last reboot back into the new account that is now connected to your cloud and other Microsoft Services.

 

What you lose. In my case I had to create all my e-mail accounts once more in Outlook. The system will download settings from the cloud in accordance with how had that feature setup for the original account. Means, if you turned off syncing it won’t find much. But most are synced and it will find your desktop wallpaper, your splash screen for login. You moved your bookmarks. Those are there. But, for example, I need to go to my old user and export my FTP users, paths, and PWs. It got most my PWs from the cloud but, keep in mind, it may not. I had to rebuild my Modern Tile interface. All my programs were there because I never install a program and limit it to any one user. But if you do, you may have to reinstall. It found all my Microsoft service PWs. Did ask me to confirm a few. And you always have the option of trying out Microsoft’s steps above. Maybe you’ll have better results than expected.

Bottom line – I got my user path back. And I’ll leave the old account for a week or two just in case I missed something on the file copy. When I’m sure everything is working I’ll get rid of it.
