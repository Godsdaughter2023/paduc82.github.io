kevin j baird say: Batteries don't expire due to an arbitrary date.  They generally have a maximum number of charges, but typically they just lose capacity.  So at one point a new battery might give you 3 hours of useful life, and then over time it might only give you 2 hours, and then 1 hour, etc.  If the battery goes to zero, this is typically a sign of failure.  Again, it may have failed due to heat, or even a possible short.  Batteries have some fail-safe circuitry in them to prevent them from exploding or catching fire.  So if something isn't right in your machine, the battery can potentially trip one of these fail-safes and they then cease to function.

In most cases, with batteries, with average use, they can last for years.  With heavy use, they can expire, but I've never seen them just sit at zero charge status unless the battery had failed.

Your network drivers being fouled by a security update is potentially possible but unlikely.  Again, I'd really suggest taking it into a shop and having them run diagnostics on it.  There's a lot of potential for problems.

If you don't have the money right now to take it in.  You could try a system restore.  Restore back to an earlier point to when you knew the wireless network was functioning and see if that works for you.

How you activate Windows 10 after reinstalling it depends on how Windows 10 was originally installed on your device.

　

If you activated a free upgrade to Windows 10 or bought and activated Windows 10 from the Windows Store, you have a digital entitlement for your device. This means you can reinstall the same edition of Windows 10 that your device has a digital entitlement for without entering a product key.
