Integrating dual bay JBOD: Volumes -- Simple vs Spanned vs Striped - Microsoft Community

nweissma say:

Integrating dual bay JBOD: Volumes -- Simple vs Spanned vs Striped

The first option that i need to decide on now is not formatting, but rather whether I want to configure as a Simple Volume, Spanning Volume, or Striped Volume. I ask for knowledge: what is Spanning used for and what is Striped used for? And for that matter, what is Simple used for? I know that i can search this information, but if you can relieve me of the labor of plowing through mountains of unintelligible English composition then I'd be grateful: to wit, what is the difference between a VOLUME and a PARTITION? and what does it mean to "mount" a volume?

thomas ferguson say: I have decidted to attempt an answer to those questions which you posed even although I regard it as a risky enterprise. Word useage can differ widely and wildly. I decided this because I was startled to see a paragraph which both used colons properly and properly punctuated 'to wit'.

I am starting at the end: VOLUME and PARTITION. If technical use of the terms in the jargon of computer geeks were compliant with standard english, the PARTITION would be the location or address on the media which marked one end of a data structure. Similarly, VOLUME would be the data locations or addresses whereto the data was recorded. It is similar to WALL and ROOM. Technical use of the terms does not differ much. When one speaks of a PARTITION, one is referring to a data construct which fixes the type, position and extent of a storage container. When one speaks of a volume, one referrs to the storage locations which have been defined for the storage of data. Often, a drive letter is assigned to the volume. It is then a LOGICAL DRIVE.

Note that many people use the term PARTITION when they are actually referring to the space between partitions, actually a volume.

It starts to get confusing when one starts sub-dividing into PRIMARY and EXTENDED PARTITIONS. Suffice it to say that a primary partition originally referred to one defined so it could to hold an operating system. It was limited to one volume which was assigned one drive letter. The exrtended partition was intended for non-executibles, i.e. data. More than one volume could be created within the partition and more than one drive letter was assigned, one for each volume created. Each volume is a logical drive. Now-a-days, the distinction between primary and extended is blurred in use and (worse[?]), some OS installers will allow you to install the OS to an extended partition.

Now, back to Simple, Spanning and Striped.

Simple refers to the fact that the entire volume is on one physical drive in either a primary or extended partiton and is fixed in size.

Spanning refers to the fact that the fact that the partition is defined to start on one physical drive and continue onto one or more others,

Striped refers to the fact that the contoller will deliberately write parts of the data stream in defined, fix-sized chunks to each of the drives which are being used for the volume.

Now, as to which of simple, spanned, striped to use.

Spanning is used mostly when one has to have a very large space for storing large files. On some systems, it is used as a convenient way to increase the size of a volume without requiring the wholesale shifting of data. Avoiding the data shift avoids the time delay particularly with large files and the data integrity problem of having to subject the file to read/write operations.

Striping is a form of Spanning. To the spanning aspect is added the notion of dealing with pre-defined chunk length and balancing the data between physical drives. (I refer here only to setups using the entire drive for the set.) It is preferred to simple spanning (JBOD, for example) where data integrity is paramount. As implemented, it provides various means of error detrection and correction.

Mounting refers to the process of the OS identifying the drive and placing the instructions and data structures in place to enable disk reads and writes.

My suggestions:

If you need large, contiguous storage space, use a simple volume on a large drive. If you need speed, use RAID 0 and make sure you back up critical data frequenthy. For Windows, it is probably not a good idea for most users to use drives larger than 500 gb or 1 TB per RAID 0 set. Use "Enterprise Quality" drives, drives engineered to run continuously for long periods.

I hope this gives you a place to start. Pease don't let it be the only place. You would be well served by reading and consulting widely.

Now, let the discussion begin... or not. ;-))
