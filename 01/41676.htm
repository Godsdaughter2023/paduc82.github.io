lemp say:
Oh no! I hadn't thought of that. So the default BIOS is what it's got to be running on now.  After I replace the c-mos battery should I look for and download an update on the BIOS?
Most modern computers do not need a battery to maintain their settings or to be reconfigured when the battery is changed.  That's why this battery is more accurately called the RTC (Real Time Clock) battery. 

It also is sometimes called the CMOS battery because that is the type of memory (Complementary Metal Oxide Semiconductor) in which settings that the BIOS needs used to be saved.  These days, those settings are saved in a different type of "nonvolatile random access memory" (NVRAM) that doesn't need a battery to maintain the memory.

Back in the old days (perhaps as recently as when Win95 was in use), the electronics might not be able to detect the type of hard drive in use (and possibly other hardware) and those configurations had to be manually set. Because CMOS memory does need a battery to maintain it, when the battery died, you had to reset those configurations.
