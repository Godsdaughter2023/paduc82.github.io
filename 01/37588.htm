The WdiSystemHost and WdiServiceSost services are part of Operating system self-healing framework - those "Checking for a solution to the problem." messages that Windows 7 displays when it's trying to automatically fix something come from the work performed by those two services.
The "Service Hang Report" messages you're seeing do not technically describe a crash. Instead, the Service Control Manager has decided that something has gone wrong in the Wdi services.

The idea of putting your user data on a separate partition is excellent. That way, if your computer crashes you haven't lost your vital records and irreplaceable memories. Let's say your computer crashes (which never happens to me, of course) and you have to restore a backup image from last week. (You probably already know this but) you can move your Documents, Downloads, Desktop, Favorites Music, Pictures and Video to a different partition. If you're using an email program, like Office Outlook, you can store your .pst inside your Documents. So every time you backup your documents, you're also backing up your email, contacts and calendar. Clever! Don't try to move other parts of your user profile - that's not supported.

Here's why your software* needs to be in the same partition as Windows. (You noticed the asterisk, right?) Your software uses system files from Windows. Your software also reads and writes keys from the registry. The integration is important. Separating them is like separating newlyweds. Sure, it's physically possible, but you're looking for trouble. And if you ever need to re-install Windows you'll need to re-install all your software anyway, because re-installing Windows breaks the integration.

*Some applications are completely standalone. They don't rely on Windows system files or the registry. These applications are sometimes called 'portable' because you can put them on a USB flash drive and use them on any computer. And Windows Store apps install in a completely different way so they can be installed on any partition.
