rohn007 say:
BSOD can't really be predicted. One cause of BSODs is file corruption. Another is other hardware failure

File corruption can happen a few ways:
- power interruptions / spikes / brownouts while writing files
- disk sectors going bad.

You can add "power cleaning" tools to level out your power supply. Most often I've seen them included as part of SOME power bars or UPS's, Uninterruptible Power Supplies. In power bars it typically takes the form of electronics that "clamp down" on spikes. But they don't handle brownouts. SOME, not all, UPS's provide "clean power". They do this by providing smooth, "clean", power to the computer from the battery. The battery is constantly charged from the outlet. There is no direct connection from the outside power supply to the computer.

Hard Drives have built in technology to try to deal with small patches of the HD, called sectors, becoming unreadable. When one is found, it is marked unreadable and the system no longer uses it. But if one of your files, system or personal, is located on that bad sector the file becomes corrupted. In addition, most (all?) current HD's have a system called "SMART" that tracks the pattern of sectors going bad to hopefully notify you BEFORE the drive fails completely. SSD's have logically similar technolgy that uses different processes. But none of this tech can keep you completely safe from a drive sector dying with a piece of a file.

Here is a tool to help deal with the BSODs after they happen.

BSOD TROUBLE SHOOTER
https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/help/14238/... shoot-blue-screen-errors


OTHER HARDWARE Failures:

Other hardware (other than HD/SSD) failures can be caused by RAM that has gone bad, problems in the CPU, or other random errors triggered by overheating components.

There are free utilities you can download from the internet to "stress test" your system to see how it is holding up.

************************

The code 0x000005 is very generic. It is the technical way MS says "Oops". There is no single cause or fix for it. They use it when a more appropriate, unique, error code has NOT been generated by the problem.

***********************

Your old system is not totally out of date. Even a 6 year old i5 should be adequate CPU power today. 4GB RAM is still well above MS's stated "official minimum", but is on the lower side of todays operational realities. But it is probably not worth paying to upgrade this old of a system. You can buy a low end system that will provide much better than any single upgrade, for not much more than the price of doing an upgrade on the old system.

If you do plan to buy a new system, the one feature I always look for now is a SSD for the Operating system or a Dual SSD / HD system. I think that is the single most performance impacting piece of hardware on current computers.
