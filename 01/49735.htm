a. user say:

Hi,

Restore points are automatically created each week by System Restore and when your PC detects change, like when you install an app or driver. System restore requires a certain amount of space on your hard drive to function properly. Thus, the number of points may vary depending on the size of your hard drive, how much is free and how much have you done to your computer lately. If a newer one needs to be created and you don't have enough space on the hard drive, the older ones get purged.

In addition, these restore points can be created manually. To do this, you can follow the steps provided on this support article.

Let us know should you need further assistance.

We appreciate the prompt response. At the moment, the created restore points are being managed by the system and doesn't provide an option to relocate to another location. However, you an set the amount of space that system will use for the restore point. To do this, follow the steps below:

  1. Search for Create a restore point from the taskbar and select it from the list of results.
  2. On the System Protection tab, select Configure.
  3. Under Disk Space Usage, move the slider depending on your preferred memory allotment for the restore points
  4. Click OK.

jetta48 say:

The Standard VGA graphics driver is a basic driver that Windows uses when it cannot, for whatever reason, use the correct driver specific  to your graphics adapter. It has very limited capabilities.

The usual solution is to download and install the correct driver for for you graphics adapter. In the first instance go to your PC manufacturer's website and download the latest Windows 7 graphics drivers (assuming they exist). If they are not available there, try the website of the manufacturer of your graphics adaprter instead.

If you are unsure of where to look, post the make and model of your PC (or model of your graphics adapter) here and someone will point you in the right direction.

 

Make sure you pick the 32 or 64-Bit drivers according to your version of Windows*.

*To find out whether you are running 32 or 64-Bit Windows  click:

Start->Control Panel->System

then look where it says, "System type".
