Cisco RV180 (RV180W) small business router and multiple Xboxes - Microsoft Community

plxel hunter say:

Cisco RV180 (RV180W) small business router and multiple Xboxes (NAT errors/port forwarding)

I spent several hours troubleshooting how to get a Cisco RV180 small business router working in my home with 2 Xboxes, and think I got everything working! Since there was such a dearth of information on this model router, I wanted to share my experiences so others can benefit from what I learned. If you just want the settings for a Cisco RV180 to work, without the history, jump to the last paragraph.

.

First of all, I was doing my troubleshooting the weekend after Christmas when Xbox LIVE was having service issues, which just made things all that more difficult!

.

Anyway, I originally had a Linksys BEFSR41 wired router. The problem with the consumer level gear is it tends to fail every 18-24 months and I was getting sick of replacing them (routers, modems, wireless access points) so often. So I chose to go with the Cisco RV180 which is a small business class router. It is a little more robust (software and hardware quality) than the consumer level stuff, and I hope that between a longer life and more options, this router will last me a good many years.

.

I have 2 Xboxes on my home network (house is wired with Cat-5) and the Linksys was configured with Port Triggering for the ports required by Xbox LIVE. This worked... somewhat. If only one Xbox was on at a time, port forwarding worked fine and I got no errors when running the Network Connection test on the Xbox. However, once both Xboxes were on, there were some wonky issues that cropped up now and then. Particularly when my wife and I played Borderlands 2 together, the chatting would cut out randomly. Seems the BEFSR41's OS just couldn't handle port forwarding to multiple Xboxes at the same time.

.

I spent hours trying to get the RV180 working correctly. My first attempts were to manually set up Port Triggering, which didn't work even with only one Xbox on at a time. (Don't ask me why... I don't know.) The Network Connection test on the Xbox always came up with those NAT errors we all love to hate. I called both Cisco and Xbox support and spent a total of about 3 hours on the phone. We tried turning on UPnP, QoS, and fiddled with the port forwarding and port triggering and nothing worked... the Network Connection tests on the Xboxes always threw the NAT error messages. Plus, I started having trouble logging into Xbox LIVE at all!

.

After giving up with support, I did one last Google search and came across the following site: http://www.unofficialguidetolive.co.uk/faqs/60-two-xboxes-using-one-router-internet-connection

.

This was good stuff and started to make more sense about why the NAT errors cause so many problems. I was about to bit the bullet and go out and buy another consumer grade D-Link router that was known to support multiple Xboxes. Then I read the paragraph about trying to see if your router will work, and lo and behold, once I made the following setting changes, both my Xboxes have been working flawlessly, passing the Network Connection tests (at the same time) and playing Borderlands 2 with my wife no chat problems.

.

On the RV180, I made sure all port forwarding and port triggering was cleared out. I made sure UPnP was turned on. QoS was already on, so I left it that way. Then I went to each Xbox and set it to automatically obtain a network address. (This is important, because I had been using static IPs and they did not work.) As soon as I made these setting changes, everything worked! I began seeing port forwarding data in the UPnP tablel on the router, which I didn't see any time previously. The 169.254.x.x range of an IP address is also known as a link-local address, or APIPA (Automatic Private IP Addressing). This address is a self assigned address that host machine uses when communication can't be resolved between a host machine and the DHCP server.

If you haven't alrady, change the Ethernet cable you use between your console and the Fritz box.

I would also suggest that you disable Port Forwarding and DMZ (if these features are still active).

Make sure UPnP (Universal Plug and Play) is ticked enable.

Make sure to save your changes and reboot your gateway afterwards.

Go to Xbox Network settings and select Alternate MAC address and select Clear.

Reboot your console and let us know if your console can obtain IP configuration from the DHCP server and not the 169.254.x.x range of an address.
