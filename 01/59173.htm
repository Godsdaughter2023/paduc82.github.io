arminatorx say:

You don't stream Steam games to the console. The console's remote display feature is able to stream any display content (including Steam games) from your PC to your console. 

Since the game is not running on your actual console (the console just acts as an "extension cord" to your PC), you can mod and cheat, and hack and do whatever the heck you want, and it won't have any effect on your Xbox or your Xbox Live membership (unless you're streaming a Xbox Live enabled game on the PC). 

However, be aware that if you stream a Steam game on your console, the Steam terms of use take effect, so if you use unauthorized mods or cheats, and Valve's anti-cheat feature kicks in, your Steam account is still at risk. 

However, unless you're playing a Xbox Live enabled game on Steam (like the upcoming Halo Master Chief Collection on Steam), you can do whatever you like, and it will have the same effect, as if your Xbox console were simply turned off. 

The Xbox has nothing to do with your Steam behavior, as it is simply a display for whatever you do on you PC.

¡firedog say:

In this forum, there seems to be a difference between e-mail accounts and messages that I do not understand.

Account unfortunately means different things in different contexts.

You can think of it in general as being analogous to a bank account which you hold with a bank (your ESP - Email Service Provider - in your case Yahoo, AOL, Gmail and MSN = Hotmail). Incoming and outgoing messages are the credits and debits on the account. There is an account number (your email address) and (in some cases) a username (your Yahoo or AOL screen name) and a password for each account.

You also have a different kind of account with your ISP, with different credentials (username and password). You can't connect to the Internet without such an account, but in some cases you don't need to give any credentials, for example when using free wi-fi.

When mail clients mention exporting accounts (in WLMail 2011, when you press Alt F E A and click Export, for example), that is sloppy shorthand for exporting account settings - email address, display name, username, reply-to address, organization, server addresses and ports and authentication and security settings, but no passwords - so they can be re-used elsewhere. This meaning is also the one intended when you read about exporting accounts, messages and contacts.

Another possible source of confusion is Windows account, or Windows User account. You cannot use a Windows computer (at least XP or later) without having an account on that computer, even though you may not notice if you are the single user of a single computer not connected to any other except over the Internet. Most of the program settings for WLMail are stored in your Windows user account, also known as your user profile.

Messages is used in WLMail as shorthand for mail messages, newsgroup posts and RSS feeds, and in most cases simply means mail messages. These are individual files whose contents you can read in context in WLMail's user interface. They are plain text files with the extension EML, so you can also open them with Notepad or preview them in Windows Explorer.

WLMail has no inherent automatic backup function. If you want to use backup software to maintain emergency copies of important messages, you should instruct it to back up the following folders in your Store folder:

(a) Account folders, identified by the domain name followed by the username in brackets, e.g. Msn (andre) or Gmail (andre). The folder name will be truncated if that occupies more than 11 characters, to the first 11 characters followed by a space and three random characters.
(b) Storage folders

To find your store folder, in WLMail 2011 press Ctrl-Shift-O for Options. On the Advanced tab, click Maintenance and then Store folder. This folder is hidden by default.

Windows Live Mail has its own support forums at the Windows Live Solution Center. Please direct any further questions you may have to one of the forums on the right-hand side of this page. You will probably be asked to give your versions of WLMail and Windows so that the answer can be more precise. To find your program version, in WLMail press Alt-H A. If this doesn't show you the version and build number, press Escape twice, then Alt-F B.
