Hi Maureen,

 

Does your router have security protection enabled?  My guess is it doesn't and that's how that person is using your wireless connection (just like someone visiting you with a laptop could if you set them up).

 

Typically, access to the router is by typing http://192.168.1.1 (or whatever your gateway address is) into your browser address bar and you'll bring up the router's configuration settings.  It will probably want a username and password.  Sorry, I can't help you there because I have no idea what it might be.  Try admin/admin or admin/password or administrator/admin or administrator/password or admin and no password or administrator and no password (the classic default settings).  Your ISP or the router manufacturer's technical support department may be able to assist you with setting this up.  After that, how to set up the security is completely dependent on the ISP, the router, the type of operating system you have, the type of internet connection you have and a number of other factors that I have no way of knowing even if you told me that information.

 

Actually, to see if this even applies, go to Network and Sharing Center and click on Manage Wireless Connections.  Select and double-click on your network.  Click on the Security Tab and see if it is configured with entries in the first two drop boxes and dots in the Key indicating a password applies.  If this does not exist, then there is no security on your wireless router.  If it exists, then your network is probably secured.

 

When you see the connection active, go to Start / All Programs / Accessories / and double click on Control Panel.  Type IPCONFIG /ALL and see if you can identify this other computer connection.  It should provide more information about it for identification purposes.

 

If your router is not secured, you need to secure it and that should resolve the problem.  If your router is secured, then you should change the router's password/key to see if that resolves the problem (and then you'll need to change the key to all your wireless network computers and devices to match the new one).  If that still doesn't work, then post back and we'll come up with some other ideas (assuming the ISP or router manufacturer can't help you with this).

ethan b. say:
Hey KurtChau,

This is a recovery partition brought into view in 1803. You cannot remove, but only you must remove its mapping of its respective drive letter, which is K. Once you do this, it brings it back to its intended visibility, being hidden and out of sight. Once you do this, you won't be pestered with low disk space notifications anymore. You will need to remove the drive letter and mount point in DiskPart in order to do this.

Hit Windows key + R together to go into Run. Type in the following: diskpart

Enter the following in the new DiskPart window:

select volume K
remove letter=K

Does not work after a reboot? Try entering this instead in an administrator session of Command Prompt: mountvol K: /D

a. user say:

Hi Ahmed,

I will certainly help you with the issue you are experiencing in connecting with Ethernet.

This issue might occur, if the network adapter driver is corrupt.

Please follow below mentioned steps to fix the issue:

Step 1:  Troubleshooting Network adapter.

  1. Press Windows Key + X from your keyboard and select Control Panel option.

  2. Change to Large under View all option, you will see at right top corner of the window.

  3. Click on Troubleshooting, Go to View all option (you will see at left pane), select Network adapter from the list and click on troubleshoot.

Step 2:

  1. Press Windows key + X and click on Command prompt (Run as admin).

  2. Enter the below command and press Enter.

    ipconfig /renew

  3. Now restart your PC and check the Ethernet settings.

If the issue still persists,

Please refer the following Microsoft article on Windows 10 help & how-to to fix issue with Network.

https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/help/10741/windows-10-fix-network-connection-issues

Please get back to us, if you still facing issue

barb bowman say:
Make sure your Surface is fully charged and plugged in, then try a complete
reimage from a downloadable recovery image:
 
Go to https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/surfacerecoveryimage   (with a
different Windows computer if yours is not working properly)  and download a
recovery image for your Surface. You will need a 16GB USB stick formatted FAT32.
Unzip/extract the recovery zip to the formatted USB stick and then-
 
To reset your Surface from a USB recovery drive:
Step 1:    Make sure your Surface is turned off and plugged in (hold down the power
button for 30 seconds).
Step 2:     Insert the USB recovery drive into the USB port on your Surface.
Step 3:    Press and hold the volume-down button while you press and release the
power button on your Surface.
Step 4:     When the Surface logo appears, release the volume button.
Step 5:    When prompted, select the language and keyboard layout you want.
Step 6:     Select Troubleshoot > Reset your PC.
    If prompted for a recovery key, select Skip this drive at the bottom of
the screen.
Step 7:     Select the target operating system you wish to reset. This
refers to the current operating system installed on your Surface.
Step 8:    Select Yes, repartition the drives, and on the next page, select Next.
Step 9:     Choose Just remove my files.
Step 10:    Select Reset.
    Surface restarts and the Surface logo displays while the reset process
continues (this can take a while).
