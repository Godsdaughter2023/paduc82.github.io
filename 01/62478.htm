james vinyard say:

Yea, those are the charge life cycles.  However, Lithium-Ion batteries are unpredictable, but, store electricity better than nickel-cadmium or lead acid.  Plus they are lighter in weight.

As for unpredictable, you can get anywhere between 200 to 5,000 charge cycles out of them depending on what model.

Now, for the charging of batteries; there is a circuit that cuts off charging while plugged in when it detects that the battery reaches 100%.  That is a safety factor to keep the battery from catching on fire or exploding.  Lithium-Ion retains that charge almost 100% for a lengthy period of time where the other two types bleed off in the atmosphere.

In essence, keeping it plugged in all the time has no effect to the battery at all and does not change charge cycles that, that particular model's specifications apply.  It doesn't do anything to the battery.  Your car does the same thing with it's battery and alternator combination.

As I said, it is bogus.  The number of charge cycles will vary between different models of Lithium-Ion batteries and that is a 'best guess' number based on testing them.  I have seen new batteries last only two days.  I have worked on a laptop that stays plugged in all the time (user uses it at home as their only computer) and it is over 12 years old and still has the original battery.  It was rated for 4.5 hours and it gets 4.5 hours still (very rare in today's standards).

During a life of a battery, there will always be a reduction in voltage of cells.  Lithium-Ion has the least degradation compared to other model types.

I have seen a lot of people worried about leaving a laptop plugged in all the time and what the manufacturers recommend is to "exercise" the battery at least weekly or monthly.  Deplete them down to about 10% and then recharge them.  You do that, and you will get the most life out of the battery and still be able to use it on A/C for the bulk of the time.

For the tablet being docked would be the same as being hooked up to the A/C adapter in which, that is what the docking station is acting as.

Exercise the battery frequently per month (I would recommend) and you will get a long life out of them.  If they fail, it is almost instantly.  The other two types fail gradually and also lose life per each recharge (especially nickel-cadmium because they are not designed to be recharged even though you can).

Wired has good articles and they are basing that on mean time averages of Lithium-Ion batteries in general.  Some last longer than others.

TrustedInstaller is used by a service called “Windows Module Installer” which is used to install Windows 7 system modules. The system modules include Windows Update and Automatic Updates which automatically scans your system to check for any new updates and hotfixes available. So when scheduled Windows Update is performing its update scan or check on your system, the TrustedInstaller.exe process increases the CPU usage.

 

The simplest resolution for the TrustedInstaller high CPU usage problem is to wait for a while for the TrustedInstaller.exe to complete its job. Once it finishes running the tasks, the CPU usage rate will return to normal.

 

However, if you want to stop TrustedInstaller.exe from taking up all your CPU resource, here’s a suggestion that may help.

 

Method 1: Disable Windows from automatically updating.

 

Here’s how: Turn automatic updating on or off

 

After disabling automatic updates, depending on your preference, you can change the way Windows notify you about updates.

Refer this article: Change how Windows installs or notifies you about updates

 

Method 2: Try to run windows update troubleshooter:
http://windows.microsoft.com/en-US/windows7/Open-the-Windows-Update-troubleshooter

 

Method 3: Check the update history.

http://windows.microsoft.com/en-US/windows7/See-which-Windows-updates-are-installed
