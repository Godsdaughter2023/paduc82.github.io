andre da costa say:

Yes, you can upgrade a OEM System Builder Home Premium to Professional by using either a retail upgrade or full version key or Anytime Upgrade key.

 

If the license for Windows 7 Professional you have access to is either a retail upgrade or full version, you can use the product key and Anytime Upgrade to move from Windows 7 Home Premium to Windows 7 Professional. First, make sure the edition of Windows 7 you are upgrading from is already activated (if its not you will run into complications and you might have start all over by doing a clean install). Click Start, type Anytime Upgrade, click the option to enter a key, enter the Windows 7 Professional key when requested, click Next, wait while the key is verified, accept the license agreement, click upgrade, wait while the software upgrades, (it might take 10 minutes or more depending on if updates are needed), your computer will automatically restart, after restarting, Windows 7 will notifiy you its updating the computer, the computer will restart one more time automatically and the upgrade will be completed, a windows will pop up notifying the upgrade was successful and your computer is ready to use, click close, you should be upgraded to Windows 7 Professional your files, programs and settings preserved.

 

Purchasing through Anytime Upgrade:

First, make sure the edition of Windows 7 you are upgrading from is already activated (if its not you will run into complications and you might have start all over by doing a clean install). Click Start, type Anytime Upgrade, click the option to purchase a key. Wait while the Anytime Upgrade page is loaded, click the Windows 7 Professional Upgrade to buy it.

Click Next, fill out your contact information (it is important you do this, since the product key will be emailed to you as a backup just in case you need to reinstall it) enter your credit card information, click Next to begin the upgrade, wait while the key is verified, accept the license agreement, click upgrade, wait while the software upgrades, (it might take 10 minutes or more depending on if updates are needed), your computer will automatically restart, after restarting, Windows 7 will notify you its updating the computer, the computer will restart one more time automatically and the upgrade will be completed, a windows will pop up notifying the upgrade was successful and your computer is ready to use, click close, you should be upgraded to Windows 7 Professional your files, programs and settings preserved, proceed to activate Windows 7 Professional.

 

Note:

Please note that Anytime Upgrade does not support OEM, MSDN-Academic, Volume License (student faculty licenses). If you are in posession of any of these media, you will have to back up and do a custom clean install. If you wish to do this, please backup your Windows 7 Home Premium or whichever edition you currently have installed. Certain licenses for Windows distributed to students at the tertiary level are not perpetual, meaning you can't continue using the software after completing a course or graduating.

a. user say:

You'll never find a time when everybody agrees that an update is 'safe,' whatever that means. Some people will never be able to successfully install updates because their computers are too messed up, and an update can't work with a messed up computer.

If you don't have the Professional edition of Windows 10 - which costs an additional $99 over the Home edition - then you really should, because it gives you the ability to delay updates. That's important, because the conventional wisdom, going back to the beginning of Windows, is to wait at least 3 or 4 months before installing an upgrade. Leave it to the professional testers and corporate IT departments to uncover the issues that are virtually guaranteed in a project as huge as Windows, and give Microsoft an opportunity to fix them. Then the rest of us can install the update.

You can't install updates whenever you want - you have to wait for them to come to you. The way updates work: They are released in waves over many months to selected groups of computers. After every wave, Microsoft waits to find out how successful the update was - or not. This helps Microsoft catch problems before they get sent out to every computer.

In my case, for example, I delay feature updates for 90 days. That means my computer isn't even eligible to receive an update for that length of time. After 90 days, my computer becomes eligible to be included in a wave, which may come the next day, next week, next month, or some other time.

A restore point is not a backup. If you want to restore your computer to the way it was on a particular date, you can only do that by restoring an image backup that you made on that date. With third-party image backup software (which is really the only way to make an image backup on Windows 10) you can do this every day, easily and automatically.

If you're a developer, you have to compromise your software so that it will run on as many different variations of Windows as possible. And if you're Microsoft, you're not able to provide the same features, bug fixes or security updates to all your users, because some of your users don't have the necessary code in their variation of Windows.
