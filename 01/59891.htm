a. user say:

Hi,

Thank you for contacting Microsoft Forums.

On the desktop window, click the Task view icon from the taskbar.

From the displayed bar just above the taskbar, click the + sign to add a new virtual desktop.

After a virtual desktop is added, although all the virtual desktop screens will have the same desktop icons, opened applications on one desktop will not be displayed on the others However, if you have added a few virtual desktops, you can move applications from one virtual desktop to another by following the instructions given below:

  1. Make sure that you are on the desktop screen that has the application that you want to move.
  2. Ensure that the application is opened and you can access its window.
  3. Keeping the application’s interface open, click the Task view icon from the taskbar
  4. Once the mini bar appears and the desktop screen gets dimmed, on the desktop screen, right-click the title bar of the opened application that you wish to move to another virtual desktop.
  5. From the context menu, hover mouse to the Move option.
  6. From the submenu that appears, click the name of the virtual desktop where you want to move the opened application.

Hope this helps.

tryx3 say:

Are you sure that the S-Video on your PC is an input?  I have only seen them as PC output connectors.  Check your PC manual or your PC maker's support site.  Your PC maker would also, if it really is an input, explain how to make use of that input and might even provide software to do the job.

If it really is an input on your PC then all you'd have to select would be the software and I'd recommend ArcSoft for the reason described below.  You would need to check that their software can handle inputs coming through s-video input connectors as that is not a question I considered at the time I looked into their software.

You would need to get a cable to get from your VCR to your PC but that would be simple and, if you tell me which output connections you have on your VCR, I can explain what cables / connection adapters you would need.

- - - - - - - - -

Otherwise,

1  Your own recordings on DVD

You can backup your own DVDs onto your PC by copying them from your DVD drive.  The software you need to do this depends on the specific equipment & format used to write the DVD in the first place.  Most equipment that can make DVDs has the capability of connecting & transferring directly to a PC so you would be better off using that.

I once had a Sony DVD recorder that wrote in DVD-RW "-VR" format with a file extension .vro and the only software I found that could copy that onto my PC correctly was ISOBuster. 

2  Commercial recordings on DVD [films etc]

In some jurisdictions but not others, you can backup commercial DVDs onto your PC if you then retain the original DVD as proof of your licence for the recording.  The software I last tested for this function was called DVDSmith Movie Backup but I cannot find any notes about where I got it.

3  VCR recordings

I once tried to use an adapter kit to transfer video tapes to my PC.  The adapter is called a Video capture adapter and it will connect to an output of your VCR player and send the data to a USB on the PC. 

The software that comes with it can then take the signal coming in to the USB port and record it to a video file.  Note that it will only do so in real time - if you want to transfer a 2 hour recording then it will take 2 hours.  The software was called ArcSoft Showbiz. 

The adapter I tested could connect to my VCR's "RCA" connectors [a set of three connectors - commonly red, yellow & black/white] or to my VCR's s-video output connector or to a Scart/s-video adapter on the end of my VCR's Scart output. 

I found that I could not transfer widescreen recordings properly but that was 4 years ago so perhaps they have improved.  The firm that sold the adapter were awful but I was extremely impressed by ArcSoft's Tech Support team - they tried very hard to help me get everything working even though the problem was the fault of the adapter not their software [their software had just been bundled with it so I had not even paid them for it directly].

If I was to go through this again, I would start by looking into the latest versions of ArcSoft "video capture" software.  I would also look for their recommendations for suitable adapter hardware [I think they have user forums so you'd be able to get the opinions of their users as well].  All these adapters look the same but if you find one that ArcSoft recommend then I would expect you to have a better chance of success.

4  Editing videos on your PC

[If you get ArcSoft "video capture" software then you might find that it can help you edit the recordings as well.  I don't know as I didn't get that far.]

I have found VideoReDo to be much better than others I have tried.  It really impressed me with its ability to sort out problems with TV recordings when there had been interference to the TV signal during the recording.

I think there are lots of others around and some of the free ones might do a good job for all I know.

- OEM versions do not offer any free Microsoft direct support from Microsoft support personnel

- OEM licenses are tied to the very first computer you install and activate it on

- OEM versions allow all hardware upgrades except for an upgrade to a different model motherboard
