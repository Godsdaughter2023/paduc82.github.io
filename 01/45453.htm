how to remove write protection from floppy Disk - Microsoft Community

josephwilson7.1 say:

how to remove write protection from floppy Disk

How do I remove write Protection from a 1.3Mb floppy disk 
langware say: Sometimes the little "slider switch" on 3.5" floppies were removed (to make the floppy permanently read-only). If that's the case, then just put a small piece of opaque tape over the "hole" (where the slider switch was) ... and you will be able to write to the floppy.

If you hold the floppy disk so you are looking at the back, the slider switch (or hole where the slider switch was) is at the bottom RIGHT.

Please note that the 3.5 inch floppies came in various capacities (the two most common were 720 KB and 1.44 MB. If you hold the floppy so you are looking at the back, then there will be a hole on the bottom LEFT that is used to tell the drive what the floppy disk's capacity is. If memory serves me correctly, there will be a hole at the bottom LEFT (when viewing the back of the disk), for disks with 1.44 MB capacity, and if no hole exists on bottom LEFT, then the floppy is 720 KB.

My point .... if you need to put a piece of opaque tape over the hole where the  read-write slider was (to make the disk writable), be sure to put the tape over the hole at bottom RIGHT (when viewing the BACK of the disk ... and by "back", I mean the side without a label and where you can see the disk's spindle).

Windows 7 Enterprise suddenly says it's not genuine

I am running an government enterprise version of Windows 7 on a VM.  I am a support vendor.  All of a sudden after a year my OS tells me it's no longer genuine. 

I have read a few posts related to this on here.  I work remotely; therefore, I am not in the office 99% of the time.  I read somewhere on here that i need to actually revalidate periodically with the management server.  Is that a standard thing?  Running into the office is kind of a big deal for me, and I support my customer via the VM.

hairyfool say:

All Enterprise licenses are issued under Volume Licensing and these require a License Management Server on the corporate network to activate initially and as you have found need to revalidate at set periods with the original server

License Management was tightened up significantly with Vista so yes it is a "standard thing". You need to be able to connect your VM directly to the Management Server and the only real way to do that remotely would be through a VPN connection.

Otherwise you would need a single user license purchased which would exclude Enterprise.

andre da costa say:

Windows 7 Enterprise is a volume license client. Some volume license clients can use either MAK licensing (Multiple Activation Key), which works like a retail license or KMS (Key Management Server), which requires connecting to a work place Domain or VPN every 180 days (6 months).

It sounds like you are using KMS licensing, which would require reconnecting to your business network periodically to maintain its activation status. If you are unable to do this, you might want to contact your IT department about obtaining a MAK license for Windows 7 Enterprise then update the product key values.
