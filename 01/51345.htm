inn0v8shin say: Kennywally, thank you for posting back to the Microsoft forums.

I would advise against using mineral oil as you are simply trying to make sure there are no water spots on the platter, you are not trying to shine it or lubricate it. Scratches should not be an issue unless you overheard a clicking or banging noise coming from within the drive when you attempted using it. I have seen many a drive that has been flooded with water and usually they did not show signs of physical damage as the drive returns to what is called the "Landing Zone" (edge of platter) when the drive encounters issues.

Mineral oil if used would then need to be cleaned off as it is oil based, my suggestion is to first start with the most unabrasive solvents. Alcohol, and maybe Methyl-Ethyl-Ketone, but start with alcohol first as it is the most unabrasive and will not leave residue behind on the platter. Keep in mind the lens spray you mention will usually be a mix of isopropyl alcohol (not to be confused with isoproponol) which is two parts, alcohol and oil used to both clean and fill tiny scratches in glass lenses. Which in turn still leaves residue behind, as do all other solvents except rubbing alcohol and MEK, MEK is pretty strong however we used to use it to remove the protective film from computer motherboards prior to repair. Hence my recommendation to first start with alcohol.

Use a lense cloth, or microfiber keeping them slightly damp to avoid static, but not too damp to where they will cause streaking. (alcohol & MEK will not streak)

Now assuming you do clean the platter correctly you will still have the issue of other parts being damaged from water. Those of note would be the actuator arm, the electric motor, and the actuator solenoid. The electric motor that spins the platter and the solenoid are both comprised of electric coils wound around a set of magnets held in place by floating bearings secured by tiny circlips. You would have to clean both the electric motor and the solenoid out completely, as water can wash away the lithium based grease on the insides causing the "floating" characteristics of the bearings to disappear, once this happens tolerances change and drive characteristics do as well. And while it is true that the hard drive can compensate for small tolerances it cannot compensate for large differences due to a platter spinning differently. As for the actuator arm if it touched the platter then it may have damaged itself and the platter. In a perfect situation the actuator arm never actually touches the platter, it merely floats above it.

This is why I recommend just cleaning the platter and finding an identical drive to place the platter in as overhauling this drive will be a serious undertaking.

Good luck with this process
regards,
Aaron Barth

inn0v8shin say:

Kennywally, thank you for posting back to the Microsoft forums.

10 times out of 10, the outer seal that lines the top cover of the hard drive is never the entry point for water to enter the drive. It is always the breather hole.

If you have a 40GB that has the same physical likeness as the drive in question then you could probably use that and replace both the platter and the circuit board on the underside. I myself have saved a couple drives which have had faulty circuit boards on the bottoms. I learned that no harm came from using the incorrect board with a different sized platter, though the drive did not operate correctly. (and Windows did not even try to mount it)

Do not worry about alcohol dulling the surface, that will not happen, besides most consumer rubbing alcohol is actually 70% alcohol and 30% water. The reason why I advise against mineral oil is because you want to NOT handle the platter as much as possible, shining it then cleaning it then buffing it might compromise things. Start with the simplest most non-volatile approach first.
1.Disassemble
2.Clean platter with alcohol, make sure it looks pretty
3.Replace in drive and reassemble
4.Test

Then proceed with further steps if previous steps failed.

Hard drive manufacturers usually make their products in such a universal way that housings, circuit board pin-outs, and rotation speed usually remain the same across different drives. (same manufacturer of course) Of course platter densities change and so do the circuit boards. Once you get it open I am sure you will be disappointed with how much water actually got in there, as it wont be much at all, (mostly condensation and tiny droplets) just heed my advise and start with the most unabrasive solvents first and remove those water spots. If this becomes an ongoing project for you and you need to store the platter store it back in the drive itself. (Some data recovery places store customer platters in a lead-composite based case.)

And no your not dreaming, a lot of the Maxtor 40GB slim hard drives had single platters.

Good luck sir.

inn0v8shin say: Kennywally, thank you for posting back to the Microsoft forums.

I told you, you would be disappointed by what you would see in the actual hard drive. If there is no visible damage from water/water spots on the platter then the platter is okay, and should be handled as least as possible.

As for the dust, just attempt to blow it off the platter, if the dust seems stuck to the platter then you may attempt cleaning with alcohol. The same rules apply to cleaning a platter as they do when cleaning a CD, straight lines from center to edge of disc.

It looks like its time to start swapping parts around, the main parts to tbe concerned about are: platter, circuit board, rest of unit. You can mix and match parts based on work needed and ease of interchangeability. Meaning if the inside looked okay and you have an identical drive then leave the platter in-place and start by swapping the circuit board, if it still fails then remove the platter and then transfer platter and known-good circuit board back to known-good case/unit. I cannot stress how important it is to start with the easiest most non-invasive of things first and then work your way to the more involved processes.

I should also tell you I have run a hard drive before without its top cover just so I could extract data from a customers PC. Her hard drive showed signs of the "click-of-death" and would get worse as the unit heated up. An old trick is to place the drive in the freezer then attempt data recovery. I froze her hard drive then quickly removed the top cover as I loosened the screws the day prior. I then recovered all of her data, though it wasnt much just ~20GB.
