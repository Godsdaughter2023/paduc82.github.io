vibroplex say:

What happens if you do Settings | System | Network | Wired | Test Xbox LIVE Connection ?

---

If the test is AOK then it sounds like the local profile is out of date or corrupted, and when an app or routine asks for data from it, it causes the involuntary disconnection. I'd start by clearing the system cache (it's just temporary files plus game updates, automatically reloaded) by:

1) Please do Settings | System Settings | Storage

2) Move over any hard drive or memory unit (not the Cloud) & press (Y) on your controller & pick Clear System Cache.

3) When asked to confirm storage device maintenance, choose Yes.

4) Repeat that twice more

* Re-download *

5) Tap the silver Xbox Guide button

6) Select Download Profile (and if you don't see it, press the blue X button to make sure you're not signed in) and proceed with Profile Download.

7) Enter the Microsoft Account addresse for your Xbox LIVE gamertag when asked.

8) Enter the password for the Microsoft Account.

8A) If your account is an Xbox LIVE child account, the parent’s Windows Live ID & password is required as well to download your profile.

9) Pick the storage device for your profile (choose Hard Drive if you have one, or Memory Unit if you don't have a Hard Drive).

10) When the download completes you will be asked if you want the console to remember your password. If it's your home console, I'd say yes, otherwise I'd think carefully about leaving my profile unprotected on somebody else's Xbox!

11) Sign in, and retry.

--

If the test fails and if only the Xbox fails to connect, then your Router, Modem or ISP are accidentally blocking Port 3074 traffic. 'Ports' are numbers encoded in each Internet data packet which tell the router what to do with your data. If that data is blocked or delayed, you won't get connected, or you won't be able to connect to friends, or you will get a NAT warning.

A sequential power cycle is the first step in clearing connection problems, and the order and timing of the steps is important to success:

1) turn off the Xbox and unplug the power supply cord from the back of the Xbox then

2) unplug power from the router (Some folks have a combination modem-router AKA 'gateway' or only use a modem with no router. If either, skip steps 3 & 6).

3) unplug power from the modem.

4) Wait at least 5 minutes then replug the modem/gateway power.

5) Wait 'til the light on the front of the modem/gateway which says BROADBAND, ONLINE, READY, CABLE or DSL is on solid (not flashing), then plug in the router. If you have a gateway instead of a modem and router or a modem with no router, then when that light says it's ready, skip to step 7.

6) Wait until the WiFi light on the router is on; you may or may not use WiFi, but it's still a good indicator your router is finished booting up.

7) Plug in and turn on the Xbox.  If the problem persists, please send us the info we need  so we may work on this further:

nick1946 say:

Win 7 - Windows Image Backup recovery error 0x8078002c


Upgraded from Vista to Win 7 64 bit, completed a window image backup successfully to external usb hard drive without any problems.  Then had the dreaded blue screen. Sys repair didn't work. Had to re-install Win 7.  Tried to recover windows image got an error 0x8078002c one backup file is corrupt.

Can the file be repaired or can I get any of my data retrieved?  Otherwise, I have lost everything. Thanks for any help or advise.

frederik long say:

You can access individual files or folders in an image file with the procedure below. Whether it works on a corrupted image I do not know. Note also that image files are not intended for backing up your data. The recommended process is to back up your data files to at least one but preferably two alternating external storage media that are kept well away from the PC most of the time.

  1. Use Windows Explorer to locate the .vhd (virtual hard disk) file on your USB disk. I will look like this:
    E:\WindowsImageBackup\PC1\Backup 2010-12-12 175726\0417acc3-2173-11df-9f99-806e6f6e6963.vhd
    Note the .vhd extension.
  2. Launch the Disk Manager (diskmgmt.msc).
  3. Click Action, then Attach Virtual Hard Disk.
  4. Use the Browse button to locate the file you found in Step 1 above, then click OK.
  5. Note the new drive letter that Windows assigned. It could be G:. If no drive letter was assigned, right-click the new disk and give it a drive letter.
  6. Use Windows Explorer to open drive G: and retrieve your files.
  7. When finished, close Windows Explorer.
  8. Go back to the Disk Manager.
  9. Right-click the new disk on the left side of the panel where it says "Basic" and "Online".
  10. Click "Detach VHD".

volunteer j say:

Movie Maker has compatibility issues with MPEG files...
it may be worth a try to convert the MPEG file to the WMV
format before importing into Movie Maker.

There are many programs that can do conversions...
The following freeware is one example...:

(FWIW...it's always a good idea to create a System
Restore point before installing software or updates)

Format Factory
http://www.videohelp.com/tools/Format_Factory
(the 'direct link' is fastest)
(the file you want to download is : >FFSetup260.zip<)
(FWIW...when installing...you may want to uncheck
'all' the boxes on the final screen)

First you will need to unzip the file or just open the
folder and drag FFSetup260.exe out of the folder
and drop it on your desktop. Left click it to install.

Next...after downloading and installing Format
Factory...you may want to open the program and
left click the 'Option' button on the toolbar and
select an 'Output Folder' / Apply / OK.
(this is where you will find your files after they
are converted)

Drag/drop your video clips onto the main screen...

Select 'All to WMV' / OK...

Click...Start...on the toolbar...

That should do it...

Good luck...
