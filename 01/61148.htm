edmaste say:

I found the answer in a Microsoft forum:

WhitePhantom replied on March 26 2010
 

.      Log in to Windows as a Power User or Administrator - you'll need elevated priveleges to access Registry Editor.


.      Click the Windows Start button, then "Run" (or press the WINDOWS + R keys on your keyboard.) In the Open dialog, type REGEDIT then click OK. This will open Registry Editor.

 

.      To change the setting for all users of the PC, browse to HKEY_LOCAL_MACHINE\Software\Microsoft\Windows Desktop Search\DS.
To change the setting for the current User account only, browse to HKEY_CURRENT_USER\Software\Microsoft\Windows Desktop Search\DS.

 

.      In the right window pane, double-click the "ShowStartSearchBand" item. In the Value Data field, change the "1" to "0". Click OK, then close Registry Editor.

 

.      Now, when you right-click a folder in Explorer and select "Search", the Search Companion window will open instead of WDS.

 

If you do it for both "LOCAL" and "CURRENT" then the search companion shows without having to reboot.

Hi Mathew,

Thank you for posting your query in Microsoft Community.

Ideally for a 1366 X 768 resolution, a refresh rate of at least 75 hertz generally produces no black borders or any flickering issue on the screen with most of the graphics cards. 

You might need to change your screen resolution before changing the refresh rate because not every screen resolution is compatible with every refresh rate. The higher the resolution, the higher your refresh rate should be.

Refer to this article to know more information about choosing the best refresh rate for your monitor: http://windows.microsoft.com/en-in/windows-8/get-best-display-monitor

Follow these steps:

  1. Swipe in from the right edge of the screen, tap “Settings”, and then tap “Change PC settings”.
    (If you're using a mouse, point to the lower-right corner of the screen, move the mouse pointer up, click Settings, and then click Change PC settings.)
  2. Tap or click “PC & devices”, then tap or click “Display”.
  3. Click “Advanced Settings”.
  4. Click the “Monitor tab”, and then select a new refresh rate. The monitor will take a moment to adjust. If you want to keep the changes, click “Apply”.

Note: If you don't apply the changes within fifteen seconds, the refresh rate will revert back to your original setting.

Further, I would like to let you know that drivers are updated occasionally. Windows can update them automatically, or you can install the updated drivers yourself. It's important for you to have confidence in the updated drivers you install. Windows notifies you if it detects a suspicious or unsafe driver you shouldn't install.

Download and install drivers

On the other hand, if the issue is a recent one and you have not made significant software or hardware changes to the computer, I would suggest you to restore your system to a point in time before you made any changes in the display settings.

To restore your PC to an earlier point in time

  1. Swipe in from the right edge of the screen, and then tap Search.
    (If you're using a mouse, point to the upper-right corner of the screen, move the mouse pointer down, and then click Search.)

  2. Enter Control Panel in the search box, and tap or click Control Panel.

  3. Enter Recovery in the Control Panel search box, and then tap or click Recovery.

  4. Tap or click Open System Restore, and then follow the instructions.

Disclaimer: When you use System Restore to restore the computer to a previous state, programs and updates that you installed are removed.
