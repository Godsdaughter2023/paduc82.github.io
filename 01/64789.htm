Look, error codes (like 0x800f0922) don't usually pinpoint a specific problem. That's true for two reasons:

1- error conditions can be caused by many different things that often can't be determined without further analysis; and

2- error codes were not originally written for you and me. They were written for technical professionals, to give them a jumping off point for working on a problem.

Microsoft never anticipated that users would fix their own computers, and they still don't. Even though there is a great amount of information about these codes on the internet, Microsoft didn't put it there, or countenance others to put it there, so that we the people can fix our own computers. However intelligent and well-intentioned, most people simply don't have the technical background. (Hand raised.)

After enough people experience the same problem and write about it on the internet, certain commonalities emerge that make it possible to suggest this fix or that fix. But specific suggestions don't work for everyone, as you (and I) have discovered. Unless you have specific knowledge of what caused the error code in your particular situation, you're basically taking a shot in the dark.

That leaves us with a tedious and frustrating process of trying to understand what went wrong and then working through suggested fixes one by one, which is what you see here. Hopefully, luck is on your side and a suggested fix actually works. Barring that you'll be advised to reinstall or clean install Windows 10.

What I do, and what has frequently worked for me, is to do as much research as I can on an error code (avoiding websites that exist mostly to sell software) and then work through the most-likely-to-succeed ideas, one by one, until I hit on the one that works for me.

In your case, if you know that your computer's hardware is supported on Windows 10, then a clean install will always return a properly working computer. But a clean install is a lot of work, and it often does away with customizations that worked in the past because you're dealing with all new code.

The best way to endure all this is to be discerning, so that you're not following advice from someone who really doesn't know what they're talking about and is only copying-and-pasting generic troubleshooting that may have nothing to do with your problem.
