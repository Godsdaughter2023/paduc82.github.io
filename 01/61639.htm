willyburz say:

Bitstream setting-Why forced to choose DTS or Dolby?!

Why when you pick bitstream does it force you to choose a format.  Normally bitstream settings automatically throw whats being fed.  IE a blu ray is DTS MA your receiver will show whats being fed and decoded.  Or a game is in dolby, itll auto switch under any bitstream setting.  Bitstream is bitstream thrown to a receiver and your receiver decodes it, and shows what its decoding automatically!

gza say:

I believe that on the Xbox One the native game audio is NOT encoded and that because of the One's design (seamless switching, Snap, etc.), the Xbox is decoding all encoded audio (from TV, BluRay, etc.), mixing in the other audio from Snap etc., (re)encoding all of it using the format you select, then outputting.  It is not just passing through the source audio and cannot do so without compromising the Snap feature etc. Because of this need to (re)encode, it gives you a choice of formats for the (re)encode.

gza say:

Absolutey stick with 5.1/7.1 uncompressed over HDMI if you can.  It is not a simple firmware fix because, for Snap etc. to work as designed, the One must decode the Dolby/DTS audio to mix in the other sound sources. If your receiver has HDMI and supports uncompressed 5.1/7.1, why would you want it to reencode that uncompressed mix back to Dolby/DTS only to have the receiver to decode it again?  This extra step certainly could cause sync issues because each encoding or decoding takes time and processing power.

It seems to me that many folks here believe that Dolby and DTS are synonymous with surround/multichannel sound and that, if they don't see those lights or words on their receiver, they are not getting multichannel sound. This is simply not (necessarily) the case.  Dolby and DTS in their various forms are just codecs for compressing multichannel audio, similar to how MP3/FLAC/etc. are codecs for compressing stereo audio from CDs etc.

A backup tool will back up user data. It will not back up system files. If you had to replace your hard disk then you would be able to restore your data files but you would not be able to restore Windows.

An image creation tool will take a snapshot of the whole partition. If you had to replace your hard disk then you could restore this snapshot complete with boot files, system files and data files.

You will find the image creation tool in the Control Panel / Backup and Restore, top left-hand corner.

Before you pass final judgement you might want to examine the image file. It has a .VHD extension. Here is how it works:

   1. Use Windows Explorer to locate the .vhd (virtual hard disk) file on your external disk. I will look like this: E:\WindowsImageBackup\PC1\Backup 2010-12-12 175726\0417acc3-2173-11df-9f99-806e6f6e6963.vhd
