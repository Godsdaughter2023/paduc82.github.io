a. user say:

I don't need all of the folders that file explorer came installed with.  I want to get rid of them.

This approach is a common mistake made by people who are starting to delve into the inner workings of Windows. 

If you see something that you don't think you'll use, leave it alone. It's not causing you any harm. If you throw out a folder sitting on your desk at home, the rest of your desk is unharmed. The objects you see in File Explorer are coded into the operating system, and if you tinker with them, the operating system can be harmed.

The harm you may be doing may not be immediately apparent to you. But next week, next month, or next year, your computer may start to behave strangely and then you'll complain in this forum that an update from Microsoft 'destroyed' your computer when the truth is that your computer had it coming.

Here's another thing: Maybe you don't need this or that object, but a software application you may install next week, next month, or next year may be looking for the very object you deleted or re-arranged and, not being able to find it, the application will crash. And you'll be back here to complain about how incompetent is Microsoft, they need to fire everyone, how can I sue them, etc.

You sound like a reasonable person. Don't be like too many of your fellow posters who decide to take matters in their own hands and, not understanding how the technology works, proceed to make a mess of their computers.

Nobody says you have to like everything about Windows 10 - well, Microsoft's Marketing Department thinks so, but what do you expect? - however, breaking the operating system is not a solution.

To paraphrase Beyonce, if you don't like what you see, don't put a ring on it. But if you want to go ahead anyway, do plenty of research on the internet first to be sure you're doing the right thing the right way. And make an image backup so you can easily put Humpty Dumpty together again.
