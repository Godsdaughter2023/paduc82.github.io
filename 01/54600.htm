demenace say:

first thing to try is a complete power re-cycle of your network.

Power off your modem, your router (if you have one), your PCs, laptops, xboxes.  Power off everything on your network.

Note:  some modems have a battery back up and can't be powered off.  Use the "Reset" button on the modem in this case

Let the whole system set unpowered for 30 minutes or more.

Then power them back on in this order.

1. Power on the modem (Wait a few minutes for the lights on the modem to settle down)

2. power on the router (Wait a few minutes here too)

3.  Power on your PC (Check to see that you have internet connectivity)

4. Power on your xbox  (Hopefully all works well at this point)

5. After your xbox is working properly and with your xbox still powered on, turn on the other devices on your network.

lemp say:

If I understand you correctly, you're not referring to the data transfer speed (upload/download) when you're connected using wifi, but that your overall system performance suffers.

Choppy audio and video could be the result of an overloaded system:  either the CPU maxing out or the RAM getting used up and thus having to do a lot of paging.

In a brief search, I found only one similar report:  http://www.majorgeeks.com/news/story/is_your_wifi_slowing_down_your_pc.html

Frankly, I'm not sure that I believe the solution described in the main part of the post.  I can't see why an allegedly "slow DHCP from the router" would cause this problem.  Moreover, your observation that it happens on different wireless networks tends to negate the idea that the problem is with the router. 

It's not difficult to configure a static IP address, so it's worth trying.  I don't have a high expectation that it will help.  The directions in the post about how to configure a static IP address are a little thin, so if you need help, just ask. 

The mention of disabling QoS in the second comment to the post linked above has some superficial appeal, but again, I doubt this is the solution.  If you want to disable QoS, you can do that from the "Properties" of the wireless adapter.

Given that the problem apparently started after you had upgraded to Win 10 and then reverted to Win 7, I suspect that you have a bad driver, which has remained installed despite your efforts to reinstall it.

I would approach this problem from two directions. 

A.  First, when connected via wifi and experiencing the problem, open Task Manager and select the "Processes" tab.  Click the button to "Show processes from all users." 

What process(es) appear to be using the most CPU cycles?

You can also select the "Performance" tab and click the "Resource Monitor" to get a better look at things.

There are downloadable tools such as Process Monitor and Process Explorer that are even more powerful (and correspondingly a bit more difficult to use), but start with the utilities built into Windows 7 and see if you can identify anything that is bogging the system down, either taking a lot of CPU cycles or creating a lot of disk activity.

If you can identify a culprit, that will go a long way to determining how to solve the problem.

B.  The second approach would be to assume that the problem is caused by a bad wifi driver. 

Out of an abundance of caution, download the driver package for your LAN adapter in addition to the driver package for the wireless adapter that I assume you have already downloaded.

Go to Control Panel > Programs and Features and uninstalling anything that mentions wireless networking.

Open Device Manager, double-click on the entry for your Atheros Wireless Network adapter, and select the Driver tab.

Click "Uninstall" and on the next screen, check the box to "Delete the driver software for this device" and click OK.

If you have never done this step before, this may be sufficient. Install the wireless network adapter driver that you downloaded from Acer and reboot the computer.  If all goes well, the adapter will be detected on reboot and the correct driver will be installed.

If you've already tried deleting the driver software this way and it hasn't helped, post back.  There are some other things to try to make sure that the old driver is exorcised, but it's complicated by the fact that your laptop has an Atheros LAN adapter and an Atheros Bluetooth adapter in addition to the Atheros wireless adapter.  Without seeing the actual files on your computer, I'm not sure whether what I have in mind might require you to reinstall drivers for all 3 devices.  That's why I suggested above to download the LAN driver -- you don't want to be in a situation where you don't have any drivers and can't connect to the Internet to get them.
