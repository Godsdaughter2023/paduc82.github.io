danielh19 say:

It's actually better to calibrate a TV using a Blu-ray player with a HD Calibration Blu-ray disc. In my experience calibrating from a PC can be tricky because you have to verify what video range your PC is outputting and what your TV is set to accept and work within. Blu-ray simplifies this as everything is in the video range (16-235).

sandm1tch say:

It would depend on what your TV is capable of. If you do not have a black level setting on your TV and are forced to use Standard reference levels, then I would calibrate with a disc too. But if you have a black level setting on your HDTV (High/light for 0-255, Low/Dark for 16-235) and set your Xbox 360 to Expanded reference levels, Then a blu-ray would not be able to provide this expanded range to calibrate for like a PC does by default. PC's default to 0-255 levels because monitors are a common display for PC's that expect 0-255 levels by default. HDTV's do not and if you use an HDMI connection from your PC to your HDTV, then the Graphics card may default to 16-235 levels unless you change it in your control panel.

-

All HDTV's have a way to EXPECT 0-255 content anyways, but they need to either be fed a PC signal (1280x720 as opposed to 720p) or have a black level setting to force it. If you are not already doing this for the Xbox 360 and an HDMI or VGA connection, then you are causing the HDTV to process the the darkest and lightest shades and that can cause input lag and sometimes uneven color balance (the color green becomes oversaturated on my TV in that case).
