A Microsoft Account is entirely optional and you don't have to use, but there are some benefits, especially if you use multiple machines running Windows 10.

Local Account – this account works like credentials used to log onto Windows 7 and earlier versions of Windows. A local account doesn’t include the modern benefits of Windows 10, such as PC Syncing, the ability to download apps from the Windows Store and two-factor authentication. A local account also requires setting up a hint in case you forget your password.

Microsoft Account – this type of account uses a Microsoft email address linked with your Microsoft account. Normally, this email address would use the hotmail.com, live.com, msn.com or outlook.com domains. You can set up Windows 10 with a yahoo.com, gmail.com or icloud.com address, just make sure that email address is associated with a Microsoft account.

So, you could create a alias account and connect it to your Microsoft account (yahoo.com, gmail.com or icloud.com address).

A Microsoft Account includes the benefits of keeping files and settings, such as themes, passwords, and apps synced across devices running Windows 10. A Microsoft Account also includes support for two-factor authentication, which prevents hackers from easily accessing your computer. Users can also access services such as Find My Device in case your device gets lost or stolen. The Microsoft Account is also required to download Universal apps from the Windows Store. Reactivation is also easier in case you reinstall Windows 10 or need to transfer your license to another computer.

lemp say:

I can not explain why the product key shown on the sticker attached to your computer has only 24 characters

However, even if you had a proper 25-character product key, it would not work on the Microsoft Software Recovery site (https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/software-download/windows7).  A product key for a pre-installed edition of Windows is not supported by that site (see FAQ 2 here: https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/software-download/faq).

Furthermore, keyfinder utilities, such as that suggested by TomJeson64 will not reveal a usable product key from a pre-installed version of Windows.  These utilities will only find the generic installation key used by the manufacturer for the initial installation.  That key is not valid for a reinstallation.  That's what the key on the COA sticker is to be used for.  Actually, in your case, such a utility would not even find that key, given that you have "installed a different OS."

You could try asking HP for replacement media, but given that Windows 7 is no longer sold by Microsoft, it may be difficult to pry a copy out of HP.

Although you may have some options for obtaining a Windows 7 ISO file (see this wiki:  https://answers.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/wiki/windows_7-update/how-to-what-are-my-options-for-obtaining-windows-7/528163c1-0b2e-4ad2-a26d-7112851d1dc2), you're not going to get very far without a proper 25-character license key.

I would like to inform that when you perform reset/ refresh on your computer by selecting keep my files, it works as below:

  • Reinstalls Windows 10 and keeps your personal files.

  • Removes apps and drivers you installed.

  • Removes changes you made to settings.

  • Removes any apps your PC manufacturer installed. (If your PC came with Windows 10, apps from your PC manufacturer will be reinstalled.)

Hence you will have to re-install all the applications that you have previously installed.

a_user_here say:

The problem with looking at the Event Viewer these days is separating the real errors from the ones that Microsoft has been logging in there for years, that have they have never seen fit to clean up.

Most people don't go and look in the Event log until they have a problem, and as such when they look in there and see errors they assume that they are all connected to the problems they are having.  They may or may not be.

On my machines which is a clean install and is working fine, I currently have 3 errors in the last hour and 43 in the last 24 hours.

For the ones in the last hour:

Service Control Manager, Sync Host_Session2 service terminated, one time, and will take corrective action in one second (restart service)

MicrosoftEdge was terminated because it took too long to suspend.

MicrosoftEdge stopped interacting with Windows and was closed.

For the 24 hours period 21 entries are for DeviceSetupManager (Metadata staging fail).  I had the same thing in Windows 8 and trying to track down the cause/fix was a useless endeavor.  And then there are 10 errors for: "Activation of app Microsoft.XboxApp_8wekyb3d8bbwe!Microsoft.XboxApp failed with error: This app does not support the contract specified or is not installed."  Which to me is clearly a bug in the program.  This has been there since day one on Windows 10.  Again other than setting something to ignore it there is nothing I can do about it.

The point of this is to say just pointing to errors in the Event log, is pointless unless you really can pin them down with your other problems.

The better approach is try to isolate a problem you are having, and then maybe going to the log to try to get more information on it by looking at log entries around the time you had the problem.
