Unfortunately Saphris, unless your using a TV that is a fixed-pixel display like a flat-panel widescreen LCD, LED LCD, or plasma HDTV, you will always get what's called OVERSCAN.

\

What Overscan is, is that its an aspect ratio (picture size) term that applies to how broadcasting systems work (like you're Cable set-top box, or Antenna TV service).

\

It occurs when the TV isn't receiving a defined and detailed resolution (like 480i, 480p, 720p, 1080i, and 1080p). It was mainly used as a way to prevent black borders from showing up from the channel provider's camera to your TV at home.

\

Since displays today are mostly made as digital flat-panel TV's as Rear-projection widescreen TV's and CRT tube TV's have all depreciated, the need for this "Overscan" is unnecessary anymore, though it is still held for situations where you don't have access to High Definition content.

You're TV is not a digital "Smart" TV. its not "Smart" in the way that it doesn't have any method of knowing how to shape the incoming picture to you're TV. CRT's are analog and do not have pixel. they instead have "lines" that are drawn to by one or more color guns that have individual light pointing to each line.

\

A digital display has what's called an EDID chip and in that EDID chip, information pertaining to what specific resolutions and refresh rates (like 50 hertz/hz, 60 hertz/hz) the widescreen HDTV supports. That's why widescreen TV's have an aspect ratio known as "just scan" , 1:1 Pixel ratio, or Full mode. These will show the ENTIRE picture, Pixel for Pixel because it knows what its receiving through you're cables.

\

HDMI (High Definition Multimedia Interface) cables will be the best at getting to show the full picture because its a PURE digital cable for your Digital Display. It is a "smart" cable because it was derived from the cable we use for computer monitors. DVI (Digital Video Interface). It has the ability to be sent the smartest most specific resolution and refresh rate through the cable from your HDMI capable device such as the XBOX 360.

\

In your case, analog cables are ones like component (Red Green Blue cables), Composite (Which is your Yellow cable, the lowest quality of all analog cables), S-video (a step up from Composite, but worse than component), and VGA (the connector you use on older  big fat computer CRT monitors).

\

I know this was A LOT to read, but for future viewers, this is the reason for overscan. So for you Saphris, you will NOT be able to get the full picture unless you use an HDTV and an HD cable.

a. user say:

The passcode for your WiFi network is set in your router.

Sign in to your router's administration console:

1- Open any web browser and type the IP address for your router, then press Enter. The IP address for your router is probably 192.168.1.1 or 192.168.0.1 but not necessarily. You can lookup the IP address for your router on the internet.

2- Enter the username and password for the administration console.

Once you're in the administration console, look through the menus until you find the menu where you change your WiFi passcode. After you change your password you'll have to click on the Apply button. (Your router may have a different word.) That will apply the change and restart the router.

Now that your WiFi passcode has been changed, you're going to need to enter the new passcode to every device that connects to your WiFi network.

flixbuster say:

Update 10 Nov 2016: the below solution works OK for Windows 10 Anniversary Update clean install (from official Microsoft ISO file).

The solution I've developed for myself in order to get Dell Latitude D620 laptop's video card NVIDIA Quadro NVS 110M fully working on Win 10 32 bit:
- get official Dell video driver for Win Vista 32 bit (Dell Latitude D620, NVIDIA Quadro NVS 110M) - correct file is named [R180799.exe];
- get official NVIDIA video driver for Win 7 32 bit (NVIDIA Quadro NVS 110M) - correct file is named [179.48_notebook_winvista_win7_32bit_beta.exe];
- unpack (use 7-Zip) Dell video driver (you should get folder [R180799] with all files inside, no other sub-folders);
- unpack (use 7-Zip) NVIDIA video driver (you should get folder [179.48_notebook_winvista_win7_32bit_beta] with some files and 2 sub-folders inside);
- from folder [R180799] copy 2 files ([nvdm.cat] and [nvdm.inf]) somewhere else;
- copy/cut all files from sub-folder [179.48_notebook_winvista_win7_32bit_beta\Display] and paste them into folder [R180799] - overwrite everything;
- copy/cut the 2 previously copied files ([nvdm.cat] and [nvdm.inf]) from where they are now and paste them into folder [R180799] - overwrite everything.

Congrats! You've now got your own mix-and-match-home-baked driver for NVIDIA Quadro NVS 110M on Dell Latitude D620.
Now go to Device Manager and in [Display adapters] section select the existing entry, right click, click on [Update Driver Software].
Select [Browse my computer for driver software] and next select the full path where folder [R180799] is.
Click Next and let Windows do its thing. If you get some red-and-scary dialog, don't worry - just tell Windows to continue.
That's all - you don't even need to restart.

Note: Most probably (I haven't tested), the same procedure will work for Win 10 64 bit if the right 64 bit driver files are used (instead of the 32 bit driver files):
- official Dell video driver for Win Vista 64 bit - correct file is named [R142931.EXE];
- official NVIDIA video driver for Win 7 64 bit - correct file is named [179.48_notebook_winvista_win7_64bit_beta.exe].
All folders names from the procedure above will change accordingly, of course.

On Wed, 25 Dec 2013 11:35:48 +0000, Samidha Shah wrote:

what is third party software ?how does it work? and how to use it . please tell me about it .

Third-party software is simply software written for Windows, but
written and sold (or given away for free) by other companies, not by
Microsoft.

How it works and how you use it is no different from software written
by Microsoft.

So, for example, you can buy Microsoft Office, and install the
programs that are part of it (Word, Excel, PowerPoint, Outlook, etc.)
from the disks that you get, and then run those programs like any
programs (Notepad, Wordpad, Internet Explorer, etc.) that came with
Windows.

Alternatively, you could buy WordPerfect Office, which is written and
sold by Corel (a third-party company), install it, and use its
component programs instead.
You could even install both Microsoft Office and WordPerfect Office
and use both--
