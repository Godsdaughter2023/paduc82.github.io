davem121 say:

All Windows 10 systems take longer to restart than to start.

The reason for this is that when you shut down your PC, the state of Windows is written to your hard drive. Then when you start your system that data is read from the hard drive enabling fast startup. This uses a mixture of technologies including the old Hibernation Mode in older versions of Windows

When you choose to restart your system, this does not happen, Windows is in fact shut down without its state being written to the hard drive. Upon starting from a restart all Windows processes have to be started and moved to RAM.

Therefore it takes longer to start from a Restart than a full shut down

With all that being said, your system does seem to take a long time to start from both a shut down and a re-start

Follow the advice given here and remove as many applications from your startup as possible to reduce the startup time on your system

Check the Startup Tab on Task Manager as explained by suat.cini

Also Press Windows + R keys together and type msconfig

  1. In the resulting dialog, click in the services tab
  2. Check the box marked 'Hide all Microsoft Services
  3. Uncheck anything that you do not need like software updaters and other services
  4. Do not uncheck anything to do with your Anti-virus (if you use one)
  5. Click 'Apply' and reboot

lemp say:

Best:  Subscribe to one of the several cloud storage backup programs.

You could also use a "Network Attached Storage" (NAS) device, which is essentially a fancier external drive.

Less desirable:

  1. Optical disks -- unless you only have a very small amount of data to back, creating a set of optical disks (DVDs/BluRay) is not practical.  There are conflicting reports concerning the longevity of home-burned optical disks.  If you're intending to keep a backup for a significant length of time (typically, not necessary), get the highest quality disks you can find and store them carefully.
  2. USB flash drives -- generally not considered to be a safe method for backup.  See, e.g., https://www.howtogeek.com/218390/can-a-usb-flash-drive-be-used-reliably-as-a-manual-backup-drive/
  3. A secondary hard drive installed in a drive bay in your computer (assuming that you have a desktop/tower with an available drive bay).  Better than nothing (and probably better than a USB flash drive), but if your computer suffers a catastrophic failure (or gets stolen), your backup may go along with your working drive.  See, e.g., https://www.pcworld.com/article/2029824/why-you-shouldnt-backup-to-an-internal-hard-drive.html

Good summary here:  https://www.drivesaversdatarecovery.com/2018/03/30/best-backup-solutions/
