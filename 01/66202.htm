gary vandermolen say:
Regarding the first (sending) error message, you are running into a port 25 block. Whoever was providing your Internet connection at the time does not permit connections on port 25 to other servers. As a general rule, the owner of the port 25 SMTP server you are using must be the same as the entity from which your Internet connection is coming at that moment. For more on this anti-spamming issue see
 
http://www.postcastserver.com/help/Port_25_Blocking.aspx
 
The available workarounds when sending away from home are as follows:
 
1. Use webmail for sending (via your browser).
2. Ask your home ISP if they have a port other than '25' for SMTP.
3. If you use the same away-from-home connection frequently,
substitute the SMTP server belonging to that connection. This is a bit
tricky, but if you want to go that route, I can provide more details.
4. If you frequently send from multiple locations, get a free Gmail account,
configure it for POP access, set it up in Windows Mail, and use that for
your sending chores. Gmail uses port 465 for SMTP, which is not blocked
like port 25 is.
 
Regarding your second (receive) error, are you sure that mail server supports port 995? Try it with port 110 and no SSL.
 
Gary VanderMolen, Microsoft MVP (Mail)

trouble with the OS(c:) hard disk drive__

I am trying to transfer all my documents, pictures, and videos from an external hard drive to a new computer.  But everytime I do it saves it to the OS (c:) drive and I run out of room.  How do I get it to save in the other drive??

 

ken blake say:

On Wed, 7 Jul 2010 19:52:48 +0000, Csanfo2 wrote:

I am trying to transfer all my documents, pictures, and videos from an external hard drive to a new computer.  But everytime I do it saves it to the OS (c:) drive and I run out of room.  How do I get it to save in the other drive??

Three points:

1. Exactly how are you doing it?
The fact that it's an external drive is irrelevant, and the fact that
they are documents, pictures, and videos is irrelevant. You simply
want to copy stuff from one place to another.

There are several way to do this, but here's one. Open the external
drive in Windows Explorer. Then open Windows Explorer a second time
and open the other drive (whatever folder in the other drive you
want). Then simply drag the files from the external drive to the other
one.

2. However (and it's a big however), what is this "other" drive?
Did it come with the computer? How big is it? It is probably just a
second partition on your only physical drive, rather than being a
second "drive." And if it's what many computer vendors do these days,
it's probably a small recovery partition put there in place of giving
you a DVD to reinstall Windows from, if you need to.
If that's what it is, be sure not to put any documents, pictures,
and videos there. In fact, don't try to put anything there. You can
easily screw up your ability to recover Windows, should you ever need
to.

3. One other issue: how many GB do these documents, pictures, and
videos on the external drive take up? How big is your C: drive? The
fact that they won't fit on the C: drive of a new computer is very
suspicious.

Ken Blake
