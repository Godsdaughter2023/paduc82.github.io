kevin j baird say: Ok, I did a bunch of research on this issue.  The problem lies with Blizzard, for the most part.

World of Warcraft is a 32bit game.  Because it is strictly 32bit, it only has access to 2gig of memory.  Windows controls the other 2gig of addressable space. 

Most game developers flag their games before compile time to use larger amounts of memory.  Blizzard doesn't do this, because their anti-cheat system, and third party tools rely on the 32bit addressing, and 64bit addressing would mean they would all have to be rewritten.

What is happening in the game is that your large memory footprint of your graphics card is mapped into the game.  Typically this is when your settings are high, because it uses the most memory, which would explain why the game is playable when you use minimal settings.  Because your particular graphic card setup uses nearly 2 gig of RAM itself, you quickly force the game to run out of memory space that is addressable.

To fix this, you may be able to play at reduced settings.  Lowering your resolution is typically the best way to restrict memory usage, since it exponentially increases RAM use when you increase resolution.  You will likely have to play around with various settings.  You can open up Task Manager and monitor your WoW game's usage of memory to see where the sweet spot is on keeping your memory under control.

Blizzard will have to make the hard choice of eventually enabling extended memory access.  As more and more people buy advanced hardware such as yours, this problem will become more and more common.

To do at least some of the following procedure you will need to have Administrator privileges.  Also, I don't know how the locations of some of the items I recommend that you "select" or "click on" may vary depending on how you have your visuals set up.  You should probably do two or three complete "dry runs" through the procedure first before actually changing anything just to make sure you can find everything.  And, all I can say is that this worked on my computer, I can't guarantee it will work on any other computer.

Apparently, the "Upgrade to W10" update file was deleted from my computer by the rollback process from W10 to W7, but it still sits in the list of Window update files at Microsoft, ready and more than willing to down-load into your machine, so you need to be careful "not to encourage it".  

1. Open the Control Panel, then open "Windows Update".  If the "Upgrade to Windows 10" blurb is still showing in this page, for heavens sake STAY AWAY FROM THE "Get Started" button.

2. Select "Change Settings".

This next step is, I think (and Canadian Tech apparently agrees), necessary to keep from being "reinfected" by W10 stuff.  But rather than turning WU fully off, I did the following:

3. Under "Important Updates, select "Check for updates but let me choose whether to download and install them."

4. Go back to the "Windows Update" page and in the lower left corner, click on "Installed Updates".  It may take some seconds for the list to show up.

5. In the list of updates scroll down under the MS Windows section to "Update for MS Windows (KB3035583)".  Right click on it and left click on "Uninstall".  (Update KB2952664 will be in the list also, but it reappears every time I've uninstalled it and it seems not to have any effect on the system, so don't bother with it.)

6. Go back to the "Windows Update" page and if the "Update for Windows 10" blurb is showing, click on "Show all available updates" outside the blurb at its lower left.  If the blurb is not there, then inside the regular update box, click on "## -------- updates are available".

7. In the resulting section that lists the available updates for Windows 7 should be a preselected line "Upgrade to Windows 10".  Unselect that line by clicking on the checkmark in the square at the beginning of the line.  Then right click on the line and in the resulting popup box, left click on "Hide Update".  There should also be a preselected line for KB3035583 in the list of updates.  Do the same unselection and "Hide Update" steps on this line as described previously.

8. Go back to the "Windows Update" page.  Note that the "Update for Windows 10" blurb, if there previously, should now be replaced by the regular Windows Update box.  Click on the "## ------- updates are available" line.

9. Both of the "hidden" updates should be absent from the listing of available updates.

10. Close the Control Panel.

This procedure worked on my computer, BUT, TO KEEP MY MICROSOFT OPERATING SYSTEM AND SOFTWARE UP TO DATE, "SECURE", AND "UNCONTAMINATED" BY POTENTIAL WINDOWS 10 STUFF, I'M GOING TO HAVE TO THOROUGHLY INSPECT EVERY NEW MS WINDOWS OPERATING SYSTEM UPDATE FILE BEFORE ALLOWING IT TO DOWNLOAD.  This proviso doesn't apply to non-operating system updates or security updates (I hope).  I hope that this will not be necessary indefinitely, perhaps no more than the "free" year.

As Canadian Tech strongly implies, it is very important to keep your system up to date, so if you do follow the above procedure, DO NOT ignore the updates.

Hi,

Win 8, 8.1 and 10 have a new feature called Fast Startup. It can sometimes cause problems like you mention as well as others.

The easiest way to tell if this is the problem is to turn it off and see what happens.

This feature only works when you do a SHUTDOWN and then Boot.

It doesn't effect a RESTART.

To turn it off or on...

Go to Control panel...Power Options, and select Choose What the Power Buttons Do on the left.

Then select Change Settings That Are Currently Unavailable near the top center of screen...

Lower down on the window, uncheck Fast Startup.

This may or may not help your problem, but is easy to check.

The actual cause of your problem is probably a driver that is not working properly with Fast Startup... The difficult part is determining which one..
