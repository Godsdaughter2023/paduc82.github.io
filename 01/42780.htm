30 GB for a system drive for Windows 7 is very small.  Microsoft's minimum requirements (which are always unrealistically low) are 16 GB available hard disk space (32-bit) or 20 GB (64-bit).  Unless you have no applications and no data (or have taken steps to move these directories elsewhere), even 60 GB will get cramped quickly.

All of those files look fairly normal, even a bit smaller than normal.  If you have a desktop rather than a laptop and do not want to use hibernation, you can turn that feature off.  This will remove the hiberfil.sys file.   https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/kb/920730

Although you say that you haven't downloaded Windows 10, my research indicates that the $windows.~ws file is generated by using the media creation tool for making a Windows 10 ISO file.  Perhaps it's also related to something similar from a previous upgrade.

canadian tech say:

What I find unfathomable is why you would worry over 10, 20, 30 or even 100 GB, when you have 500 to work with.

I never recommend a drive in a Win7 system be partitioned.  Use all the space there is in it for C:, of course leaving aside the system required partition and a factory restore partition.

The idea of logical partitioning of drives was born in a day when a 20 G drive was huge and Windows of the day could not even address a "big" drive.

With NTFS and the drives of today, that kind of thinking is really only ever going to cause you problems.

For the kind of user you are, a typical 500 G drive has maybe 100 to 140G in use and the rest is for expansion that is unlikely to ever happen.

Windows files/folders do grow.  So what in a drive of that size.  You are simply wasting energy worrying about that.  If you start to get out to 400 of that 500 in use, THEN you really have to start investigating.

Meantime, you are much more likely to mess up your system trying to optimize something that does not need optimization.
