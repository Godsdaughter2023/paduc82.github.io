considerate_guy say:

You can't natively rotate video inside of Windows Explorer or Photo Gallery.

About all you can do is to create a new movie using the vertical video as source, but rotate it within there. So open up Movie Maker, add the rotated video, click the Rotate left or Rotate right buttons, then save that as a new movie.

A few caveats:

  1. This will put the video into a horizontal rectangle with black bars on the sides and you'll also lose some resolution in the video.
  2. The original video is unaffected; all you are doing is creating a new movie with the same content, but rotated.

You might want to look into video conversion software that can do a better job of this. In particular, Freemake Video Converter (for example), can take the vertical video and correctly turn it so it can be viewed vertically rather than on its side; it won't add black bars. But if you do install Freemake, you must choose "Custom install" and uncheck anything it wants to load as an add-on.

A better solution going forward is to never film vertically. Enjoy the following humorous take on "VVS":

PSA - Vertical Video Syndrome

    What is the reason for a huge Master File Table? - Microsoft Community

    okieseeker say:

    What is the reason for a huge Master File Table?

    Recently I added a 2TB external USB drive for big files I rarely access. I'm using the Auslogics utility for defragmenting, and noticed something odd. A large portion (30%) of the written data this new drive is occupied by the "Master File Table." The percentage of space used by the MFT on my other drives is much smaller (10% or less). Can anybody help me understand this? Anything that will reduce the table size?

    gerry c j cornell say:

    The MFT contains the locations of files. The larger the number of files the larger the MFT file. However, when you delete files the space should does not reduce.
    http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NTFS#Metafiles

    Is the external drive formatted as NTFS or FAT32?

    lemp say:
    See http://support.microsoft.com/kb/174619 

    By default, NTFS reserves 12.5% of the volume space for the MFT.  It's not clear whether you're seeing the "MFT Zone" or actual space used by the MFT, but in either case, it shouldn't be taking up 30% of your volume unless you have a huge number of small files.

    Also note that " When files are deleted from an NTFS volume, their MFT entries are marked as free and may be reused, but the MFT does not shrink. Thus, space used by these entries is not reclaimed from the disk."

    As described in the above KB article, the size of the pre-allocated space can be changed by editing the registry.  The options are

    1 12.5% of free space
    2 25% of free space
    3 37.5% of free space
    4 50% of free space

    and those options are used in KEY_LOCAL_MACHINE\System\CurrentControlSet\Control\FileSystem as the "data" for Value Name: NtfsMftZoneReservation


    I suppose its possible, but not very likely, that your registry has changed.

    For best results, you probably should make any such changes to the size allocated for the MFT zone before the volume is formatted, because otherwise the MFT zone may be fragmented and the built-in defragmenter can't defrag the MFT. 

    Some third-party defragmenters can defrag the MFT (e.g., Diskeeper).  According to the Auslogics site, "Currently we are working on the MFT defragmentation support, so that your MFT can also be defragmented by the program. This feature will be available in one of the future major versions of the program."  I have no idea whether defragmenting an extra-large MFT that included a lot of unused file entries would shrink the overall size.


    okieseeker say: It's formatted as NTFS

    markhaus say: Consider using MFT de-fragger Contig v1.7 from SysInternals, to optimize this file. Find out current size using NTFSinfo from SysInternals as well.
