Step 1:    Make sure your Surface is turned off and plugged in (hold down the power
button for 30 seconds).
Step 2:     Insert the USB recovery drive into the USB port on your Surface.
Step 3:    Press and hold the volume-down button while you press and release the

Step 4:     When the Surface logo appears, release the volume button.
Step 5:    When prompted, select the language and keyboard layout you want.

Surface restarts and the Surface logo displays while the reset process continues

canadian tech say:

Windows Easy Transfer creates a single very large file that is used to transfer all of your DATA to a new or rebuilt computer.  You can not access anything within it.  WET also includes all your settings -- there are myriads of them.  Obviously, the WET file must be stored on a different drive.

A proper backup provides you with duplicates of your DATA which can be accessed individually or in mass and then put back.

I have done this many times.  I recommend you do both.  On the distant chance that WET does not work or move everything, you have the backup as a backup.  WEP almost always does an amazing job.

There is no way to backup or copy your programs.  They must be re-installed on the re-built hard drive.  Same goes for drivers, although Win7 is really great at providing its own drivers for you.  Then, after Win7 is up and running and you start the lengthy Windows Update process, including other stuff from Microsoft, WU will then provide updates to those drivers.  Drivers that do not get loaded this way will likely be available on the Dell Support site.

The most important driver is really the Network one.  I would make certain you download that Win7 driver before you begin.  If the Win7 install does not provide that driver, you can get into some messy situations.

Remember, when doing the clean install to delete your current partition.

Now I am going to give you a suggestion that most will not tell you about:  After you have loaded Win7, up to date drivers are in place, your Office software and any other stable software, and every Windows Update you can get; create a system image of your hard drive on DVD +R's (not -Rs).  It will take about 4 of them and about an hour.  Also create a System Repair disk on a CD.  When you have done this, you can re-create your drive in less than an hour, should your hard drive fail and need replacement or your system becomes hopelessly corrupted.  Very nice and inexpensive insurance policy.

canadian tech say:

System image is a great tool too.  But, again, remember that the image is a way of totally replacing everything and I mean everything currently on the drive with the image.  If data was included in the image, it too will be there.  This is not a good backup methodology.  It is great if you have to fix a totally corrupted hard drive or even replace the drive itself.  Keep in mind that an image is quite large and you will need an external storage system for it.  I use DVD +Rs, but only use it when the drive contains only Windows and stable programs completely updated.  That will take about 4 DVD's.  If you do this including your data, this could take 10s of DVDs and a lot of time.

The System Repair disk is used to start a system in which the hard drive is so corrupted it can not repair itself.

You can use Windows built-in backup program.  Your choice.  But, keep in mind that if you store that backup on the same drive and it fails, you lose it too.  Always store your backup no matter how created on an external device.  If you do not have a high-capacity external hard drive, you should have one.  They are not expensive and the best solution.

I suppose you can use some sort of "cloud" based backup system, but I do not use or recommend it.  Nor do I like any Backup program.  I simply copy my data using simple drag and drop from the C: drive to a folder on my external hard drive.  Almost all data is in your User folder, with the minor exception of some stubborn programs like Quick Books and others.
