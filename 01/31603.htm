maxen say:

People you add are added to your Xbox One friends' list. These are your friends. However, on their profile, you appear as a follower. They can then choose to add you to their friends' list too. Thus you become each other's friends.

However, Xbox One friendships do not yet to carry over to Xbox.com, Xbox 360, Windows Phone or Windows 8. For this you still need to add each other the old way. The fact that you cannot suggests that one of the following is true:

- Your friend's list (limit 100) is full;

- Your friend's friend list is full; or

- One of you does not have the correct permissions (parentally-imposed) to either add or receive friendship requests.

The 100 limit on friend's lists is the sum of confirmed friends and pending requests. For instance, you may only have 5 friends confirmed, but if you leave 95 incoming requests pending, that would still fill up your list and leave you unable to add anyone else.

Clock losing time - Microsoft Community

rksajp say:

Clock losing time

My clock on my Desktop slowly loses time. What could be causing this?
ken blake say:
On Wed, 2 Jun 2010 16:10:36 +0000, RKSAJP wrote:
 
> My clock on my Desktop slowly loses time. What could be causing this?
 
 
Before anyone whose clock is running slow rushes out to buy a new
battery, he should first take note of whether he is losing time while
the computer is running or while it's powered off. If it's while
powered off, the problem *is* very likely the battery. But if it's
while running, it can *not* be the battery, because the battery isn't
used while the computer is running.
 
 
How much is it losing? Over what period? If it loses very little time,
it's probably just inaccuracy in the clock. It synchronizes with a
time server periodically. You can check what server it uses at Control
Panel | Date and Time | Internet Time | Change Settings, and change
to a different server there. That may help, but chances are it won't.
You can also switch to a third-party program such as TClock.exe, and
have that synchronize more frequently.
 
 
Ken Blake, Microsoft MVP (Windows Desktop Experience) since 2003
 
Ken Blake
