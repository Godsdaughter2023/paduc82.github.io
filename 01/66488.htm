lemp say:

For must home users, laptop hardware problems are more difficult to deal with than those of desktop/tower computers.

  1. Make sure that the wall outlet that you're using is live.  Plug a lamp into the outlet and confirm that the lamp lights.
  2. Some laptop power adapters have a small LED light. 
  3. Most laptops have a small LED light that comes on when the power adapter ("brick") is connected to the laptop and plugged into the wall outlet.  From what I can tell, on your laptop, the light should be right next to the jack where the power connector plugs into the laptop.

If your power brick has a light and it doesn't come on, or if the laptop light described in 3 above doesn't come on, the most likely problem is that your power brick has died and that the internal battery in the laptop has such a low charge that it can't power the laptop.

If you think that the power adapter has died, you have 3 options:

  1. Test the power adapter using a voltmeter -- if you have a voltmeter and are comfortable doing this
  2. Assume that your diagnosis is correct and buy a replacement power adapter.  These are fairly inexpensive:
    https://www.amazon.com/Touchsmart-15-f110ca-15-f111dx-15-f113ca-15-f118ca/dp/B00SZH57M6
    https://www.amazon.com/Adapter-Charger-15-f100dx-15-f111dx-15-f125wm/dp/B01ARISDLW
  3. Take your laptop to an independent computer repair shop (not a big box store or other chain store)

Because you could have a different -- and more difficult and expensive -- problem, you might want to go with option 3.  Be aware, however, that the shop will likely charge you more than Amazon's price for a power adapter just to take a look at your laptop and diagnose the problem.

jeeped say:

This is becoming a growing problem with screensavers. The issue at stake is background programs and services that are performing just enough tasks that the computer never thinks it becomes 'inactive'. The programs may not be performing any real work, but are cycling through their individual operations enough to keep the computer 'awake'.

Try starting your computer with a 'Clean Boot', (see the Microsoft Support articles below). If this solves the problem and you absolutely want your screensaver, you'll have to discern which background programs and services you can live without being active all of the time and disable them.

How to troubleshoot a problem by performing a clean boot in Windows Vista or in Windows 7
http://support.microsoft.com/kb/929135

Perform a clean startup to determine whether background programs are interfering with your game or program
http://support.microsoft.com/kb/331796

Location and control of the startup programs can be found by clicking Start and typing msconfig then hitting <enter>. In the System Configuration dialog's Startup tab there is a list of programs that are configured to start every time Windows is started. Uncheck any that you feel you can do without. (I'll virtually guarantee there are a least a couple) If you find you have inadvertently turned something off that you need, simply retrace your steps and recheck the item, Note that unchecking programs here does not shut them down. It merely stops them from starting the next time you restart Windows.

For what it is worth, the systems I've looked at recently with this problem have had 'smart phone' synching software recently installed that is constantly looking for something to synch with regardles of whether they are connected to anything or not. Changing the startup of these programs/services to 'on demand' from 'always' resolved the issue in those cases.
