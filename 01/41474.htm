Notice how you've been ignored?

Even if you were given an answer, it would be either "evasive" or "unclear."

Microsoft has occasionally answered this question by stating "The Windows 10 Operating System that was updated for free will _always_ be free on the computer it was originally installed on."

However, that does _not_ answer your question, does it?  You specifically want to know if, at some point in the future, you will be required to pay a _fee_ for future UPDATES.

Consider this... 

1) The license for Windows 10 is written in completely different legal language than all previous versions of Windows.  Nowhere in the license is the subject of update status, or _future_ update status, mentioned.

2) The Microsoft "Office 365" subscription scheme is set up in such a way that you are paying for the _updates_, not the software.  In fact, if you do that math you'd see that when you buy "Office 365", you're really buying "x" amount of time of updating service for the program.  Therefore,  after the "free" update offer expires for Windows 10, and a "price" is set on retail installation software, who's to say (now anyway) that what you're actually "buying" is "x" amount of time for the update service,  just the same as you are when you currently buy "Office 365"???

3) Microsoft has advertised that they are aiming for "x" million of FREE computer updates to Windows 10 within one year.  Their largest shareholders were initially raving mad over this, but are now no longer causing a hoopla over it.  Why?

4) Windows Update in Windows 10 _cannot_ be turned off.  Why?  Especially that the current "cumulative update" is wreaking so much havoc with _so_ many user's computers.  We're talking _very_ serious problems with _very_ significant numbers of users.  The fact that Windows Updates are forced upon the user is causing alarm in businesses around the country, and the latest flaws with the "cumulative update" have resulting in businesses literally threatening Class Action lawsuits... _all_ over the singular issue of the inability to turn off Windows Update.  WHY?

5) The rumor mills says (and I am "suspect in agreement") that after the "free upgrade" period expires, Windows 10 will be sold either extremely cheaply, or the sale price will include one to two years of update "service."  Supposedly, the update subscription service will be called "Windows 365."  Also, anyone who subscribes to "Office 365" will be exempt from paying for Windows updates.  Supposedly, the "fee" will amount to about $5 dollars per month (cheaper per year, and cheaper per several year subscriptions). 

If you think about it, the whole idea of a "Windows 365 Subscription Service" just makes sense.  The current "reason" for the "free upgrade" is that it makes it easier for Microsoft to update just one system instead of three; (Win7, Win8 & Win10).  That stated "reason" doesn't hold water.  There will be people who will not update.  Microsoft will _still_ have to program and issue separate updates even if there are only a dozen pre-Windows 10 users.  Bluntly stated, their "reasoning" cannot save them a dime.   That leaves "x" million of _free_ upgraded computers adding up to being nothing more than a _big_ loss of revenue for Microsoft... precisely what their largest shareholders complained about when they first announced this whole "free upgrade" scheme.

Now we get to my opinion of the upgrade itself.  I am fortunate that I decided to hold back my main computer from Windows 10 upgrade until after seeing how all of my other computers did with the upgrade.  I upgraded four other computers.  The _only_ one that has not been a problem (so far) is an _older_ computer, with a 32 bit system.  One of the other three computers failed (twice) to complete the upgrade, and it now has a Windows Update "corruption," which I have been unable to repair; and it will probably need to have Windows 7 completely reinstalled.  Another computer had it's validation key corrupted upon "roll-back" and because the sticker under the computer had several letters/digits worn off, I could not leave Windows 7 on it.  I had to resort to installing Linux.  (Which it turns out I like _a lot_).  The other "failure" was a 6 month old Pipo W2F tablet that was updated via "Windows Update Reservation" and worked flawlessly for several months until today when I turned it on and it auto-updated to the latest "cumulative update."  The tablet auto-rebooted after the installation, and never fully restarted.  The power button now does nothing at all.  The Windows 10 "cumulative update" literally _bricked_ my tablet.

There are Windows Registry edits you can do to forever prevent Windows 7 (or 8) from _ever_ upgrading to a newer version (Windows 10 or otherwise).  I have entered these edits into the Windows Registry of my main computer, and the fact that it _cannot_ be even forcefully upgraded to Windows 10 is of at least some consolation for all of the grief I have had with Windows 10 failures on my other computers. 

Due to the simple fact that Windows Update cannot be turned off, I have truly felt all along that _any_ computer that has been upgraded to Windows 10 is a potential disaster waiting to happen.  Apparently my fears were confirmed today by the loss of my tablet to the latest "cumulative update."

It is my opinion that anyone who has not upgraded, should avoid it like the plague.  Anyone who has recently upgraded and is still within the 30 day window to "roll-back", should take advantage of the (soon to expire) opportunity to do so.  Lastly, anyone who is capable (via software & license key) to "go back" to either Windows 7 or 8 (8.1) should do so before a year goes by; (Note: Any previously registered Windows licensed computer that has not been updated via Windows Update for more than _one year_ becomes _invalid_ for re-registering the same license key.  Many people are not aware of this, even though it has been Microsoft policy for over a decade).
