I clicked the Update Driver button....results showed the most current driver already installed.  However, the Intel web site shows the most current version is 15.40.28.4501.  Is the built-in Driver Update utility not reliable?

a. user say:

tl;dr: If your OEM (HP) hasn't issued newer drivers for the OEM hardware in your computer, the hardware doesn't need newer drivers.

You have OEM hardware; the drivers on the Intel website are for Intel's own hardware.

That said, a hardware manufacturer's own drivers will often function perfectly well on OEM versions of their hardware, but that's not a rule. In general, OEM hardware should only be updated with OEM drivers.

You should not worry if the version of Intel's own drivers is more recent than your OEM drivers. It's a very common misconception that a computer should always have the most up-to-date drivers. Sometimes that's true, for example:  if you installed a new version of Windows you should have the most recent Windows 10 drivers for your hardware (presuming that your hardware is supported on Windows 10.)

New drivers are published to solve problems with the hardware. They don't improve performance other than to solve problems. That's why, for example, gamers want to have the very newest drivers. Otherwise, drivers follow that old rule of 'if it isn't broken don't fix it.' In your case, if your OEM hasn't issued newer drivers for the OEM hardware in your computer, the hardware doesn't need newer drivers.

a. user say:

The difference between brand name hardware and OEM hardware is not easy to appreciate.

First, an OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturer) is a company that assembles a product from parts. That certainly applies to the big consumer computer manufacturers like HP, Dell, Lenovo, Sony etc. etc. These companies don't actually build hard drives or CPUs or video cards; they buy those parts from the companies that actually do build them, like Intel, Western Digital, AMD, Broadcom, etc. as well as plenty of lesser-known companies.

The OEMs custom design their computers, so they often ask their suppliers to build customized versions of their hardware. These customized components often need special drivers created uniquely for the customized version. That's why, for example, the driver that Intel creates for its own components may not work properly on the customized version of the very same component that Intel made for an OEM.

Finally, if you recall what I wrote about 'if it ain't broke don't fix it' that particularly applies to hardware that doesn't change its role after it's been built. A CPU, for example, almost never needs an updated driver so the drivers you'll see in Device Manager are old. Also, to maintain inter-operability between key hardware components and Windows, once a basic driver has been signed off by both Intel and Microsoft (typically) it's not going to change.

Contrast this with a graphics card which needs to adapt to all the new effects in the latest 3D games. In that case the driver needs to be updated frequently or the card won't display the game properly.

It is certainly true, as I wrote earlier, that you can sometimes update an OEM driver with a newer version built for the hardware manufacturer's own product. But you won't be accomplishing anything because an update wasn't needed in the first place.

It's counter-intuitive, but you only update a driver to fix a problem with the hardware. Updating a driver just because a newer version is out will usually not accomplish anything, and you're taking the risk that some incompatibility between the manufacturer's own hardware and the OEM's customized hardware will cause problems that no one can predict. The problems are unpredictable because hardware manufacturers don't test updated drivers on OEM equipment - what for? - and OEMs don't test stock drivers on their customized equipment - no reason to do that.

Hey guys,

I have managed to set my Xbox One up in exactly the way you describe. I bought an HDMI to DVI cable (without sound) and plug that into a PC monitor. I get just the picture that way, no sound.

Then I bought a digital to analog optical converter that converts TOSlink/SPDIF optical into an analog signal that can be consumed via L/R RCA audio or 3.5mm headphone jack.

I bought mine from eBay from the following seller:

http://www.ebay.co.uk/usr/hdoutlet4uk

The title of the item was "Digital to Analog Optical Cable Coax Coaxial Toslink RCA Converter + 3.5mm Audio".

This is the cheapest one I could find, it works a treat, it came the next day and comes with a free optical cable - bonus!

It comes with a USB power lead, which is perfect because you can just plug that into the back of your Xbox.

Make sure you configure the Xbox so that the optical audio out is in Stereo mode. If it's in the other mode then this converter will not work.

I plugged in a couple of X-Mini II speakers (http://www.amazon.co.uk/Generation-Capsule-iPhone-Laptop-Speaker/dp/B001UEBN42) and it all worked nicely.

This isn't my permanent setup but it's much more portable for when I go away than taking my 40" TV.

Hope this helps somebody...!

(c) In File Explorer (or in Disk Management), browse into the WindowsImageBackup folder,

     & "R-Clk, Mount" the largest of the 3 .vhdx files in it. This is the Windows partition.

     (Ignore the error message, if you get one.)

(d) In Disk Management, give the newly mounted partition a drive letter, if it didn't automatically get one.

     Now, one has access to all the files in the backup image using File Explorer.

(e) R-Clk START, & select "Command Prompt (Admin)".

     The following DiskPart commands show that the reserved partition indeed was handled well by Windows System Image Backup. It isn't a separate .vhdx - but it is part of the one that is Windows. (The other 2 .vhdx files are the ESP (EFI) partition & the WinRETools [recovery environment] partition.)
