jonathan gin say:

Considering your xbox is only 20 ft from your wireless router(not sure how many walls or other forms of interference you may have), I think upgrading your wireless router would be negligible if you only plan on using Xbox LIVE for multiplayer gaming. You may be able to reduce latency slightly due to less dropped packets from less interference when using the new standards or using 802.11a/n (5 GHz), but that band also doesn't penetrate walls very well so your signal may or may not work at 20 ft. You also have to remember that when using some of these other "standards" or "speed boosts" on certain routers, they are only compatible with similar gear, so you may need to be a wireless network bridge with the same "standards" or "speed boosts" for your xbox to be able to use them. I think the one thing to mainly keep in mind is that the current standardized wireless rate is 55 Mbps which is usually halved actual rate so unless your actual ISP provides you with more than 22 Mbps, there would be no point of upgrading it for your Internet connection.

That being said, if you plan on using Windows Media Center on your xbox 360 to stream television or movies in HD through your own internal home network, I would recommend using wired ethernet to have the best experience because you'll be on the borderline of streaming HD if you're using wireless and it'll fluctuate quality.

samuel3201 say:

Stick with an "N" standard router. Wireless N is technically a standard of speed and range. Companies like Cisco Linksys, Belkin, and Netgear limit/cap the speed that is available to you. You should pick a router based off what speed you have coming into your house from your ISP (Internet Service Provider). If your Xbox is not that far away from your router, you still want to make sure you pick a router that will not limit the speed coming in from your ISP, but also make sure your not picking a more expensive one, just because it is more expensive. If you pick a higher performance router, but the speed coming in does not compensate, then it would render the new router speeds useless. In my opinion, almost every cable internet company would not have speeds over what the Linksys E2500 could handle. I would say the Linksys E2500 would be a better choice for you, unless you are on a business connection, or a higher bandwidth connection, such as T1+.

samuel3201 say:

Also, I wanted to add that having a dual-band router is also useless if the Xbox does not function on 5GHZ. If the internal network card on your Xbox 360 does not support 5GHZ, and only 2.4GHZ then buying a dual-band router would also be useless.

jonathan gin say:

To Samuel3201 point and your question about 2.4/5 GHz simultaneous. The xbox 360 will not be connected with 2.4/5 GHz simultaneously. Your router being 2.4/5 GHz simultaneous/dual band just means that it will be able to route traffic on both the 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz clients at the same time, but not combine them.
