Buffering or Streaming media are multimedia that are constantly received by, and normally presented to, an end-user while being delivered by a streaming provider (the term "presented" is used in this article in a general sense that includes audio or video playback.

 

A media stream can be streamed either by live or on demand. Live streams are generally provided by a means called true streaming. True streaming sends the information straight to the computer or device without saving the file to a hard disk.

 

There is no option within the Operating system to stop buffering or to increase the speed of buffering. Only way to stop or to increase the buffering speed to certain extend is to get a connection with greater network bandwidth. a. user say:

No.

System Restore Points "live" (by default) on a system for 7776000 seconds (90 days) based on the "Time To Live" registry setting:

HKLM\Software\Microsoft\Windows NT\CurrentVersion\SystemRestore\RPLifeInterval

Depending on how much space you have set aside for Restore Points, you may have less than 90 days worth, but you will never have more than 90 days worth.  If your Restore Points only go back a month, there is a reason for that but we don't know enough to tell you why.

And remember that System Restore is not a time machine.

Since System Restore does not know or care about any of your user/personal files (documents, emails, images, music, etc.) and only cares about Microsoft things, if you were to restore your system back to a year ago, you would be undoing a years worth of Microsoft updates and have to download and install them all again and that does not sound like much fun.

Your system may be overheating.  Laptops are not really made to run in your lap and need good circulation to keep cool.  You may notice an overheating problem when you are doing CPU or video intensive things like playing games, streaming videos and when a system overheats, to prevent permanent damage, a built in thermal protection mechanism shuts the system down without warning and that means it powers off completely. 

To get things going again, you have to use the power button and sometimes you might even have to wait a little while for things to cool off then things work okay again for a while.  Typing random characters just before the incident could be a sign the the CPU is starting to get too hot to behave properly.

There is no time to even write any information to the System Event Log about the problem so there is not a good way to track it down.

Dell has an article about overheating laptops that may help get you started on resolving laptop overheating problem:

http://www.dell.com/support/troubleshooting/us/en/19/KCS/KcsArticles/ArticleView?c=&l=&s=&docid=279891#Issue3

There is free monitoring software to see your system temperatures, but I would just work on the basics first and be sure your system is well ventilated.  Sometimes a visit to a reputable computer shop recommended by your friends may be in order where they can address overheating issues in more advanced ways.

I understand that some folks really love their Norton, maybe you paid for it, you may be forced to use it or it came free with your system or from your Internet Service provider but just because your AV software says your system is 'clean' (no matter what it is) doesn't necessarily mean it is clean.  I interpret that result to mean that your AV software just didn't find anything that it knows about. 

It is prudent to supplement your AV with some other respectable free tools and if you want your system to reward you with better performance, you might consider replacing your Norton with something like Microsoft Security Essentials which has a much smaller footprint.  You can always reinstall your Norton again later if you are compelled to do so, right?  Maybe that is a project for another day though.

To wit, I still suggest the following even with your Norton:

No matter what else you are using for malware protection, please do this:

Download, install, update and do a quick scan with these free malware detection programs:

Malwarebytes (MBAM):  http://www.malwarebytes.org/products/malwarebytes_free
SUPERAntiSpyware: (SAS):  http://www.superantispyware.com/

Be sure to select and choose to delete any threats, reboot, then scan again to be sure the threats have really been removed.

SAS will probably report a bunch of tracking cookies and you can just let it delete them.

SAS may install a startup item and launch every time your system reboots so if it is your desire to disable that, from the SAS splash screen, choose Preferences and uncheck the box "Start SUPERAntiSpyware when Windows starts" and the startup item will be removed from your configuration.

SAS may also create a Scheduled Task to run every day which can also be disabled or deleted if that is your desire.  To do that, choose Preferences, Scanning Control tab, Configure Scheduled Scanning and it may remind you to upgrade after 14 days. You don't have to do anything to keep SAS installed to continue using it with updates and run on demand actions.

Do a full scan once in a while when you have more time (perhaps hours).

They can be uninstalled later if desired.

When the scans run clean, reboot and then troubleshoot any remaining issues.

If you think you are going to replace your Norton (even temporarily), you need to be sure NOT to use Add/Remove programs to uninstall Norton, but use their special Removal Tool that you can get here:

https://support.norton.com/sp/en/us/home/current/solutions/kb20080710133834EN_EndUserProfile_en_us

Then you can get Microsoft Security Essentials (MSE) for the low price of free here:

http://windows.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/security-essentials-download

Hopefully something good will happen for your system now and let us know what you discover!
