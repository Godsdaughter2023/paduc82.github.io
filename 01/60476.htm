I don't have personal experience with this issue, but from what I've seen, this appears to be a hardware problem.  Although some people have had (at least temporary) success by re-seating the wireless module (which requires some disassembly of your computer), the actual problem seems to be a defect in the motherboard, perhaps defective soldering of one of the chips and/or overheating.  For the latter, see https://youtu.be/XQQbN5o3cYY

If the problem recurs and you don't want to work on the innards of your computer, your best option is to buy an inexpensive USB wi-fi adapter.  For example only, see https://www.newegg.com/Product/ProductList.aspx?Submit=ENE&IsNodeId=1&N=100158094%20600014288

If you mainly use your computer physically near your wireless router, any of the "mini" or "nano" adapters should be sufficient.  If you use your computer in a location where the wireless signal is weak, consider getting an adapter that has a better antenna, even though these devices occupy considerably more space.  Do read the reviews, especially the one- and two-star reviews.

bossdweeb say:

This is to inform you that Win7/8/8.1/ all  have three versions of DirectX . DX9, 10 and 11.

Win10 has DX12, but there very few of these as yet.

- It's a common misconception that Win7/8/8.1 have only DX 11.

 It is necessary that all versions of DirectX are installed as programs use different sub-versions of the

DirectX 'dynamic libraries' ( .dll files ).

The vast majority of programs still in use, that use DirectX,  still rely on DX9.

- The following is a list of the Direct X .dll's you will have when the files are up to date.
Go to the Windows / System 32 folder, (and SysWOW64 for those who have 64bit).  
They are in alphabetical order and will start with d3dx9 - 24 > 43 . Then d3dx10 - 33 > 43 & finally d3dx11 - 42 > 43.
There should also be - d3d9, d3d10 and d3d11 these come before the D3Dcompilers - 33>43.

There are more DX files for audio and input, but these are most of the graphics related Direct X  .dll's

"i didn't believe them so,i want a answer from microsoft.........."

Well, I have nothing to do with MS, but  your are entirely correct.

There is no difference in performance when running 32bit Win7 or Win 8/8.1 with 4GB RAM.

The main issue is that a 32bit OS can access only about 3.25GB of the 4GB installed. While a 64bit OS can

access the full amount.

This does not make the system any faster, unless whatever you are doing on the machine actually requires

more than 3.25 GB.

I don't know how much RAM a typical Win8/8.1 uses at idle, but mine uses a minimum of 1.5GB RAM,

and is using 1.8 GB at the moment, with a couple of browser windows open

- Having said that. I don't know why you would want to 'upgrade' from Win7 to Win8/8.1 .

Perhaps if you have a laptop with a touch screen you may like it for the 'METRO' interface, but this 'feature'

was very unpopular with most Windows users.

There is no benefit regarding performance using Win8/8.1 vs Win7.

- If you wish to experiment with a new OS Win10 may be the better option, but I would not personally advise 'upgrading' to Win10 if you are not using a very recent machine, particularly if you are using a laptop more than

four years old.

Many people with 'older' laptops have problems with drivers, but their biggest headache/heartache comes

when they try to 'rollback' to their previous version of Windows, and that rollback fails.

vijay a. verma say:
This is most likely a display adapter issue.

Please try these options one by one and check after each option. These options are independent and can be executed in any sequence.

Option 1 - Use the Hardware & Devices Troubleshooter

Type Troubleshoot in Windows Start Search box > Click Troubleshoot > Scroll down & Click Hardware & Devices > Click Run the Troubleshooter & follow on-screen directions > Restart the computer after troubleshooting is completed

Option 2: Update display driver

Windows Key+X (Or right click on Start) > Click Device Manager > Expand Display Adapter > Right click on entry below > Click Update Driver Software > Click Browse my computer for driver software > Click Let my pick from a list of device drivers on my computer > Choose the driver > Click Next to update > If this does not work, select Search automatically for the updated driver in place of Browse my computer for driver software > Restart your computer

Option 3: Update driver from manufacturer's website

Go to the website of the display adapter's manufacturer or computer's manufacturer > See if some driver is released for your model and OS > If yes, download that > In previous option, you click Update Driver Software, in place of this, click Uninstall > Install the downloaded driver > Restart your computer
OR
https://www.lifewire.com/how-to-find-and-downlo... (Thanks to Rob, MVP for the link)

Option 4 - Rollback the Driver

Windows Key+X > Click Device Manager > Expand Display Adapter > Right click on entry below > Click Properties > Go to Driver tab > If there is rollback option, click it
