rohn007 say:
That requirement applied to Windows 8 and earlier that had a factory installed and activated Windows license. An "OEM" license. The factory used a special "bulk" key to activate the installation. The user would have to use the unique Product Key on their sticker to reactivate Windows if they had to re-install it. It was the users responsibility to keep a record of that product key. If the key on the sticker was damaged, they were out of luck.

Starting with Win 10 MS switched to a "digital" method of tracking product keys. When a Windows installation is activated, MS records a digital "fingerprint" or "signature" for the computer hardware along with the key. The next time you install Windows 10 on that computer it is supposed to "automagically" be activated for you, without any intervention.

I did a quick check of my Win10 laptop and do not see any sort of Windows Product Key sticker on it. There is a makers sticker with general specs: CPU, RAM, HD, OS (WIN10) and a bar code that I assume is the maker serial number.
