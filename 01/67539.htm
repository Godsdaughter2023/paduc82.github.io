ri7425ck say:

move the My Documents folder to another drive

Hi! I just upgraded from Win XP Pro to Win 7x 64. no problems! I would like to relocate the My Documents folder, from my C drive to my D drive. I was able to do this in XP, but do not know the procedure for win 7? Any advice or information will be greatly appreciated. Thank you.
steve winograd say: The My Documents folder is part of Windows 7's new Documents Library.  A library combines multiple folders that contain similar types of files.

To move your documents to the D drive:

1. Create a new documents folder on the D drive.
2. Right-click the new folder and click Include in Library > Documents .
3. Click Start > Documents .
4. Double-click My Documents to show its contents.
5. Drag and drop the files to the new folder.
6. Press F5 to refresh the view.
7. Under Documents Library , click locations .
8. Right-click the new folder and click Set as default save location .
9. [Optional] Click My Documents and click Remove .

Do more hard drive partition affect performance of pc? - Microsoft Community

rverma9797 say:

Do more hard drive partition affect performance of pc?

I have i3 pc with windows 10 it has 1tb hdd. So I am thinking about to split hdd into more than 3 partitions. But before it I want to khow that having more partition will increase my pc performance or decrease it?

hydranix say:

If partitioned strategicallly, it can greatly increase the performance of a pc.

For example:

(When viewing a hard disk drive as a horizontal partition abstraction, the further left a partition is, the faster it is to read/write/access the data there. The further right you go, the longer it takes to read/write/access. This is in the context of a horizontal partition abstraction, such as found in Windows diskmgmt.msc)

So if you were to create a partition equal in size to your system RAM, in the left-most side, and dedicate it entirely to pagefile/swapfile usage, you'll gain a good performance if your system uses the pagefile a lot.

Moving the boot partition to the right most edge of the disk will provide a few hundred megabytes of space in the better-performing area of the disk, since booting is a state that your system will be in the least amount of time that it is used, it makes sense to move it to the least performant area of a disk. In reality though moving the boot partition is not going to gain any noticeable performance benefits.

To directly answer the question, in general no, more partitions will not negatively impact performance. In fact, using one very large partition for everything will be more of a negative impact. The filesystem we need to be more complex and automatic system maintenance will have much more data to process if the system files share space with a large amount of non-system data.
