Not enough memory SSD - - Microsoft Community

mgp01 say:

Not enough memory SSD -

I purchased a new computer from HP Win10, 64bit, 16GB RAM with 1TB SSD hard drive. I was shocked that after just a few days it gave an error message "Not enough memory." (COMPLETE SOLUTION BELOW.)

After some research, I opened DiskManager (diskmgmt.msc) and THOUGHT I saw that the computer had a partition in the hard drive leaving only 120GB in the C:\ and 900+GB in a drive labeled D:\ (Data). I could not extend the C:\ because the D:\ was not "on the right side" of C:\ . Deleting the D:\ just left unallocated space which was also not on the "right side" and there for could not be used for extension.

I reinstalled Windows but the "partition" remained. I have had HP computers for years and until now they have had an impeccable record in helping me and backing up their product. So I got on chat with HP support hoping for a quick solution.

The first Tech I spoke told me to BUY high end support from HP!! for a NEW purchased computer with extended warranty... YIKES.

I finally got through to a second guy who assured me he knew all about hard drives and my problem and ultimately told me that they would mail me a new Windows media disk in 5-7 days for a re-install!! I could only get it sooner if I purchased the disk.... NOT HAPPY. I told him I was doubtful that it would solve the problem (because I already did a Windows reinstall) but he assured me that it would. I insisted that he could and should send the disk overnight or provide a download link, but he was insistent that was the best he could do and refused to allow me to talk to his supervisor.

After some thought, I realized I could download Win10 directly from MS, which I did onto a thumbdrive and reinstalled Windows from there. IT DIDN'T WORK, the SSD memory allocation remained.

Then, thank you God, I was inspired. I remembered something from years ago working with BIOS. I saw on DiskManager "Drive 0" and "Drive 1" and the light went on and I realized this stupid computer has TWO SSDs!! 

The smaller one had Windows and was C:\ UGGH!

SO... if you have this problem, you can figure it out yourself, as I did (thanks to combining other postings and wasting time with some really bad methods) or do the following (why this isn't done automatically for us, I have no idea):

1.  In your D:\ create a new USER folder. If you have multiple users, combine them under one folder called USERS (it is better to use standard Windows file names). 

2.  Open BOTH folders, C:\[USER1] and D:\[USER1] in separate windows. 

3.  COPY all files from the C:\ [USER 1] to D:\ [USER 1].

4.  Delete the ONLY the OneDrive folder from [USER 1] (We will come back to this)

5.  Go to the first file in C:\[USER1] and right click then click "Properties"

6.  Click the Location tab, click "Move" and then navigate to the SAME folder in D:\[USER 1] and select it. Back in the Location Tab, Click OK. The C:\ folder should DISAPPEAR and Windows will now recognize the new folder in D:\ [USER1] as the Windows folder. 

7. This is TRICKY... Open OneDrive and associate the NEW OneDrive folder in D:\[USERNAME 1] with it. The option to select a new folder (D:\) is in one of the first screens, after putting in your MS account info, but it is VERY SMALL hyperlink and you have to go slow and look for it, DO NOT speed through the process!! If you get to the screen where you pick the files to sync and didn't change the drive, exit out and start over.

NOTE:

DO NOT delete the USERS folder or [USER NAMES] in C:\

The file "Roaming" will NOT relocate, nor will hidden files (e.g. Application Data). Just leave them alone.

IF YOU MAKE A MISTAKE like I did by moving a Windows User file to the wrong folder, you can first try to move it to the right one or (like I did) re-install Windows and start over. 

Best of Luck!!

How do I find out who made my video card so I can update the driver?

Right-click on your desktop, select Properties. Go to the Settings tab. Under the little monitor it should show something like "NEC 430v on NVIDIA GeForce 9600 GT." In this case, it's a Nvidia 9600GT.

If you have a notebook, it may show a little differently. Go to Start, Settings, Control Panel, and open System. Go to the Hardware tab, then click on Device Manager. Click on the + sign next to Display Adapters and you'll see yours. Double-click on it and go to the Driver tab. That will show you who the driver manufacturer is and what version is installed. Then you can go to your PC manufacturer's site (if it's a notebook or an onboard video chip in a PC) for drivers, or if it's an add-in card, go to the card manufacturer's site for newer drivers.

Is there any particular problem you are having that makes you want an updated driver, or are you just trying to stay current?
