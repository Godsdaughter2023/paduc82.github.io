a. user say:

Hi Marianne,

Windows Hello enables biometric sign-in for Windows 10 such as fingerprint, iris, or facial recognition. When you set up Windows Hello, you're asked to create a PIN first. This PIN enables you to sign in using the PIN when you can’t use your preferred biometric because of an injury or because the sensor is unavailable or not working properly.

If you only had a biometric sign-in configured and, for any reason, were unable to use that method to sign in, you would have to sign in using your account and password, which doesn't provide you the same level of protection as Hello.

If you're experiencing issues signing in using your PIN, you can check your PIN or reset it. To do that, simply go to Settings > Accounts > Sign-in options and under PIN select I forgot my PIN.

a. user say:

I never thought your problems were heat related...  the temps you reported before seemed fine and well in range for your CPU.  Heat problems don't generally result in a BSOD anyway.  When your CPU overheats, it just shuts down to prevent thermal damage.  There is no time to write out a crash dump file or a BSOD.

If you want to see it, take a hair dryer or heat gun on high and aim it right at your CPU for a minute.  Your system will just power off like it is supposed to.  There will be no BSOD, nothing written to the event log and no report.  Depending on your BIOS and CPU, your system may report a thermal event occurred the next time you boot up (after things cool off).

If you are running Media Center Edition (MCE), I think you should stay far away from McAfee since I have had to fix too many MCE systems where MCE specific files come up "missing" and the reason they are missing is because McAfee interpreted them as threats and quarantined (effectively deleted) both the working copy and the backup copy from the system.  Maybe they have fixed it but I am not installing McAfee to find out.

When somebody finds MCE specific files missing, I am going to ask if they are running McAfee and the answer seems to always be yes (followed by "how did you know that?").   Replacing missing files is generally not a problem if you happen to have a genuine bootable installation CD that is the same Service Pack as your installed Service pack, but the fact in the real world on the planet called Earth is that most people do not.  They either never got one with their system, they lost it, the dog ate it or something.  This is the reason there are hundreds of XP and MCE files on my SkyDrive - so people without CDs can replace files that have been quarantined by things like McAfee or otherwise come up missing.  I used to like McAfee just fine, but now I will not even install it even to test anything to see how it works.

The same for Norton - it used to seem like a good thing, but now I think it has too big of a footprint and as you can see, their support is just so so and/or they want you to pay.  I won't even install it to test anything anymore either - too messy for me.  And we already know it was responsible for some of your crashes.

If you want just free stuff (which is fine), I would stick with Microsoft Security Essentials (MSE) supplemented with the free versions of Malwarebytes and SUPERAntiSpyware.  MSE has a small footprint and has really gotten better over time, but remember that MS is not in the malware protection, detection and removable business - that is not their job.  You need to also use products from companies where that is their job and that is all they do all day long.

It is also important to appreciate that you need more than one reputable package (but only one with real time protection).  You can have so much protection that your system performance will suffer.  And just because one thing says your system is clean, doesn't necessarily mean your system is clean.  I interpret that to mean that the application just didn't find anything that it knows about and there doesn't seem to be any one package that knows about everything.  It is prudent to use more than one scanner to get good coverage.  New threats seem to be discovered every day.

    augus7 say:

    Increasing Virtual XP 's RAM memory in Windows 7

    Currently my Virtual XP is having 512 RAM distributed from 4GB RAM of Win 7.  How do I increase the 512MB RAM to 1GB? Because its a bit laggy at times when I run some softwares in Virtual XP.
    divya r say:

    Hi Augus7,

    Take a look at the steps below to increase the size of the Virtual Memory.

    Virtual Memory

    If your computer lacks the random access memory (RAM) needed to run a program or operation, Windows uses virtual memory to compensate.

    Virtual memory combines your computer’s RAM with temporary space on your hard disk. When RAM runs low, virtual memory moves data from RAM to a space called a paging file. Moving data to and from the paging file frees up RAM to complete its work.

    The more RAM your computer has, the faster your programs will generally run. If a lack of RAM is slowing your computer, you might be tempted to increase virtual memory to compensate. However, your computer can read data from RAM much more quickly than from a hard disk, so adding RAM is a better solution.

     

    Refer the following link to know about “Virtual Memory”.

    What is virtual memory?

    http://windows.microsoft.com/en-us/windows7/What-is-virtual-memory

     

    Method 1: Here are the steps to be followed to change the size of the Virtual Memory:

     

    You must be logged on as an administrator or a member of the Administrators group in order to complete this procedure. If your computer is connected to a network, network policy settings may also prevent you from completing this procedure.

    1. Open System in Control Panel.

    2. On the Advanced tab, under Performance, click Settings.

    3. On the Advanced tab, under Virtual memory, click Change.

    4. Under Drive [Volume Label], click the drive that contains the paging file you want to change.

    5. Under Paging file size for selected drive, click Custom size, and type a new paging file size in megabytes in the Initial size (MB) or Maximum size (MB) box, and then click Set.


    If you decrease the size of either the initial or maximum page file settings, you must restart your computer to see the effects of those changes. Increases typically do not require a restart.
