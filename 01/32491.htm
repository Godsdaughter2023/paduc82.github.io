Windows 10 security concerns - Microsoft Community

grandpa jim say:

Windows 10 security concerns

With earlier versions of Windows, Ed Bott et al. recommended using a non-administrator account for daily computing.
My question: Are there any good reasons for using a non-administrator--or a "local"--account for daily computing (bill paying, e-mail, reading the news, personal record keeping) rather than a "signed-in" Administrator account?
Put differently, are there valuable Windows functions that would be missed if an Administrator account is not used? For examples...?
What is the best recommendation for safety and security?

a. user say:

I hope I'm as astute as you when I get to be a Grandpa.

First, some terminology:

A local account exists only on the computer on which it was initially created. You can only use a local account when you're sitting in front of the computer on which it was created.

An online account exists on the internet (or 'the cloud' as it's popularly known.) You can use an online account on any computer that connects to the internet.

A 'Microsoft account' is simply Microsoft's brand name for their cloud account, just like a Google account is Google's brand name for their online account an iCloud is Apple's ... you get the idea.

Whether you choose a local account or a cloud/online account is entirely up to you. Of course, Microsoft would love it if everyone only used a Microsoft account. However, most people use some features of both types of account. For example, I do all my work on a local account but I store files on OneDrive, which is part of my Microsoft account.

Administrator and Standard accounts are about something completely different. An Administrator account has the authority to make changes that affect the entire computer, like installing software. The tech-y name for that authority is 'permissions.' By contrast, a Standard account only has permissions on that account, so a Standard user can only make changes that affect their own account, like which picture of the beautiful grandchildren appears on the desktop.

A local account can be an Administrator account or a Standard account, and so can a Microsoft account.

Now on to Ed Bott et. al.

The old advice is still true: Your day-to-day computing should always be done with a Standard account - whether that's a local account or a Microsoft account. You should only use an Administrator account for tasks that require Administrator permissions. I'll explain why in a minute.

If you're working in your Standard account and you try to do something that needs Administrator permissions, Windows will stop you and ask for an Administrator password. Once you supply the password Windows will 'elevate your permissions' to enable you to do whatever you were trying to do, then you'll drop back to Standard permissions. Only rarely will you actually need to sign in to your Administrator account.

Here's why it matters: Let's say your user account gets hacked, despite all your efforts to prevent that from happening. If your account only has Standard permissions, the malware will probably run wild inside your account but it won't be able to affect the rest of the computer. On the other hand, if your account has Administrator permissions then the malware will be able to use those elevated permissions to damage the entire computer, your home network, and hundreds or thousands of other computers on the internet.

In the same vein you should always password-protect your user accounts, even if you're the only one who ever touches your computer. A hacker on the internet doesn't need to touch your computer to get inside. All they need is an internet connection. Why make their job easier by leaving the door open for them?
