Were you using the built-in account named "Administrator"?  If so, the behavior you describe is "a feature, not a bug."

Windows XP hides the Administrator account when a user account is created (because it's a good idea to not use Administrator routinely; it should be saved for emergencies).

You access the built-in Administrator account differently depending on whether you have XP Home or XP Pro.

For XP Home:

You need to restart in Safe Mode.  Restart the computer and start tapping the F8 key as soon as the initial boot process is complete.  If you see the Windows logo, you waited too long and you'll have to reboot again.   If you do it correctly, you'll see the white-on-black Advance Boot Options menu (image).  Use the up/down arrows and the Enter key to select Safe Mode because your mouse will not work on this screen.  Once the computer boots into Safe Mode, the Administrator account will appear on the Welcome screen.  If you haven't changed the password, the password is blank (no password).

For XP Pro:

Either follow the directions for XP Home, or while at the Welcome screen, with no user logged on, press CTRL+ALT+DEL twice.  The "classic" logon box will appear.  Type "Administrator" (without quotes).  Again, the default password is blank.

Once you get into the Administrator account, create an account with "computer administrator" privileges for those occasions when you need it (e.g., to run System Restore or to create new accounts).  You can use any name you want (other than Administrator), such as MyAdmin.

a. user say:

Private network vs. public network applies to laptops, and it's about how much access other computers on the network have to your computer. When you're at home you want to use the settings for a private network.

The easiest way to think about it is that a private network is your own home network, and a public network is every other network. When you're on your network, you trust that other computers on the network, if any, aren't going to try to attack your computer, because nobody gets to use your network unless you let them.  Since you trust these other computers, you're okay with letting them have access to your computer and to printers that are on your network.

On a the other hand, anyone can connect to a public network, and for all you know, one of those other computers may indeed try to attack your computer. It happens all the time. So when you're on a public network you're going to sharply restrict the access that other computers on the network have to your computer.

A computer is discoverable when it allows other computers on the network to notice that it's on the network. The alternative is playing hard to get. There might be a technical term for that, but it eludes me at the moment.

Homegroups were - notice the past tense - a simplified method for connecting computers on a private network. The past tense is because homegroups are going away - Microsoft has discontinued them.

Most important is to understand that every time you connect to the internet, even if it's just to use your browser or download a game, thousands of people all over the world are trying to attack your computer. A naive or uncaring computer user is their best friend. Don't be their friend! You need to learn how to protect yourself on the internet.
