dogtamer say:

Hello, els3160

     You're correct, virtualapp/didlogical listing under "Generic" in Credentials Manager can be used to authenticate your particular local instance of Windows Live Essentials to Microsoft's servers; there is @ least anecdotal evidence that it can also used for other Microsoft applications, such Live Search Toolbar, Windows Live ID SignIn Assistant, Virtual PC, Silverlight, & others.  The only way I'm aware of to prevent this credential from being employed is to uninstall WLE (or whatever Microsoft program may be creating it, such as the afore-mentioned Live Search Toolbar, etc.) or to at least prevent the local Services that employ it from running.  In my experience, whether one uses any of WLE's programs is largely immaterial to this; as long as they are installed on a computer the authentication will occur - usually at startup; this is by design.  Similar creds you may find listed under "Generic Credentials" - such as "WindowsLive:(token):name=[YOUR hotmail/live/Outlook.com e-mail address];serviceuri=auth.bay.livefilestore.com", which are associated with authenticating e-mail accounts incorporated into a user's local Windows Live Mail e-mail client; or "OneDrive Cached Credential", which is used to authenticate your OneDrive (formerly, SkyDrive) account, are of the same nature. 

     It may be worth noting that there seems to be a lot of feedback from non-technical folks who've decided this "backdoor" represents a security concern, a view I do not share. I have a W7 Home Premium box that's been authenticating regularly in this fashion since 2010, and have yet to experience any difficulties.

     By the way, you mentioned having found it necessary to remove a trojan; might I recommend a couple of Answers Community threads/wikis to which I typically refer folks who wish to "do-it-themselves"?  

The thread "My Windows security Center isn't working therefore I have no firewall, defender etc. My security essentials are still working?" (see Kosh Vorlon - MS MVP's June 17, 2012 post) and the wiki article created by Stephen Boots, "List of anti-malware product removal tools", both have much good info that's proved helpful; among other good resources is Bleeping Computer's "Answers to common security questions - Best Practices - Anti-Virus and Anti-Malware Software".  The links in "Where do I get additional support for Security, Privacy, and Accounts issues?" might also be of use.

High CPU issues can be caused by many factors. Some of them may be:

  • Third party security applications like Antivirus, Firewalls, etc

  • Other startup items set to start when you boot your computer

  • Drivers not compatible with Windows 7

  • Low System requirements (slow processor, little RAM, etc)

Method 2: Safe mode
Boot your computer in safe mode and check for the issue.
To start your computer in Safe mode
http://windows.microsoft.com/en-US/windows7/Start-your-computer-in-safe-mode
 

Method 3: Clean boot.

If you do not face the issue in safe mode, then put your computer in clean boot and check for the issue.
How to troubleshoot a problem by performing a clean boot in Windows Vista or in Windows 7
 

http://support.microsoft.com/kb/929135
