Are you referring to Windows 8/8.1 help tips that shows up around the corner of the screen while navigating to different apps and settings?

These notifications wouldn’t show-up once you follow their path by navigating the same way they notify.

NOTE: This option for using group policy is only available in the Windows 8.1/RT Pro and Windows 8.1 Enterprise editions.

a) Type “cmd” on the Start screen. Right Click on the Command Prompt and select Run as administrator.

b) Type “gpedit.msc” and hit Enter to Group Policy Editor.

e) Select the radio button that says “Enabled”.

f) Click on OK.

a. user say:

Backing up can be as simple as copying your personal data (Documents, Pictures, etc.) to a USB drive. Third-party backup software will make that easy and convenient, and you will be able to easily schedule your backups so they occur automatically.

To backup your entire hard drive, including Windows and all your installed software, you'll need to make a disk image. This is more complicated and requires an investment of your time to learn how to do it. However, once you've learned how to do it, you'll wonder how you lived without it.

Third-party software makes a disk image backup easier to understand and easier to perform. I use Macrium Reflect but there are other good choices you can try. Macrium Reflect makes disk images automatically, overnight, so I am never farther than 24 hours away from a complete working copy of my computer. I can also run images manually at any time.

miran noorul haq say: So confirm me one thing, to backup my stuff i would require an external drive? And also i found the create disk image in win 10, so why i will need third party software?

Last time i had to use recovery was in boot menu, so does disk image helps in boot recovery?

a. user say:

The whole idea of backing up is to copy your data to somewhere that's not on your computer. That way, if your computer gets damaged, you won't lose your data. A USB drive is inexpensive and easy to use: Just plug it into a USB port and you're ready to go. After you've made your image, you can remove the USB drive and store it somewhere safe.

You can certainly use Windows' own disk image creator if you want. I'm only saying that third party software is easier to understand and has many more features than the built-in utility.

An image is an exact copy of a hard disk, so if you make an image of a boot-able hard disk, and you restore that image to a hard disk that won't boot, you will again have a boot-able hard disk.

But you should know that hard disks can fail to boot for many reasons that an image can't fix. For example, if your hard disk is physically damaged, or if your BIOS or UEFI is corrupted, restoring an image won't fix that.
