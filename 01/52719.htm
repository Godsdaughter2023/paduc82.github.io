kosh vorlon say:

Hi sugi2k,

 

Using BCC is also how I send group messages, but I do it primarily so individual members of the group don't get to see all the email addresses of everyone to whom I'm sending the message.  As far as I know, there's no difference security-wise between using the To field or the CC field to send e-mails (though BCC does a bit better in hiding the address, but it can still be found if you are the recipient or sender of that BCC).  In general, this is not how malware and hackers work.  It is simply too cumbersome and time-consuming to do it by checking addresses in the individual e-mails being sent (though it is possible and very difficult to defend against if they can intercept the mail - but typically it occurs from the recipient's account and not the sending account and usually because most people choose to add new email senders to their contact list automatically and that's where the vulnerability usually occurs).

 

Here's an article about the differences between BCC and CC: http://email.about.com/od/emailnetiquette/a/cc_and_bcc.htm.  You'll note that it addresses anonymity as the primary value over security (but there are some security advantages in terms of that anonymity and the inabiility of other recipients to view the BCC recipients).

 

Most malware and hacking occurs because someone gets to your contact list and/or address book or just simply sees your email address somewhere (perhaps from one you sent to someone who was infected or to an actual hacker) and uses various techniques to make it seem like SPAM and other things they send are coming from you when you aren't actually sending anything.  If they got to your address book or contact list or someone's on which you are listed as a contact(probably using another method - I don't believe those fields themselves are any more susceptible to malware or hacking than anything else on your computer - if they have installed keystroker malware or programs, then everything you type can be available to them unless you detect and remove the malware and/or hacking tool), they may send it to all of them (probably with an attempt to infect their systems included as well).

 

In short, while they are not invulnerable, they are as safe as anything else (though BCC will help limit the number of people who see the addresses of the entire group which can help from their side where you don't know if they have been compromised) on your system.  The real issue with emails isn't in sending them but in receiving them and getting "a surprise" along with it that compromises your system and gives someone access to your contacts and/or address book - but they don't often also go for inputs into sent messages and if they do, then entries to any of those fields on your computer if it has been compromised is possible (not just e-mails but documents and pretty much anything).

 

If you're that concerned about such things, then use some program to encrypt the messages and require the recipients to have a decryption code to open them.  It can still be cracked by a hacker, but is much more difficult and requires a much more sophisticated hacker.

 

I hope this helps.

 

Good luck!

frederik long say: Things are now getting a little tougher. You might be able to fix the problem by restoring a backup copy of your registry files. The process is involved and requires some careful typing. Ask a computer-savvy friend to assist you if you lack experience in this area. Note that it may reset your password to an earlier value.
1. Keep tapping F8 during the early boot phase.
2. Select Repair from the menu that looks like this one:
   http://windows.microsoft.com/en-US/windows-vista/Advanced-startup-options-including-safe-mode
3. Log on as Administrator if prompted.
4. Select Repair when prompted.
5. Select the Command Prompt from the menu that looks like this one:
   http://windows.microsoft.com/en-US/windows7/What-are-the-system-recovery-options-in-Windows-7
6. Identify the correct drive letter for your Windows installation. It could be a drive other than C: but it won't be X:. You do it by typing the commands
    dir C:\Win*
    dir D:\Win*
    dir E:\Win*
    until you find the Windows folder.
    I will call that drive "Q:".
7. Type these commands:
    cd /d Q:\windows\System32\config
    xcopy *.* Q:\MyRegBack\
    cd  RegBack
    dir
8. Examine the dates of the Software, System and SAM files. Were the files created before or after your problem became apparent?
9. If they were made before the problem became apparent then you can type these commands:
    copy /y software  ..
    copy /y System  ..
    copy /y Sam ..
    (the two dots are part of each command)
10. Reboot normally. If things do not work out then you can backtrack by copying the original registry files from Q:\MyRegBack to Q:\Windows\System32\config.
