ralf_g say:

Make a backup of your user files, photos etc. before you do the upgrade, as insurance.

The "Rollback" feature that is incorporated into the Settings app is the easiest way to convert back to Windows 7. In theory the process should leave your user files intact. Rollback is only active for 1 month though and disappears after that. One potential hitch with Rollback is that some of the major updates from Windows Update have been known to break the rollback function, so it is best not to allow Windows Updates to install automatically until after you are certain you want to keep Windows 10. That is something you'll have to turn off as soon as Windows is installed.

The second easiest way to convert back to Windows 7 is to clone your Win7 installation to an external harddrive before upgrading so that you can recover it back just as it was.

Other methods all involve re-installing Windows 7 and all of your software from scratch, whether you use the computer manufacturer's recovery media or a retail copy of Windows 7 to do so.

wtom1 say:

> What are the chances that it sent a surge to the Xbox though, since the power brick has a surge protector built-in?

Term 'surge protection' is a vague and subjective expression that says something exists - but does not say what.  The expression say nothing useful for a technical conclusion.

Electronics are soldered and then washed with water.  Water does not damage electronics.  But water must dry before powering electronics.  You (apparently) did not do that.  So water may have made connections even across large safety gaps from 120 volt AC to low voltage DC.  You powered an electronically modified circuit.

Power supplies have overvoltage protection so that a supply does not damage its load (ie Xbox).  However you may have compromised those protection circuits with water.  Meaning they may not have been robust enough.

Nothing on a load can damage  a power supply (to answer your last question).  And nothing in a power supply can damage the load (Xbox) - except when you modified the design with water.

If an Xbox is undamaged, then a new supply will work just fine.  Meanwhile, ignore the expression surge.  You did not have a thousands volt spike trying to find earth via your Xbox.  You had (at worst) an overvoltage.  Something so low that even a surge protector strip would ignore it (since it did not well exceed 330 volts).

Now, learn from the mistake.   Power brick must be dry before being powered.  One popular trick is to store it in a sealed container of rice - for weeks.  Since electronics are not damaged by water. But electronics can be destroyed  if powered when still wet.
