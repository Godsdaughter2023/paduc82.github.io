sc tom say:

A disk image is (simply put) a compressed copy of your whole drive in a single file. Depending on the compression used, it will be anywhere from 50% to 90% of the occupied space, so you'd need at least that much free on your external drive. You wouldn't be overwriting anything on that external drive. Once the image is created, you would shut down your PC, put the new drive in, boot from the imaging software boot CD, and restore that image to the new drive. It will then be like it was at the time you made the image, with all files on the new drive that were on the old one.

Others have brought up a valid point- if the drive is too damaged already, or it "hiccups" while creating the image, you may still have problems with the new drive. But it will be a file problem, not a hardware one, and (unless it's completely corrupt) fairly easily fixable, assuming the new drive will boot.

 SC Tom

This works!

For those with no CMD experience:

- type CMD in the Start Menu's Search box (Cortana, for Win 10)

- right-click the 'Command Prompt' that it finds, and click on Run as Administrator'

- paste netsh winsock reset in the black (DOS) window and hit Enter

- restart Windows and Chrome will work.

If you still have problems, your AV/Malware software may be "protecting" the corrupt Winsocks stack. If so, make sure you have an off-line copy of your AV/Malware software:

- disconnect (physically) from internet, remove your AV/Malware software, run the NETSH command after the reboot, and re-install your AV/Malware Suite. Plug back in and test.
