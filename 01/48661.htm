a. user say:

AlanD2,
Your wireless mouse runs on the 2.4GHz frequency, as do almost all wireless consumer electronics.  It can experience interference from any number of devices: your wireless LAN adapter, your wireless router, a 2.4GHz cordless telephone, a baby monitor, a security camera, a microwave oven, etc.  The list is almost endless.  When you went to the library you did not experience the issue so it is most likely due to interference with some new device in your home that you may not even be aware has a wireless component or may not have thought of as a possible source of interference.  Do some sleuthing - start noticing exactly when the freezes occur, how many minutes apart, how long it lasts, what times of day, etc.  You should start to see a pattern.  I have had people find out that similar problems occurred only when the phone rang or only when the wireless router was updating DHCP addresses or only when someone was making microwave popcorn.  I know that sounds far fetched, but I ran a wireless business for many years and saw some very strange issues caused by interference.  You said your local wifi provider was offline for two days and you did not see any interference.  Do you have wireless Internet - are you running wireless from your house to a tower that provides your Internet service?  If so, then your mouse is getting interference from your wireless radio that communicates with the tower.  And your mouse is interfering with your radio, which is causing your wifi service to be flaky.  Like I said, I ran a wireless ISP for years and have seen this happen many times.  You may not be able to use a wireless mouse if you are using wireless Internet.

majortom617 say:

This was the first link on my search for "Windows Media Player lossless" and I notice it's almost 3 years old, has over a thousand views, and just two posts. I'll add a third, and hopefully be as helpful.

Ripping to WAV will give you what is on your audio CD, two channels (or separate recordings, usually left and right for stereo) of LPCM digital audio sampled at 44,100 cycles per second (hertz), with 16 bits (the ones and zeros) for each sample. This adds up quickly, 44,100 X 16 = 705,600 bits per second to be precise. That's still a lot, even by today's standards. I want my vast music to sound as good as possible, but if I'm not loosing anything by storing it in a slightly more complicated container, then I'm not loosing anything, just gaining some complication, and some digital storage space.

Sometimes digital audio is recorded at 48,000 hertz (48 kHz), or 96 kHz, or even higher, which is arguably better, despite diminishing returns. These samples can also be recorded at higher bit rates (24 bit, 32, etc.) This is also arguably better, again despite diminishing returns. This extra data adds up even more quickly, so much so that some fancy math is needed to shrink down the total size. If done losslessly, no data is lost. It will sound exactly the same because the computer does the math to play the original recording.

So, what's the best? I like FLAC (Free Lossless Audio Codec). I like using it with Audacity, which lets me choose various speed/size settings. Since my computer is pretty fast, I choose the setting that takes the longest time to complete, but more so because this results in the smallest file size. All settings sound exactly the same. All lossless codecs (provided nothing goes wrong and they're outputting the same sample and bit rate they take in) sound identical. I came here because this is the question I wanted answered:

What are the differences between FLAC and WMA when ripping in Windows Media Player? Can I adjust these differences to gain better performance/smaller file sizes?

After testing a few albums and averaging their file sizes, I've decided to use WMA. All files were slightly smaller.
