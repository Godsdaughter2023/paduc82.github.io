Here's something to try...

Connect the camera to the PC via USB cable and
turn the camera on.

If any windows open...close them.

Go to...Start / Computer...

Look at the drive letters and see if one of them
represents the camera. It may say Removable Disk

If the camera is recognized...right click the drive letter
and from the menu choose...Open or Explore.

The directory of the memory card in the camera should
be displayed and if you navigate through the various
folders you should find the photo files.

Once found...you can copy/paste the photos to a folder
on your hard drive.

Simple and best solution IMHO is to use an internal
or external USB Memory Card reader.

It won't drain your camera batteries and may be faster.
FWIW...downloading from the camera when batteries
may be low after a long photo shoot could result in
corrupted photos.

Do you have an internal or external USB Media Reader?

Insert the memory card in the correct slot on the Media Reader...
if any windows open...close them.

When the card is inserted...if you go to...Start / Computer...is
the card reader listed as one or more Removable Disks?

Removable Disk (E:)
Removable Disk (F:)
Removable Disk (G:)

If_Yes_you can download the photos from there. Left clicking
the drive letter that corresponds to the memory Card slot should
display the directory of the card and you can copy/paste or
drag/drop the photos of your choice to a folder on your hard
drive.

Just right click a selected group of photos and choose...Copy...
then navigate to the folder you want them in and right click the
folder and choose Paste.

Battery life - Microsoft Community

tigermack say:

Battery life

I understand a laptop's battery life can be lessened by a number of factors, but can the battery life be damaged by overcharging or constantly maintaining a 100 percent charge?
hairyfool say:

That advice is somewhat out of date for Lithium Ion batteries used in most laptops.

It was the case with Ni-Cad batteries that they would suffer damage and loss of capacity if left on charge for long periods and not discharged. They did not NEED complex charge management so few manufacturers incorporated any with the net result they would overcharge.

Modern high capacity Li-ion batteries must have proper charge management incorporated (as evidenced by the Samsung issue) so are protected from overcharging. The also do not have the charge memory issues of old and do not need to be discharged at intervals to maintain capacity.

In fact the reverse is true, the more heavily these batteries are discharged the less life they have. The main reason to deliberately discharge the battery is to prove that they still have a good runtime. That obviously does not include running on battery for the intended use.

If you can leave it on charge most of the time do so, let the electronic charge management do its job. Periodically take it off AC and let it run down to around 80% to demonstrate running capacity. If you actually have used it away from the desk then you don't need to run the test discharge.

My Dell L702 was initially regularly discharged through use for the 1st year I had it, the last 2 it has been used on the desk and only occasional discharge done. It has virtually the same runtime now as when it was new.
