Sounds like the instructions assume that if you are buying a microphone you would know if you have a sound device or not.

Are you saying that when you click Start, Programs Accessories, Entertainment there is no Sound Recorder option or the option is there but when you click it nothing happens?

The Sound Recorder menu option is just a shortcut to an executable file on your system, so you need to determine if the shortcut is just missing/broken or the exectutable file is really missing (or both).

If the shortcut is missing that is not hard to fix.  Sometimes whole chunks of accessory type things are missing from Accessories - like Accessibility, Communications, System Tools,Entertainment (that might be you).  It is not hard to replace the entire section if all or parts of it are missing.  

To find out if the Sound Recorder executable file is on your system, click Start, Run and in the box enter:

%SystemRoot%\System32\sndrec32.exe

Click OK and report if the XP Sound Recorder opens or not.

If the %SystemRoot%\System32\sndrec32.exe file is missing (nothing happens or an error message) it is in a few other places on your system so copying one into the right place is also easy.

You just need to figure out exactly what is wrong and then you can fix it.  Clearly state the problem.

Most of the time (in recent history), motherboards come with some kind of sound device built in.  Optionally, you can add a better one later.  If you found a place to plug in your microphone and speakers, you must have something but maybe it is just not working.  Without a little more information, we can only guess what you have.  You can provide a few details and we will know exactly what you have and there will be no guessing.

Click Start, Run and in the box enter:

msinfo32

Click OK, and when the System Summary info appears, click Edit, Select All, Copy and then paste the information back here.

There will be some personal information (like System Name and User Name), and whatever appears to be private information to you, just delete it from the pasted information.

This will minimize back and forth Q&A and eliminate guesswork.

For audio information, expand the Components, click Sound Device, click Edit, Select All, Copy and then paste the information back here.

A "recovery" disk is not a copy of your installation (as I think you guessed) but a disk to diagnose/fix boot problems with your computer.  Here's (another) guide to creating a "system image".  Bit surprised that USB isn't mentioned as an option - it should be I think - BUT will need to be rather large.  How much space is your stuff taking up?  Don't forget to count all drives IE C:  D: 

e.g. if C: has 40GB used and D: has 100GB used you get 2 options :-

1. create a system image of just C: (so your computer will be bootable but not include D:)

You will need a USB stick of 64GB

2. create a system image of the whole lot.  You will need a USB of at 256GB.

Guide here :- http://www.tenforums.com/tutorials/5495-system-image-create-windows-10-a.html

Although I haven't tried it, you could try removing the "Client for Microsoft Networks" from the machines that you do not want to access network shares.
