Unfortunately, the suggestion Nielo gave won't work. In trying to understand the bugs that cause this problem, I tried it and found that it won't solve anything. :<

Video is rendered incorrectly now, causing a mismatch in the conversion between input levels and output levels. This happens regardless of which color space you select for output, including YCbCr BT.709.

RGB+Expanded can in some setups have two separate bugs (the bug in video rendering, and another bug that always flags limited range over HDMI) counteract each other and produce correct video levels. But instead game and dashboard graphics will get crushed black and clipped white levels. As will the interface graphics in the apps.

RGB+Intermediate can produce a compromise between washed out video and crushed game graphics. It's not a good compromise, but it's the only balanced compromise.

In both cases, the display must treat the HDMI input as limited range. This should be the case if the HDMI levels setting is on Auto in the display, but if your display has a Full Range option you need to switch that back to either Auto or Limited range.

zacabeb say:

Whenever you change the Reference Levels, it triggers another bug that clips the black and white in the dashboard graphics, scales the range and makes the picture more contrasty than it should be. It may seem like the themes look better, but that's because the dark gradient at the top makes them pale (since it's not entirely opaque, it produces a dark gray in many themes rather than black). Other parts of the dashboard that are properly black will get crushed though.

So it's when you boot or return to the dashboard that you're actually getting the correct output levels. It's confusing - but remember that this whole issue is a combo of three bugs that interact to some degree (the incorrect handling of the range upon changing reference levels, the incorrect handling of the range in video apps, and the incorrect flagging of range in the HDMI signal).

Interestingly, this means that if you boot with RGB+Expanded and then set things to RGB+Standard, the net result is it doesn't change the levels at all, but simply clips black and.white and leaves the range in between as is.

So you don't have to worry about changing the reference levels when you boot if it's already set to RGB+Expanded.

At the bottom of the Task manager window, click on More details.  That will show you what you expected.

I don't understand why it is Task manager you want to see when you are referring to drivers.  Did you mean Device manager [provided in the Control panel]?  That will allow you to check the status of device drivers.

If Lenovo have not released updated drivers for your hardware then you will indeed be told that your existing drivers are up to date.  If they have not released any new drivers for two years then the chances are that they have stopped product development for your model completely.  Do check that the drivers you are now trying to install are actually updates - look at their version numbers and compare them to the version numbers you see in Device manager.  It can be worth downloading all the relevant drivers now in case of future need but there is no point in installing them if you are not trying to fix a device fault.  Perhaps there are Lenovo user forums somewhere on the Lenovo website where you could get guidance from other users of your model.

If you are installing updated hardware drivers, Lenovo ought to advise you about the appropriate installation procedure [including its sequence].  I would expect to find this advice on the Lenovo website or in their user forums.  Generally [using my equivalent Dell guide], the installation sequence is

1 anything described as overarching system-wide software [Dell calls some software "Notebook system software" and advises that it is installed first but I have no idea if this term is used by other manufacturers].  Given the age of your computer, I would not expect to see any system-wide system software updates anyway.

2 Chipset drivers

3 Display-video drivers

4 Network cards [LAN, WiFi]

5 Audio drivers

6 Modem drivers

7 Touchpad, mouse & keyboard drivers

8 other

Most updated device drivers deal with the job of removing older drivers themselves without any interference from you.  Personally, I would therefore just run the updates and see if they object to anything.
