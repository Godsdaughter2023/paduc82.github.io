chriscolley say: Sorry guys for not getting back to this sooner. I have managed to sort out my problem. 

I figured I'd test out all the HDD's in the storage space and ran a benchmark on them to find out if they are all playing ball. Turns out one of them wasn't. Its read/write speeds were about 1% of what all the other drives were getting.

So I think problem solved.... WRONG! My Storage pool was split up into numerous spaces most of which contains media files on simple spaces plus personal files and photos etc on two way mirrored spaces. It turns out you can't just remove a drive from the pool like you can in WHSv1 if there are simple spaces in the pool. I ended up having to back up all my data to another PC(luckily I do anyway!), delete all the spaces from the pool and only then would it let me remove the drive from the pool. After this I could finally rebuild all the spaces and then copy all the data back over.

If MS wants this technology to be taken seriously, they NEED to implement a way to replace failing or upgrade to larger drives without having to remove all the data or it is next to useless.

internet expert say:

The clutch must be pressed down while you shift gears. You let go of the clutch after you’ve changed gears.

You could try manual without clutch so you could practice finding the right time to shift gears. Gears should be shifted up when the tachometer starts to get high (in RPM’s) which you could also hear in the car’s engine (it sounds higher pitched). Keep an eye on the tachometer and an ear on the engine noise as you race.

Acceleration is the rate of change of speed with respect to time. Every gear lets you accelerate through certain ranges of speeds (e.g. Gear 1 does 0-15mph, Gear 2 does 16-30mph, and so on -- but this is only an example as cars are different), and will eventually stop accelerating as the RPMs get too high, which is when you want to shift up to get more speed. I like to shift up just as the needle hits the red.

You should shift down a gear (or two) on turns, as you are usually wanting to slow down at that point. How many gears you shift down depends on how much speed you’re losing from braking.

Hope this helps. For the record, I am not a Forza/car expert. I kinda learned this when I began playing Forza Motorsport 5 and I’ve been winning races fine ever since. Like real life driving, practice makes perfect.

m3tal daz3 say:

Practical actions:

1. release gas
2. shift up (clutch + shift-up button. What I do is a swipe action since the clutch for me is A and the shift-up is B)
3. press on gas

Considerations: 

1. Don't let off the gas. Not necessary in this game and it's actually faster to not lift of gas when shifting.

2. Everyone fast uses MC because the car accelerates better due to reduced shift times. Clutch kicking is a benefit but is not the reason why manual with clutch is used.

3. The benefits of using the clutch vary depending on transmission type. This is more of a car by car issue and only applies to a handful of modern cars and race cars.

4. Your power curve will determine when and where to shift. Generally you can get away with running well into the max rpm but if it's a torque monster, early shifting may be faster. This is highly car dependent.

5. The ping you hear when up shifting is normal. Not much you can do with that. Now if you are hearing a loud and long ping you shifted to late and did lose time on your up shift.

With the crazy builds you need in order to be fast in FH3, the amount of power in a car can compensate for lackluster clutch usage.

It's also harder to see the benefits. In the Motorsport titles its much easier to see...especially when folks are in bone stock cars.

andre da costa say:

Windows 7 64 bit uses 8 to 10 GBs of disk space on a clean install.

Please note, you might seeing less disk space because of the following reasons:

System Retore might be using some of the disk space, see instructions below to reduce that.

Also:

Depending on the amount of RAM you have installed, it will require more disk space for paging, hibernation and dump files. So, 8 GBs of RAM will use 8 GBs of disk space for the page file.

Configuring System Protection settings

To configure System Protection, click Start, type: System Protection, hit Enter. Under Protection Settings, click the Configure button. A dialog will appear with various settings for configuring Restoration and Disk space usage. Under the Disk space usage tab, you will see the current amount of space in use by System Restore while the Max usage displays the amount allocated. Use the Max usage knob to configure how much disk space you want to allocate to System Protection. In the above screenshot, you will see that I am using 21.05 GBs for Restore Points, while I have allocated 22.36 GBs of disk space for System Protection. You also have the option of deleting all Restore Points which stores System settings and previous versions of files. I suggest you leave this, since you will be able to individually restore changes to files you are working on. So for instance, if you edited an image and would like to restore it to a previous version or original version of the file.
