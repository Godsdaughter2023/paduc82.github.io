wm shake say:

Hi,,

 

Commercial movies on VHS used very basic Macrovision copy protection schemes, primitive by today's variations. But it's still enough to be recognized by today's digital recording devices, such as DVD recorders and video capture devices. And, by law, digital recording devices MUST be able to recognize copy protection AND refuse to pass it through (DMCA, or Digital Millenium Copyright Act).

And that's the whole issue. Regular copyright law DOES permit you to make an archival copy for your own use, but DMCA then turns around and tells you NO. There IS a legal way around it, though. There is NO law that prohibits you from cleaning up variations in the video as long as it's done in Real Time. So a basic proc amp is all you need. Consumer ones are called video stabilizers, or digital video stabilizers and can be legally purchased online for $25 - $40.

Since VHS is already substandard quality, the stabilizer does not degrade it any further, and since VHS can only be processed in real time, you haven't violated the DMCA, either.
