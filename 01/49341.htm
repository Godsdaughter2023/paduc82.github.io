Your laptop's ability to switch between the Intel and AMD graphics is usually managed by proprietary Dell drivers, not Windows. When you run a game or other app that requires higher graphics performance, your laptop should automatically switch to the AMD graphics chip.

You're getting the Microsoft Basic Display driver when you remove the Intel drivers because Windows needs to use the Intel UHD graphics in all other cases, so it installs generic drivers when the Intel ones are removed.

normgeltz say:

Hello again, Lynette,

We're still checking on this and we will get back to you.

A quick trick to try is this.

Remove all peripherals from your system e.g. any and all USB devices (including mouse & keyboard), printer, etc. - anything which is a peripheral remove it from your PC/laptop. You may have a driver (it could be ANY driver), which is loading into the 'stack' in the incorrect order. The first thing the OS does is to load your drivers (after POST, of course). If you have a game driver, a program driver i.e. any driver which may be loading first and corrupting the driver 'stack', it can (sometimes) cause many additional drivers not to load.

So, with NOTHING connected to your PC/laptop (remove everything) then turn OFF your computer. With your computer turned OFF, plug in your PS2 wired mouse. The IRQ (interrupt request address) to support a wired mouse should not fail - a PS2 mouse has a dedicated IRQ (just like the computer clock - it's dedicated).  If it fails to instantiate a 'wired' mouse we can look further. Again, do not have ANY other peripheral plugged into your PC/laptop, then turn your PC/laptop OFF (with nothing plugged in). Then, plug in your PS2 wired mouse and turn your PC to ON. It should most certainly instantiate your PS Mouse and it should work. If your PS wired mouse is now working, unplug your wired mouse.

Next, plug in your Wireless Desktop Receiver. Since you haven't been able to use either your wireless mouse or your wireless keyboard (which both depend on the wireless desktop receiver, be certain to 'pair' them correctly. First, ensure you actually see lights on each of them (check for bad batteries if you see no lights); next, under the mouse and on the desktop receiver push the button on each to pair them together.

Windows 10 should be able to find those drivers since they are Microsoft hardware and software equipment - you shouldn't need external drivers (on your USB drive). Windows should either automatically load the drivers by default or search the Internet for the most up-to-date drivers - in which case, plug your Internet cable into your PC/laptop (if you're wired); or, the WiFi will automatically be enabled and begin searching automatically for you.

My single-most biggest point here is remove ALL peripherals, turn off your computer plug ONLY your wired mouse into the computer and it should definitely enable your PS2 wired mouse. Next, remove the PS2 wired mouse and plug in your wireless desktop receiver. We're removing all external hardware device drivers from enumerating on bootup.

  1. Restart the computer

  3. You should get to the "Advanced Boot Options" screen, which is black with white lettering.  If you get the Windows logo and Windows starts, you waited too long to start tapping.  Repeat from step 1.

  5. When Windows starts, if you see the Welcome screen, there should be an icon for "Administrator."  If you get the same login box shown in your screen shot, this time entering "Administrator" should work.

lemp say:

Without meaning to sound condescending, I really doubt that your computer doesn't have Safe Mode.  I suspect that you're just starting to tap the F8 key too late or not rapidly enough. 

If you tap too rapidly, you may get a "keyboard error" message.  If you tap too slowly (or wait too long to start tapping), you get the Windows logon screen.  You have too tap "just right" -- -- which is at a rate of about 2 taps per second. 

Even in the highly unlikely event that you can't get into Safe Mode, however, there are several other methods of retrieving data from your computer. 

I suggest that you try once more, and if you are not successful, bring your computer to a local independent computer repair shop, preferably one for which you can get a recommendation from friends or colleagues.  Do not go to a big chain store or "geek squad" type of place.
