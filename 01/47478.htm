lemp say:

Have you always experienced this problem?

If not, what happened to your system shortly before the problem started?

Have you added any components to your computer or otherwise had the computer case open?

Do you have a laptop or a desktop/tower computer?

"No signal" is a message generated by your monitor when it doesn't detect a video signal from your computer. 

It is normal to see this message when your computer "goes to sleep," because when that happens there is no signal coming from the computer.

I don't know what your comfort level is with respect to working with the hardware inside your computer or what diagnostic equipment and/or spare parts you may have available.  Although you can install a new video driver yourself and check the video cable connections yourself, the other possible causes of your problem described below may be better handled by a computer repair shop. 

Computer hardware repair is not everyone's cup of tea, and there is no shame in bringing your computer in for professional help.  If you decide to do that, I suggest going to a local independent shop rather than a consumer electronics store, an office supply store, or a similar "chain store."  Ask friends and colleagues for recommendations.

If you sometimes get the "no signal" message when your computer first turns on, it may mean that your video card is not turning on when the rest of the computer turns on.  This could be caused by:

  • a bad driver for your video card
  • a failing video card
  • a video card that is not properly seated in its socket in the computer
  • a bad connection between the computer's power supply and the video card
  • a failing power supply
  • a failing motherboard

If you sometimes get the "no signal" message after the computer has been on and properly working, the problem could be:

  • An overheating video card
  • The cable between the computer and monitor is loose.  Check the connections at both ends
  • The cable between the computer and the monitor is damaged.  This is more difficult to check for, but after making sure that the connections at both ends are secure, move/bend the cable a bit to see if you can cause the "no signal" message to appear

Sure you should be concerned.  Any program which makes no obvious benefit but gets itself installed and then takes any steps whatsoever to make uninstall difficult should be presumed guilty until proved innocent.  There are many lucrative, nasty things a program can do to your PC once you have given it the OK to install.  Programs which run all the time and have been installed with the privileges you granted - that is why Windows asks you if you permit the install - can do things like snoop your files or watch as you type in a password.  The fact that it tries to avoid uninstall should be taken as evidence that its makers see some benefit to keeping it on your machine, and that makes it suspicious.
