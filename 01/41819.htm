Aren't there restrictions on my using music from my music library - Microsoft Community

juliabakerbell say:

Aren't there restrictions on my using music from my music library as background on my movies in Movie Maker?

I am very new to Movie Maker - as in 20 minutes of use.  I want to upload many many 1 - 3 minute videos of dogs I use on adoption sites and I want to add music.  YouTube has music i can add once it is uploaded but can I use my own music library to add audio while editing my videos in Movie Maker?  Surely there are restrictions on what can be used but I have no idea what the rules are.  Can't find anything in the printed questions.  

considerate_guy say:

If you are talking about technical limitations, Movie Maker will only let you add music files that do not have DRM (digital rights management).  You also must import supported file types (like .mp3, .wma, etc.).  But within those limits, you can add music from your own library and they will play along with your photos and videos in Movie Maker.

 

It sounds like your question is more around the rights you have to use copyrighted music in your movies.  Generally, if you aren't the copyright holder (e.g. you didn't create the music yourself), you must have permission to use and rebroadcast that music.  In most cases, the music companies and artists are not going to grant you that right so you should not use such music, especially if you plan to have a public performance of your movie.  In this case, even sharing your video on the web is considered a public performance and would require you to get permission or pay a royalty to the original artist to do so.

 

YouTube, for example, will ask you to verify on each upload, that your movie only contains content that you own or have rights to use.  If they detect that your content matches that of a copyright holder, they may remove your video, add ads to your video, or even close down your YouTube account.

 

It is your responsibility to respect the rights of copyright holders.  If you need more information, The Copyright Office has a FAQ on Copyright Law.  Additionally, YouTube has a page on Copyright, Fair Use, Creative Commons and more.

 

Finally, if you still want to use music created by someone else, look for either music in the public domain (e.g. not covered by copyright) or that is licensed under a Creative Commons license.  For example, if you go to the Free Music Archive, you can find music that is free to use if you include credit to the original artist.
