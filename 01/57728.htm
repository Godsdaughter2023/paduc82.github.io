Fix for Windows 10 System Tray icons shifting automatically. - Microsoft Community

brmevans say:

Fix for Windows 10 System Tray icons shifting automatically.

I've come across a few threads where some "all in one" or newer laptops that may have touch screens have issues with the system tray resizing or shifting. I happened to come across one of these today. It was a laptop, and being certified by the vendor to repair it I was able to obtain the repair manual for the system. We removed the Keyboard, Trackpad and Battery cables from the system and let it run normally while connected to the DC power supply and used an external keyboard and mouse to log in. Ironically, the system behaved just the same under the original user account, as well as my account, so we ruled out that it is a problem with a user profile setting.

The next thing we tried was disabling the TouchScreen feature in the BIOS and rebooting with the internal components still disconnected, and voila. There was something with the driver controlling the TouchScreen feature after it booted in to Windows that started the odd behavior with just the system tray. The screen was perfectly clean and did not have any physical anomalies on it (we checked every inch to make sure there was no gum or nasty residue). Once we reconnected everything back up to the system board and restarted, the behavior returned again, to both accounts. Seeing as how it would a time waster to disconnect everything, we just disabled the TouchScreen feature once more and restarted, and the jerking around stopped once more.

We're not sure if this is a permanent fix to the issue or what is causing it, but it worked for us on a Dell laptop with a touch screen feature. Not all of them have this problem, but if we see it again, we'll be sure to disable the TouchScreen option in the BIOS before yanking wires :)

Tony, you certainly COULD fill in most forms using your computer and printer -- there are several different ways.

The easiest way is if you can obtain a "fillable" form from whoever is asking for the information.  For example, many government forms are available as a PDF file which you can load on your computer, fill out the form, and print out the completed form.

If you cannot obtain a copy of the form that is intended to be filled out on a computer it may still be possible to fill out the form on your computer but it can be much more complicated.

If you cannot obtain a computer copy of the form, then the next easiest way is to simply type the entire form on your computer.  This works reasonably well for simple forms such as medical office questionnaires which were originally created in a word processor.  If the blank form is just plain text with lines for you to write your answers, it often is not too difficult to just retype the entire form in your own word processor.  You may need to experiment with the font and font size to make your form look like the original, but it isn't too hard if the original form is fairly simple and you are experienced using your word processor.

The hardest method is to create your answers in a word processor then print them onto the blank form.  The problem with this method is trying to make your answers line up with the spaces on the original form.  Back in  the days of the old Word Perfect or other serious word processing software for DOS, it was possible to precisely locate where text would be printed.  With modern Windows word processors it is easy to see approximately what the printed text will look like, but it is nearly impossible to accurately position exactly where each part of the text of a document will print out.  Today, this is practical only for forms that have large areas for your answers and your answers are much smaller than the space provided.

One method that can work well in Windows word processors is to type your answers as text overlaid upon a graphic (picture) of the form.  Generally this requires having a full-size scanned image of the form,  You would have to insert the image of the form as a background image in the word processor document, then type in your answers so that they are properly positioned on the image of the form.

Finally it may be possible to talk with the people who require the form to see if you can just provide all the answers printed out from your computer for them to either attach the printout to the form or for someone to enter your answers into the form for you.

Keep in mind, that if you have any physical or mental handicap which, such as poor vision, shaking hands, or arthritis of the hands that prevents you from completing a required form, then the government agency or business that is requiring the form has a legal obligation under the Americans With Disabilities Act  to provide you with any required assistance or accommodation.  For example, they could have someone read each part of the form to you and then write in your answers. All you would have to so is say your answers and then sign the completed form. 
 
First you should try politely asking about getting a copy of the form that you could fill on the computer or some other reasonable accommodation -- if they say there is nothing they can do to help you, try politely reminding them of their obligations under the Americans With Disabilities Act -- if that still fails, check with your city or state government to find the office that handles problems for people with handicaps.  You may not get exactly the assistance that you would prefer, but you will get assistance to resolve the problem.

This is security gone mad!!

 

cmdrkeene say:

Sorry for the inconvenience.  This is intended behavior, like you said it is security gone mad.  If you could inject files in there, so can anything that runs with your credentials.  Since web browsers and plugins do run with your credentials, a compromised website could inject files into your program files without your knowledge.  This security keeps your system from being impacted by malware.

 

Most users very rarely have to save a file to this location.  In fact, more than 99.2% of users will never do this based on Windows 7 telemetry data.

 

This small inconvenience the few times that you might do this will keep your system safe.  However you are the administrator and if you want you are free to remove this security if you choose.

cmdrkeene say:

Some programs can elevate themselves to access secure areas.  Only a couple come to mind: SysInternal's Process Explorer, the Windows Defrag utility, and Task Manager.  Even after the program is already running in a limited context, it can elevate when required (actions that require elevation show that shield icon).

 

Notepad (or whatever program) could be re-written to have elevation handling, but it's probably just not worth the effort.  Still I agree with you that it would be useful, it's frustrating in the scenarios you mentioned or when you want to, for example, modify your HOSTS file.

 

Best thing you could do in terms of security is probably what you did do:  save the file then drag it into the desired location.  Or if you want, right-click Notepad (or whatever program) and "run as administrator" for that session so it can access all those places.
