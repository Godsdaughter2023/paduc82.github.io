Hello there,

I had the same issue after installing the Windows 10 November update 1511 on HP Probook 4540s, but i noticed it was a fingerprint device driver issue & did the following to activate fingerprint login in Windows Hello

  • Open device manager
  • Under Biometric devices, select Validity Sensor & click "Uninstall". A dialog box appears prompting to confirm device uninstall, Check "Delete the driver software for this device" & click "OK"
  • On the device manager window, click "Action" & click "Scan for hardware changes", an unknown device will appear, Right click it & select "Update Driver Software" to download the updated driver from Microsoft
  • After the system has installed the driver, restart your computer
  • Open "settings", click "Accounts" then click "Sign-in Options" and check under Windows Hello you'll see an option for fingerprint.

The above steps successfully worked for me & can now use my fingerprint for login. Hope it helps you too.

Automatic Updates - A Tale of Lost Work - Microsoft Community

artkingjw say:

Automatic Updates - A Tale of Lost Work

Hello,

It probably seems very common now to see people complaining about the inability to control automatic updates in Windows 10. I've read through the various solutions out there now and have concluded that there seems to be no way to turn off this "feature", without messing with some risky/dangerous settings on the non-Pro version of Windows 10. So this is not a question on how to control it, but rather a discussion to raise some issues, which I think the smart cookies at Microsoft should be aware of, and are hopefully trying to address.

Windows 10 now has the Notify to Schedule Restart feature, which is much better than not having it, as we are now afforded at least, some control over how updates work. However I do not think that this is enough, here is an example of my scenario:

- I go to bed at 11pm -> Updates install some time in the night (say 12am) -> Automatic restart scheduled for 3.45am -> I wake up at 8am to find that my computer has restarted -> rage quit smash keyboard punch hole in the wall 

Why? here are a couple of possible reasons, all of which have occurred:

1) I am running some form of software that needs time to process. Examples include programs such as a CFD (Computational Fluid Dynamics) solver, or meshing program. These things do not magically finish in a few minutes, and depending on your hardware and setup, can take hours, days to run. So if the computer shuts down at 3.45am, and I get up at 8am, the amount of work loss is not 4 hours, but a lot more, possibly as long as the whole project duration. This program can be somewhat mitigated with the clever use of auto-saves in the CFD solver, but this is not the case for every single application, and certainly not friendly to storage space.

2) I am downloading a large file, of some importance/urgency. I have a slow internet connection, which cannot be helped (believe me I have tried). Therefore, it takes a long time to download, I often let it run through the night. When I get up at 8am, I find that the entire download has been compromised, because Windows 10 decided it wanted to take a nap. Naturally, the time lost, is the duration of the whole download, not to mention any issues with data caps (which thankfully I don't have, because I paid for it).

3) I have several tabs open on my internet browser, all of which are lost in the reboot. I value privacy so I do not want my browser to remember history. Not the biggest issue, but definitely annoying.

4) Not all programs have an auto-save feature (where it auto-saves your work every 15 minutes or something). I have to consciously remember to save all my work before going to sleep, in fear that my computer will eat it as a midnight snack. Again, not the biggest issue, but definitely more hassle than it should be. 

I can see the intention behind the current Windows 10 method of handling updates, Microsoft doesn't want anyone to lag behind on updates to put it simply, which has many advantages (as well as disadvantages, but I won't delve into that here). But the way in which this is handled is less than desirable, at least for me, or for users like me (surely I am not alone in this?).

There are definitely work-a-rounds to solve this issue, but not everyone has enough resource to pay for Pro or Server or whatever version it is that allows for better management of updates, especially not when the only real 'use' of upgraded is to control update behavior. Not everyone has the privilege of living in areas of good network connectivity, not everyone can afford unlimited data caps, not everyone can afford to upgrade to a business grade data connection, especially when they don't need it all of the time - it is probably hard for people who have good internet connections to imagine what people with slow connections have to deal with.

A problem seems to be in how Microsoft thinks of its customers/users and how they use their machines. A good example of this is back in the Xbox One "Always Online" days - remember that? Yea... look how that turned out. For all their desire of progress, and all things shiny in the future, never forget that 95% of your users are still stuck in the grimy past Microsoft. I think there needs to be a reevaluation of what a typical Microsoft client is. Design goals are aimed at the top 5%, but if you really want to drive adoption, and adoration, you need to appeal to the 95%; solutions need to be engineered to accommodate the lowest common denominator. If I wanted to use my computer, only in the way my OS creators wanted me to, I would buy a Mac, not a Windows machine.

If a machine is to be useful to me, it needs to serve me. Work-a-rounds are good, but the more complicated they become, the more it becomes an issue of me serving the machine, and why should that be the case? I buy a machine to make my life easier, not give me extra stress.

As for my issue, I've decided to wait for the next batch of updates to come (even if it means I have to stay up), when it asks to schedule a restart, I will schedule it to a time when I will almost definitely be using the computer everyday, for example, 11am. That way, if it does need to reboot, I will know in advance and can save my work, and do what I need to do to avoid loosing work. I am not keen on a downgrade to Windows 8.1, as I found that OS experience very buggy on my system, so there goes that work-a-round.

Feel free to discuss this, post suggestions etc. I hope someone from Microsoft sees this as feedback.

I mean, the solution to this problem could be as simple as adding a hidden feature, where once activated, the scheduled restart will not occur unless user permission is explicitly granted, you could ask it to give you 15-30 minutes to save your work, after which it will reboot regardless. At least you have that last barrier, or safety net to prevent lost work. You make it hidden so that it is not available by default, so if someone were to use it, they will have to know what they are doing before doing it. This is similar to how Android's "Developer Options" is hidden, you tap on the "build number" 7 times - neat trick.
