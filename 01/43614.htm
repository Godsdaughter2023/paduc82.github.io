Thanks, people :)

Since I don't have the Netgear Nighthawk, I don't know what they mean by "high priority", but on my router, if I enable QoS (Quality of Service), the router will monitor the used bandwidth, and if a lot of devices use all available bandwidth, the QoS settings can prioritize certain applications (like for example VoIP telephony or video streaming) that require real-time response and throttle "less important" traffic like downloads or Torrents. QoS makes sure I can still have a stutter free phone call via VoIP, even though all devices try to use up all available bandwidth.

If you set the Xbox Ones to "High Priority" and the consoles start downloading game updates, they will grab all the bandwidth they can get, causing other devices to disconnect or slow down their downloads, or cause stuttering during real time phone calls via VoIP or video streaming. 

Since you mention you have two consoles, and your ISP set up port forwarding, ask them if they port forwarded you through their data center, and you are still behind their NAT (mention "CG-NAT" or "Carrier Grade NAT" via DS-Lite). 

If so, then they will have to change this to give you an actual public IP, without Port Forwarding on their end. 

After that, you can then go to your both console's advanced network settings, and give at least one of your two consoles a custom port setting. 

If the router "understands" the console's UPnP request for forwarding ports, you're already done. 

If you get a "Moderate" or "Strict" NAT on the Xbox's Network settings screen, you need to manually port forward these custom port(s) to the respective console. 

In regards to the MAC, QoS, Static IP stuff you mentioned: 

The MAC address "uniquely" identifies your devices (i.e. your two consoles) to your router. In the router settings you can then set up the router's DHCP server (the program responsible for giving your devices an IP-address automatically) to always give a console the same IP. 

Since DHCP leases out IP addresses for a certain period of time, it can be possible, that your console will get a different IP if you don't use it for a few days. 

With a different IP, the manual port forwarding that you set up will not work anymore, as the IP that you forward the ports to is still the old one in your router, while the DHCP has given the destination console a different IP. 

Giving a device an IP address that doesn't change is a "static IP", and is required when you manually port forward.
