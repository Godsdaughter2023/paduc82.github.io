patrick barker say: As an experienced BSOD analyst, I am more than 90% sure it's the cause of the crash. It all lines up. The problem with keeping the system overclocked is we have a constant 'what if' creating false positives. We can troubleshoot various things that would not be as big of a potential part of the crash, and it would always in the back potentially creating issues.

When a CPU is unstable from an overclock, many many things can go wrong. It entirely depends on what exactly goes wrong. Things can come out of sync if a specific processor has an error because the overclock is unstable, etc.

An i5 2500k at stock is 3.3 GHz and 3.7 with Turbo. Match that with your GPU and you should have great performance in video games. I don't think you'd get much of an FPS boost at all going from 3.3 to 4.5 (6-7 frames)? Also, I would much rather have a working computer that wasn't crashing than a few extra frames with blue screens on the side.

With all of this said, bring the system to defaults. Use it as you would regularly. If you don't receive crashes, you know it was the overclock and that's that. You can then either work on getting it stable (testing various voltages, looking into other settings, etc) or leave it at stock and have yourself a computer that doesn't crash.

I understand taking pride on overclocks and being proud of achieving what is believed to be a 'stable' overclock. I am a Moderator on OCN with over 14,000 posts, so I know first hand how disappointing it can be to figure out that your hard work and hours of stress testing has left you with an unstable overclock. However, like I said above, leaving the system overclocked is most likely the problem, and IF it's not, it's creating a huge 'what if' at all times. At least for troubleshooting purposes disable it.

Regards,

Patrick
