I had the opposite experience.  I never had a recording quality setting to begin with. I've been using Win 7 since 12/2008 and never used Vista. Since analog recordings are being encoded as they are being recorded, it makes sense to tell the encoder what level of quality is wanted.  For digital recordings, a digital stream is captured and stored.  It would be possible to change the recording quality by re-encoding the entire recording after it is captured, but it's not something I'd expect a system to do on the fly.

If users want to change the quality of recorded TV, it can be done once the recordings are finished.  The amount of quality that would be lost in order to get a recording to a certain size would vary so much from one recording to another that it would make no sense to target size, and targeting a quality level in terms of percentage of degradation would not be very meaningful given that it might save a lot of space on some recordings and almost none on others. Plus, it would overwhelm the CPU on many computers. There are third party reencoders that could be used, and a few format conversions involved, but if you want to tie up a computer for hours to save space, it's an option. Or you can add a 1.5TB drive for about $100.  Even a typical 1 hour full HD recording is only about 7GB, so that might make more sense to many users.

hagrinas say: But with digital recordings, there's no encoding going on. There's capturing going on. The computer has to process the digital stream coming in and flush it to disk. What you want is for it to handle the full digital data coming in and dynamically re-encode it to a lower resolution. That's not how things work. Essentially, the way MPEG works, in an oversimplified and abstract sense, is to have a jpeg for a frame, a jpeg for the next frame, and look at the differences. Only the data for the differences would be needed in a transmission. So compression algorithms can look at the entire data stream for a recording and figure out how to compress it best, and for the best compression it would require several passes over the entire data. So it's something that for practical purposes would need to be done after the recording is completed.  But you can do that right now. It is independent of the recording process. 

You are asking for a switch from something that records data to something that processes it instead and creates an output file from it. With analog data, there's no choice but to encode something to digital because there's nothing to put on the disk otherwise. When you upgraded from a previous version of Windows or receive a new computer preinstalled with Windows 10, what happened is the hardware (your PC) will get a digital entitlement, where a unique signature of the computer will be stored on Microsoft Activation Servers. The Windows 7 or Windows 8 genuine license you were previously running will be exchanged for a diagnostics key.

 

Anytime you need to reinstall Windows 10 on that machine, just proceed to reinstall Windows 10. It will automatically reactivate. You will prompted to enter a product key a couple times through the installation, click I don't have a key and Do this later.

 

There is no need to know or get a new key, but if you have issues with Digital Licensing or the Activation Troubleshooter, you can utilize an existing Windows 7 or Windows 8/8.1 product key you already own to reactivate if necessary. or use the reset function in Windows 10.
