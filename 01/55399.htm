graye say:

Sorry for the confusion...

Most manufactures of residential-style wireless routers make it easy to do "port forwarding" by the use of a DMZ (Demilitarized Zone).   However, this typically works best when all of the forwarded traffic from the outside is sent to a *single* PC inside your network.  If supported, the alternative is do port forwarding by hand.

Port forwarding (either manually or via a DMZ) is required because the IP addresses inside your network can't be exposed to the internet.  Instead, they are "translated" to use the single IP address of your ISP's cable modem.  This is called Network Address Translation (NAT).

So, if you need a more complicated port forwarding system that diverts incoming traffic to more than one PC, then that might not be a viable option for your wireless router.  Some manufactures only support the "dumbed down" DMZ... and do not allow for manual port forwarding.

Yet another alternative is to contact your ISP and ask them for a static IP address (or two).  You could have one static IP for your external traffic and then use the other for your internal traffic.
