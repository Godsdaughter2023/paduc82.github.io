lemp say:

Don't give up hope.  Although TechpumpkinWD sounds like a Western Digital employee, you should consider the following.


In general, you can convert a basic disk to a dynamic disk without losing the data on it. 

In general, if you convert a dynamic disk back to a basic disk, you delete all of the volumes and thus lose the data. 

Some third-party disk management utilities, however, claim to be able to convert from dynamic to basic without losing data.  See, e.g., http://www.partition-tool.com/resource/change-dynamic-disk-to-basic.htm


I don't know when Western Digital incorporated hardware encryption but it is likely to have been long enough ago that most WD external drives in use today are so equipped.  The encryption is carried out by a chip in the enclosure, so if you still have the enclosure (and it's still functional), you can probably recover your files.


Normally, when you can see a drive in Disk Management but not in My Computer, all that you need to do is to use Disk Management to assign a drive letter.  How does Disk Management describe your drive?  You should have one (or possibly more) "simple volumes."  If no drive letter is assigned, you should be able to right-click and assign a drive letter.


If you can assign a drive letter and thus view the drive in My Computer, you'll be able to determine whether or not the contents are encrypted (if you can't access them, they're encrypted). 


If, as is likely, the drive is encrypted and -- I assume -- the WD enclosure won't recognize the dynamic disk, you have two possible options, if you have the external enclosure and it isn't damaged:


1.  Use a tool such as Easeus Partition Master Professional (linked above) to convert the drive back to a basic disk and then put it back in the WD external enclosure.


2.  Go to a professional data recovery company that claims to have expertise in this area, such as http://www.datarecoverylink.com/blog/bid/155666/Data-Recovery-of-Encrypted-Western-Digital-drives

Neither option is free, although option 1 is obviously cheaper ... but riskier because you aren't an expert in this field.  I should also be clear that I have no information about either the Easeus software or the "Data Recovery Link" company other than that revealed by the linked web pages.  I can't vouch for either.

Only you can decide what your data is worth.  If I were in your situation, I'd at least talk to some data recovery experts before doing anything further with the drive.  The more "experimenting" you do, the more likely it is that you will irretrievably lose the data.
