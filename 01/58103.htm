Windows Hello won't recognize my fingerprint reader after update - Microsoft Community

matthottenstein say:

Windows Hello won't recognize my fingerprint reader after update

I just updated my Surface pro and  Hello no longer gives me the option to use the built in fingerprint reader. The preinstalled version of Windows worked fine with it. How to fix?

andre da costa say:
Give these a try:
1. Reset Facial Recognition and/or Fingerprint Options

Press Windows key + I to open Settings.
Click Accounts and select Sign-In options.
Locate Fingerprint or Facial Recognition options and click Remove under them.
After that, click Get Started and follow the on-screen instructions to reset Fingerprint and Facial Recognition.
Reboot your PC if needed.

2. Modify Group Policy Settings

Creators Update might have modified some of the crucial settings of Group Policy. Therefore, we would recommend to launch the Group Policy Editor and check/edit the settings related to Windows Hello functionality.

Pres Windows key, type gpedit, and press Enter.
Click on Computer Configuration and open Administrative Templates.
Select Windows Components and open Biometrics.
Click on Facial Features and right-click on Configure enhanced anti-spoofing option.
Select Edit and disable the Configure enhanced anti-spoofing feature.
Reboot your PC.

3. Update drivers

If some of the system drivers, most probably webcam and fingerprint reader drivers, were corrupted during the upgrade process, Windows Hello may fail to perform. To fix that, try to update the corresponding drivers:

Right-click on Windows key and select Device Manager.
Locate the Hello, webcam, and fingerprint drivers individually and right-click on each of them.
Select Remove driver software and wait for the removal to be finished.
Repeat the removal with all Windows Hello related drivers and then reboot your PC.
Once done, open Device Manager, click Action -> Scan for hardware changes.
The system should automatically detect and reinstall the drivers.

Signing into A PC with a Microsoft Account question

a. user say:

Okay, there's a lot going on in your question. Let's break it down:

If you can't sign in to your Microsoft account (MSA) that doesn't necessarily mean that there's something wrong with your MSA. The problem may be limited to your own computer, and you may be able to sign in on another device.

Same applies if for some reason your computer is damaged or hacked. It may have nothing to do with your MSA and you may be able to sign in on another computer.

A local account is not a backup to a MSA. They're two separate things. A local account can only be accessed - i.e., you can only sign in to a local account - from the computer on which it was created, whereas a MSA is a cloud account - it exists primarily on the internet - so you can sign in to it from any computer that has internet access.

Even that is not so cut and dried. You can have a local account and store things like documents or pictures in your MSA so you can reach them from any computer. Or you can have a MSA but store all your data on a local computer.

Your MSA doesn't "obviously" need to have Administrator privileges. Local vs. MSA and Standard vs. Administrator are also two separate things. The former is about how you sign in to the account while the latter is about what an account is permitted to do on the local computer.

Ultimately, you can setup your computer any way you like; it's your choice. Microsoft would love it if we used a MSA for as much as possible and they employ the usual carrots and sticks to persuade us to do that. Can you think of why that may be? Hint: Follow the money.

All these different types of account are confusing, that's for sure, so it's a good idea to learn about the distinctions in more depth than I can explain here. The internet is my go-to for doing research.

Until recently, a Google account was the closest thing to a purely cloud account. You could only access your Google account from the internet, and everything you did with your Google account - email, spreadsheets, photos, etc. - had to be done on the internet. When Chromebooks started to become popular, Chromebook users asked for some local processing ability so they could get work done even if they couldn't get on to the internet, for example, if they were in a place with no WiFi signal.

a. user say:

Basically I'm just a bit concerned about what would happen if I lost access to my PC ....

Your protection against that is to make daily backups. It's easy to do and practically effortless, once you know how.

You'll need third-party imaging software - like Macrium Reflect that I use but there are other great programs. An image is an exact copy of your hard drive, compressed into a single file. It takes just minutes for the software to make the image, and just minutes to restore the image. When you restore an image your computer goes back in time to the way it was when the image was made. You can also backup your personal data - documents, music, etc. - either with the image or separately on its own.

Now let's say something goes wrong with your computer and you can't even start your computer. As long as your hardware is not damaged, you can restore a recent image and you're back at work like nothing ever happened. And with a backup of your data, you can easily restore it to your own or any other computer.

Keep in mind that a backup only preserves data on your own computer. If you store data in the cloud - like in a Microsoft account - your data is still there even if your computer goes down, so there's no need to backup data stored in the cloud (but you can if you want to.)

a. user say:

Local account or cloud (Microsoft) account - the choice is yours. However, it's always been a best practice to do your day-to-day computing and store your files in an account with Standard permissions. You only need to sign in to your Administrator account for operations that need to be done with full Administrator permissions.

The above is an example of the 'principle of least privilege' which means that you have an account with the least privileges needed for your role. The most common benefit is that should your account become compromised, the attacker won't be able to gain full control over the computer.
