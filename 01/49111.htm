a. user say:

If you are thinking of using XP encryption, you better be careful...  Be sure to read, understand and follow all the safety rules that come with your power tools (or XP EFS encryption).

Many is the time that somebody will encrypt things and later, if they move their files, replace their HDD, reinstall XP or forget their encryption key there is no good way to recover the encrypted files.

Here are some notes I have regarding encryption including a suggestion from somebody who's name escapes me.


EFS Encryption:
    * You had an XP Pro machine that you use Windows Encrypting File System (EFS) to encrypt some data
    * For some reason you had to re-install Windows and you restored the encrypted files from a backup.

If you have encrypted folders/file and transfer them to a fresh installation of XP (or from a system restore) you can only decrypt them using the encryption key from your original XP installation.
 
If this is the case, the key to decrypt these files was managed by Windows as a Certificate in your Certificate store of your original installation. These certificates are, in turn, encrypted to your Secure ID, your password, and many other things.

This information would be lost on a new XP install for sure and I think likely if you used System Restore.

The easy way to restore access to your files is to restore your certificate from the backup that you dutifully performed according to the paragraph "Why you must back up your certificates" in the article:

"Best practices for the Encrypting File System"

http://support.microsoft.com/kb/223316/en-us

Be sure to read this section:

How to back up your certificate

If you don't have a copy of your certificate, then you must restore your old system from an image-style backup, login, and then extract your certificate.

Otherwise, your data is history.  EFS is quite secure and there are no back doors.

I found this link on the Internet but have no experience with it:
http://www.elcomsoft.com/aefsdr.html

Because the instructions for encryption are often never read, understood or followed or the importance of backing up the certificates is not followed or fully understood, EFS has affectionately been referred to as the "Delayed Trash Can".

Allegedly, you may be able to hire someone that specializes in recovery of encrypted files to try to help you.

Here is another concept for future consideration that I noted from somebody I can't remember who:

Looking forward, consider the freeware "Truecrypt" for your encrypted files.

http://www.truecrypt.org/

I've used it for about ten years and have never lost any encrypted data despite having several Windows OS shot out from under me.  This is because with this program, I maintain the key in the form of a passphrase instead of relying on the OS to maintain the key. It also has the advantage that file names are also hidden/encrypted until the container is mounted as well as working on any version of Windows since Windows 2000.

azam k say:

Hi,

1.    Do you have a laptop or a desktop computer?

2.    Are you referring to internal speaker or an external speaker?


Below are the steps to resolve the issue. Hopefully one of these helps you in removing the echo.

Method 1:
Do you have microphone connected? If so mute the mic and test. It could be a feedback.
a. Right-click on sound icon in the taskbar and select “Recording Devices”.
b. Highlight the microphone and select “Properties”.
c. Go to Levels tab and drag the slider to the extreme left.
d. This mutes the microphone.
By dragging the slider towards right, you may turn on the microphone volume and adjust accordingly.

Method 2:
Test the sound from Control Panel.
a. Here’s how: Right-click on sound icon in the taskbar and select “Playback Devices”.
b. Highlight on the Speaker and select “Properties”.
c. Go to “Advanced” Tab and click on Test. Is there any echo?

Method 3:
You may try disabling audio enhancements if that improves anything.
Here’s how:
a. Open Sound by clicking the Start button, and then clicking Control Panel. In the search box, type sound, and then click Sound.
b. On the Playback tab, click Speakers, and then click Properties.
c. Click the Enhancements tab, and then select the Disable all enhancements check box.
If you don't see an Enhancements tab in the Speakers Properties dialog box, those features are not available for your speakers and this procedure is not applicable to your computer.

Method 4:
If disabling audio enhancements solves the audio problem you're experiencing, this typically indicates that your audio driver needs to be updated. Check to see if an updated driver is available for your computer.
