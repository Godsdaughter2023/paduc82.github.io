arminatorx say:

First of all, you probably got a defective cable, as the standards provided by you for both cables should be sufficient for the Xbox console and most home environments. To clarify some terms for you:

[quote user="LIVEMAGMALIVE"] back to my other question, is there any problem with using a 2-pair vs 4-pair? UTP vs STP? 26AWG vs 24AWG?[/quote]

2-pair vs. 4-pair designates the number of data pair cables. For normal 100Mbit Ethernet 2 pairs are sufficient (green/green-white and orange/orange-white). If you want to go Gigabit speed (and the Xbox does not have a Gigabit network port) you will need 4 pairs of cable (green/green-white, orange/orange-white, blue/blue-white and brown/brown-white).

UTP means "Unshielded Twisted Pair" and STP means "Shielded Twisted Pair". With Unshielded Twisted Pair the cable pairs mentioned above are simply twisted around each other in pairs without any shielding. STP means, that every pair of cables is twisted around each other, and wrapped in shielding foil in pairs. Depending on your environment and other cables near your network cable, other electronic cables can induce noise, impairing the data sent through the cable. To prevent that, several types of shielding can be used. For example: if you have the UTP cable lying near an electrical power cord for example, the electromagnetic field can induce noise into the cable, making a connection more unrelieable. In longer cables it is also possible, that the wire-pairs inside the cable impair each other (crosstalk).

AWG is short for American Wire Gauge. This simply means the diameter of the copper wiring in each of the wire pairs. For your lengths of cabling this makes no difference.

Normally, both cables you mentioned should be more than enough for your home use. But if you want to make extra sure, get a shielded twisted pair cable the next time. As for the other stuff: For the Xbox and the distance you need to run the cable there is no difference between 2- or 4-pair or the diameter of the wires.

taidara say:

As per ArminatorX's answer: 2-pair works with 10BASE-T, 100BASE-TX, or 10/100 ports devices only. For 1000BASE-T Gigabit Ethernet and IEEE 802.3af Power over Ethernet (to deliver power over the data cable) uses all 4-pairs. In strictly terms, Cat 5 cable need to have 4-pair of wires (1/2, 3/6, 4/5, 7/8) to be certified as such.

As for STP, this type is typically not required for homeowners, (like yourself) as most residential systems are not faced with challenges that is generated by EMI/RFI, in contrast to industrial environments etc. The STP cable also require ground connection for proper performance.

Keep us posted!
