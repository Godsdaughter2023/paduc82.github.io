bruce hagen ~ mvp say:

Try a little TLC.

1: Do not archive mail in the Inbox or Sent Items. Create your own user defined folders and move the messages you wish to save to them. Empty Deleted Items folder daily. Although dbx files have a theoretical capacity of 2GB, I recommend about a 300MB max for less chance of corruption.

Information about the maximum file size of the .dbx files that are used by Outlook Express:
http://support.microsoft.com/?kbid=903095

2: After you are done, follow up by compacting your folders manually while working *offline* and do it often.

Click on Outlook Express at the top of the folder tree so no folders are open. Then: File | Work Offline (or double click Working Online in the Status Bar). File | Folder | Compact all folders. Don't touch anything until the compacting is completed.

3: Turn off e-mail scanning in your anti-virus program. It is a redundant layer of protection that eats up CPUs and causes a multitude of problems such as time-outs and account setting changes. Your up-to-date A/V program will continue to protect you sufficiently. For more, see:
http://www.oehelp.com/OETips.aspx#3

For some programs, turning it off is not enough. You may have to uninstall the AV and reinstall in Custom Mode, opting out of e-mail scanning when offered. 

4: Tools | Accounts | Mail | Properties | Advanced - Uncheck: Leave a copy of messages on the server if it is checked. If you need it checked for some reason, at least check to delete after X days.

Occasionally, a large or corrupt e-mail can prevent a download. In that case, go to your server's Webmail site and delete the offending message from there.

Bruce Hagen ~ MS-MVP [Mail]

a. user say:

PnP stands for Plug and Play.

The manufacturer of a PnP device supplies Microsoft with a driver for that device, which Microsoft includes in Windows. When you plug the device into a connector on your computer, Windows automatically installs the manufacturer's driver. There's nothing you have to do to setup the device, so you are free to immediately play with it.

Microsoft introduced PnP with Windows 95. In the early going, PnP didn't always work so well, and people were left with hardware devices that they couldn't use. You never knew if PnP was going to work properly until you tried, so people famously referred to it as plug and pray.

bill smithers say:

Hi,

No; you can not use it on another PC.

OEM preinstalled Licenses are much cheaper to buy than Retail Licenses and that is one of the reasons why they can not be transferred

 

If the Laptop/Computer came with the Windows Operating System preinstalled (Dell, HP, Acer, etc) or is an OEM System Builders License, that Product Key that came with the Laptop/Computer is for the preinstalled OEM License and is non-transferable.

 

The OEM License/Product Key (preinstalled Operating Systems on Dell, HP, etc machines) and OEM System Builders License are tied to the first machine it is installed on and dies when that machine dies.

 

Only Retail Operating System License, that you buy and install yourself, are allowed to be transferred to another machine.

And Retail Licenses can also only be installed on one machine at any particular time.
