a. user say:

You made several mistakes that caused your present problems:

1- You perform your everyday computing from an account with Administrators permissions. Everyday computing should be done from an account with Users permissions, aka a Standard account. An account with Administrators permissions has substantial capability to make changes that affect the entire computer. (That's why a hacker will always try to gain access to an Administrators account.)

And if you run from the built-in Administrator system account, instead of an Administrators user account, your computer is even more messed up than you think.

2- You loaned your computer to someone else and let them run from your Administrators account instead of creating a Standard account for them to use.

a) There's no way to know the full extent of what happened to your computer while that person was using it.

b) Your Administrators account could not have been merged if the person wasn't using it. There's no un-merge function.

3- You deleted keys from the Windows registry without understanding what you were doing.

Here's how to make things right again:

Backup your personal files and do a clean install of Windows 10. Done correctly, a clean install creates a single local user account with Administrators permissions.

Use that Administrators account to create an account with Users permissions, aka a Standard account. That's the account you use for day-to-day computing. If you want, create an additional Users accounts for times when other people will be using your computer. In practice you will almost never need to sign in to your Administrators account.

It goes without saying that you need to password-protect all your user accounts.
