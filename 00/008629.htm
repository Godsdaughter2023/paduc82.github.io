brian tillman [outlook mvp 2007-2019] say:

By following Diane's advice, you'd make it very difficult for someone to recover your deleted items, but with the proper forensic tools, they could still be recovered in part or in full.  A complete full format (not a quick format) that writes every sector of a hard drive is the best way to ensure your data won't be available to someone who gets your computer, but there are forensic tools that may be able to recover data from remapped sectors.  Another good way to protect your PC is with a tool like TrueCrypt.  Someone stealing your hard drive would see only random bits if they were to read the hard drive.

In answer to your second question: exporting doesn't delete anything.  It makes copies.

Why are you using the cable company email services?  All of the cable companies are notorious for receiving spam, since they are cable TV companies and not truly communications or Internet services companies.

There's no reason you need to use most cable supplied email services at all, though a few may require that certain alert messages be sent through their own email service.  I don't use my own and never have, instead using a Microsoft provided email account that I've had for over 15 years, during which I've used AT&T dial-up, DSL and two different cable company's ISP services.

The mistake most people make is assuming that spam should be dealt with on their own systems, when in most cases this should happen at the ISP email servers.  The only reason for anti-spam on the local PC is when the email service is inept, so get rid of the real problem.

Pick your email service provider based upon which services you prefer, such as Microsoft's if you primarily use a Microsoft Account for your Windows 10 PC, since then you'll only need to login once to access both the PC and email, along with saving files to OneDrive.  If you instead use mostly Google services for things like file storage then using their email might make sense, but for most typical Windows 10 PC users the Microsoft Outlook Email account makes the most simple sense.

I can't say what's triggering this (it's not a common error). All I can say (obviously) is that something Outlookis detecting corruptions in these entries.

You could try purging the Exchange cache in Outlook (Ctrl-click the root of the account in the left side pane to get to the properties and from there you can purge the cache: it will force redownload everything).

When you have used Alt-Tab to give the dialog the focus (even if you can't see it), press and release Alt+spacebar, then press the M key.
