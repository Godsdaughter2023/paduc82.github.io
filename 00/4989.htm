I use PowerPoint for this type of scenario all of the time. :-) It looks like you're missing two things:

This is quite easy if you know the trick. I have blog posted about planning across timezones from Outlook 2007. With previous versions of outlook it requires some calculations. Trick is to write down everything to its correct timezone, and don't care what it does in your own timezone.

 

This even works for flights: when an airline gives you a ticket, it will state local departuretime and the length of the flight. If you respect those, it will automatically end correctly in your arrival timezone. So a flight of 9 hours from Amsterdam to Seattle, departing at 13:00, will arive at 22:00 Amsterdam time, which is 13:00 Seattle time.

 

Planning the rest the same way, will work. Only thing you have to do, is to change the timezone on your device (switch from Home to Visiting). All appointments drop into place.

stct80,
Complex formulas can be a real bear to get properly structured and to troubleshoot if they don't work. And there are a few other little gotchas that can "trip up" an unwary user. rod gill say: You're creating a nightmare formula to maintain! I would leave your original formula (that worked) alone and create a new one in another column to add the next level of complexity.
This new formula is easy, assuming formula is in Text1:

IIf([Flag11]=Yes,IIf([Text1]="PastY","Red",IIf([Text1]="Yellow","Red",[Text1])),[Text1])

Always try and break complex formulae into simpler steps. Massively quicker and easier to maintain !
