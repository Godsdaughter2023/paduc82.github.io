The following macro lets you specify the range to copy and the destination.

Sub CopyAndTranspose()
    Dim rngSource As Range
    Dim rngTarget As Range
    On Error GoTo ErrHandler
    Set rngSource = Application.InputBox(Prompt:="Select the range to copy", Type:=8)
    Set rngTarget = Application.InputBox(Prompt:="Select the top left cell of the destination range", Type:=8)
    rngSource.Copy
    rngTarget.Cells(1).PasteSpecial Transpose:=True
ExitHandler:
    Exit Sub
ErrHandler:
    Select Case Err
        Case 424
            MsgBox "You didn't select a range!", vbCritical
        Case 1004
            MsgBox "You can't paste here!", vbCritical
        Case Else
            MsgBox Err.Description, vbCritical
    End Select
    Resume ExitHandler
End Sub

techfreak_ say:

Is is not explained thoroughly in any FAQs. It has been mentioned in several Skype Blogs, notices, the answers Community and Skype Ideas. Original Skype peer to peer history was only recent (a few days). Later this was expanded to 30 days in the cloud. The Skype Classic 7.x client only retrieves up to 30 days of cloud history. Any history beyond 30 days is stored in the main.db file. Any shortcuts in the main.db to cloud history data (like images) can still be retrieved with the older client (to the ~2 year cloud limit). If your main.db file is corrupted some older data may not exist. If it is deleted. only 30 days can be downloaded the next time you sign in. Be aware that file attachments are not included in your ~2 years. They are only retained for 30 days unless you saved them locally.

Using the export tool in the new software*, you should be able to archive or view any older data stored in any main.db file located on your computer. You can also use 3rd party software like SkypeLogViewer[nirsoft.net] to perform this task as well (in text-only form). There is currently no export tool for history cached in the newer Skype clients.

Skype for Windows Desktop 8.x only (not an option in Skype for Windows 10 14.x)

The newer Skype downloads up to 2 years of cloud history. This information was released in late 2017. Use of the software itself backs up this information. Scrolling through my own history on any of my old accounts allows me to retrieve cloud history to about mid April 2017. This lets me know the 2 year mark will be surpassed and overlap around mid April 2019. After that date I expect the history to be 2 years from the current date, for accounts that are 2 years or older. It is already well-known that no export feature currently exists for the new Skype. The only options that exist are the copy and paste functions that are built-in and standard copy and paste.

If you are looking at alternate unofficial methods to obtain/extract data you can see of of my replies mentioning it here:

Skype.db


Unless  you create a method to filter the data, it is difficult to be legible and organized. It may be simpler to await an official solution. I hope the FAQs will be updated when/if they finally release an official archive solution for the new Skype releases.

harry stevens say:

Hi,

Sorry to hear that you've received such notification. When you viewed your session activity, are you referring to this site?

https://account.live.com/Activity

If you have received an email about the recent login, instead of the website above, it is possible that the email that you received may not be from Microsoft. Please ensure that the email sender that has "microsoft" as the domain name. For example, @account.microsoft.com.

If the sender is indeed from Microsoft, and confirmed it in your recent activity, then you might have a big problem.

It's even more so that the hacker was able to get your password, despite severe measures like formatting your system. I'm sorry but I don't think I can help you with investigating how the hacker is able to repeatedly steal your account password.

At that point, I can only advise you to create a new alias for your Microsoft Account. What this means is that you'll be going to rename your Microsoft Account, or disable the email address that you're using to login. But you will still have access to the services associated with the account. This way, if the hacker only knows your email address and password, if they try to login that address, they'll be told that it doesn't exist. But in reality, you just disable its option to be logged in.

To do this:

1. Login your account at : https://account.live.com/names/Manage

2. Click on "Add email" (you can also use a phone number as your login as well btw.)

3. Type in a new name for your Microsoft Account.

4. Once added, it should be on the list of "Account aliases" that you have. Click on "Make Primary" next to the new name.

5. Moving forward, you'll be using this as the login for your Microsoft Account. To stop the hacker from using the old email address:

6. On the same page, click on "Change sign-in preferences".

7. Uncheck the old email address. This way, the old email can no longer be used as the login.

To summarize:

This will create a new alias for your Microsoft Account.

This will disable your old email address as the login for your Microsoft Account.

You won't lose anything during the process.

All services associated to your Microsoft Account is kept.

Emails being sent to the old email address will still be received by the same Microsoft Account.
