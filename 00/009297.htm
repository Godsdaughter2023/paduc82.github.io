joeu2004 say:

First, do not call any phone number or go to any website.  It is very likely a scam.

Second, do not listen to or trust anyone who cannot answer your question directly.  It is a trivial question.

The curly braces around an entire formula in the Formula Bar indicates that it was

You cannot enter the curly braces manually.  Usually, that results in Excel treating the "formula" as text.  That is, Excel does not actually evaluate the formula.

If you edit and re-enter the formula by pressing just Enter, sometimes Excel will display a #VALUE error.

But sometimes, the formula will appear to work.  Be very wary of the latter:  the result might be incorrect, due to an unintended interpretation of the non-array-entered formula.

After any modifications, you must press ctrl+shift+Enter instead of just Enter into to re-array-enter the formula.

-----

PS.... For this particular example, a better formula is:

=SUMIF(H$8:H$27, "+", $F$8:$F$27)

or

=SUMIFS($F$8:$F$27, H$8:H$27, "+")

Those formulas are normally-entered:  just press Enter, as usual.

suzanne s. barnhill say:

Word counts in general are notoriously unreliable. For example, see http://blog.garycorby.com/2009/05/word-count-in-openoffice-writer-and.html. It seems that Word's counting algorithm is not documented anywhere, but clearly it differs from that used by Windows. I believe that Word counts an empty paragraph as a word, and probably also section breaks, hard page breaks, and the like.

 

It may also be that Windows is counting anything between two spaces as a word; for an example of this, see https://bugs.freedesktop.org/show_bug.cgi?id=38983, which describes this behavior in LibreOffice Writer.
