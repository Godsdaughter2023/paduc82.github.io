tcsenter say: In order to expose the entire 4GB RAM to the OS, the system hardware must actually support MORE THAN 4GB address space and implement a feature called memory remapping or memory hoisting.

The Intel 945 chipset can decode 4GB of DRAM but there is no way for the BIOS to expose all 4GB to the OS because the chipset is limited to a maximum 4GB address space and does not support memory remapping.  The lost memory address space is reserved to all your hardware in order to function, and thus cannot be used for addressing RAM (two different things cannot utilize the same physical address).  This is a hard limit of the Intel chipset.

Google "intel 945 chipset, 4GB RAM, limit" and you'll get a ton of hits on this issue.  e.g.  http://bizsupport1.austin.hp.com/bc/docs/support/SupportManual/c00667512/c00667512.pdf

A BIOS update *might* be able to free up an additional hundred megabytes or two, but you'll need a new chipset/mobo/system to utilize any more than ~3.25 GB "ish" RAM.

a. user say:

It's easy enough to scan a typewritten page to your computer, but it will be saved as an image ... as though you took a picture of the page and saved the picture on your computer.

To actually edit a typewritten page, your computer needs to be able to read the words. For that, you'll need what's known as OCR software.

OCR stands for Optical Character Recognition. OCR software is a program that can interpret the words on the page as text instead of an image.

Good quality OCR software can be expensive. It's worth paying the price if you'll be editing hundreds of typewritten pages. Otherwise, there are two ways to go:

If you have an office supply store nearby, they probably offer a service that will do the OCR processing for you. Just give them a USB flash drive and in a few minutes, and for a reasonable price, you'll get your flash drive back with a Word document (or whatever format you prefer.)

Another solution is to get it done online. There are websites on the internet where you can upload your scanned image, have the website do the OCR processing, and download a finished Word (or other format) document. These websites are usually free. Just be sure you're not sending your writing to a spy agency!

It's great that an 86-year old can dabble in OCR. Can you imagine a 26-year old trying to fix a carburetor? Not a chance.
