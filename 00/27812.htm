Hello AY - 2014 and others with his/her problem,

I too have been struggling to solve the problem of Internet Explorer’s (IE11) inability to open PDF files.   I believe I have found the answer to this mystery, especially as the explanation is totally logical and in keeping with my understanding of the way Windows 7 works.

Like AY - 2014, I discovered the problem about 4 days ago, but suspected it began 2 weeks ago when Microsoft updated IE11.  Also, I tried everything that AY - 2014 did in almost the same order, but I soon discovered that the problem was not only with PDF files, but involved the failure of links to other types of files and processes as well.   In short, what at first seemed like a problem with Adobe Reader soon looked more like a problem with IE11

As I began to rummage about in the IE11 add-ons, I discovered that Dell, though supplying me with McAfee's useless "security" software, had not loaded a copy of Java Runtime Environment, a critically needed add-on necessary for the successful performance of many internet sites.    Needless to say, I was sure I had found the answer to our problem.  After all my chasing of dead ends, I was excited to have found a truly logical explanation.  So I spent half a day farting around with the loading and testing of Java.  Still no solution!.  I could not believe it!

Finally, still convinced that this was an add-on problem, I turned to Microsoft's often-helpful internet publications and saw an IE11 feature, new with Win7, and entirely unknown to me:  Enhanced Protected Mode (EPM):  You can find a description here:  

http://windows.microsoft.com/en-us/internet-explorer/manage-add-ons#EPM&ie=ie-11-win-7nternet  This feature can be turned on in IE11 (Tools|Internet Options|Advanced tab|14th item from the bottom of the list).  It will find and notify the user of add-on conflicts and failures.  Furthermore, it sets the status of troublesome add-ons to "Incompatible," making them easily identifiable.  This feature has been a critically needed one for as long as I can remember.  Here, then, is the fix I’m suggesting:

  1. Open IE and find your way to the check box labeled "Enable Enhanced Protected Mode."  (Tools|Internet Options|Advanced tab|14th item from the bottom of the list).
  2. Be sure it is checked.
  3. Note that you must then restart your computer to enable the protection.
  4. Restart.
  5. Go to one of the screens with a link which has been failing.
  6. Click the failing link and watch for the message from EPM.  It should appear at the bottom of your screen in the manner of a download or Active-X message.
  7. The message will identify one or more of the troublesome add-ons, but EPM will disable all that it finds by marking them as "Incompatible."
  8. The link should now be repaired (of course, the link itself was never in need of repair; the problem resulted from incompatibilities among the add-ons).
  9. Return to the IE11 Tools menu and open the list of tools.
  10. Click on "Manage Add-Ons."
  11. Select "All Add-Ons" on the left drop-down under the word "Show."
    12. It is interesting to note that, at least in my case, the principal suspect would have to be the

    Lync Browser Helper by Microsoft dated May 20, 2014.

    Two other add-ons identified as incompatible are:

    CIESpeechBHO Class by Qualcomm dated December 27, 2012, and

    PlusIEEventHelper Class by Zeon Corporation dated February 6, 2009.

    Clearly Microsoft’s add-on is a recent addition, and they may have "poisoned their own well" by installing it during a recent update of IE.

    Though not a part of this particular problem solution, it is important to consider enabling the two most important add-on families:  Oracle's Java and Adobe's Flash, thus avoiding many similar browser problems in the future.  These links are included FYI:

    http://java.com/en/download/manual.jsp/

    http://www.enable-javascript.com/

    http://www.adobe.com/software/flash/about/

    http://get.adobe.com/flashplayer/
