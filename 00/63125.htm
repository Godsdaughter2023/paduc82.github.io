a. user say:

Of course it's not legit.

duggeek say:
I have received an email asking for my password to be reset.. It is asking for personal login info and telling me my account will shut down in 3 days unless I do it. Is this legit? I'm guessing its not but don't know how to stop it


This sounds like a classic "phishing" scenario; the message is an attempt to get you to visit a corrupted website that will take your login details and most likely hijack your account on Hotmail.

 

The "shutdown in 3 days" warning is very likely a way to get you to act desperately, without thinking.

 

Here's some quick 1-2-3 steps for detecting phishing attacks:

  1. Is the sender even real?
    Check that the email actually shows an address (not the alias, but the address beneath the alias) that ends with a sensible source; such as @hotmail.com or @microsoft.com. If you see something completely different, then it's probably fake.
  2. Does the provided link make sense?
    These kinds of messages usually have a button or link, don't click it! Look closely at the link address to see if it goes to a site that makes sense. Most email apps will let you 'hover' on a button or link to view the address. If you see a web address for a site different from Hotmail or Microsoft, then the message is most certainly fake.
  3. Is there really a problem with my account?
    When in doubt, go to your account information using another way. Instead of using the link in the message, use your own bookmark or Favorite for it. In this case, you would just go straight to hotmail.com and log in. Check your account status there. If there's no problem, then the message is positively a big fakity-fake-fake!

Use these same steps for other suspicious emails that appear from time to time, there are a lot of them out there! Just be sure to check the details regarding the account mentioned; lately, there have been some attacks aimed at Facebook and LinkedIn as well. Above all, be sure to use common sense. If it doesn't make sense, then it's probably trying to fake you out.

 

I wanted to add something about reporting these kinds of messages. It does have an effect. Even if it doesn't alert the FBI or bring the National Guard down on the culprit (wouldn't that be great!?) reporting phishing messages should help the webmail (any of them) to filter-out those messages in the future. The effect may not be immediate, but it does count over time.
