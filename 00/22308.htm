john mcghie say:

Hi Laura:

Basically, you shouldn't be trying "layout" in Word.  It's built to prevent it, and it will fight you every inch of the way :-)

Word is a "text flow application" (a word processor!)  Word processors are built to re-flow their text and repaginate their documents whenever a change is made.  You cannot prevent this, you need to deign your layout so that Word can automatically re-flow text and re-paginate the pages and the document will continue to look good (it can be done...)

The secret is to use the properties of styles to help Word decide where you DON'T want a page break.  Don't get caught chasing hard page breaks in Word, it will drive you mad.  Basically, never use hard page breaks.  Use Keep Together and Keep With Next (both properties of paragraph styles) to tell Word where you DON'T want page breaks, then sit back and let Word do it all for you the way it was designed to.

If you use hard page breaks, every time you edit, you will need to move them all.

Don't justify text in Word, it looks disgusting and makes the text very hard to read.

Try to avoid floating objects in Word.  As far as possible set pictures and tables "In line with text" and use their paragraph properties to position them.  Word will then automatically maintain the document for you, and the users will find it easy to work with.  If you want to flow text around objects, hook the text into InDesign and use the tool designed for the job.

Do ALL your formatting with styles.

Accept that you CANNOT pre-format "pages". Word has no concept of a "page" in its files.  Pages are created at print time when Word outputs the file to the printer; the pages are never stored.  So while you can pre-format, you have no control over which page your formatting will print on.

For things such as Front Cover, Front Matter, Table of Contents etc, you "can" use Section Breaks that will cause designated sections to start on the designated side of the page.  Which will achieve the effect: the Front Matter will begin on page iii provided the users do not add any text before the front matter, or provided they know what they are doing when they do so.  Again, set the section breaks up right and Word will paginate correctly for you.

You can also use Protect Document to "lock" some sections so the users cannot change its content.  A "section" can be as small as one character or as large as a whole book.  You can control where you put the section breaks.  You can have an effectively unlimited number of section breaks in a document (32,767). While you can protect the document to lock users out of sections, don't: it causes misery for the users: they will hate it, and they will go out of their way to defeat it.

I never use themes in Word: their effects are very unpredictable and it's pretty much impossible to prevent users from changing them.  All a theme does is enable users to change Fonts and Colours in a document.  But it's a bit of a lottery "which" text will change and which will not, depending on how the user formatted that text.  And you don't WANT the users changing fonts and colours anyway, that's your job!  Do the fonts and colours in the styles of the document.  Don't use a theme.

Professional template users often create special styles: "Heading Base" and "Body Base".  They use these styles to set the colours and fonts.  All other styles inherit from the Base styles.  The Base styles are never applied to text and always hidden.  They enable skilled users to rapidly restore the original formatting to a document users have "fiddled" with.  Things like corporate colours are usually specified in RGB, and can be a bear to re-create if you do not have the RGB values, so put them in the Base styles. Do not use CMYK or Pantone colours in Word, it can't cope: you must use 24-bit RGB.

You have already caught on to using the standard Microsoft fonts: stick to that or you will indeed cause great pain :-)

If you create a custom theme it will be contained in the template you send: you need to send only the template.  Do not attempt to explain this to users: they're not interested.  Don't give them custom theme files, you do not want them changing themes.  Remember: no matter what they do to the Themes, YOU will still get the blame: it will be YOUR product that "doesn't work" :-)

You can send template files from the Mac to the PC, but it is not wise to attempt to bring them back.  The PC adds extra components in its template files that will cause problems if the Mac attempts to edit them.

Word 2007: No.  You won't need that unless you are adding VBA to your template.  Which you could, but do your level best not to: it gives you problems attempting to deploy and train users.  Word 2010 was the bug fix for Word 2007: grab yourself a copy of that and upgrade that PC.  Word 2010 is cheap (it's in the remainder bin...) and it's nicer to use.  Word 2013 is horrid: nasty garish all-white-on-white colour scheme.  Working on it all day makes your head hurt. And I think it's currently still too buggy for professional work.

Stephanie Kreiger's book is not bad: http://www.amazon.com/Office-Document-Designer-Package-Easy/dp/0735620377 or http://www.amazon.com/Documents-Presentations-Workbooks-Microsoft-Noticed/dp/073565199X/ref=pd_sim_b_1/187-9170820-2424769?ie=UTF8&refRID=1VTFKNMC9CB9FA2BT4DE

Stephanie used to be an MVP in here, she's now a Microsoft staffer (an MS employee cannot remain an MVP...).

jacques raubenheimer say:

Update to match selection:

You have some text (which may or may not include paragraph formatting) selected (or have the I-beam positioned in a word, which makes that single word "selected"), and want your style definition to match the formatting of that text rather than having to define the style yourself. Word will then look at the formatting of the text as it is in the document, and build the style definition to match that.

This is a bit of a shortcut, but if you use styles properly (i.e., explicitly), then that means that your formatting will never (except in the case of exceptions) be manually applied to text, but only through styles, so the scope for the use of this tool will be limited then.

Automatically update:

In my opinion, this should always be deselected. It allows no exceptions. Essentially, it says that if you want to make a change to the style, you don't have to open the Modify Style dialog, you can just make the formatting change to the text, and Word will add that change to the style definition. So if, for example, you wanted to make one word in your document bold, and that word was in a Body Text paragraph, then when you make that one word bold, all Body Text words in your whole document will become bold (since they share the same style definition and that definition has been updated to bold). Not a good idea. A possible exception to always deselecting this is for Word's built in heading styles, although even there I prefer having this off.

Style based on:

You are OK with this one, but in my own usage, I see to it that all the styles I used are either based on a sensible parent style (e.g., Body Text Indent is based on Body Text) or based on no style, but I never allow my styles to be based on Normal. This does require a little work, as being based on Normal is the Word default. But the Normal style (because of its generalist purpose), is qualitatively different from all other styles, and so I avoid having it in my documents, with the only exception being for the formatting of tables--table styles don't play nicely with any other paragraph style than Normal.

Style for following paragraph:

What style do you want when you are finished with this paragraph and press Enter (i..e, what style is the next paragraph going to get). I normally set mine to Body Text (and recommend that you use Body Text for the bulk of your document text, rather than Normal). So if I have a Body Text indent paragraph, it makes sense that, normally, the paragraph following that is not going to be indented any more, so I set the style for the following paragraph to Body Text. But let's say I created a style called Dialog (e.g., dialog in a play, script, etc.). It makes sense that after one person has spoken, another will reply, so then the next paragraph should stay in the Dialog style, and so style for following paragraph will be Dialog.

I hope that makes sense.

I have written a whole chapter on Styles which includes more detailed information and with copious screen shots, so you can search my website for information on the book if you want to go into more depth (but, of course, there is no obligation to do so).

suzanne s. barnhill say:

I'll just add a couple of comments to Jacques's excellent explanation:

Automatically update: I agree with Jacques that this can cause more problems than it solves, but there are situations where it can be helpful. When you're first setting up a template and making frequent changes in formatting to get everything exactly right, you may frequently change your mind about the formatting of styles (a little more Spacing Before and a little less Spacing After for Heading 1, bold instead of italic for some other style, and so on). While you're still experimenting, this setting can be a timesaver, provided you remember to turn it off once you're done. It is also the default setting for TOC styles, and this is a big help because accessing TOC styles to modify them is not simple.

Update to match selection: This can be a shortcut to replace "Automatically update." As you experiment with formatting, once you've settled on the formatting you want for a style in a given paragraph, you can tell Word to modify the style to have that formatting. But it can also be a huge help when you have reams of paragraphs in Normal style with direct formatting. Instead of selecting an appropriate style (Block Text, say) and trying to match the formatting in the Normal paragraph, you can just select that paragraph, right click on Block Text, and choose "Update to match selection." Block Text will now be formatted to match the direct-formatted Normal paragraph. Where you can get into trouble with this is heading styles, especially numbered ones, because of the way styles cascade. I got into quite a mess when I decided I wanted all my Heading 2s to be Heading 1s but didn't want to change the formatting. Ouch!

Style based on: What Jacques says about Normal is valid but less important in Word versions in which you can set document defaults instead of modifying the Normal style. For more on this see "The Relationship Between Document Defaults, the Normal Style, and (No Style)."

Style for following paragraph: I usually leave this as the same style except in situations where the next paragraph will always be in a different style. By default, all Word's built-in heading styles have Normal as the following style. I always change this to Body Text. If the document is a printed book, the style following a left-aligned heading will be Body Text, but the style following Body Text will be Body Text First Indent. Sometimes I define alternating styles (such as Q&A styles) where each is the following style for the other. This can be a great timesaver.
