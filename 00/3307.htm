    If IsNull(DLookup("[ProtionID]", "[tblProtion]", "[TeacherID] ='" & strTeacher & _

john w. vinson say: If TeacherID is a Number field, you need to get rid of the ' delimiters:
    If IsNull(DLookup("[ProtionID]", "[tblProtion]", "[TeacherID] =" & strTeacher & _
        " AND [ProtionStartDate] = #" & strDate & "# AND [ProtionStartTime] = #" & _
        strTime & "#")) Then

You do need to be sure that strDate and strTime  do in fact contain valid date/time strings. 

As a rule, it's best to use a single Date/Time field rather than separate StartDate and StartTime fields. Regardless of format, an Access Date/Time value is stored as a number, a count of days and fractions of a day since midnight, December 30, 1899; as such a ProtionStartTime of 8:00am is actually stored as 0.33333333333 and corresponds to #12/30/1899 08:00:00#. Consider combining the two fields into one, it'll make your queries simpler!

teylyn say:

OK, if the S, N, E or W are always just after the number and you are happy to replace them with Find and Replace operation, you can use a formula to split off everything after the first space.

=mid(A1,find(" ",A1)+1,99)

Put that formula into the row of the first address and adjust the A1 to the cell where the address is. Then copy down. Now you see the address without the street number. Copy all the results and paste with Paste Special > Values, so you get only the text, not the formulas. You can then use Find and Replace on that text.

For the OP's benefit, the yellow cells (B1:B4) are where you can input data, and C1 converts the metric Fibre diameter to imperial units with the formula:

=B1/25.4

The number of windings in B6 is the number of times you can wrap the fibre across the traverse, and is given by:

=INT(B2/C1)

I've assumed here that it has to be a whole number. Similarly, the number of layers in B7 is given by:

=INT(((B4-B3)/2)/C1)

That represents the number of thicknesses you can have before the fibre starts to fall off the reel, so again it is a whole number.

I put this formula in E1:

="Length of layer "&ROW(A1)

and copied it down to row 10, although in reality it could be copied much further. Similarly in F1 I had this formula:

=IF(ROW(A1)>B$7,0,B$6*PI()*(B$3+(ROW(A1)*2)*C$1))

and this was also copied down. Originally I had:

=IF(ROW(A1)>B$7,0,B$6*PI()*(B$3+(ROW(A1)*2-1)*C$1))

as this takes the middle of the fibre as the length, but it didn't match the website results as closely.

Finally, this formula is in B9:

=SUM(F:F)*25.4/1000

which converts the length to meters.

So, just copy E1:F1 down as far as you like, to cope with larger flange diameters. You might also like to put some data validation on cell B1 and allow the user to choose the fibre diameter from a pre-defined list elsewhere on the worksheet (as in the website).

Hope this helps.

Pete

I agree with your choice of using the outer diameter of the winding.

I had independently worked out a different solution using the inner diameter.  But I am now convinced that is wrong.  The inner diameter of a winding is more likely to compress than we are likely to stretch the outer diameter.

Putting yours and my ideas together, your algorithm can be reduced to the following closed formula, using your cell assignments:

=B7*(B3+C1*(B7+1))*PI()*B6*25.4/1000

That agrees with your SUM(F:F)*25.4/1000 and the online calculator for the example you chose.

However, the result of your algorithm and my formula, which continue to agree, do not agree with the online calculator for transverse (width) of 4 inch, barrel diameter of 1.5 inch, flange diameter of 5 inch, and fiber diameter of 3 mm.  We get about 120.6 meters; the online calculator gets 130 meters.

I believe B7 (number of layers) should be rounded up, not truncated.  It should okay for the thickness of the fiber to extend above the flange as long as part of it is below the flange.  (Arguably, in reality, it depends on how much is below the flange.)

Even with that change, your algorithm and my formula do not agree with the online calculator; we return about 133.9 meters.  Moreover, with your original parameters, the change in B7 results in about 7.6 meters, which no longer agrees with the online calculator.

Go figure! ;-)

Note:  No one says that the online calculator that I found is correct.  I have not bothered to look for others.  Nevertheless, even though I believe that Pete's design is a reasonable approximation, I wonder if there is a more precise formula that accounts for the fact that the windings do not exactly form concentric circles, and they slightly overlap the previous layer at each end.  Even so, I find it hard to believe that would account for a 9-meter difference for 15 layers of 33 windings of a 3-mm fiber.
