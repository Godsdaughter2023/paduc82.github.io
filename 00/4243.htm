heather ackmann say:

Yes, I am also confused. This might be a misunderstanding in what we are calling a "trigger" or a simpler misupderstanding of how PowerPoint animates objects. But here it goes...

 

1) You don't really "hide" shapes, technically, in PowerPoint. Rather, you tell PowerPoint via an "entrance animation" when to display an object. Or you can tell PowerPoint via an "exti animation" when to "hide" an object, as you put it. The default (i.e. no animation) is that PowerPoint will display an object when the slide is displayed.

So the answer to this question is...you add an entrance animation to the shape. That way, when the slide comes up, the object will not show until you advance the animation (which is another topic in and of itself).

 

2) Which is the correct method to display a shape ONLY on-click of another shape? That would be a trigger.

 

3) As John mentioned, triggers don't really work this way.You can fake it though by adding a transparent shape behind all other objects on the slide and adding a trigger to that shape that reverses all other animations on the slide (again...depending on what exactly you have going on in this slide, this can prove difficult.

 

But please, gonen, do tell us more about what you have going on in this presentation! I'm quite curious!

Many OEM manufacturers provide trial versions of anti-virus programs preinstalled on computers as part of their software package. It is not uncommon for a computer manufacturer to partner or enter into an agreement with a well known anti-virus vendor (Norton, McAfee, etc) to include their product as part of a marketing strategy for generating revenue. Computer advertisements that say they include anti-virus protection is a selling point for many buyers.

Pre-packaged trial security and other software allows the user to receive the benefit of a fully working version for a limited time in order to experience all features and functionality. When the trial period expires, the user will have to either remove the software or purchase the full version in order to keep using the software. Since most folks know very little about computer security and anti-virus software, many tend to keep whatever came with their system when they first purchased it. Thus, the anti-virus vendor recaps the cost of providing the trial version for free. Bottom line...it's all about increasing profits and finding new and creative ways to do so.
