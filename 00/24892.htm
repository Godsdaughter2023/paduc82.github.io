rohn007 say:

Short form.  For the "average user", nothing significant that is "good stuff". 

The most significant change is in the license terms.  Office 2010 was a one payment, yours "forever" license limited to install on 1,2 or 3 computers at the same time (with or without right to transfer to a new computer).  Office 365 is now licensed to a specific email account (user) rather than specific computer. The "subscription" is good for 1 month, 1 year or up to 4 years, then you have to pay again.  The subscription allows the user to install on 1,2 or 5 computers at the same time.  So, if you have 4 or 5 computers at home in use at the same time, then the subscription is cheaper. If you have 1 or 2 computers, then in the long run the subscription will be much more expensive.  Think of it this way.  I have only 1 computer at home.  I paid $150 for Office 2003 (Home and Student). I am still running it. Since then I bought 2007, 2010 and 2013, each for about the same amount, and I am still running ALL 4 versions on my computer. Total cost about $600.  If that had been a 365 type subscription I would have paid $80-100 per year, total $720-$1200. AND, I would no longer have access to the 3 older versions, just 2013!

The second significant change is the switch to "Click-to-run" installation.  Office 2010 installed everything on your local computer.  Office 365 is installed inside of a "virtual computer" on your machine.  The installer file only installs small "stub" programs in this virtual computer. The rest of the programs are downloaded from the internet as required.  MS has lots of silly videos showing 365 installing in stupid situations, parachuting, driving a car, in a boat etc.  But the reality is that 365 requires a good internet connection.  Many people have had very strange, UNSUPPORTED BY MS, installation and running errors that eventually track back to various network connection issues.

Microsoft Office 2013 Click-to-Run virtualization

http://support.microsoft.com/gp/office-2013-click-to-run

Briefly describes CTR virtualization. It includes a list of linked monthly version numbers that provide information on the monthly updates.

One of the alleged benefits of 365/CTR is that MS will automagically update and upgrade the appications for you, no more Windows Update.  Part of this is to theoretically roll out "new features" faster than for the 2010 local install approach.  Local installs only get bug fixes through Windows Update, while new features are typically only rolled out in a "Service Pack".

Office 365 Montly Updates

http://blogs.technet.com/b/office_sustained_engineering/rss.aspx

A large number of people have complained about the "new look" of the Office 2013 ribbon.

Don't forget, that if you are asking about Office 365, you should be considering Office 2016.  2016 will probably be released sometime in the next 6 months or so.  Your Office 365 subscription will automatically be "updated" from 2013 to 2016 at some point shortly after that.

bob jones aka: cybertaz say:

For Normal Text: The Tab & Backspace only increase/decrease indentation of a paragraph which already exists. IOW, Pressing Tab at the start of a new paragraph does not indent the paragraph, it simply tabs in to the first default [.5"] tab stop. With the insertion point at the beginning of the 1st sentence Tab sets a first line indent at the .5" tab stop. If the insertion point is at the start of any other line of the paragraph (or if you select the entire paragraph) pressing Tab sets a left indent [indents the entire paragraph] to the .5" tab stop.

A left indent also can be applied when starting a new paragraph or in an existing paragraph by using the Control+Shift+M keyboard shortcut. That keystroke works regardless of whether the Tabs and backspace set left indent preference is checked.

For Bullets/Numbers: The AutoFormat As You Type boxes should be checked for those feature to enable starting new lists. You still need to type the starting bullet or number followed by Tab or a space, but the indentation doesn't occur until you type the text & press return. Pressing return applies the bullet/numbers formatting. In an existing list, Tab/Backspace demote/promote the item one level at a time.

If not familiar with them you might want to consider using either Outline View or Notebook Layout View. Have a look at a summary of each: Views in Word

Also: About notebook layout view

Or you may want to considerMac App Store - Microsoft OneNote
