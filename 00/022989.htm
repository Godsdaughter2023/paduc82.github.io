Word grammar check does not understand gerunds. How to get Microsoft - Microsoft Community

lisamv66 say:

Word grammar check does not understand gerunds. How to get Microsoft to fix this?

It's bad enough that professional speakers, such as newscasters and politicians, as well as tv ads, use improper grammar on a more regular basis than ever before, but to see that Microsoft doesn't seem to hire professional grammarians when programming Word is really disappointing.  I've put up with Word's failure to recognize correct gerund usage for years. I'm finally calling them out on it and would like to see it fixed.

Here's a good example, taken from a letter I wrote to a client who was waiting for his employment authorization card to arrive:

Usually, the work authorization card is issued approximately 90 days after the documents are filed with the government.   Your working without proper authorization will not prevent you from getting your green card.  The company’s employing someone without authorization could subject them to USCIS and/or DOL penalties and fines.

In the 2nd sentence, the gerund "Your" is the possessive that must be used to modify the gerund "working." (And the 3rd sentence demonstrates correct usage again.)  Many people improperly say, and write, "You working without ...." But that's just wrong.  Word highlighted "Your" for me, trying to tell me that this is a "word error" and suggesting I use "You're." C'mon!!

This is nicely explained at www.getitwrite.com (http://www.getitwriteonline.com/archive/022205posscasegerunds.htm):

A gerund is a verbal--a word that is formed from a verb and retains certain characteristics of a verb (e.g., tenses, the ability to take an object) but no longer functions as that part of speech. A gerund always functions as a noun. It is formed by adding "ing" to the verb.

Because a gerund functions as any other noun does, we must use the possessive case of nouns that modify it. We would write, for example, "My sister’s singing always pleases my father." In this sentence, "singing" is the gerund and "sister’s" is the possessive noun modifying it.

jay freedman say:

I'm not a Microsoft employee, but I have met some of the people in the Natural Languages group. They are indeed professional grammarians. The difficulty is not with their professionalism but with the maddening variability of languages, especially English.

In your example, I expect that the program looks at the beginning of the sentence and matches it with the common error of writing "Your" instead of "You're" in the phrase "You're working".  In that case, the rule you cited wouldn't apply. Of course, it makes hash of the rest of the sentence.

In the third sentence, there's no common error to confuse the interpretation, so the program is forced to consider the rest of the sentence and conclude that it's correct.

The Natural Language folks are always looking for unusual cases, and I'll pass this one along to them.

suzanne s. barnhill say:
To add to what Jay has said, the situation is complicated by the fact that sometimes the noun/pronoun + verbal represents an actual noun and a participle rather than a possessive and gerund.  For example, in sentences such as "I love to see my sister skating" or "I remember my mother reading us bedtime stories," a possessive would be incorrect because "skating" and "reading" are participles. Human beings can (ideally) distinguish, but it's much harder for an algorithm. In some instances, such as "He relied on her doing it," even a human being may be hard put to it to say (without further context) whether "her" is an objective or possessive pronoun.

Thanks a lot for sending this on. This is a tricky one because it’s caused by the Context Sensitive Spellchecker, which flags frequently confused words, and not by a Grammar rule.

The Context Sensitive Spellchecker is based on a statistical model which looks at word sequences and their probability of occurring next to each other. john w. vinson say:

I'd suggest using a Join:

UPDATE [Table one] INNER JOIN query1
ON [Table one].AgentID = query1.AgentID
AND [Table one].PDate = query1.MDate
SET [Table one].[Rent] = [query1].[Rent];

You may need to create a unique Index on the combination of AgentID and PDate in Table1 for this to work.
