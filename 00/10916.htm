albert d. kallal say:

Ah yes, the long time and long living listview. Anyone from VB6, c++, FoxPro, Delphi and even many a Access developers have used in the past.

 

In fact just about anyone using an development platform that supports ActiveX likely at one point for the last 15+ years will have considered using the listview control.

 

http://msdn.microsoft.com/en-us/library/aa443482(v=VS.60).aspx

 

And for sure it is a great idea that you using continues forms in Access an advoding the use of a listview.

 

However, prior to access 2007/2010, you could not display images in a continues form, so listview was likly the top choice here. Today we can with ease in a continues form.  (however we still can not have different sized row text boxes like a listview can).

 

>Does loading a ListView with a recordset that's immediately closed after loading limit connections to the backend?

 

It might, but this does not normally matter. I mean when we close the continues form, the connection is closed also.

 

Also, having 2 or 50 connections open to the back end will not affect performance anyway.

 

So my answer to your question will not help in terms of performance. In other words having MORE connections to a back end does not slow down that back end. It is only if those connections ARE DOING something does the network load increase.

 

I cannot think of any reason why using a listview + recordset would be faster than using a continues form. In fact the continues form in many cases can perform better than the listview since Access is often quite smart about loading records into a form.

 

In fact Access often "pages" and only pulls records as you navigate. Thus it will page in records as you move and not all records necessary have to be pulled from the table like they would with the reocrdset loading up a listview and then closing that reocrdset.

So you might save a connection, but it not going to make any difference in performance because the "existence" of a connection or not.

 

The issue not connections, but what data you're going to transfer over the network, and they are two separate concepts.

Limiting connections will not improve performance unless you limit the data pulled. So 2 or 50 connections will not slow down your application, but needless pulling of data over the network will.
