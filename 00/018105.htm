Your 'Last Step' indicates that the document was corrupt. Corrupt documents can often be 'repaired' by inserting a new, empty, paragraph at the very end, copying everything except that new paragraph to a new document based on the same template (headers & footers may need to be copied separately), closing the old document and saving the new one over it.

Similarly, corrupt tables can often be 'repaired' by converting the tables to text and back again or by saving the document in RTF format, closing the document then re-opening it and re-saving in the doc(x) format. That can result in some unwanted changes, though. The alternative is to cut & paste only the tables to another document that you save, in the RTF format, close & re-open, then copy back to the original document.

Ostensibly:

formatted as Time with subtype of the form hh:mm.

joeu2004 say: Zack wrote:
The reason you get a negative time value is because Excel only goes to 15 digits.  When you take 12:00 minus 7:00 minus 5:00 you don't get zero, but a very, very small negative value.  Excel is smart enough to make this calculation zero, but if you take that very minute value and minus zero, then you do in fact get a negative value.  Not sure why you'd want a calculation like that though.

Actually, the reason for the infinitesimally negative result is because Excel calculation does not "only go to 15 digits" [1].  Note that =ROUND(A3-A2-A4-0,15) results in exactly 00:00.

(But ROUND(...,15) is not a valid way to round time, in general. [2])

And Excel is not "smart" about converting the infinitesimal result to exactly zero [3].  Note that
=IF(A3-A2-A4=0,TRUE) returns FALSE(!) even though =A3-A2-A4 returns exactly 00:00.

As for why someone might do =A3-A2-A4-0, flai's example might be distilled from a more general situation where the formula in A5 is (should be) =B5/24, where B5 is decimal hours, coincidentally zero.

Alternatively, consider the following example:  A2=5:00, A3=13:00, A4=7:00, A5=1:00.  =A3-A2-A4-A5 returns infinitesimally negative time, but =A3-A2-A5-A4 returns exactly 00:00.


-----
[1] Excel uses the computer's 64-bit binary floating-point to represent time as a decimal fraction of a day and to perform arithmetic [4].  Consequently, most times cannot be represented exactly.  Instead, they are represented by the sum of 53 consecutive powers of 2.  Excel arbitrarily limits the input and display of decimal numbers to 15 significant digits.  But the exact internal representation has greater precision.  See footnote [2] for example.  And Excel arithmetic uses the full precision of 64-bit binary floating-point, not just 15 significant digits.

[2] Consider this example.  Enter 13:56 into A1.  Enter =ROUND(A1,15) into A2.  Note that
=IF(A1-A2=0,TRUE) returns FALSE(!), indicating that A1 and A2 differ infinitesimally.  In fact, the exact internal representations are:
13:56                        0.580555555555555,60243163881750660948455333709716796875
ROUND("13:56",15)    0.580555555555556,04652084866756922565400600433349609375
The difference is about 38 picoseconds. :-)

[3] Converting (some) infinitesimal arithmetic results to zero is due to the dubious heuristic poorly described under the misleading title "Example When a Value Reaches Zero" at http://support.microsoft.com/kb/78113.  But the algorithm is designed poorly, and it is implemented inconsistently, which explains the "odd" behavior of the examples above.

[4] Actually, Intel-compatible computers use 80-bit binary floating-point to perform arithmetic.  But Excel converts each pairwise operation to 64-bit binary floating-point.

Zune Battery replacement - Microsoft Community

john c wright say:

Zune Battery replacement

I have an older Zune (30GB), and my Zune battery does not hold a charge very long any more.  How do I get the battery replaced?  What is the cost to do so?
nsilmike say:

John-

Before you try Sara's advice, take a look at what happens on your device. Watch the battery gauge-  does it show charge still remaining when the charge actually runs out?  If so, it's a calibration issue, not a worn out battery.  You can easily calibrate lithium batteries-  1) charge it to full.  2) set it to play and let it go until it dies.  start it, and set it to play again...repeat until it stays on less than a minute or so only.  3) set it to charge and let it charge with absolutely no interruptions until full.  

What you are really doing here is training the charging circuits to correctly recognize empty and full.  It's normal for lithium battery gauges to lose calibration over time- and doesn't indicate any real problem.  (FYI, calibrate it once...if you still have issues, it really is a dying battery.)

Good luck!
