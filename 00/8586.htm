Excel NPER and RATE aren't loan shark friendly! - Microsoft Community

andrea jones - all about resources say:

Excel NPER and RATE aren't loan shark friendly!

In these days of loan sharks and the Wongas of this world charging 3000% plus interest rates it is extremely disappointing that Excel's NPER and RATE functions don't seem to be able to handle annual interest rates greater than 479% (i.e. any interest rate where the rate argument results in a value of 4 or greater).  It is strange because the PV, FV and PMT functions all work fine with interest rates such as 3000% but NPER and RATE fail with a #NUM! error :-(
joeu2004 say: I wrote:
the following example [...] demonstrates that NPER and RATE do indeed work with very large periodic rates, when they are used correctly.
[....]


Consider a loan of 153.00 (E2) at a periodic rate of 5850% (B2) [3] for 5 periods.  B3 is the exact periodic payment returned by PMT() [1].  B5 is the periodic rate returned by RATE().  B7 is the number of periods returned by NPER().  The amortization schedule in D2:E7 demonstrates that the rate in B5 takes the balance down to nearly zero in 5 periods.
[....]
I hope this helps Andrea and Peter.  If not, please provide counter-examples.

I should have said:  please provide real-world counter-examples.

The point I demonstrated previously is:  it is incorrect that RATE and NPER have arbitrary limits, and categorically, they do not work with large rates like 3000% (per Andrea) or with large rates for terms longer than "2 years" (per Peter), which I suspect means:  more than 24 periods.

As I noted in footnote 3 of my previous posting, if you believe those are real-world situations, you probably misunderstand the terms of the loan.  In particular, it is a misunderstanding of the terms of the Wonga APR.

Nevertheless, it is not difficult to find absurd examples.  In the paradigm below, consider a loan of 153 (B1) with a periodic interest rate of 6000% (B3) over a term of 36 payments (B2).



RATE() and NPER() fail, in part at least, because the combination of such numbers in B1:B3 exceed the computational limitations of the computer's 64-bit binary floating-point representation and arithmetic, which Excel relies on.

Ostensibly, the following formulas calculate the computational limit of the term (number of periodic payments; B2) given a periodic rate in B3, or the computational limit of the periodic rate (B3) given the term (B2).



So, if the periodic interest rate is 6000%, the number of periodic payments cannot exceed 8.  Conversely, if the number of periodic payments is 36, the periodic interest rate cannot exceed 177%.

Those formulas ensure that (1+r)^n does not exceed 2^53, so that (1+r)^n-1 is different from (1+r)^n.  That is necessary to ensure that PMT exceeds r*PV (rate*Loan), ignoring signs. [1]

However, take those limits with a grain of salt.  Apparently, there are internal implementation factors that further limit the precision of PMT(); perhaps also NPER().  That causes PMT() to be equal to r*PV when r and n are close to their computational limits, which should make NPER() non-computable; perhaps RATE() as well sometimes.

In part, that is why I arbitrarily round down to integer max n and unit percentage max r.  And that does work with my example of PV = 153 (loan); that is, RATE() and NPER() do not return #NUM errors.

However, that depends on the magnitude of PV.  For example, those max n and max r work for PV <= 1000, but max r does not work for PV >= 10,000.  (Max r must be 1.3% less than the calculated limit.)

Nevertheless, those formulas should get you "close" to the computational limits.  You can experiment downward from there.

-----
[1] [EDIT] The following might help to understand those limits.  From the formula given on the PV help page, we can write:

PMT = -r*PV*(1+r)^n / ((1+r)^n - 1)

assuming FV=0 (to simplify), PMT and PV have opposite signs, and r > -100%.

If (1+r)^n > 2^53, then (1+r)^n - 1 = (1+r)^n due to the limitations of 64-bit binary floating-point.  Thus, PMT = -r*PV.

We can also write:

n = log(PMT / (r*PV+PMT)) / log(1+r)

If PMT = -r*PV, the first term becomes log(PMT/0), which is not computable.

Therefore, we must satisfy the restriction (1+r)^n <= 2^53.  Solving for n and r results in the formulas in B10 and B11 above.
