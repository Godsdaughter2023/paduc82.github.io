The address location is controlled by the position of a frame that is part of the definition of the Envelope Address style. To put it back where it was, do this:

  • Press Ctrl+Alt+Shift+S to open the Styles pane on the right side of the window.
  • If you don't see the Envelope Address style in the list, click the Options button at the bottom right corner and choose "All styles" and "Alphabetical" in the dropdowns.
  • When you can see the Envelope Address style, click its arrow button and choose Modify.
  • In the Modify dialog, first click the option button for "New documents based on this template". That will update the Normal.dotm template for all future envelopes.
  • Click the Format button at the bottom left and choose Frame from the menu.
  • In the Frame dialog, the default settings for the Size section are Width: Exactly, At: 5.5", Height: Exactly, At: 1.38". The default settings for the Horizontal section are Position: Center and Relative to: Page. You can restore these defaults or set values that are better suited to your use (for example, to make the frame wider).
  • Click OK in each dialog. When you exit Word, if you're prompted to save changes to Normal.dotm, say yes.

MS Project - Suggestion for New Type of Task Duration Units - Microsoft Community

stevethetechie say:

MS Project - Suggestion for New Type of Task Duration Units

In MS Project, we have e-durations (edays, ewks, and emons) and we have the standard days, wks, and mons.

edays are understood to be 24 hrs/day with no down time.  

A day is understood to be the number of hours per day set in the MS Project Scheduling options... and that value is used *regardless of the calendar in effect*.

However, there are situations when it makes more sense to have the concept of a day, week, or month tied to whatever calendar is in use.  For example, for a 5 day x 24 hr calendar used for some (but not all) tasks, it may make more sense for a "day" to be defined as 24 hrs *only for the tasks using that calendar*.

Because of this, I would like to suggest the following new duration units:  cdays, cwks, and cmons     These units would be tied to definitions that are *calendar specific*.   For example, for my hypothetical 5x24 calendar mentioned above, I could define a "cday" as 24 hrs/day for any task using that calendar.    Likewise, based on definitions saved with the calendar, cwks and cmons could also be tied to the calendar in use for a specific task.

Comments?  Thoughts?

1 person found this helpful

trevor rabey say:

It is an interesting suggestion.

I have several thoughts on the subject, and you won't like them.

Your suggestion should first start by explaining why the definition is needed in the first place.

This is why. The current method of defining a single conversion factor for an ambiguous duration unit such as day, week and month is there to convert the familiar common use measures into an unambiguous and precise one, ie hours, or minutes or seconds.

An alternative would be to go in the other direction and remove the possibility of inputting the ambiguous measurement unit at all, and forcing the acceptable input to be in hours, or minutes or seconds. But people would not like it.

Currently, MSP uses an overall project calendar, task calendars for individual tasks, and resource calendars for individual resources. Your suggestion would introduce a further additional layer of complexity and achieve no improvement or benefit or payoff.

Of course, you could write your own application, put it on the market and see if anyone buys it. Then gauge the user response for 20 years.

tom_bpc say:

I agree with Trevor – Given their user base, Microsoft has to be very careful with respect to increasing complexity to Project.

The suggested calendar-specific duration conversions are the rule in other scheduling tools that I use every day.  I’ve always been a little irked by Project’s simplistic insistence on a single, project-specific, set of conversions.

In practice, however, it hardly seems to make any difference.  Experienced users will continue to make duration entries in days – avoiding weeks, months, or anything “elapsed.”  They will still have to remember that durations – once entered – are stored in minutes and must always be interpreted with respect to the applicable calendar.  Inexperienced users (and managers) will continue to be confused and frustrated by the consequences of variable calendars on the Gantt chart.  The experienced scheduler will continue to alleviate this confusion by hiding “Duration” and instead presenting a custom “Calendar Duration” field where necessary.
