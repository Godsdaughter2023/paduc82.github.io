What major website(s) were you browsing when these popups occurred? Were these the same each time you've experienced the popups?

Typically these more recent popups scams aren't malware at all, so security applications like Malwarebytes will at best provide a temporary reprieve by deleting the browser popup itself, while they can do little to nothing to stop them from displaying in the future.

This is due to the fact that the popups are typically redirections from some other advertisement being displayed on the web page(s) you were browsing in the moments before the popup was displayed.  In some cases, this initial advertisement might even be purposefully silent, though in most cases the popup will display within seconds after the original ad.

The only truly effective way to stop these or at least reduce their number is to change how you are seen by the advertising networks that display the ads, since your browsing profile is used to select you as a target for their display.  This profile includes to view.

This browser personal behavior (e.g. privacy) monitoring, is a standard operating procedure in today's world of "free" or really advertising-supported news and information websites.  It's something that few consumers understand and the advertising networks as well as the malicious (e.g. popup) advertisers know well and take advantage of to choose their potential scam victims.

What this implies is that the types of browsing behavior you (or others) do on your PC within your account have been found to be susceptible to these scams in the past, so your PC has been chosen as a target to display them in hopes of reeling you into the scam as well.

Of course, this doesn't mean you will actually fall for the scam(s), since that's also dependent upon your gullibility or past experience, it just means your browsing history places you in a group that has been more likely to call the scammers and take the bait in the past.

So from this you can see that the only way to stop these is either to drastically change your browsing behavior by switching websites and what you read or since that's probably not likely, find a way to reduce what the advertising networks are able to learn about you and if possible what they know about you already.

Fortunately, Microsoft provides a way to at least partially manage what both they and the other major tracking firms like Google know about you, both in their browsers and Windows itself.  In general these are called "tracking" and include both older technologies like cookies as well as the newer privacy controls built into Windows 10 or 8.1.

Though I personally have set [3rd-party] tracking cookies to Block and 1st-party cookies (e.g. those from the websites being browsed) to Prompt under the Privacy tab, Advanced button in the Internet Explorer browser, this is a bit extreme, noisy due to popup prompts when accessing a new website and thus not something most consumers wish to deal with.

Delete and manage cookies - Windows Help

On the other hand, the Windows 10/8.1 privacy setting for this is titled; Let apps use my advertising ID for experiences across apps.  As stated next to this setting, "turning this off will reset your ID".  This has the desirable effect of immediately changing what the ad networks know about you, so it may cause the popups to stop or at least reduce greatly almost right away.

General privacy settings in Windows 10 – Microsoft privacy

The other selection in the Edge browser that's not really difficult and may help is to set the Send Do Not Track requests selection to on in View advanced settings.  This setting is found in the Security section near the bottom of the Advanced tab in Internet Explorer.

The following article discusses these settings and more as they relate to the Edge browser specifically, while for other 3rd-party browsers you'd need to look for what's available on their own support websites.

Microsoft Edge, browsing data, and privacy – Microsoft privacy

Note that though these privacy settings are voluntary and so limited by the website's willingness to accept your requests, in most cases the major websites like Microsoft and Yahoo will honor them, which has the effect of blocking most advertising networks from accessing your browsing history.

The privacy setting for the advertising ID tends to block their ability to track entirely, though it's possible for some such as Google to bypass this using their own tracking methods which are more difficult to block.

rob koch say:

Actually Don, after handling literally hundreds of these during extreme browser testing over the last few years, I've seen only about one in a hundred I couldn't more cleanly shut down using the following, which also totally avoids the issue with the ad returning the next time the browser is opened.

In order to close the often multiple windows these popups typically create, just play with a combination of the Alt-F4 keys and clicking either the red "X" close button in the top right-hand corner or the Cancel button usually displayed in the lower portion of a dialog box.

The only trick here is to determine which window(s) need to be closed by clicking the buttons with the mouse and which (almost always the background window) via the Alt-F4 keys.

I know this sounds complicated, but once you learn how it quickly becomes second nature and as I mentioned, almost always works and once it does, is virtually guaranteed not to return once the browser is restarted.

I just place the mouse pointer over the button I want to click and position the fingers of my other hand over the Alt-F4 keys, then click the mouse followed as closely as possible by the Alt-F4 keys together.  Usually you can be pressing the Alt key before clicking as well, which then only requires a quick press of F4 in order to close the background window.

If the option dialog to either "Close All" or "Close Current" is displayed, only the current button typically needs to be clicked for the offending background window to be forced to close.

As I mentioned, this sounds complicated and though it really isn't, is a bit easy to fumble the order or press the wrong key(s) the first few times you try.  However, eventually you'll get the sequence right and then closing these in the future becomes second nature and far easier than crashing the browser, since in most cases only two or at most 3 windows or other popup dialogs need to be closed.

The browser crash method will usually work, but often requires additional tricks to restart the browser properly and also has the undesired side effect of crashing anything else you might have had open in the browser as well.  So once this better technique is learned, you'll typically be back to browsing within a few seconds.

Only in a rare case is the browser so locked up by the offending popups that nothing will close them, with the common symptom that either clicking or pressing keys causes the characteristic "beeping" sound that indicates the keyboard buffer is full, in which case even using Task Manager doesn't always work.  At this point the browser itself is badly hung and only rarely will it return from this condition within a reasonable time frame if left alone.
