bob jones aka: cybertaz say:
Word doesn't compress images automatically. In fact, Word 2008 doesn't even have an image compression feature that you can impose.

My understanding of what's happening is based on the fact that Word is designed to be a powerful word processing program for the production of printed documents. It's intended to handle large volumes of text-related content as efficiently as possible. Images are one of the most detrimental elements that can be inserted into a document.

Although high quality images can be stored in a document they are not fully translated to the screen in order to maintain speed. The full quality is output when printed, but a low resolution preview is displayed on screen while working while viewing/editing the document. Copying captures only the low res preview, not the image file itself. That usually results in a less than desirable version when pasted elsewhere because all of the image file data isn't copied.

Additionally, copying involves the OS X Clipboard which causes a conversion of the image. It's one of the most certain ways to degrade an image... It's a convenience provided for saving time & effort at the cost of quality.

I don't mean to insult your boss :-) but using a Word document as a repository for images -- especially if fidelity is a concern - really isn't an effective approach. Perhaps s/he'll reconsider :-) Most email software have an Insert> Picture feature as well as a provision for creating Signatures that include images.

jim gordon mvp say:
Before I comment about Word, I have to preface my comments with my opinion about the idea of distributing scans of signatures. I can't think of a more open invitation to fraud. A scanned signature can be affixed to ANY document that says ANYTHING, including contracts and forms, at the whim of anyone and everyone.

Now about pictures in Word. You can always get at the original picture contained within a Word document. Make a copy first, then change the file extension from .docx to .zip, Double-click the zip file and then navigate the resulting folder to the Media folder. Your original pictures are there.

Word displays a rendition of the original picture on the screen. The rendition may have effects applied, been scaled smaller, be recolored, etc. It is the rendition that gets copied to the clipboard, not the original picture.  So even if you had a high quality large picture, what you copy to the clipboard is what is displayed in the Word document, not the original, even though the original is stored within the .docx file.
