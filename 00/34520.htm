    AVG PC Tuneup and other "Tuneup" Software - Microsoft Community

    j. schmid say:

    AVG PC Tuneup and other "Tuneup" Software

    AVG, a reliable free antivirus company, is known for its amply reliable virus and spyware protection for the casual home user.  Unfortunately, its program "AVG PC Tuneup" will forever tarnish the name.  AVG PC Tuneup and many similar "tuneup" programs claim to speed up your PC by finding such things as registry errors, unused shortcuts, "junk files,"fragmentation and the like: subsequently removing them from your computer and saving you the advertised "massive quantities of RAM and storage."  Although this intuitively makes sense, these programs are far from a tuneup in Windows 8.  They indeed find the advertised files and after seeing the thousands of initially scanned files, tuneups scare anyone (including me) into thinking their PC may melt down at any moment and that premium performance parades only one click away.   However after recently running AVG PC Tuneup on my PC and allowing it to "clean" approximately 3-6000 shortcuts it found, I quickly found myself in a world of hurt.  Within those files were all the links between nearly all programs available in the search bar (add remove programs, setting and yes, even system restore) which cripples the user from accessing even the most basic of Windows 8 functionality.  Luckily, the Microsoft system restore, still accessible through the command prompt, saved the day and I was able to reboot my surface to a date prior to these "improvements."  In sum, most all "tuneup programs," particularly AVG PC Tuneup, are best left where they are, online and not on your PC.  If your performance has slowed, try running some of the common antivirus and more reputed anti-Malware programs out there.  Your PC, despite any advertising assuring you of some impending doom, does not need "tuned up" any more than a surgeon needs an accordion.  The "Defragmentation" tool (accessible through your search bar) does a fine job of cleaning up the hard drive.  If you should find yourself gripped in the clutches of a tuneup, a simple system restore can save the day.  I hope this helps whoever may make the same mistake I did.  Happy computing!

    J. Schmid

    a. user say:

    Hello Eleanore, 

    Thank you for posting your query in Microsoft Community.

    If your printer produces blurry, faint, or incomplete printouts, it might be low on ink or toner. Many printers provide status about ink or toner levels directly on the device. To find out, refer to your owner's manual or go to the printer manufacturer's website.

    Some printers can inform Windows about how much ink or toner is remaining. Here's how to check:

    1.   Open Devices and Printers by clicking the Start button, type Devices and Printers and select same from the list.

    2.   Click a printer.

    If the printer is designed to provide ink or toner status, the information should appear in the status area at the bottom of Devices and Printers. You can also double-click a printer to see more information about that printer.

    If your ink or toner cartridge needs to be replaced, check your owner's manual or go to the printer manufacturer's website. The company that made your printer knows the best way to change an ink or toner cartridge for your specific model.

    Hope this will address your query.

    philberthfz say:

    I am sorry that this response did not get to you sooner, you have probably already solved this problem or moved on. For anybody else who has had this sort of problem, what has happened is that Windows 10 automatically installed a basic driver for the printer. It's good for printing... and not much else.  The recommended solution is to go to the printer manufacturer's website and get the latest version of the driver for your printer from them. Typically, this includes a bunch of bloatware you probably don't care about ("Click here to order more ink!" "Register your printer!" "Click here to order prints because trying to actually print photo quality pictures on a home printer is stupid expensive!"), but - crucially - it includes a driver that actually monitors ink levels.

    Typically, printer manufactures do not include this feature in the basic driver that's given to Windows Update for automatic install. Why? So you install the full version with all the extra monitoring crap and built-in advertising.
