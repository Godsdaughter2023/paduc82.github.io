muleridersi say: Thank you, fumei2.  I am attempting to format a novel in MS Word for conversion to Kindle. macropod say:
You can not have a pilcrow in the middle of a paragraph. By DEFINITION a pilcrow is the terminating (last) character of a paragraph.


Actually, you can... In Windows, a paragraph break consists of two characters - 0D 0A (ASCII 13 & 11). Text imported from non-Windows apps sometimes have just the 0D (ASCII 13) , which looks all the world like a paragraph break in Word but isn't. Then, of course, there's the actual pilcrow character: ¶ (ASCII 182).

To delete them you can use Find/Replace, where:

Find = ^13 or ^0182

Replace = nothing or, perhaps, a space

Don't use 'Replace All' with Find = ^13, though, or you'll delete all intended paragraph breaks.

First, you need to enable the Developer tab in Word. I assume you have already done that or you wouldn't be able to look at the code.

To insert a plain text Content Control,
  - in the body of the document, click where you want to put the Control
  - in the Developer tab, Controls group, click the second icon from the left on the top row
  - click Properties, and 
      - in the Title field, type the name of the control that you want to appear in the document when the control is selected.
      - in the Tag field, type the name of the Excel column as it is retrieved by the ADO Recordset. That will normally be exactly as it appears in Excel, but there may be circumstances in which ADO "mangles" the names (as MailMerge does). Ideally, keep the names short and simple.
      - check "Content Control cannot be deleted" (optional, but can save you some grief).

rohn007 say:
If it contains the characters "OEM" in it, it is almost certainly the Product ID. Which is useless for activation. The Product ID is found in the Help about dialog.

If the number you have is the Product ID, the letters "OEM" in the second group of characters indicates that it was an OEM license, installed and activated at the factory.  That introduces another problem. Normally I would provide you with tools to extract the product key from the HD. But if it is an OEM installation, that product key won't work for you now. It only works when connected to the factory network.

If it is an OEM license, then what your friend needs to find is the paperwork that came with the computer. He needs to find the "Certificate of Authenticity" or some similar paperwork with the Product Key.

The the sticker on the bottom of the laptop/computer is rarely the Product Key for Office. If you look closely, you will probably see that it only mentions a Windows version, nothing about Office. Years ago, "came with" installations had a second "Product Key" sticker on the computer, one for Windows and one for Office, but MS seems to have gotten away from that. For OEM purchased installation you will get some sort of “paperwork” as proof of purchase. Such as a “Certificate of Authenticity”
