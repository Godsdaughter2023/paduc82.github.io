tom ogilvy say:

formulas don't remove data.  Formulas return a value to the cell in which they are located.

You can have a cell on another sheet reference the cell where you scanned the data, but the formula references that cell.  If you change the data in that cell, then the formula will change likewise.

So I could see using formulas on another sheet if you are going to keep the values scanned in in the original sheet. 

That said, it sounds like you want to see a value that is always associated with one of the scanned in values.  For example, an employee ID and the employee's name.

You could have a table somewhere in the workbook that has that data = ID and Name.  Then you could use a lookup type function in the second sheet that references the Employee ID scanned in and retrieve the corresponding employee name or other information if  it is in the table based on the employee ID.

PC Build 16226:  Install Fails Due to .NET 3.5 Migration

 

Hello Insiders!

Today we have released build 16226 for PC to Insiders in the Fast ring.  As part of this release, there’s a known issue that will affect some users during installation.  Please read below for additional details:

Issue Details

Who:                     Insiders installing build 16226 to their PC

Issue:                    Your PC may fail the install during the SafeOS phase of the install (“Working on Updates” between 0% and 30%) and roll back to your currently installed build

Why:                     There is a known issue with the .NET 3.5 migration that causes the new OS installation to fail.  This issue only affects build 16226.

What to do:        There are no lasting effects from this issue.  Please see the workaround steps below to bypass and re-try the update

 

Workaround Steps

Before attempting to install build 16226, or if you have .NET 3.5 installed and OS build 16226 install fails, complete the following steps:

(If you have not yet attempted to install 16226, skip to step 3)

  1. Wait for your PC to successfully roll back to your current build
  2. Log into your machine
  3. Open Control Panel
  4. Select Programs
  5. Under “Programs and Features” select Turn Windows features on or off
  6. Un-select the box next to .NET Framework 3.5 (includes .NET 2.0 and 3.0)



  7. Click OK
  8. Allow Windows to complete the necessary removal operations
  9. Reboot your PC
  10. Navigate to Settings > Update & Security > Windows Update and attempt to install 16226.

 

Build 16226 should install successfully.  Once the install is completed, please return to step 3 and re-install the .NET 3.5 feature.  Reboot your PC one final time and you should now be updated and ready to go.  If you encounter any issues, please post up and let us know!

 

As an additional note:  the underlying bug for this issue is understood and we are expecting to have a fix in place prior to the next build release for Insiders.

john - project say:
Pug,
Hopefully you will get more than one reply/viewpoint but here is mine.

First, why did your account manager "pad" the proposal? Was it simply the difference between cost and price? In other words, does the extra $15,000 represent cost + overhead + benefits + general and accounting + profit? Or perhaps part of the $15,000 is for those items and part is reserve to address unknown unknowns, although this doesn't sound like a development program where there will be any unknowns.

Second, don't try to do your financial accounting in Project. If the program was sold at $30,000 then that's what should be represented in the Project plan. Your actual costs, (i.e. the real actual costs, not those used for EV in Project), should be handled by your accounting application, totally private and not exposed to the customer. This is normal and accepted business practice.

Third, I think its pretty unusual for you to plan, schedule and try to control effort to be performed by the customer. Since your effort is somewhat dependent on the work they do, you should track the effort, but in my opinion, their effort should not be in your plan as any kind of earned effort. I mean, is your company going to pay your customer back for their effort? No.

If you set up the plan to reflect the negotiated contract price and keep customer effort out of the plan, except for effort duration with $0, then the earned value metrics and reporting are all easy and open for disclosure to your customer. Keep in mind that it is perfectly acceptable to take out a reserve (e.g. 10%) for contingency, (i.e. the total plan budget is $27,000 with $3,000) in reserve), as long as your customer understands what you are doing and why.

John

dr. paul dgiammalvo say:

Hi Pug,
As a CONTRACTOR, you are doing earned value EXACTLY the way it should be done.

You have quoted the client your SELLING price and when you submit your earned value report, along with your monthly billing, it should reflect the physical % complete X your selling price. (your BCWP and the clients ACWP) Unless it is a cost plus contract, you are under no obligation to them to show them your (contractors) actual cost of work performed. (ACWP)  Under a firm fixed price or even LoE contract that is none of their business.

Now, when you calculate your own INTERNAL ACWP, then you will find out if you are over budget against YOUR plan (as the contractor) or not, but unless it is cost plus, you are under no legal or moral obligation to disclose that to your client.

When calculating the BCWP for owner/client tasks, you can assign an arbitrary but "reasonable" value for that work.  I am coming from a background in construction, so I use the cost information published resources, such as RS Means or Richardsons whenever including owner activities on my schedule.  Just be sure that when you do your own internal reporting that you deduct or otherwise account for work you are not responsible for, or your analysis will surely be wrong.

The fact that you think you can finish it in 30 and not 40 days means nothing, EXCEPT that it may mess up the plans for the owner activities.  So as you get "ahead of schedule" you need to be sure to communicate to the client that the work is proceeding FASTER than planned so they are able to plan their activities accordingly.  One way to so this VERY effectively is to show BOTH the early and late date curves.  This is a "best practice" and while it is a little bit more work (for small project like this I don't bother with MSP or P6, I only use Excel) it will say a lot about your professionalism.
