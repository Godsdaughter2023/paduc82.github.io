ken sheridan say: It sounds like you have a many-to-many relationship type between Invoices and Contracts, i.e. each invoice could be paid from one or more contracts, and each contract could pay for one or more invoices.  For this you need a third table, InvoicePayments say, with two foreign key columns, InvoiceID and ContractID referencing the primary keys of the two referenced table respectively, and an Amount column to record the amount paid in each case,

There would now be no ContractID foreign key column in invoices as the relationship type is modelled by the InvoicePayments  table.  This type of table is sometimes known colloquially as a 'junction' table.

By joining the three tables in a query you can return the payments for each invoice/contact.  You can also return the balances per contact at any time by summing the payments per contract and subtracting this from the contact provision.  This type of information would normally be presented in reports based on the queries.

You'll find an example of a basic many-to-many relationship type and how it can be represented in a form/subform as ParentActivities.zip in my public databases folder at:

https://skydrive.live.com/?cid=44CC60D7FEA42912&id=44CC60D7FEA42912!169

While the subforms in this little demo file include only the one control, a combo box, in your case there would also be an amount text box in a contracts subform within an invoices form, or conversely an invoices subform within a contacts form.

john w. vinson say:

Ken's suggested third table is how Access (or any relational database!) implements many to many relationships. The table is related one-to-many to Contracts, and also related one-to-many to Invoices; between them these give you a many to many relationship. Each record in the third table will refer to one contract and one invoice associated with that contract; if one invoice references three contracts, there'd be three records in the table for that invoice. Similarly, if one contract had eight invoices, there'd be eight records all with the same contractID but different invoices.

john w. vinson say:

No, it won't automagically populate! Think about it: if you had 50 Contracts and 120 Invoices, there would be 6000 possible combinations. Access has no way to know which invoice goes with which contract - that's your job, to tell it (by filling in the third table). Typically you would use a Form based on one of the "one" side tables, with a Subform based on the junction table; i.e. if you want to assign multiple invoices to a Contract, you'ld have a Form based on Contracts, a subform based on the junction table, and on the subform a Combo Box based on Invoices to let you assign an invoice to that contract.

ken sheridan say:

is there a way to designate a "many to many" relationship?



Did you take a look at my ParentActivities demo to which I posted a link?  If so, open its relationships window and you'll see how the third table resolves the many-to-many relationship type between the two referenced tables into two one-to-many relationship types.  This is how a many-to-many relationship type is always modelled.

For data entry you use a form based on one of the referenced tables, either Invoices or Contacts in your case, and within it a subform based on the third table.  My demo has two forms one for activities by parent, the other for parents by activity.  Model your forms on these and add a text box to the subform for the amount, in addition to a combo box for the contract or invoice, depending on which table the parent form is bound to.  The linking mechanism between the form and subform automatically inserts the correct value in to one column in the 'junction' table, the other values are inserted when you select and item in the combo box and , in your case, enter a value into the amount text box.

That describes a common situation: windows are opening off-screen or on another monitor.  In either case they can be moved back into view on the current monitor using keyboard shortcuts.  E.g. if on a different monitor press Win+Shift+CursorRight.  More generally you can use the thumbnail's right-click menu's Move command and (important) a CursorRight keystroke to engage your mouse pointer in a move of that window; then just drag it into view.

I keep hoping that the Task View (Win+Tab) will have its command repertoire enhanced to help us move windows to another monitor.  Then Win+Tab and right-click on the Task View thumbnail might be a more intuitive procedure than the arcane ones given above (especially the oldest one which involves the necessary trick of using a cursor key to engage the mouse pointer in a move operation.)

I understand that your question is related to the fact that you are not able to locate your phone with Find My Phone option available after signing in to the following website:

http://www.windowsphone.com/

 

If the phone is not visible it means that:

- The phone has not been properly added when Microsoft Account was added to the phone

- You are checking wrong Microsoft Account

- The phone has been reset and the Microsoft Account on the phone has been removed from the phone

If one of the above is true then the Find My Phone function will not be working.

In order for Find My Phone to be working the following is required:

- The phone must be visible on www.windowsphone.com

- The options 'Always use push notifications (not SMS) to send commands and apps to my phone' as well as 'Save my phone's location periodically and before the battery runs out to make it easier to find' need to be selected on the phone.

- The phone has to have Internet access or mobile network access

- The device needs to be charged and turned on.

Besides, if you have IMEI number of your phone (you can usually find it on the box or on the purchase agreement or at the place of purchase), then you can contact the police and they might be able to help you find the phone.

https://account.live.com
