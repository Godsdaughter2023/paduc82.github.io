steven. s say:

Hey Casey Starrenburg,

 

Mixed content occurs when non secure (http://) items or data is transferred along with secured (https://) items or data. For instance, you log into your bank account and the webpage is displayed. The bank’s logo is displayed at the top of the page, but is displayed by using http:// instead of https://.

 

Most instances of mixed content aren’t harmful or potentially malicious, but there is the possibility that your data may get sent over a http:// instead of https:// depending on how the website is designed.

 

It is worth noting that if you were set to prompt and choose to not display mixed content or if you disabled all mixed content you wouldn’t see the http:// items. The prompt or enable doesn’t prevent the transferring over http://, but will prevent the display of the http:// elements on a https:// website or vice versa.

 

Ultimately it becomes a choice of convenience or protection. You could have a mixture of both by adding the sites you trust to your trusted sites and changed the settings only in the trusted sites so that normal sites display with a prompt or disable to mixed content, but those choices are up to you.
