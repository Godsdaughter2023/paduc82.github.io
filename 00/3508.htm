The redunancy of "BuildGuid" Hash address in the Registry File - Microsoft Community

johnbatie say:

The redunancy of "BuildGuid" Hash address in the Registry File

If the "BuildGuid" address format is not changed, this will continue to cause problems, in the future. The "BuildGuid" Address should have a unique address format for each  "Operation System - OS." The uniqueness of the hash address makes it possible for each "OS" such as "Win 7 Start, Win 7 Pro, Win 7 Ultimate, Win 7 Home premium, and Win Enterprise" in the past to support Changes and Upgrades to the various "OS". This uniqueness was carried over  from Windows XP, Window Server 2003, and Windows Server 2008 R2." The present Hash address does not support the uniqueness for the present and the on going Systems. The present address format - "ffffffff-ffff-ffff-ffff-ffffffffffff" is there to address a 32bit /64bit format.

john w. vinson say:

Access is not "Excel on Steroids". It's quite different in its storage and logic! Applying Excel techniques (as in this case) will be an exercise in frustration!

That said, Access does AND and OR logic, actually IMO better than Excel does: try this query (copy and paste it into the SQL window of a new Query):

SELECT * FROM yourtable WHERE [Product Code] <> 111 AND [Product Category] IN ("Hat", "Cap")

The real solution, though, is to correctly normalize your tables, If the [Product Category] value depends on the [Product Code] you really should have a ProductCodes table with the Product Code as the Primary Key, and just store the Product Code without the Category in your SKU table; use a Query to link to the ProductCodes table to look up what a 111 should be. You're using a relational database, not a spreadsheet; life will be much easier if you use it as such!

spewhole say:

Hi Veeshun,

 

Do you spend a lot of time browsing the web like me?  You should consider changing the history retention setting from 20 days to something less.  Try 10 days to start with.  I just had to do the same thing.  Basically, the amount of sites I am visiting is simply too much in the span of 20 days to not reach the cap of 16MB for the index.dat.  Browsing some 350+ sites a day fills up that history index in no time.  :-)  And once you hit that 16 MB limit, nothing else is recorded until there is more space.  So I've been reaching my 16 MB cap before the 20 days is up and noticing my history wasn't recording because of it.  And then each night at midnight, another days worth of history is deleted, so when you start browsing after that point, you'll see a few of your sites until the limit is reached again.  That's part of why the problem is so hard to pinpoint.

 

I know it seems awesome to be able to track your own internet history back to 20 days, but ask yourself how often do you really use this feature?  If it's vital, you could start backing up your history. There's probably a program or way of doing that automatically.

 

But as for me, I'm just going to set it to ten days instead of twenty as long as I plan on browsing this much. It stands to reason that the less days of history you are storing, the smaller your index, and the less chance you have of reaching the limitation of 16 MB.  I hope this helps.

 

Best Regards

spewhole say: Further, 16 MB is not a lot of space at all for massive indexing.  That's less than 1 MB to index history per day.  At a mimimum, each history link is 4 KB if your hard drive is NTFS and larger than 2 GB.  That means that if you view 4,000 pages in less than 20 days, you've already reached your limit!  A good rule of thumb would be to set your history retention days less than the amount of time it takes you to view 4,000 pages. Good luck and happy browsing.
