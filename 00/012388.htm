dennis zac say:

To assist you with your concern, we suggest you perform a hard reset on your Windows Phone. The hard reset will bring your phone back to factory settings, the same state as when the device was first turned on. All apps, messages, media, personal settings, etc will be removed from the phone. Then, turn on your backup under Settings so you can restore your apps and settings after performing a hard reset (as long as you initialize the phone with the same Microsoft Account). Note that resetting your device will not remove any OS or firmware updates. You will remain on the same version of Windows Phone after the reset.

If you can turn on your device you can reset your phone under Settings: Settings  > about > reset your phone
This procedure is described in details in this official how-to article.

If your phone is unresponsive and you can't reach Settings , you can reset your phone using the hardware buttons:

  1. Press and hold the volume down and Power buttons at the same time until you feel a vibration (about 10–15 seconds).

  2. When you feel the vibration, release the buttons, and then immediately press and hold the volume down button until you see a large exclamation mark.

Why the ""s? - Microsoft Community

coachrobmd say:

Why the ""s?

So, I have a formula:

=SUMIFS(O27:O28,M27:M28,"2",N27:N28,"alp") and each criteria (number and text) needs ""s around it.

But here, a regular SUMIF (vs SUMIFS):

=SUMIF(M21:M22,">0") the ""s are required as well.  

(M21:M22,>0) doesn't work because it lacks ""s; it returns an ERROR message)

 

But here,

=IF(COUNTIF(I44,5),TRUE,FALSE)  the criteria 5 does not need the ""s.

 

So, please tell me, why do some fxns need ""s around their criteria (numbers AND text), and others do not. In this example, the SUMIF/SUMIFS fxns?

 

It's impossible for me to memorize all 10,000 fxns and which need ""s, and which don't. Is there a clue somebody can give me? A list? A # shouldn't require quotes unless it's text and not a numeric value (nominal vs. ordinal #'s). Does this make sense?

BTW: I didn't make some words blue and other words black; the forum did that

rick rothstein, mvp - excel say:

Let me make this question it's most basic:

(A) =SUMIF(M21:M22,">3")

(B) =SUMIF(M21:M22,>3)

If you were relatively new to Excel, which would you think searches for values >0? The one with quotes (A) or without them (B)?

If you answered (A), I think you're lying, because like most people (other than MVPs of course) associate ""s with text. Honestly, you DO.

So, again and in English, please explain why the ""s are needed around >3, and just plain >3 won't suffice. (We all the time use if(B3>3,TRUE,FALSE) without quotes, do we not?) I've read other ways of accomplishing these tasks in some responses, but that's not the question which is simply this:

Why are ""s needed when the criteria is a # value and not a text value? Please, no more formulas, just words to help me make sense of this. Otherwise, I have to memorize more things than I care to do.

Okay, I think I see what you are asking. The problem has to do with the intuitiveness of the human brain versus the rote exactness required by the computer. Let's go look at your last example...

=SUMIF(M21:M22,">3")

You wanted to know why ">3" as opposed to just >3. The answer is to Excel, the > symbol is a comparison operator... when it sees that, out in the open (without quote marks surrounding it in anyway) it tries to do a comparison between two values... but you have only one value, the 3. So, in order to get the expression into the function so it can compare the values in the first argument, the writers of the function decided to take the argument in as text. Doing that shields operators from the Excel expression parser so that it does not try to compare anything. Okay, so now you want to know about something like this...

=SUMIF(M21:M22,99)

If you don't provide a comparison operator, the SUMIF function just assumes you will want to test for equality... its a convenience for the user that the writer of the function built in. The expression is identical to this one (where the quote marks are required)...

SUMIF(M21:M22,"=99")

NOTE: Please mark the message or messages (yes, you can mark more than one) that answer your question as the "Answer" so that others will know your question has been resolved.

ronrosenfeld say:

On Wed, 29 Sep 2010 14:43:51 +0000, coachrobmd wrote:


So, please tell me, why do some fxns need ""s around their criteria (numbers AND text), and others do not. In this example, the SUMIF/SUMIFS fxns?

I think I see where you are getting confused.

Numbers do NOT need ""s around them.

Text and operators do.

And, although ">0" will work, it is not because the zero needs the
""'s.  It is because the Greater Than Operater needs a quote.

In other words, =countif(range,">" & 0) would also work.  Excel
happens to interpret ">0" the same as ">" & 0.
I think for a general rule: Text and Operators require quotes, and
everything else does not, you'll probably be OK.

using CDOSYS to send email via SMTP

Split from this thread.

I have run into the SAME issue.

I am using a LICENCED mailbox user/passw to attempt to submit email to 365

The CONFIGURATION settings are:

    objMessage.Configuration.Fields.Item _
    ("http://schemas.microsoft.com/cdo/configuration/sendusing") = 2
    '// set timeout - default = 30 seconds
    'objMessage.Configuration.Fields.Item _
    '("http://schemas.microsoft.com/cdo/configuration/smtpconnectiontimeout") = 30
    '// name or IP of remote server
    objMessage.Configuration.Fields.Item _
    ("http://schemas.microsoft.com/cdo/configuration/smtpserver") = "smtp.office365.com"
    '// Server port (typically 25)
    objMessage.Configuration.Fields.Item _
    ("http://schemas.microsoft.com/cdo/configuration/smtpserverport") = 587
    '// Use TLS (AKA SSL)
    objMessage.Configuration.Fields.Item _
    ("http://schemas.microsoft.com/cdo/configuration/smtpusessl") = True
    '// turn on authentication
    objMessage.Configuration.Fields.Item _
    ("http://schemas.microsoft.com/cdo/configuration/smtpauthenticate") = 1
    '// username
    objMessage.Configuration.Fields.Item _
    ("http://schemas.microsoft.com/cdo/configuration/sendusername") = SENDusername
    '// password
    objMessage.Configuration.Fields.Item _
    ("http://schemas.microsoft.com/cdo/configuration/sendpassword") = SENDpassword
    '// update the message configuration
    objMessage.Configuration.Fields.Update
    '// send the message
    objMessage.Send

1.Authentication: You must be able to configure a user name and password to send email on the device.
