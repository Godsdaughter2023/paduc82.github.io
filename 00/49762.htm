greginmich say:

For several years now, folks have been left floundering in a sea of misinformation with regard to the Windows Defender scheduled scan – and this can be largely credited to the reply of

https://answers.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/forum/windows_8-performance/can-one-prevent-antimalware-executable-to-start/14d43f26-c682-4b3d-80b6-59ae754b8649

This reply manages to pack one whopping payload of misinformation into an amazingly compact package:

1.  It falsely attributes the Antimalware Engine’s chronic and debilitating resource consumption to the Windows Defender Scheduled Scan task.

2.  It falsely assumes that this scan would have been scheduled by the user.

3.  It falsely claims that unchecking the options in the task’s Conditions tab will “unschedule” the scan.

And nowadays you’ll see this same nonsense parroted all over the web, although many of the current versions of this “fix” have been revised and “improved”. I’ve responded to the latest iteration in the thread below, but since isolating the actual sources of this issue can often prove quite difficult – people will inevitably prefer the simplicity of modifying the Windows Defender Scheduled Scan task – regardless of the fact that the method is based on assumptions that can easily be shown to be false:

https://answers.microsoft.com/en-us/protect/forum/protect_other-protect_start-windows_10/antimalware-service-executable-very-high-disk/84168369-39ca-44df-9c5a-4c054735abcd

As to the whereabouts of the Windows Defender settings, you’ll find all of the classic settings and more in the PowerShell Defender Module:

https://docs.microsoft.com/en-us/powershell/module/defender/set-mppreference?view=win10-ps

You can display the current settings with the Get-MpPreference command. And yes, you can actually use the Set-MpPreference cmdlet to completely remove the Windows Defender Scheduled Scan task.

Right-click on the Start button and launch Windows PowerShell (Admin); and then copy, paste, and enter this command:

Set-MpPreference -ScanScheduleDay 8

But of course that’s something that I wouldn’t recommend, since the chance of this actually resolving any chronic resource utilization issues is slim to none, as I’ve tried to explain in the thread above – although it could potentially eliminate some of the resource usage during system idle states (when Automatic Maintenance runs the Windows Defender Scheduled Scan task). Since the issue is normally with Defender’s always-on file-system-activity scanning (real-time protection), it usually needs to be dealt with by using the general troubleshooting steps and exclusion methods that I’ve outlined in the above thread – or by replacing Defender with a third-party AV app.

Something which you do not take into account though... In fact, nobody seems to take this into account...

Mechanical action.

It seems to me to be safe to assume that the Microsoft offices are full of top end PCs with solid state drives. I have mechanical drives, which I can hear chugging away, as it is taking a long time to load Windows Explorer.

Back in Windows XP and earlier versions, the Disk Defragmenter gave a visual representation of the files on the hard drive. At the left side would normally be a big chunk of contiguous files (where you would expect the Windows OS to be sitting on the drive). Then there would be a small white space, before the rest of the files, be they contiguous or fragmented. Then, about three quarters of the way across would be a Green block, representing the paging file and other unmovable files.

When the defragmenter was running, you could see it juggling the files around, fitting them into any available space, in order to achieve a nice contiguous block at the bottom end of the drive, (centre of the disk)… Leaving a nice clear space for new files. All tidy.

Unfortunately, the new method of "Updating" Windows, is the method preferred for "Upgrading" to a new version of Windows. Which leaves a 30GB (on average) Windows.old file = the old OS files. Where are they sitting? Yes, at the bottom end of the mechanical drive. So where are the new OS files? Well, depending on how much stuff you may have on your drive, and the size of the drive, they could be spread in little chunks all across the drive. Depending on how often the Defragmenter runs on your computer, and the health of the drive before update. Also the reliability of your internet connection during update - could mean that some of the new OS files are indeed fragmented.

As I said, with each update they downgrade things. The Defragmenter was downgraded about Windows 7 I think, and since then people seem to have forgotten that, with a mechanical drive you will achieve optimal performance when the OS and most accessed files are in a nice contiguous chunk at the "bottom end" (centre) of the disk. As it takes less effort for the read-arm to find and access the files.

When files are fragmented across the drive, especially when a 30GB OS is fragmented across a mechanical disk, it takes longer to start up, access files, and run.

In my opinion and belief it is best practice to disable the automatic defragmentation of OS containing mechanical disks. If you download files, copying them onto another disk or to another location - to manually defragment them, and delete the downloaded copy, can keep your files contiguous and tidy for optimal performance, far better than the automatic service.

https://answers.microsoft.com/en-us/protect/forum/protect_defender-protect_scanning/windows-10-1511-10586-windows-defender-full-scan/436b7fd5-8e87-46c4-99fa-db4a0cf09fc1

I guess the missing cache post update might also add some time to the real-time scanning of startup files – but any issues that I’ve seen with startup times following a version update have always paled in comparison to the lag put on Defender’s manual and scheduled scans.

As per the description, I understand that you have recently bought Xbox one controller and getting error message: “The software for this device has been blocked from starting, code: 48” on the system.

I certainly understand the concern.

Error code 48:  It is a device driver error code that users can encounter on any Windows 2000 operating system and later versions.

It occurs when the connected peripheral device cannot be run as the Windows operating system’s driver required to install the programs for peripheral devices has either been corrupted or is incompatible with the system. It prompts the user with the following message on the screen:

The software for this device has been blocked from starting because it is known to have problems with Windows. Contact the hardware vendor for a new driver. (Code 48)

Like most other Windows error codes, error code 48 is triggered by the following factors.

  • Corrupted system registry files
  • Presence of viruses in the device
  • Device driver is out-of-date

I would suggest you to try the following methods and check if that helps. 

Method 1:

I would suggest you to run a full computer scan with the Microsoft Safety Scanner to make sure that the computer is virus free.

Identifying and removing the viruses in the system registry and in the connected devices is the easiest method to fix the corrupted registry files.

Viruses such as spyware and malware infect the registry entries and eat up the files that lead to incomplete or missing data. This creates problems for installing external devices as the driver required to install it is not complete.

Microsoft Safety Scanner, please follow the steps in the link provided below.

http://www.microsoft.com/security/scanner/en-us/default.aspx 

Note: Any data files that are infected may only be cleaned by deleting the file entirely, which means there is a potential for

If the issue persists, follow method 2:

Method 2:

You can also use system restore to eliminate the problem. Here is how you can use it:

  1. Log in using an Administrator account
  2. Click ‘Start’ button and select All Programs > Accessories > System Tools > System Restore
  3. Click ‘Restore my computer to an earlier time’ and click ‘Next’
  4. Select the last Windows restore point from the ‘On this list, click a restore point’ list and click ‘Next’
  5. Click ‘Next’ on the confirmation window to proceed
  6. Restart your PC after restoration is complete

By restoring the system via a last saved system checkpoint, you can obtain undamaged Windows system registry files that can help resolve the error code.

Note:  When you use System Restore to restore the computer to a previous state, programs and updates that you installed are removed.

If the issue persists, follow method 3:

Method 3:

Manually uninstall and reinstall the device driver

Since the Error Code 48 is due to a missing or an out of date device driver, uninstalling and then reinstalling the device driver can help solve the error.

You can do this by firstly logging in as Administrator and opening Device Manager. Select the device that is causing the problem and double click it; make sure that the peripheral is connected properly to the PC.

Upon opening, click on the ‘Driver’ tab and then select ‘Update Driver’. Make sure to refer to your system documentation that you received with your PC or computer to check for the motherboard details and driver specifics.

http://support.xbox.com/en-US/contact-us
