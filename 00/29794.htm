There is no ordinary way of re-using or copying or backing up the default calendar (the one you see when not signed in) in Windows Live Mail. If the versions (builds) of the program are the same on the old and new installations, you may be able simply to copy the calendar database from the old to the new. The database would be the folder


{olddrive}:\Users\{olduser}\AppData\Local\Microsoft\Windows Live Mail\Calendars\DBStore

to be copied to


C:\Users\{newuser}\AppData\Local\Microsoft\Windows Live Mail\Calendars\DBStore

You would do this with Windows Live Mail closed, and it would be wise to check the ownership and permissions for the folder in its new location before launching the program and testing.

This is the sort of bother you would have avoided simply by signing in in Windows Live Mail.

Windows can be installed on a completely blank hard drive. You don't need Windows 7 to install Windows 10. You don't even need an internet connection.

Simply remove your old hard drive, install your new SSD, and then boot your computer from your Windows 10 installation DVD.

I suggest that you backup your personal files from the old hard drive before you begin. You don't have to do that, but it's the easiest way to backup.

An internet connection is needed for the additional steps Microsoft hopes you'll take after Windows is installed.

The problem would seem to lie between the %WINDIR%\Fonts folder and the HKEY_LOCAL_MACHINE\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Fonts registry key. If they are incorrectly cross-referenced no font can be displayed so the system uses the Marlett font as a final resort.

If your Fonts folder appears to be populated, I might try deleting or renaming the folder's StaticCache.dat and/orfms_mettadat.xml files and restarting the computer. If they are not rebuilt to defaults, then reinstalling all fonts from within that folder may resurrect your system.

light759 say:

If you are getting a tv I'd say no. From what I understand, many 120hz tvs only accept a 60hz input and double the frames. This means you wont see any real improvement as the console will think its connected to a 60hz screen. Plus any smoothing effects will create huge latency and can be pretty bad for a game. You could search out a tv or large pc monitor that accepts the native input but I highly doubt we will see any 120hz games that aren't pretty simple. They will most likely be pushing for 1080p with maybe 60fps and focus the rest of the power on effects and other graphical areas.

That said the panel in a 120hz tv has the potential to be better due to faster refresh, therefore less ghosting. It could also benefit any non gaming activities the One wants to push for if you enable smoothing. In my experience though, it can have issues, especially if we see a rise of 30fps games again. I recently had to decide between a 60hz and a 120hz PC monitor for my consoles, and the 120hz had a strange slow motion effect that I guessed was from having to show a frame upwards of four times, but that's just a guess.

My advice (TL;DR): Just stick with a good 60hz tv or monitor and wait until we see any 120hz capable games. I highly doubt we will see any, and unless you plan to use it for other purposes, it might be a waste of money.
