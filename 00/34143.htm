The device you have may be a Surface Book or Surface Book 2, since these are the models which have the detach feature. Check the SKU to see what Surface model you have.

If it turns out that your Surface is a Surface Book or Surface Book 2, the icon you've been clicking on your taskbar is the detach button. This works the same way as the Detach key on the keyboard, which is next to the Del key. If you want to learn more about detaching your Surface and using different modes, check this page.

If you want to remove the detach button from your taskbar, you can do that by going to Task Manager (Ctrl + Shift + Esc) > Startup tab > select Surface DTX > Disable. About the "Surface Detach: Action Required" error that you were getting, this could mean that an app/s are using the graphics supplied by your keyboard. Close out programs that use the Graphics Processing Unit (GPU) first if you would like to detach the Surface. Also, take note that the batteries for both the tablet (Battery 1) and the keyboard (Battery 2) need to be above 10% for you to be able to detach the Surface.

I would also recommend that you download and run the Surface Diagnostic Toolkit to get the latest driver and firmware updates for your device. It will also run several tests which will scan the system for software issues. After running the tool, please perform a forced shutdown. While the device is on, just press the power button for 10 seconds until the screen goes off. The Surface will be able to apply the updates and reload the software upon restart.

In case the information above does not clarify how the detach key works or if you have other concerns, please let me know by responding to this post.

kokebberhane say:

From Ted Miller @

http://xpt.sourceforge.net/techdocs/nix/disk/boot/boot11-WindowsSpecificBooting/ar01s05.html

This type of syntax was invented as a sort of portable naming convention for the boot firmware of NT-capable RISC machines. On x86, such "firmware" is essentially a wrapper around the BIOS.

multi(n)
nth "multifunction" adapter/bus. Always 0 on x86 machines since the firmware emulation doesn't concern itself with the distinction between various busses.
disk(n)
nth disk controller on the adapter/bus. Again always 0 on x86 machines because the firmware emulation doesn't care about which controller the disk is on.
rdisk(n)
nth disk (technically, on the controller, irrelevent on x86). The BIOS reports a total number of drives via int13 function 8. The valid range for rdisk(n) is 0 through the value reported by the BIOS minus 1.
partition(n)
0 refers to the whole physical drive. Partitions are numbered sequenatially starting at 1 based on their positions in the partition table. Logical drives within the extended partition are numbered after all primary partitions.

When manufacturers preinstall Windows (since 7) they create a recovery drive, equivalent to the CD or DVD that used to come with your pc. This can be used to reinstall Windows, mainly when returning it to factory state. (For recovery you should create your own DVD for 7, 8.1 or USB for 10).

The recovery drive is hidden from explorer. In disk management it is visible as "disk 0 partition 2" and perhaps " disk 0 partition 5" or "6".

BTW, disk 0 partition 1 is the "EFI system partition" used by Windows to manage booting.

a. user say:

Disregard the clueless "Microsoft Support Engineer."

Upgrading to a SSD is a great idea and the prices have really come down over time. You will not lose your activation by replacing your hard drive. For the future keep this in mind: If you replace your motherboard or CPU, Microsoft considers that to be a 'new' computer and you will lose your activation.

By far the fastest and easiest way to migrate to a new drive is with disk imaging software. It's practically effortless (once you learn how to do it) and you won't have to re-install, re-configure or re-update anything.

In general, the much improved speed of a SSD benefits software, but not your personal folders. What people used to do (and what I did) is to separate your software and personal folders; the software migrates to a SSD and your personal folders stay on the HDD. The way prices of SSDs are dropping, that may not be necessary any more.
