Outlook 2016 - Image Quality Reduction/Compression

Greetings Outlook Experts!

Our company started to run into a problem  as soon as we upgraded to Outlook 2016 and we need the community's assistance! Here are the details and the exact behavior that we see:

1. When we copy/paste images directly into the body of Outlook 2016 e-mails, they go in as .PNG files

2. Once the recipient opens the e-mail, the image quality and file type remain unchanged

3. If the recipient is to reply to the e-mail, the original image will THEN get auto-converted to .jpg and the quality significantly deteriorates. 

4. With every additional e-mail reply, the image quality gets even worse. After less than a dozen replies, the images become completely unrecognizable.

We've spent months looking at image DPI size, outlook settings, going through blogs and many others. Every time we think we have a solution, we're proven wrong after a few tests. For image capturing, we use Snag-It and we've played with every setting that we could.

***Post moved by the moderator to the appropriate forum category.***

payson slc say:

Greetings!

After talking to Microsoft, going through endless number of blogs and talking to some vendors, we were able to find the exact root cause for this behavior. It is very simple:

  • If you copy/paste a .png image that has 32 bit depth, there will be no issues. As people reply to the e-mail, the image will remain as .png and the quality will not detoriate in any way
  • If however your in-line screenshot is 24 bit, as people reply to the e-mail the following will happen:
    • Outlook will auto-convert the image to .jpeg
    • With each e-mail reply, the quality will become worse and at some point no one will be able to read the screenshot as it will be just a blur

Despite of my push to ask Microsoft to address this (or at least escalate it), there doesn't seem to be much interest. I'll paraphrase - if it behaves like that and 24 bit images don't work as expected, it's probably by design because there are system limitations.

If you use a screen capturing tool and it has the option to set all images to 32 bit by default, go for it! If not, you'll be stuck and have to use time consuming workarounds.

From what I can tell, even screenshot's taken with Windows 7 are 24 bit by default.

If you run into this yourself, I encourage you to open a ticket with Microsoft as well. The more companies and individuals they hear from, the bigger the chance for a fix is.

marshall barton say:

Drawing the lines in a blank area of a report section is a little tedious to code, but pretty routine.  It should look something like:

Const lngCols As Long = 4      'Change these to your specs
Const lngRows As Long = 10   'there are 1440 twips per inch
Const lngV As Long = .3 * !440    '3/10 inch per row
Const lngH As Long = 1.2 * 1440  '1.2 inches wide
Const lngToptPos As Long = 2 * 1440  'vertical position of top line
Const lngLeftPos As Long = .5 + 1440  'position of left most line
      'draw horizontal lines
For k = lngToptPos To lngToptPos + lngRows * lngV STEP lngV
   Me.Line (lngLeftPos, k)-Step(lngCols * lngH, 0)
Next k
      'draw vertical lines
For k = lngLeftPos To lngLeftPos + lngCols * lngH STEP lngH
   Me.Line (k, lngToptPos)-Step(0, lngRows * lngV )
Next k

tideways say:

I had to reate a report which had little in the way of dynamic data but lots of empty table layouts to be filled in by hand during interviews. I was also perplexed by the lack of a control to create a tabular grid and eventually resigned myself to the fact that I would have to draw it by hand, however, I took a couple of steps to make it a bit easier:

  1. Standard cell height in a MS Word table is 0.58 cms  If you're happy with this, great, if not, design a table in Word and look at the cell height (it's a good idea to make a note of the widths too).
  2. In your Access report, use the line control to create the top edge of your table.
  3. In the properties panel, look at the property called top (this is how far from the top of the section your line begins on). Let's say it said 12.785cm
  4. Open a blank spreadsheet in Excel and in cell A1 put in whatever was the number above (12.785 in my example, but put in whatever yours was).
  5. In cell B1 type:  =A1 + 0.58 and click enter. Drag fill down the page so that cells B2 to say, B20 are filled in.
  6. In cell A2 type  =B1 and once again drag fill down the column.

You will now have a set of values to use for the Top property of your horizontal lines,so. click on your original Top line, Ctrl+C, then Ctrl+V .... then type in the next Top value from your spreadsheet. Repeat the process and all your lines will be 0.58cm apart.
