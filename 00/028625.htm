rohn007 say:

My mistake, sorry. Win 10 has similar backup options with different names.

Back to Win 8 for a moment. Lets do a little "knowledge transfer", aka education ...

In Win 8 MS was really pushing hard on the new "Metro" (cr)apps and the Start Screen. The Win 8 "desktop" was an afterthought.  So the Win 8 Refresh was designed to automatically backup the "Metro" apps, but it ignored the "old" Desktop applications, like Office, even though MS had not (and STILL has not!) come up with a Metro version of Office.  Win 8 Refresh had the option to handle desktop installations through the RecImg tool. But they didn't bother mentioning it to the general public.  So the Win 8 Refresh was a good tool that could restore your computer to a known good state without touching your personal files. But it was flawed if people were not told about RecImg because it needlessly forced them to re-install desktop applications.  It was a form of blackmail intended to "encourage" people to use the nonexistent or deficient Metro apps.

In Win 10 they renamed the backup options, and unfortunately dumbed them down.

Win 8 "Refresh" is renamed "Keep my Files", but now it is without the RecImg tool.  It still keeps the Metro (now called Windows Store) (cr)apps but there is no longer an option to include the desktop applications. Instead, it is supposed to tell you BEFORE starting which applications will be lost, and it is supposed to put that list on your desktop after the restore so you can re-install them.  Very depressing how they keep pushing customers away.

The other option is now called "Remove Everything". That is what it does, it resets the computer back to "factory fresh".

There is a 3rd option, that they discount, is the traditional "image copy". This backs up the whole system, all applications and personal files.  Again, this resets the computer back to a known good state, but it affects your personal files.

So, of the 3 Win 10 backup options, my personal favorite is the old school 3rd one. 

Do periodic image copies. But I prefer to do it with a twist.  I "partition" my HD into 2 parts. The C:\ drive for Windows and the applications and the D:\ drive for personal data files, basically everything under the c:\Users\.... directory.  This way I can do periodic image copies of my C: drive. Basically after doing major Windows updates and new application installations.  Actually, my new computer naturally supports this. It has 2 drives, a "small" SSD for C:\ and a large spinning disk for D:\.

As well, the is the Win (8) 10 "File History" backup. That works well with the 2 drive approach.
