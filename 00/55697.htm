a. user say:

Man, you need to take it easy. Everything you need to know is out there on the internet; you just have to read it.

In Windows (and probably other operating systems) every user must be unique, for security. That means 'you' on the old computer is different from 'you' on the new computer. Even though they're both you.

So when you try to access those files on the external drive, as far as the operating system is concerned, you're trying to access somebody else's files. Capisci? The same thing happens to everyone.

To solve the mystery, use an account with Administrator permissions. Administrators have the authority to take ownership (that's the correct technical term) of files and folders. Here's how:

1- Sign out of your Standard user account - the account that wants to receive the files.

2- Sign in to an account with Administrator permissions, and use it to temporarily elevate your Standard account to Administrator.

3- Sign into your newly elevated Administrator account and move the files from the external HDD to the corresponding folders in your account (e.g., Documents, Music, etc.) When you are asked if you want to take ownership of the files, just say yes.

4- Sign out of your temporarily elevated Administrator account and switch to your real Administrator account. Now demote your own user account back to Standard.

5- Sign out of your Administrator account and sign in to your Standard account. You're done. The files and folders that you moved to your user account will automatically inherit the access permissions of the folders into which they were moved.

That method always works - I've done it lots of times - unless you previously changed the default access settings of your computers or your external hard drive. Don't ask how I figured that out.

If you add a secondary language to Windows 10 version 1803 that shares the same base language as the Windows display language, neither language can be removed.

For example: If English (United Kingdom) is the current Windows display language, and you then add English (India) to the Preferred languages list, afterwards neither English (United Kingdom) nor English (India) can be removed from the Preferred languages list.

Note: The most notable effect of having multiple related languages in the Preferred languages list is additional entries in the text input selector. You can remove unwanted language list entries using these steps:

1. From the Start menu, search for powershell.

2. Right-click Windows Powershell in the search results and choose Run as administrator.

3. Type or copy/paste the following command at the command prompt, and then press Enter.

Get-WinUserLanguageList

After typing this command, make note of the “LanguageTag” listed for the language you wish to remove, as you will need it in the next step.

4. Type or copy/paste each of the following commands at the command prompt, and then press Enter. In the following commands, instead of typing <languagecode>, replace it with the language tag you wish to remove, such as en-in for English (India).

$LangList = Get-WinUserLanguageList
$MarkedLang = $LangList | where LanguageTag -eq "<languagecode>"
$LangList.Remove($MarkedLang)

Set-WinUserLanguageList $LangList -Force
