For me, the problem began simply enough.

My wife was trying to view on her PC an MPG video she had created on her Mac.  She couldn't.

In the meantime, I thought I better put WMC on my computer because I sometimes assist her in her video design and postproduction decisions.  And what a great way to test the flexibility of this Technical Preview I'm evaluating (not just for myself, but for clients who rely on my recommendations).

[I bet you all saw this coming, didn't you?]

So, after a circuitous journey tracking down "My Computer" and right-clicking on the link and selecting properties, I arrive at the general information screen that should be available to anyone at almost any time.

And there it is, the old Win 8 Link to "Add Features", which I select, and then add "Media Center" to my Win 10 system.

[Note:  this option is no longer available in build 9879, for those lucky enough to have that build.]

And then my joyride began.

MS, to protect itself from piracy, had a unique way of adding Media Center to a Win 8 system:  It changed the Key.  That is, it now had an identifier saying you had a right to both the Win 8 version you were using and to the WMC package you added.

And that's what happened to me.  The collision of my purchased key with my Tech Preview Key produced internal changes that kept the Tech Preview from being identifiable any longer to the MS site as a valid product.  In other words, no more Tech Previews.

Okay, I would uninstall the Media Center package.  Well, uh, no ...   You see, Media Center wasn't just an add-on; it was a merge-with; and it couldn't be unmerged.  No uninstallation.

All right, I couldn't get a new preview which would re-establish a Tech Preview (without Media Center, it seems).  I couldn't uninstall Media Center.

I had a solution.  Reinstall the Tech Preview and it would keep all the settings and data and new program info for programs I had installed into the Preview.

Well, uh, no.  Again.  The Preview was happy to be re-installed, but only as a virgin.  Unlike the step-up and -into we got offered from Win 8 to the Tech version, no such option was for Preview to Preview.

That meant, restoring my original Win 8 system (oh, grief), then reinstalling the earlier build of the Preview (hours of waiting - more grief), then suffering through the upgrade preview (my own stupid fault for not selecting "fast previews" which I shan't forget).

---

All of which reveals a couple of planning flaws in this edition:  One of these is common to almost all beta testing as well as to this Technical Preview:  The lack of knowledgeable personnel who actually check these boards and try to solve some communal problems.  In this case, updating the key recognizer to accept the modified key+Media Center key enough to allow us to step-up to the next preview level and removing the Media Center that way.

I do understand all the caveats of beta- (excuse me, preview-) testing.  I am back here, you see.  But the real point of such testing is to find out how well the product works under real conditions, not the sanitized environment of a beta-tester reluctant to be forced back to ground zero with every unusual choice he or she makes.

I think Win 10 has the potential to be a strong comeback for MS, but only if all the earlier testers unite in a positive response for the product.  That means MS has to give us the same care and responsiveness they feel they would owe to a paying customer.  After all, if the product succeeds, that's what they would get a lot more of.

Oh, and that reminds me, why isn't there an "Another" option for the Windows Topics.  After all, the idea is that we may see something the MS team didn't, and that thing may not even belong to any of the topics the team thought of.

Call Barring enabled

a. user say:

Hello,

1.  Contact your carrier to request that call barring be cancelled on your phone.

2.  Ask the customer service representative for the call barring password should you need to edit your phone's  settings. Write this password down for easy reference later.

3.  Test your phone’s ability to make and receive calls. If barring is still enabled, you will need to manually disable it.

1.  Press “Menu” and then “Settings.” Select “Phone” and then “Call barring.”

2.  Press “Menu” and then “Settings.” Select “Phone” and then “Call Barring.”

3.  Select “Outgoing Calls” and then press “Options.” Choose “Deactivate all Barrings” from the resultant menu.

4.  Enter the call barring password provided by your carrier.

5.  Repeat this process for “Incoming Calls,” “International Calls” and any other desired barring modes you wish to disable.

6.  Test your ability to make and receive calls.
