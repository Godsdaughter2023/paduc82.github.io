vijay a. verma say:
Following is the way to clean non printable / visible data and you can build this within your formula.

CLEAN will clean up code 0 to 31
Trim will remove leading and trailing blanks (and also will remove in between blanks if more than one blanks)
You will have to use SUBSTITUTE function to remove 127, 129, 141, 143, 144, 157, 160

Hence, generic formula would be
=SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(TRIM(CLEAN($B$2:$B$20)),CHAR(127),""),CHAR(129),""),CHAR(141),""),CHAR(143),""),CHAR(144),""),CHAR(157),""),CHAR(160),"")

In your case, you will have to use following Array formula as one single formula and drag down (You can not B:B in array otherwise it will drag down performance)

=VLOOKUP(A2&"",SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(TRIM(CLEAN($B$2:$B$20)),CHAR(127),""),CHAR(129),""),CHAR(141),""),CHAR(143),""),CHAR(144),""),CHAR(157),""),CHAR(160),""),1,)

Note - Array Formula is not entered by pressing ENTER after entering your formula but by pressing CTRL+SHIFT+ENTER. If you are copying and pasting this formula, take F2 after pasting and CTRL+SHIFT+ENTER. This will put { } brackets around the formula which you can see in Formula Bar. If you edit again, you will have to do CTRL+SHIFT+ENTER again. Don't put { } manually.

rob koch say:

Peter,

The reason you don't have evidence is the limited breadth of experience you can attain with a single system, while MSE itself, as well as the other Microsoft security products using the same engines and definitions are installed on literally 100's of millions of systems.  With this huge install base we've typically seen only a handful (a few dozen) reporting such issues in these forums over the last 10 years, which includes OneCare that was the predecessor to MSE.

When I first read your post I searched for "HP630 laptop i5 overheat" and found quite a few posting overheat issues with this and other HP laptops as well as the i5 processor, but nothing consistent enough to indicate a known problem.  Only anecdotal evidence of a significant number of similar overheat issues displayed under a number of differing conditions.  Some of these posters did seem to feel that HP laptops experience such issues more than others, though this is of course simply personal opinion based on their own experience.

The obvious reason that some other antimalware doesn't trigger these issues is that it is inherently inefficient, spending more time performing disk access or other wasteful operations rather than using the processor at full utilization.  This may actually be a choice by those other vendors based on their experience with cheaply and badly designed hardware, which is unfortunately quite common at the consumer and small business end of the spectrum.

I personally have never owned a low end laptop, since most of mine were provided by my employers and so were high performance, business class systems costing well over $1,200.  In 10 years of using OneCare and MSE on these and the many friends and relatives systems where I've installed them, I've never experienced an overheat issue myself, though the fans do occasionally kick in during a scan as I'd expect.

It's always difficult trying to convince people that their hardware is the problem, since their minds automatically combine the overheat failure with the only program where they've experienced it, and so feel 'caused" the issue, which is a natural logical failure.  Without our years of experience dealing with this issue with dozens of others, many of whom return to confirm that either a cleaning, heat sink or fan repair/replacement or a workaround such as a manual processor throttling application resolved their problem, they have no basis to believe us.

We saw much more of this issue a few years back as the Windows XP systems began to age, but it's been more rare in the last few as newer, better designed systems hit the market.  However, I suspect that we'll be seeing more again as the Windows 7 based systems age, as well as those now being much more cheaply designed and produced in order to reduce the ASP (Average Selling Price) begin to display their weaknesses.

I can't say that I'd choose to open a laptop myself, since that's not a simple act today.  I can only say that believing this is a fault within MSE is flawed and an utter waste of both our time, since it's clearly a hardware issue.  If you don't wish to take the effort required to resolve the true problem, then any workaround including using another 3rd-party antimalware that doesn't trigger the issue is a valid solution.

Rob

graham mayor say:

The following macro - http://www.gmayor.com/installing_macro.htm  - will send a link to the current document path:

Sub Send_Link()
Dim olApp As Object
Dim oMail As Object
Dim olInsp As Object
Dim wdDoc As Document
Dim oRng As Range
Dim strLink As String
Dim strLinkText As String
    ActiveDocument.Save
    strLinkText = "Click here for Document"
    strLink = ActiveDocument.FullName
    On Error Resume Next
    Set olApp = GetObject(, "Outlook.Application")
    If Err <> 0 Then
        Set olApp = CreateObject("Outlook.Application")
    End If
    On Error GoTo CleanUp
    Set oMail = olApp.CreateItem(0)
    With oMail
        .to = "*** Email address is removed for privacy ***"        'the recipient
        .Subject = "Document"
        .BodyFormat = 2
        Set olInsp = .GetInspector
        Set wdDoc = olInsp.WordEditor
        Set oRng = wdDoc.Range(0, 0)
        wdDoc.Hyperlinks.Add Anchor:=oRng, _
                             Address:=strLink, _
                             SubAddress:="", _
                             ScreenTip:="", _
                             TextToDisplay:=strLinkText
        .Display 'Do not delete this line
        '.Send  'Remove the apostrophe after testing
    End With
    Set oMail = Nothing
    ActiveDocument.Close 0
CleanUp:
    Set olApp = Nothing
    Set oMail = Nothing
    Set olInsp = Nothing
    Set wdDoc = Nothing
    Set oRng = Nothing
lbl_Exit:
    Exit Sub
End Sub

How to fix stuck in headphone mode on Lumia 928? - Microsoft Community

coldbluenorth---01 say:

How to fix stuck in headphone mode on Lumia 928?

It took me a while to realize that the reason I could not hear calls, or audio from video clips on my phone was because the phone was stuck in Headphones mode.

After much experimenting I noticed that when I was adjusting the volume the volume indicator dialogue near the top of the screen indicated that "Headphones" were plugged in when they were not. I had tried a variety of fixes and even reloaded the OS to get it to recognize the headphones were not plugged in and nada. I tried cleaning the headphone jack with alchohol and blowing it out with air that did not fix it either.

I finally found the issue which appears to be either poor design or poor materials or both. If you hold a flashlight (small LED one would work best) and look into the phone's headphone jack, at the top of the jack on the side towards the edge of the phone is a small grey pressure switch plate. I believe that it is supposed to be a piece of conductive metal that when compressed (the headphone jack inserted) it touches the wall of the headphone jack and tells the phone that the headphones are in. When you remove the headphones it is supposed to spring back up and away from the wall which the phone interprets as the headphones having been removed.

The tiny grey metal plate was still pressed flush against the wall and had not "sprung" away when the headphones were removed causing the issue. I used a needle to gently pry the plate away from the wall of the jack and the phone immediately went back to having it's internal speakers available for calls or watching video, etc...

So I was able to fix my issue, but I think Nokia and other phone makers need to do a better job in designing the headphone jacks or use better materials...
