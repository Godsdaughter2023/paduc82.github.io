The Live service does not immediately communicate changes in your Xbox Live presence (i.e. what games you play etc.), as that would put a lot of load on the service. 

So a several minute delay for indicating on what you are (not) doing on Xbox Live through other services (like Discord) is normal. 

Also it is normal for the console to be warm even after you put it in instant on mode, as the console is instructed to update your games and it's system during that time. 

So the console is still doing things like uploading your gaming progress to the cloud, and checking for game updates. 

This costs processor power, and that processor heat as well as heat in the power supply in the back where the power cord goes in.

The console has several temperature sensors on board and on the processor. If the console detects a fault and too much heat, it will automatically put the processor into a halt state and display an error message that the console needs to cool down. 

So unless you see that message, everything your console does is completely fine.

Yes, you did. Maybe you didn't intend to, but you did.

After a PC is reset, Windows creates a list of applications that were previously on your computer that did not come from Windows Store. These are applications that you will have to re-install yourself. Just go to the developer's website and follow the instructions to download and install their software.

Here's some general information about a reset that may explain things:

In what Microsoft calls a reset, your hard drive is erased and a new copy of Windows 10 is installed. What happens after that depends on what type of reset you chose.

In what Microsoft calls a 'remove everything' reset, all you'll get is a new copy of Windows.

In what Microsoft calls a 'keep my files' reset, personal files (documents, pictures, etc.) that are stored in your user profile are backed up and restored after Windows 10 is reinstalled.

If it turns out you never acquired a license for Windows 10 Pro, please follow these steps to downgrade to Windows 10 Home:

There is one way you might be able to downgrqade to Home without having to reinstall everything, but its pretty advanced.

Open Registry Editor (WIN + R, type regedit, hit Enter)

Change EditionID to Core (double click EditionID, change value, click OK). In your case it should at the moment show Pro

Close the Registry Editor

When asked, enter your valid Windows 10 Home product key and select what to keep (settings, personal files and apps, only personal files or nothing)
Windows 10 Home in-place upgrade will be done now.
