jeeped say:
When setting up a CF rule for a large block of cells it helps to have an extra qualifier that will render the CF rule false unless the row is pertinent. For my solution, I've added the condition that the second character in column C must be a hyphen.

       

For the two stage row highlighting you describe governing the A1:BM999 block you will actually need two CF rules based upon the following formulas.

=AND(MID($C1,2,1)="-",CODE(LEFT($C1,1))>=CODE(LEFT($BM1,1)))    ◄ green 
=AND(MID($C1,2,1)="-",CODE(LEFT($C1,1))<CODE(LEFT($BM1,1)))      ◄ yellow 

I didn't know where your data actually started, so I simply started at row 1.

jeeped say:
First select cell A1 then tap F5 and type or paste A1:BM999 into the Reference: text box and click OK. This will select A1:BM999 with A1 as the active cell. The active cell is important as this is what the relative rows and columns will be based upon when supplying a formula.

With A1:BM999 still selected, go to Home, Conditional Formatting, Manage Rules. When the Conditional Formatting Rules Manager dialog opens, click New Rule.

When the New Formatting Rule dialog opens, choose Use a formula to determine which cells to format from the top Select a rule type: list. Supply the first formula in the Format values where this formula is true: text box. Click the Format button and select a green Fill. Click OK to accept the formatting, then OK again to create the new rule.

You should be back at the Conditional Formatting Rules Manager dialog. Click New Rule again and repeat the process with the second formula supplied above but use a yellow Fill.

When you are back at the Conditional Formatting Rules Manager dialog the second time, you should see your two new CF rules with the Applies to: referencing =$A$1:$BM$999. Click OK in the lower right to finalize the two rules.

While you can actually compare A to B, I thought it would be better to actually deal with numbers. An A is ASCII code 65, B is 66 and so on until Z which is 90. anthonyp91767 say:

Calcuate time in percentage - MS Access 2010

I am working in MS Access 2010. I am trying to calculate the actual per hr count based on the numbers of hrs worked by the number of items completed.  I have a field [Qty] and a [Total Hrs] field. I need to divide the [Qty] by the [Total Hrs] to come with the [actual per hr count]. When I divide (ex.  qty 10 by 1 hr) I get (10). (When I div 20 by 2 I get 10.) BUT, if I div count 10 by .25 (min) I get 40! This is what I currently have in one of the fields in my query that is supposed to calculate:  Actual Per Hr: ([Qty]/[Total Hrs]). Any help would be extremely helpful!

john w. vinson say:

What is the datatype of Total Hrs? If (as I gather) it's a Number field (Double probably) then you and the computer are at cross purposes: YOU see 1.25 as one hour and twenty-five minutes, but Access sees at as just a number, one and a fourth. If you just do arithmetic division, then indeed, 10 divided by 0.25 IS 40.

I would suggest that you store total time as Minutes - e.g. 60 would be one hour, 75 would be one hour and fifteen minutes. If need be you can use two textboxes on a form for hours and minutes, and use some simple VBA code to calculate the total time in minutes from the two textboxes, or vice versa.

ken sheridan say: The calculation is correct.  0.25 hours is 15 minutes, so if 10 items are completed in that time 40 items are completed per hour.  If you want to specify 25 minutes as a fractional value of an hour then the you have to represent it as 25/60, which is 0.416666666666667.  10/0.416666666666667  = 23.999999999999981, which is the correct result, and will be rounded to 24.

If the Total Hours value will always be less than 24 hours you can use a Total Hours column of date/time data type, in which case 25 minutes would be entered as 00:25.  As  a date/time value is a value in days you need to divide this by 24:

10/[Total Hours]/24 = 24

For 2 hours you'd enter 02:00 in which case:

20/[Total Hours]/24 = 10

If Total Hours can be 24 or more hours then it would be best to store the hours and minutes as separate columns Total Hours and Total Minutes, both with a DefaultValue property of zero and a Required property of True.  You can then compute the hourly production with:

10/([Total Hours]+([Total Minutes]/60))

If you substitute some literal values you can see how it works:

10 in 25 minutes:                        10/(0+(25/60)) = 24
20 in 2 hours:                              20/(2+(0/60)) = 10
20 in 2 hours and 25 minutes:    20/(2+(25/60)) = 8.28

The last is rounded to 2 decimal places.
