The contact pictures on my phone do not show up in Hotmail contacts though the contact account on the phone is Windows Live.

julie fka profjulie say:

You're not missing anything. The pictures you've added to your contacts on the phone sync to your Windows Live account, but you won't see them in Windows Live.  The contact pictures you see in Windows Live are for any Live Messenger contacts you might have in the contacts listing and those pictures don't sync to your phone.

If you hard reset your phone or if you get a new windows phone 7, when you sync your phone to your windows live account, you'll see that the pictures that you had previously synced from a windows phone 7 to your windows live account will be synced over to the new phone.

How to I edit a document with content control? - Microsoft Community

daniel_219 say:

How to I edit a document with content control?

I have been sent a document via email which I need to edit. There is a table within the document with a box in which an image should be added, there are also many sub-headings within the document which are to be deleted. My problem is that I cannot add an image into the table (despite there being an insert picture button) and I cannot edit these headings at all. The headings are in text boxes. By reading previous questions I believe that this is something to do with "content control" however I am unsure. If anybody could help me with this I would be grateful.
PS: I am on Office word 2010

Thanks,

Dan

jay freedman say:

"Content control" is not some way of "controlling the editing of content" as you seem to have the impression. "Content controls" are, like form fields, things that can be inserted into a document to make places to enter text (and in some cases pictures). Your issue is something else. To diagnose what that something else is, we'll need more information.

First, did you open the document directly from the email attachment? If so, don't do that. Save the attachment file to your hard drive and then open it in Word. (Regarding the dangers of editing attachments, see http://www.gmayor.com/outlook_attachments.htm.)

Second, the presence of an insert picture button suggests that the document contains a macro for that function. Because macros can be malicious (like viruses), Microsoft works very hard to prevent them from running unless you know and trust the source. In order to be macro-enabled, the document's file extension must be .docm instead of .docx (although documents created in the old .doc format can also contain macros). The file must be stored in a folder that Word knows is a "trusted location", or it must have a digital signature with a valid certificate.

Most likely, you can't edit the headings because the document is protected, either to prevent any editing or to limit editing to form fields. To find out whether that's true, go to the Review tab and click the Restrict Editing button. A pane will open. If there's "Stop Protection" button at the bottom of the pane, the document is protected. In that case, click the button. If you're prompted to enter a password, you'll have to get that from the person who sent the file. If there is no prompt, but the pane's contents change, then you've just unprotected the document. Make your changes, click the "Start Enforcing Protection" button in the pane, click OK in the password box, and save the document.

larryyu_msft3110 say:

Hi PeaBe,

Below are the answers to your questions:

Q1. Does Office 365 Exchange use TLS by default for all email?

A: No. By default, Exchange Online always uses Opportunistic TLS. This means Exchange Online always tries to encrypt connections with the most secure version of TLS first, then works its way down the list of TLS ciphers until it finds one on which both parties can agree. Unless you have configured Exchange Online to ensure that messages to that recipient are only sent through secure connections, then by default the message will be sent unencrypted if the recipient organization doesn’t support TLS encryption. Opportunistic TLS is sufficient for most businesses. However, for business that have compliance requirements such as medical, banking, or government organizations, you can configure Exchange Online to require, or force, TLS.

Q2: If I want to activate mail-encryption, what do I need to do?

A: To support message encryption, you need to have an Office 365 E3 or above (E4, E5) subscription. The IRM (Information Rights Management) also included in these subscriptions. If your subscription include the Rights Management feature, you can select it in File->Set Permission when sending emails in the Outlook client. If you don’t have these subscriptions, you may also buy the Rights Management license in Office 365 admin center->PURCHASE SERVICES.

Feel free to let us know if you have other concerns.
