I dont get this unitprice*quantity*(1-discount. in access - Microsoft Community

jackamin say:

I dont get this unitprice*quantity*(1-discount. in access

hi I have bee reading access 2016 step by step and came across this in a query design and it somehow confused me. I understand the idea of trying to multiply the price by quantity, but when it also multiply by 1 and -discount I just don't get that.

sample

ExtendedPrice: CCur([Order Details]![UnitPrice]*[Order Details]![Quantity]*(1-[Order Details]![Discount]))

ken sheridan say: Let's take it apart:

    [Order Details]![UnitPrice]*[Order Details]![Quantity]

This multiplies the unit price, i.e. the cost of one item of the product in question, by the quantity of that product ordered.  So if the unit price is £1.50 the total price of 10 items would be £15.00.

    *(1-[Order Details]![Discount])

This multiplies the total price, calculated as above, by the 1 less the discount.  Normally a discount would be formatted as a percentage, e.g. 10%, but that is just how we see the real number stored in the table.  The real number will be 0.1, i.e. one tenth.  So 1 - 0.1 gives us a result of 0.9 (nine tenths).  When we discount a price by 10% therefore, we charge nine tenths of the listed price.  Having already calculated above that the total cost at the listed price of the items is £15.00, multiplying this by 0.9 gives us £13.50.

Finally the value we've calculated is converted to a Currency data type by calling the CCur function.  In the example above the discounted price is an exact value to two decimal places, as we normally see currency values, but with different unit price and discount values it might well have come out as something very different 12.58347666 for instance.  By converting this to a currency data type the CCur function does two things: (i) it shows the value in the normal currency format set for the system, in my case in GBP for which the symbol is £; (ii) it actually converts the value to one with a precision of 4 decimal places, so with 12.58347666 would be 12.5835, the last decimal place being rounded up in this case.  By storing currency values at this precision any cumulative rounding errors which are done on calculations with the values are kept to a minimum.  We will see the results formatted to the usual two decimal places, however, even though any calculations being done behind the scenes are at the greater precision.

financial analysis of a project, getting a positive IRR for a negative - Microsoft Community

khurampervez say:

financial analysis of a project, getting a positive IRR for a negative NPV in the same year

Hi,
I am using MS Office 2010. I had been doing a financial analysis of a project.
Surprisingly I am getting a positive IRR for a negative NPV in the same year. Can someone help? I am happy to send my sheet.
joeu2004 say: Khuram wrote earlier:
So, it means that "YES", IRR can be positive even when NPV is negative. 

In a word, yes -- with your cash flows and as you are using those terms.

Khuram wrote earlier:
So let us say if we don't have the WACC which is 5% in this case, then what should be the way to calculate IRR.

Wrong question; non sequitur.

There is only one way to calculate the IRR per se [1], and you are doing it correctly.  The IRR is calculated based on only the cash flows [2].  The WACC is not a factor in the IRR calculation.

In contrast, there is an infinite number of ways to calculate the NPV simply by changing the discount rate.  So, the question you should be asking is:  what discount rate should we use to calculate the NPV?

Khuram wrote earlier:
What I want is to calculate the IRR and NPV by using EXCEL.
Please advise me as I have to finalize my analysis.

There is nothing wrong with the way you are using the Excel functions.  The only problem is with your interpretation or understanding.  "Garbage in, garbage out". [3]

I don't know why you choose to use the WACC to calculate the NPV.  Nothing wrong with it; in fact, it is quite common when the WACC is "known" [6].  But there is nothing intrinsically right with it, either.  That is, it is not the only discount rate to use when computing the NPV.

As I said previously, with your cash flows [5], think of the IRR as a break-even discount rate.

1. If you choose the IRR for the discount rate, the NPV will be zero (or close to it).
2. If you choose a lower discount rate [4], the NPV will be positive.
3. If you choose a higher discount rate [4], the NPV will be negative.

So in your example [5], one way to "analyze" (interpret) the results is:  if we can reduce the WACC to less than the IRR, we will have a positive NPV.



Hope this is helpful and not TMI.


-----
[1] There are other types of rates of return besides the internal rate of return.  For example, see http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modified_internal_rate_of_return.  I do not recommend the MIRR for your purposes.  I think it will simply add another layer of confusion.  I mention it only for completeness.


[2] Depending on interpretation, there could be different ways to calculate the cash flows, resulting in a different IRR.  Not to cast aspersions on your method.  Again, I mention this only for completeness.


[3] I am not trying to be unduly harsh.  I am just making some observations objectively.  I have trouble understanding the operation of transistors.  As a consequence, I am forever asking the wrong questions.  The point is:  there are some things that each individual has trouble understanding.  For you today, it is the relationship of IRR and NPV.


[4] I mean lower and higher than the actual IRR, not the actual value is about 0.76%, which you can see if you change the cell format.  So by "lower", I mean something less than 0.76%.  And by "higher", I mean something more than 0.76% -- actually 0.77% or more, just to be sure.


[5] [With errata.]  I emphasize "with your cash flows" and "in your example" because my comments depend on the fact that:  (a) your initial cash flow is negative and the subsequent cash flows are positive; and (b) the cash flows change sign only once.

In other situations, "the" IRR might be less meaningful.  In fact, there might be multiple IRRs; and Excel might find the "wrong" one (depending on expectation and interpretation).  The situation to watch out for is when the cash flows change signs more than once.  If you find this comment confusing, simply ignore it -- for now.  Again, I mention it only for completeness.


[6] That begs the question:  how was the WACC determined in the first place?  For some (rudimentary) insight, see http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weighted_average_cost_of_capital.

jamesyan_0708 say:

Hi Lifeis,

Changing the domain hosting provider will have an impact (down time) on the Email service and SharePoint Online Public website.
To make the down time as short as possible, we suggest you follow the steps below:

1. At the new hosting provider, add the domain you are planning to transfer, and then add all DNS records required by Office 365 on the domain. Please refer to Create DNS records for Office 365 at any DNS hosting provider.
2. Change the NS records of the domain at your domain registrar to point to the new hosting provider.

For the website service, we need to add an A record in the new DNS hosting provider to route traffic to your website. For your reference: Update DNS records to keep your website with your current hosting provider.
If you need help adding the DNS records, we suggest you turn to your domain hosting provider or your domain registrar.

About yout questions:

"Will all other sub domain records still work correctly?", yes, but only after all the required records are added and verified in the new DNS hosting provider.

"Will email accounts be safe?", as mentioned above, there will be a downtime during the changing nameserver process, but the Email hostory will not lost.

"Where would the DNS be routed to in order for the above to work?", do you mean changing the nameserver?
If yes, we need to point the nameserver to the new hosting provider.
If not, could you share more details about it?

Regards,

James
