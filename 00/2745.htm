rob koch say:

Though you've already received some good answers and advice, I'm going to point you to some additional information about the malware infections you experienced to try and help you understand the complexity of your primary questions.

 

First, both of these malware are well known, but have many variations changing relatively quickly as the following Encyclopedia search clearly shows, which is the reason that no anti-malware can truly keep current.

http://www.microsoft.com/security/portal/threat/encyclopedia/search.aspx?query=Win32/Beebone

 

The other important point is that these two detections are both known not only to obfuscate (e.g. encrypt or otherwise constantly modify to avoid detection), but also to download each other and/or other malware as part of their technical descriptions below.

http://www.microsoft.com/security/portal/threat/encyclopedia/entry.aspx?Name=Win32/Beebone#techdetails_link

http://www.microsoft.com/security/portal/threat/encyclopedia/Entry.aspx?Name=Worm%3aWin32%2fVobfus#techdetails_link

 

So though the specific malware involved may be known, it's entirely possible that they were either too new or even too old to be incuded in current detections contained on the affected PC, since the local definitions are a constantly evolving window of the millions of known malware and not a complete representation of all malware ever identified.  This is a confusing thing to most, since they don't have an understanding that all malware identified to this point would fill literally Terabytes of disk space.  So even the usually smaller signatures and definitions for these would require Gigabytes to store locally and would thus overwhelm the storage and cripple the performace of even a typical modern PC.

 

So though we don't and may never know the specific reason that the initial malware wasn't detected, it really doesn't matter.  What does matter is that the various mitigation steps mentioned by others here, some of which you've already perfromed, must be taken and constantly maintained to reduce the chances of this occurring again.

 

The best recommendation above would be to aggressively upgrade those badly outdated Windows XP based systems, since that operating system is ancient and archaic in a security sense and in fact will no longer be supported by Microsoft as of a year from this coming Monday, on April 8th, 2014.

 

Though this won't guarantee that malware will be unable to successfully attack and in this particular case would have been unlikely to have completely stopped it, the better security profile of the newer operating systems would likely have limited the successful reinfection of other systems and the depths to which the initial system became infected.  Nothing you can do with Windows XP including adding 3rd-party security software can even approach the improvement provided by replacing it with a current version of Windows 8 or even 7 for that matter.

 

Rob
