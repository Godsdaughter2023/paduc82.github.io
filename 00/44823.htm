4K Scaling: Windows 10 vs 7 - Microsoft Community

chupalo-00 say:

4K Scaling: Windows 10 vs 7

Howdy folks,

I bought a 4K monitor during the recent holiday sales and want to really love this resolution.  I'm running Windows 7 Pro 64-bit and a video card that supports 4K @ 30fps.  Some things look great, some are disappointing.

I was wondering if an upgrade to Windows 10 would make a significant difference or not with 4K resolution and DPI scaling.

Thanks in advance for any comments.

-C

ralf_g say:

"... if an upgrade to Windows 10 would make a significant difference or not with 4K resolution and DPI scaling."

IMO, most likely not.

 

You could check if there is any image quality difference when switching between the monitor's internal upscaling facility (if it has any) and upscaling using the video card drivers instead. Some devices may do a better looking job of upscaling than others. Upscaling in the video card would tend to increase the load on it and could impact performance, especially if you are already operating near the card's limits.

Upscaling that is done using the monitor's firmware won't look any different regardless of the computer's OS. A significant difference in the  video card driver for one OS version or the other might have an impact on upscaling quality if the upscaling is done using the graphics card hardware. If the graphics driver version numbers are the same for both OS's though I wouldn't expect any difference in image quality unless stated otherwise by the graphics card manufacturer. An upgraded graphics card could give more satisfying results than changing the OS.

In general the quality of the source media directly impacts the quality of the upscaled display image. Upscaling images does not increase their resolution in any way, so an image or video that is upscaled to 4k from a 1080p/720p/480i/etc. source will never have the same level of detail as an image or video that was actually captured in 4k resolution. Upscaled images only mathematically fudge in more screen pixels to display the exact same level of detail as exists in the original lower resolution source images.
