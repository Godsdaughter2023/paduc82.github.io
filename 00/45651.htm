a. user say:

Hi James,

 

Are you trying to connect the device through Bluetooth?

 

Here are a few steps which you can follow:

 

1. Make sure the device you're trying to pair with isn't turned off, low on battery power, or in standby mode (also called sleep mode).

 

2. Make sure that you typed the correct pairing code (sometimes called a PIN or passcode) for the device.

 

3. Make sure software drivers for the device have been properly installed.

 

4. Windows tries to select the best pairing method for your device and give you instructions that often include a pairing code. However, some wireless devices come with their own pairing code, which you'll need to find.

 

5. Check the information that came with your device. The pairing code might be listed there or printed on the device itself (often on the bottom). Not all wireless devices come with a pairing code.

 

6. If Windows encounters problems during the pairing process, it might give you the option of creating your own pairing code. If you choose this option, you don't need to use the code that came with the device.

 

7. Make sure that you're typing the pairing code quickly enough. You usually have only 30 seconds before the device or computer stops trying to pair. The exact amount of time depends on the device.

 

Also refer to the section 'To add Bluetooth enabled devices' from the link:

Add, remove, and manage your devices and printers

http://windows.microsoft.com/en-us/windows-8/add-view-manage-devices-printers

 
Hope this helps. If you have any further queries/issues related to Windows, write to us and we will be glad to assist you further.

You got it, Frank.  Plus I may have found a good complement to your fine detective work.  Please bear with me here.
To begin, a nice part of using the MCT/ISO Windows Setup versus the internal Windows Updater is you see Installing Windows full status with specific progress details rather than mere overall completion.  Following are the Setup steps and status process.

  • Copying Windows files
  • Getting files ready for installation
  • Installing features
  • Installing updates (1%)
  • Finishing up

When my clean install reached "Installing updates" it rushed by so quickly you could have, almost literally, "missed it if you blinked."  From the beginning of your post I worried what the various installation iterations would do when no Internet connection was found.  I should have realized neither the Updater or the executables have the capacity for a, "Hey, turn on the  #$@&%*! Wi-Fi!" pop-up.  Confirmation came when I was surprised by a short update not long after 1709 completed.

So, looking back at the status steps "Installing updates" likely ends at the Updater's 80% mark.  And that's our invariable doomsday freeze.  Conversely, "Installing updates" likely begins at the Updater's 75%, another universal freeze point.  I believe as the Updater and the executables when not in clean install seek Internet access for "Installing updates" the Ralink goes from unstable to fatal.  Perhaps updates installation is where your disabled LAN card reactivated.  Microsoft's suggestion to turn off Wi-Fi becomes pointless when a script activates a problem LAN or WAN.  

Or perhaps your LAN was reactivated during "Getting files ready for installation."  That just so happens to line up with our universal freeze at 30%.  However, I've no conjecture for why my own disabled card smoothly passed 30% with first the Updater and subsequently an MCT/ISO clean install.  As I've noted there were no freezes --

So with our dilemma likely resolved, I notice many reported failures -- perhaps a majority -- occur at identical or similar progress points.  That hints at additional LAN or WAN culprits.  Plus I now firmly believe those unable to install Windows 10 despite trying every Microsoft suggestion are overwhelmingly card/driver cases.  And while the company does emphasize updating drivers, in my case the Ralink has the latest.  Which leaves the potential benefits from aligning failure points to Windows Setup's five steps. Though I concede the steps are quite cryptic.  "Installing updates" may be the lone exception.

techfreak_ say:

It appears on August 8th Skype credit was purchased using your Apple device. This would be a purchase someone agreed to in the Skype App on your Apple device that is separate from your subscription or any possible Auto-Recharge. You or someone using your device may have made this purchase for any reason, regardless of your subscription and its current minute balance. This would have added C$6.99 to your current Skype credit balance. Skype credit would only be consumed when dialing a number not covered by your current subscription or if covered, your subscription had no remaining minutes. If you ran out of minutes on the 8th and you were prompted to purchase credit, you consumed all 1000 minutes.

Billing of your subscription occurs every month whether or not your minutes are consumed (billed 3 days prior to the expiration and minute refresh). Be aware that all consumed minutes are rounded up. You can check your minute balance at any time by viewing your subscription details in your account. Based on the above, your subscription cycle is probably the 22nd to the 22nd (billed on the 19th, refreshed on the 22nd). If you originally purchased the subscription at 1:25 PM on the 22nd, the minutes would refresh at 1:25 PM on the 22nd of the following month.

If you want to manually calculate your minute consumption you cannot simply total the minutes and seconds of your usage. Anything you total up must be rounded up to the next whole minute prior to adding. For example, if you make a 10 second call, it consumes 1 minute.  So 6 calls like that would deduct 6/7 minutes, not 1 minute. If your call lasts 1 minute and 1 second, 2 minutes are deducted from your subscription, etc.
