Then your table is incorrectly designed: you're "committing spreadsheet". What will you do when you need to deal with 2016 data in three years? You'll need to change your table structure, rewrite all your queries, change all your forms, change all your reports... OUCH!!!

 

Data (e.g. a tax year) should not be stored in fieldnames but only in fields in a table. If you have one Account with multiple TaxYears, the proper structure is two tables in a one to many relationship; the TaxYears table would have as many records as there are tax reports. To solve your report problem with your current structure, you need to be aware that the Count() function doesn't count checkboxes; it counts records, no matter what's in the record. To find how many boxes are checked you can take advantage of the fact that a Yes/No (checkbox) field is stored as -1 for Yes/True/Checked and 0 for No/False/Unchecked; so you could use

 

= -Sum([T 2013])

 

If four boxes are checked, the Sum would be -4; taking the negative of this of course gives you the desired 4.

I know how frustrating it is to be unable to receive important emails. We have to consider several causes why you are having trouble receiving emails such as:

Add the email address to your Safe senders list

1. In the upper-right corner of the page, click the gear icon, and then click More mail settings. Under Preventing junk email, click Safe and blocked senders.

2. Click Safe Senders.

3. In the text box, type in the email address or domain you’d like to always receive email from, and then click Add to list >>.

Check your Email Rules

1. In the upper-right corner of the page, click the gear icon, and then click More mail settings. Under Customizing Outlook, click Rules for sorting new messages.

Or

Note: Not all international characters are supported as filters.

Check your Reply-to address

1. In the upper-right corner of the page, click the gear icon, and then click More mail settings. Under Writing email, click Reply-to address.

3. Click Save.

Lets take another look at this to make sure we get it correct; or at least as I understand it, LOL.  

If you have a legal copy of Windows 7, 8 or 8.1 on one of your computers you will get the full retail version of Windows 10 on that computer for free.

If however you are running a NON-genuine copy of Windows 7, 8 or 8.1 you will still get a copy of Windows 10 for free but it will be the "Preview Version".  Preview versions are provided to "insiders" for the purposes of testing FUTURE releases of Windows products.  As such, they may not be fully stable operating systems meant for either home or business use.    

So if you are in business instead of an old retired dude like me who likes to play with computers, you might want to lay out some cash for a full retail version of Windows 10 IF YOU ARE USING THAT COMPUTER for business purposes or desire a more stable home computer.
