Hello ElizabethGrunza

You can use Reset PC Option:

Resetting lets you choose whether to keep your files or remove them, and then reinstalls Windows. To get started, go to Settings > Update & Security > Recovery> Reset this PC > Get started and choose an option.

Note
If you can't open Settings, you can get to reset by restarting your PC from the sign-in screen. Press the Windows logo key +L to get to the sign-in screen, and then hold the Shift key while you select Power > Restart in the lower-right corner of the screen. After your PC restarts, select Troubleshoot > Reset this PC. If you can't get to the sign-in screen, use a recovery drive or installation media to reset your PC.

Option What it does
* Keep my files
Reinstalls Windows 10 and keeps your personal files.
Removes apps and drivers you installed.
Removes changes you made to settings.
Removes any apps your PC manufacturer installed. (If your PC came with Windows 10, apps from your PC manufacturer will be reinstalled.)

* Remove everything
Reinstalls Windows 10 and removes all your personal files.
Removes apps and drivers you installed.
Removes changes you made to settings.
Removes any apps your PC manufacturer installed. (If your PC came with Windows 10, apps from your PC manufacturer will be reinstalled.)

If you're planning to donate, recycle, or sell your PC, use this option and choose to fully clean the drive. This might take an hour or two, but it makes it harder for other people to recover files you've removed.

* Restore factory settings
Reinstalls the version of Windows that your PC came with (either Windows 8 or Windows 8.1) and removes your personal files.
Removes apps and drivers you installed.
Removes changes you made to settings.
Reinstalls any apps your PC manufacturer installed on your PC.

This option isn't available on all PCs.

Note
The option in Settings to go back to your previous version of Windows is only available for a limited time after upgrading.

To get more info please visit the following page:
https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/help/12415/...

williambelmont say:

So just in case anyone wants to know,

A bug (roach, spider, whatever) touching the Sensor Chip

(Small chip to the bottom left of the Light Ring on the Front Face PCB, can cause random on/off as well as Display Resolution Reset.)

You can Trigger the yourself by simply touching the chip, even with the Eject and Power Buttons disconnected.

While all the Guides I looked at online said I had to take the unit apart, the top and bottom covers and remove warranty stickers etc etc.

I didnt have to do any of that, the front Faceplate is held on by 8 clip tabs.

once you remove the 2 layers of side vents by unclipping the tabs gently, you can safely unclip the 8 Clips for the front face plate, it takes a lot of work and light, but once they are all unclipped the front plate slides off, just dont yank it, because the touch sensor ribbon cable is attached to it. you have to unclip the pressure clip to detach the ribbon cable from the PCB.

from there I was able to remove anything and everything from around the Sensor Chip, including the spider that was camping out on the black power button back plate, and had 2 legs randomly grazing the Sensor Chip triggering the random on/off sequence.

Put everything back on the PCB, Slid the front face plate back in, it clipped on no problem, then put the side vents back on.

If it becomes a regular problem with a bug triggering the Sensor Chip. I'll see about putting something over it so it's sealed and nothing can touch it.

You'd think that if this Chip is so Sensitive to dust webs, bugs and debris, that MS woulda put a layer of clear glue or something over it to keep random items from triggering it.

Also, I Visited a friend today who had the same problem, and it turned out to be a roach that was stuck on the sensor chip, once I Cleaned it off safely and re-assembled his unit, his problem went away. (the roach was dead and just dangling on the sensor).

Account details are held in the Windows registry. You can restore your registry files manually to an earlier point by going through the steps outlined below. The process is involved and requires some careful typing. Ask a computer-savvy friend to assist you if you lack experience in this area. Note that it might reset your password to an earlier value.
1. Keep tapping F8 during the early boot phase.
2. Select Repair from the menu that looks like this one:
   http://windows.microsoft.com/en-US/windows-vista/Advanced-startup-options-including-safe-mode
3. Log on as Administrator if prompted.
4. Select Repair when prompted.
5. Select the Command Prompt from the menu that looks like this one:
   http://windows.microsoft.com/en-US/windows7/What-are-the-system-recovery-options-in-Windows-7
6. Identify the correct drive letter for your Windows installation. It could be a drive other than C: but it won't be X:. You do it by typing the commands
    dir C:\Win*
    dir D:\Win*
    dir E:\Win*
    until you find the Windows folder.
    I will call that drive "Q:".
7. Type these commands:
    cd /d Q:\windows\System32\config
    xcopy *.* Q:\MyRegBack\
    cd  RegBack
    dir
8. Examine the dates of the Software, System and SAM files. Were the files created before or after your problem became apparent?
9. If they were made before the problem became apparent then you can type these commands:
    copy /y software  ..
    copy /y System  ..
    copy /y Sam ..
    (the two dots are part of each command)
10. Reboot normally. If things do not work out then you can backtrack by copying the original registry files from Q:\MyRegBack to Q:\Windows\System32\config.

alexandria1111 say:

To all who have had this same problem...I discovered that what I described is a custom back door Trojan with browser information being redirected to a vitual fax that I guess to ending on a Linux system.  The solution I applied was a follows:

I deleted the vitual fax and unhooked its associations.

To resolve the blank gray box in the microsoft download page, I discovered that registry entries for PROTOCOLS had been changed and filters added.

Rather than worrying about all the associated registry entries, I made two significant changes that unhooked all of the associations and I am able to view and download from the Microsoft site.

I edited the key  

HKEY_LOCAL_MACHINE\Software\Microsoft\
Internet Explorer\Main

HKEY_LOCAL_MACHINE>Software>Classes>
Protocols>Filter>

HKEY_CLASSES_ROOT>Protocols>Filter>

rather than removing the keys which would make the registry unstable, I removed the CLSID information which directs the filters.

On each filter such as application/x-msdownload, this key I left intact, but modified the CLSID by deleting the information that directs this key.

But unless you have the skills and knowledge to edit the registry, I do not recommend it for novice users. Try first doing a system restore to an earlier point. The restore point would have to be a point in time prior to these changes made in the registry and finding out when this happened maybe a guessing game. Editing the registry manually can be fatal and if done wrong stop your computer from working. Consult a professional.
