ccazang say:

Winsox,

 

What capabilities each devices support is up to them, and it is not that easy to just add media capabilities support to your device. FIrst of all, the space is too wide. there are hundreds of media profiles associated with tens of media types in DLNA alone. And DLNA does not define all the formats that you can find on the market (mkv and divx are not included in DLNA). So space is too wide and you cannot please everyone. Also, to support some certain formats you also need some licensing in place, which in some cases it can add up to your price.

Normally, what Win7 does is to offer transcode capabilities that the devices can take advantage of. THat means offloading the burden of supporting a lot of formats from the device to Windows. (there is also the advantage of updating the PC much easier than the TV :).

Win 7 DMS provides several URIs for each media item (we call them res elements): one is for native format, and the others are various transcoded elements. We transcode to MPEG2 and WMV so far. But depending on how powerfull your machine is, the WIn7 PC can generate up to 40 transcoded res elements for only one file. This includes various level of resolutions. The devices have the option to choose from those what is best for them. If they think they can do native, you get the best quality possible. If they do not, then they can pick up the right resolution for their screen size.

Now, there are two main scenarios that people may try: when you pull from the device (DLNA DMP scenario) or when you PlayTo the device (DLNA DMC scenario). In the DMP Scenario, is up to the device to pick up the right element for playback. They know the size of their screen and what codecs they implement. So they have all the tools to make the right decision. If your TV is a 55'' TV and picks up the 480x240 res element, you will get the worst experience ever. If your TV is 19'' and picks up the same res element you may not be that annoyed with the experience.

In the DMC scenario, we send to the TV what we think is best for them. We verify their capabilities and push only those res elements that can make sense for them. If they support native and they do not advertise that capability, the user will get only transcoded to play for that specific content. If they do not support content and we are forced to send transcoded, we will always send the lowest resolution first (that is because there is no way to find out from the DMC point of view, the resolution of the TV). We also send the rest of the res elements that matches the capabilties of that TV, so they can ignore the one sent as a default from us and pick up the highest resolution available from the other alternative res elements that we send.

However, many devices have problems to choose and it is difficult to constrain them to do it right.

In your case, WTV and DVR-MS are formats that not many devices render natively. The XBOX 360 probably has native support for them (even though I doubt, I am not sure and I do not have an XBOX360 in hand right now), but the XBOX may go for the highest resolution available among those transcoded res elements. THis is why it works better for you.

If you have problem with the PlayTo pushing transcoding to a device that renders that format nativelly that is a device issue and there is way to workarround that (just let me know).

I do not know personally the Wild Media Server (I will check it out), but I do not think they do transcoding. So it may work for you as long as the device supports native

I hope all these answers to your questions.
