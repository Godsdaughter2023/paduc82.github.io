lemp say:

 Is that small black antenna important enough for me to hunt for it and put it back up on my desk?

Yes.

You have two antennas on your router ... but that small black antenna is what connects your computer to the router.  Think of it this way:  before cable TV, a TV station might have an antenna on the top of a very tall building (e.g., the Empire State Building in New York City) or at least on a tower that was several hundred feet high.  That corresponds to the two antennas on your router.  Your home TV set needed a rooftop antenna or worse, a pair of "rabbit ears" on top of the TV (maybe you're not old enough to remember these).  That corresponds to the small antenna connected to your computer.  If the rooftop antenna fell down, you wouldn't get very good reception.

If when the antenna fell down the wire became disconnected from your computer, that almost certainly is the cause of your problem.  If the wire is still connected, the location of the antenna still may be the cause of your problem.

Go to the back of your computer, if the antenna wire is still connected, gently pull on it.  If you're lucky, the antenna won't be caught on anything and you can retrieve it just like hauling in a fish.  When you get it, put it someplace near the router or at least where there is no metal object between the antennas on the router and the small black antenna.

If the antenna wire became disconnected and fell behind your desk along with the antenna, you're going to have to do some excavation work.

The answer to your last question is a bit complicated.

Your Ethernet connection is a 100 Mbps connection.  That's about 12.5 megabytes per second. 

The wireless portion of your ZyXEL Q1000z is a "wireless-n" device, nominally capable of 300 mbps data rate.  The wireless adapter in your computer is 802.11 b/g/n, so theoretically, you can use the full capacity of your router.  As a practical matter, however, wireless-n will probably give you a throughput around 160 Mbps or so which translates to 20 megabytes per second.  So, if you were getting a good wireless connection, your wifi would be faster than your Ethernet.

As the radio connection between the router and your wireless adapter gets poorer, either because of distance or because the antenna is disconnected or blocked by some object that is not transparent to radio signals, the speed of the wireless connection will fall markedly.

It's important to remember that those speeds (data transfer rates) are for data transferred between the router and your computer.  Unless you're dealing with other computers on your local network, your data transfer rate is likely going to be limited by the speed of your connection to the Internet.  Unless you're paying for a really high speed cable connection, your connection to the Internet is likely to be considerably slower than the 100 Mbps that is the nominal data rate of the Ethernet connection.
