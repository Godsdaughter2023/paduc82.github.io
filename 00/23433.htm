john mcghie say:
Hi Steve:

The answer is "Always use a style to apply numbering."  The reason it happened is "Because you changed the list template" :-)

Sadly, they tried to "make it easy to use" by removing most of the controls, so it is nearly impossible to see what you are doing.  A bit like making cars easier to drive by removing the sterring-wheel and brake pedal!  They also decided that the explanation was too complex for us "Users" so they removed that too.  So now, you not only can't control it easily, you can't learn how to control it at all.  I have had "words" with them about this (and been ignored...)

OK, now to try to cut the explanation down below 20 pages...

A Word paragraph is formatted by applying a set of properties to the text.  The ones we are interested in here are the "left indent", the "first line indent" and the "Restarts" property.  The restarts property is either True or False: if it is True, the list numbering goes back to the "Starts At" value (which may or may not be "1").

Normally, all of these properties are contained in a collection named a "Style" (except "restarts", which is a tag in the text).

However, if you do not use styles to format your text, Word splits the properties up into subsets, and applies "parts" of the formatting from various different containers.  Your indents may come from a "heading" style, your numbering from a "List" style, your font from "Normal" style...

The "numbering" components come from a container named a "List Template".  This sets all the numbering behaviour, including the indent, first-line indent, and tab position.

However:  The style you applied also contains "indent" and "first line indent" and "tab position" (as well as several hundred other settings).  If you have not applied a style to the text, then Word has done so for you (because it can't store formatting anywhere else).  So unless you have said otherwise, Word has applied the "Normal" style to your text.

If you applied the numbering using the button on the Ribbon in Word 2011, Word automatically switched the style to "List Paragraph" when you applied the numbering.  If the document was not created in a modern version of Word, the List Paragraph style would not exist.  Word would then "invent" a new style for you, and hide it from you.

I am skipping a large chunk here: if you're interested, post back and I will give you the rest of it...

So:  At some point in the list, you decide to re-start the numbering at "1".

If you have applied the numbering using a style (say, you customised List Number style and used that) then the reset will just happen, and you won't see the difference.

Word internally runs a command that says "Re-apply numbering here, with Restart set to True".

If you did NOT apply numbering with a style, Word re-applies the most recent numbering format used.  And that format may not have been used in the current document.  So you see a shift: often pronounced, in the indentation.  Usually inwards, but it doesn't have to be.

The cure is:  "Always apply numbering using a style."  That way, you ensure that the numbering template and every other formatting setting for a numbered paragraph are all held together in a single known container.  When Word re-applies the numbering, it re-applies the CORRECT numbering format, and your indents won't move.

The styles whose names begin with "List" are pre-set with all the correct widgets to do bullets and numbering properly.  The "Heading" series are preset to do Heading Numbering correctly (and they can't be re-started, they have a special tweak in them to prevent people trying and getting themselves into an impenetrable mess).

Rule of thumb:  Always use the "Heading" styles for numbered headings, always use the "List" styles for numbered anything else.

    e.j. gun say:

    First, try starting Excel in "Safe Mode", which will prevent any add-ins from loading.  To do this, first you must find the Excel program icon using Windows Explorer.  The Excel program is usually stored in a folder such as:

    C:\Program Files (x86)\Microsoft Office\Office12

    Go to this folder, and find EXCEL.EXE.  Hold down the CTRL key, and double-click on EXCEL.EXE.  A dialog box comes up asking if you want to open Excel in Safe Mode.  Click Yes.

    Try creating and moving your graph while in Safe Mode.  Do you get the same result, or do things seem "normal".  If things are now normal, then an add-in is likely the problem.

    If an add-in is to blame, the next step is to disable all add-ins, and turn them back on one at a time, until you find the one that is causing the problem for you.  To do this, restart Excel in "Normal" mode (not Safe Mode).  Go to Excel Options, click on Add-Ins, and then click on the "Go..." button to manage Excel add-ins.  You will see a list of all add-ins.  If any are checked (meaning they are enabled), uncheck them.  Keep a record of which add-ins were checked.  Now repeat this for the COM add-ins - turn them all off.

    Restart Excel one more time.  Try creating/moving the graph again.

    Did this solve your problem?  If it did, one of the add-ins is the problem.  Turn on add-ins one at a time until the problem shows up again.  The last add-in you turn back on will be the one causing the problem.
