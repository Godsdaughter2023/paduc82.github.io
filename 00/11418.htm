hansv mvp say:

You can turn off the ability to recalculate a specific sheet altogether by setting its EnableCalculation property to False. When you do so, neither automatic nor manual calculation will work for that sheet. When you set EnableCalculation back to True, and if the Calculation Options dropdown has been set to Automatic, the sheet will immediately be recalculated.

 

Apart from that, the Calculation Options dropdown corresponds to an application-wide setting: the Application.Calculation property. There is no workbook-level or worksheet-level Calculation property.

joeu2004 say: HansV wrote:

You can turn off the ability to recalculate a specific sheet altogether by setting its EnableCalculation property to False.


But beware that worksheet.EnableCalculation reverts to True whenever the file is reopened.

Sometimes that causes an immediate recalculation when Excel is in auto-calculate mode.

Sometimes the recalculation occurs later, e.g. after ctrl+shift+alt+F9.  But sometimes even less-pervasive events cause recalculation.  It is really pernickety.

So I include a Workbook_Open event macro to ensure that the EnableCalculation=True is reasserted.

And I use the worksheet object name as the qualifier, not the worksheet name.  For example, Sheet3.EnableCalculation instead of Sheet("sheet3").EnableCalculation.

That allows me to rename the worksheet without having to modify references in Workbook_Open.

Problems viewing or saving webpage history may occur for one or more of the following reasons:

  • This issue can occur if you have the Delete browsing history on exit box checked in your Internet options.
  • If your hard disk is getting full, you may need to free up disk space.
  • The number of days that Internet Explorer keeps a history of the webpages you have visited may be set to 0 or a low number. After that period of time, the history is automatically deleted.
  • Your history may be corrupt or locked by another process.
  • Registry keys may have become corrupt or modified.
  • Your user profile may be corrupt.

Perform the steps provided here and check.

 

Method 1: Check to make sure the Delete browsing history on exit box in your Internet Options is not checked.

 

a. Click on Start and search for 'Internet Options'.

b. Under the General tab, and then under Browsing History, make sure the Delete browsing history on exit box in not checked.

 

Method 2: Check to see if there is enough free space on your hard disk

a. Click on Start and search for 'Internet Options'.

b. Under the General tab, and then under Browsing History, click Settings. Under Disk space to use, make sure the disk space is set to 250MB, and under History, make sure the Days to keep pages in history is set to 20.


Note: If you don’t want a webpage history kept, set the number of days to 0.

 

Method 3: Delete Temporary Internet Files.

 

a. Click on Start and search for 'Internet Options'.

b. Under the General tab, and then under Browsing History, click Delete.

c. In the Delete Browsing History dialog box, select Temporary Internet files, Cookies, and History, click Delete, and then click OK.

Win-  represents a Windows Logo key on a keyboard in conjunction with another key.   So Win-U  means press a  Windows Logo key and at the same time press a U.   That should bring up the  Windows Accessibility Tools  dialog and allow you to start the XP Magnifier.   In XP the Magnifier is always docked at the top of the screen and moves everything down, even maximized windows.   So if you lose the Magnifier dock somehow without turning it off normally you could see your symptom--a window in maximized mode which does not cover the screen.  

Another way to fix that symptom is to play with the size of your Taskbar while it is docked (e.g. with its Auto-Hide option unchecked).   Then usually you will see windows which are supposed to be in maximized mode being redrawn to fill the new display area.
