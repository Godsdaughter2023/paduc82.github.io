ken sheridan say: Division of the number of days by 365 returns an approximation due to its failure to take account of leap years.  Division by 365.25 gives a more accurate result.  This can be demonstrated in the debug window with my own date of birth of 1 November 1946 as an example:

DoB = #1946-11-01#
? Round((Date()-DoB)/365.25,2)
 70.81
? Round((Date()-DoB)/365,2)
 70.85

As you can see the difference is quite significant.  The greater accuracy of the former can easily be demonstrated by first getting the number of days between my last birthday and the current date:

? Date()-#2016-11-01#
 294

This year not being a leap year we can divide this by 365 to get this as a fraction of the year:
 
? Round(294/365,2)
 0.81

As you can see this equates to the first result, where we divided by 365.25, when rounded to two significant decimal places.  This is still an approximation as the calendar year is not of a fixed length, but it is a very close approximation.  If we return the results without rounding we get:

? (Date()-DoB)/365.25
 70.8062970568104

? 294/365
 0.805479452054795

We can see the difference in the fractional values with:

? 0.8062970568104 - 0.805479452054795
 8.17604755604995E-04

which, when converted to decimal notation is:

0.000817604755604995

which is insignificant.

The degree of approximation reduces with the age of the person, e.g. with my eldest son:

DoB = #1977-09-16#
? Round((Date()-DoB)/365.25,2)
 39.93
? Round((Date()-DoB)/365,2)
 39.96

it is reduced to 0.3, and with my granddaughter:

DoB = #2010-05-05#
? Round((Date()-DoB)/365.25,2)
 7.3  
? Round((Date()-DoB)/365,2)
 7.3

it disappears when rounded to two significant decimal places.

PS: rounding to only one decimal place might well increase the discrepancy of course. e.g with my son:

DoB = #1977-09-16#
? Round((Date()-DoB)/365.25,1)
 39.9
? Round((Date()-DoB)/365,1)
 40

  1. I checked the hash code of ISO, English (United States) version of Windows 10 TP x64 Build 9926, which I used for making the UEFI bootable USB3.0 USB stick. It was the same as the value specified in the download page. So ISO seems okay.

  8. I heard some other people experienced the same problem with their ThinkPad X1 Carbon. So it is not limited to my ThinkPad X230.

Compatibility mode IS the workaround.

Word's text generation "engine" is different in Word 2010 and 2016.

Properly formatted Word documents will not experience any major problems because the text reflow will be governed by style settings rather than empty paragraphs and manual page breaks. However, such "properly formatted" documents are likely rare.

Grammar Check "have to" versus "must" - my concerns - Microsoft Community

a. user say:

Grammar Check "have to" versus "must" - my concerns

Hello,

 

I have some concerns regarding Microsoft Word's grammar check. When creating a document for a graduate English class, it asked to change my statement “you have to remember” to the suggested change, “you must remember.” This is not what I intended to say. To begin, “you have to remember” is a somewhat idiomatic expression: it acts as a familiar statement that is appropriate to a reading response, the type of writing I was engaged in when prompted with this question. You “must” remember implies a type of imperative, carrying a far more instructional connotation. As stated by the OED, “must” is “used to show that it is necessary or very important that something happens in the present or future.” While I would like people to remember the comment I was making, I hardly think it is very important or necessary that they do so. Furthermore, I do not believe my open-ended reading response necessitates such an extreme command towards remembrance.

The larger matter at stake regards a global issue regarding the wisdom and suitability of suggesting such a change as part of the grammar check’s programming. In many contexts, “have to” is a far more appropriate, and frankly, accurate representation of a person’s thinking. For example, I might say, “I have to walk the dog at six every morning.” This carries an idiomatic, recognizable format. It states that walking the dog is a necessary, albeit perhaps inconvenient part of my routine. “I must walk the dog at six every morning” seems to imply a type of forced task that should be completed thanklessly or a critical mission that shall be completed by command.

I respectfully ask that you reconsider this use of grammar check. Indeed, I do not believe that the suggested change from “have to” to “must” or “should” represents a helpful or essential service to writers. Rather, it seeks to rob writers of their intention and meaning in a misguided attempt to vouchsafe some type of elegance, clarity, or style that the initial writer does not possess or intend.

Cordially,

AW

Hi Noel,

If lots of your OneDrive files get deleted, overwritten, corrupted, or infected by malware, you can restore your entire OneDrive to a previous time. Files Restore helps Office 365 subscribers undo all the actions that occurred on both files and folders within the last 30 days. For your reference: Restore your OneDrive.

However, please notice:

1. This action restores the entire OneDrive but not only the affected folder. In case of any data loss, you need to back up other files in OneDrive to another safe location. If you still have a concern about this point, I have shared with you a private message for another suggestion. You can check there via this link.

2. If the files had been corrupted before you moved them to OneDrive, this action can’t help you.
