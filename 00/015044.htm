suzanne s. barnhill say:

I did get a quick response from the product team, and they confirm that what is operating here is the HTML paragraph auto spacing I mentioned. That it operates even across a hard page break is a bug, which they believe has been corrected by "the new layout engine" in Word 2013, but you will continue to experience it in earlier versions. You can, however, eliminate the phenomenon by checking the Compatibility Option "Don't use HTML paragraph auto spacing."

 

Since your version is actually 2010/2007 rather than 2013, I am going to correct the "Applies to" on your original post. While perhaps not relevant, it would be helpful if you would also correct the Windows version by editing your question.

 

For reference, here's what I got from the Word product team member: "This is actually a known quirk of Word's layout: when your paragraphs are next to each other, we use the space after the first one and the space before the second one to determine their distance apart (i.e. ,a paragraph with 10pt after followed by a paragraph with 18pt before are 18pts apart, not 28pts). In this case, we're applying that logic without taking into account the page break before, so the Heading 1 at the top of the second page is getting space before equivalent to the difference between the space after the previous paragraph and its own space before (e.g., if you set the space after on the previous paragraph to 18 pt, it will effectively become 6pts [24pt-18pt])."

ossiemac say:

Instead of simply opening the csv file, follow the following steps to import the csv file into a blank worksheet.

You can turn on the macro recorder to get the code required for automation but I suggest that you experiment with the process first.

Note that this method does not alter the original csv file. The data is imported into a new workbook.

  1. Open Excel (with new workbook and cell A1 as the selected cell.)
  2. Select Data ribbon.
  3. In the ribbon's "Get external data" block click the "From text" icon.
  4. Browse to the csv file, select it and click Import button.
  5. On the first dialog screen select "Delimited" option and then click Next button.
  6. Next dialog screen check the Comma option and uncheck all others and then click Next button.
  7. Under "Data preview" dialog click in the column that has the dates to highlight the column.
  8. Select "Date" option.
  9. In the dropdown field next to date option select the existing date format in the csv file. (NOT the date format that you want to finish up with).
  10. Click Finish and ensure "=$A$1" is start point for import and click OK.

Note that in step 9 it is important to select the date format that is existing in the csv file because that is what you need to tell Excel.  Excel will then recognize the date and convert it to the regional date format of your computer during the import.

Footnote Formatting

Everytime I add in footnotes, they automatically formate with a .5 inch indent and with a justified format.  How can I permenently change this so that I am not constantly re-formatting footnotes, which drives me crazy.
stefan blom say:

In Word 2007 or 2010, do the following:

  1. Press Ctrl+Shift+S to display the Apply Styles pane.
  2. Type in Footnote Text.
  3. Click the Modify... button.
  4. In the Modify Style dialog box, click Format | Paragraph and change the alignment and indentation.
  5. Click OK.
  6. Before clicking OK to exit the Modify Style dialog, select "New documents based on this template."
