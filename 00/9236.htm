me.txtActive = me.txtDescription

    Hi,

    ossiemac say:

    My interpretation of your question is that you have a table somewhere in the workbook something like the following screen capture example.

    I am further interpreting that you want to insert a formula on say Sheet1 to look up the value against Sheet1 in the table but you want the formula to identify its own sheet name. Is this correct? If so, then the following formula where the lookup value is replaced with a nested formula in the Vlookup formula.

    =VLOOKUP(MID(CELL("filename",A1),FIND("]",CELL("filename",A1))+1,255),LookupTable!$A$1:$B$7,2,FALSE)

    The A1 reference in the CELL("filename",A1) part of the formula can be any cell on the worksheet.

    Explanation: The formula  =CELL("filename",A1) returns the full path and filename of the workbook plus the worksheet name of the cell with the formula like the following.

    C:\Users\UserName\Documents\Excel\Test Macros\[FileName.xlsm]Sheet1

    FIND looks for the closing square bracket around the FileName and adds 1 to find the start of the Sheet Name for the MID function.

    What you're up against is the demand you're making for the document v. the design of your printer...

    Virtually all local printers have minimum margin settings which they observe by default when printing textual documents. You're attempting to force Word to store a default which violates those minimums on 1 or more sides.

    Many printers today do have Borderless options primarily for printing photos, but that is not their default. Rather than trying to use 0" margins on US Letter [which observes the printer's minimums thus creating the conflict], try just setting the template's Paper Size to US Letter Borderless and its document margins to whatever you wish. Make sure to use Format> Document> Page Setup rather than the Size tool on the Layout tab... That tool has been flaky & unreliable since it was introduced.

    There's no need to use 0" margins - in fact, it's better not to do so.
