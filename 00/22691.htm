Outlook 2010: Controlling SPAM - Microsoft Community

gregory winters say:

Outlook 2010: Controlling SPAM

I've been using the Outlook application since it's '97 version, and with each release, I'm again extremely disappointed that MS doesn't provide nearly the functionality required to control SPAM in email.  Anyone who has ever sent or received a single email message knows how humans interact with and prioritize browsing their Inboxes, but MS developers seem either oblivious to this information, or they outright disregard it.

When we receive messages, there are basically only two elements of the message we look at - the Sender alias and the Subject line.  Between these two, we judge the worthiness of the message to receive our attention.  Speaking for the English language only, I can say that it's not hard to use the Sender's alias and the Subject line to determine the personal value of messages in nearly every case, but the way that Outlook is designed, one would believe that the nuance of language is theleast of the requirements in email use.

For a long time, Outlook has provided the ability to create 'rules' which are (allegedly) designed to examine incoming messages based upon certain user-defined criteria, then act according to those criteria.  By now (v14), we've certainly been provided with a generous list of conditions as well as actions that we can choose to manage email messages.  What is glaringly absent from the lists, however, are two crucially important aspects of human decision making:  boolean logic and English syntax.  Let me cite examples:

Let's say that I wish to have a Rule act upon multiple criteria, but in an 'OR' condition.  The only thing a List approach permits a user to perform are 'AND' functions.  So...if I wish to eliminate all messages from 'Joe Schmoe' OR 'financial gain can be YOURS!', I have to create two rules.  The problem with this is that Outlook runs rules in some sort of sequential order, and when messages that meet both criteria are discovered, or if one rule is necessarily run before another, then errors occur.  It's far too difficult to account for all of the OR conditions in many cases to provide for separate rules in an AND structure.

Another example...I can't for the life of me understand why MS would believe that it's more important to block domains, cryptic header information, etc., than to simply provide us strong tools for dealing with aliases.  After all, the only thing the vast majority of us ever examine in our email Sender info is the alias, checking to find if it's someone we're familiar with.  What on earth is the idea behind blocking domains when, for years, every major domain on the planet has been hijacked by spammers?  If I'm getting mail I don't want from a hotmail.com domain, for example, why would I want to block that domain thereby excluding all of my legitimate contacts who use that service? 

Along these same lines, Block Sender is an equally ridiculous exercise, and excruciatingly so because MS believes a 'sender' is a synonym for an email address.  I've received thousands of messages from 'John Cummuta' over the past few years, and nearly every one of them is associated with a different email address from all over the world.

This brings me to the point of the language itself as displayed in the Sender and Subject lines of an email message.  Here is where the battle reaches the level of absurdity.  Although Outlook indeed (finally) allows the user to include the text of these areas in a Rule (note: the exact text string, letter case and all) dealing with 'specific words', it forces the user to be just as imaginative as the spammer when fighting the incoming messaging.  No wild cards are permitted, and no partial text strings - two fatal omissions which render the 'specific words' functionality virtually impotent.

At last count, I have nearly seventy different 'specific' rule criteria set up to get rid of SPAM for Viagra - all because I cannot tell Outlook to dump everything *viagra*.

Interestingly, spammers are aware of the disgraceful weaknesses of commercial email clients as it is easy to tell from their sends.  They combine words, use mixed case, substitute similar-looking characters for letters, add spaces in unnatural places, and employ other easy-to-spot tricks in order to bypass SPAM filters (and Outlook-style client Rules).

Just a few simple improvements to the Rules tool would instantly exterminate the vast majority of this SPAM.  How do I know this?  All it would take is a basic committment to WYSIWYG - the one thing that spammers must rely upon for us to take their messages seriously.  Think about it:  if Outlook knows enough to display the text in an alias, why cannot itact upon that display?  Leave filtering messages based upon IP address and the like to the geeks.  I've got no less thanfour entries in my anti-SPAM Rule exactly given as HomeImprovementLoans, but Outlook, not using WYSIWYG as its guide, still permits messages with this exact text string passage.

We've all tried to trust our mail servers' spam filters, but I've found that far too many times, these applications are not worthy of this trust.  I've been on the phone numerous times with my Exchange Server admins and ISP tech support wanting to know why certain messages were flagged, and rarely are they able to tell me what the issue was.  I've seen it all:  multiple messages from the same Sender (same domain) make it through and one flagged; messages from folks on my Safe Senders list get flagged; messages from spammers specifically marked for Junk allowed through (both server and client side).

I can tell that MS is not serious about assisting us with SPAM because of the near-facetious way theydo purport to help.  What kind of twisted logic allows the user to set up a Rule commanding that a message be permanently deleted if it meets certain criteria, only to arbitrarily pair it with a criterion that additional execution of Rules be stopped upon finding a YES condition ("stop processing more rules")?  I've heard it said that this applies only to that particular message, but ever since Outlook 97, what this has actaully meant is that the Rule stops processingaltogether.  Thus, the very first message that meets the criteria is indeed permanently deleted, but then the Rule ceases to run permitting hundreds ofother messages that also meet the Delete criteria to enter the folders, either Inbox or Junk.  (I know this is happening, because when I run the Rule manually, then it deletes the offending messages.)  Besides, shouldn't it be obvious the difference between setting up a Rule for all messages vs. just one?

C'mon folks...we don't need new ribbon bars, One Note fluff, and the like.  Nearly fifteen years ago, Mr. Gates promised that Outlook would replace Windows as the client desktop of choice and his brilliant understanding of the four elements of human interaction - calendars, messaging, tasks, and contacts - is still largely wasted in Outlook.  Thunderbird and Lotus Notes should have long been retired to the scrap heap, but they continue to flourish as reminders that until MS takes its serious-users community seriously, and provides us the real capability we need to manage our information exchanges, SPAM will continue to bethe debilitating element of our email experience.

Thank you.

Gregory J Winters

brian tillman [outlook mvp 2007-2019] say:

Put the domain in the blocked senders list to have all messages from that domain delivered to Junk Email instead of the Inbox. Rules are a particularly bad way of dealing with junk, since the target is so volatile.

Rules should not be sorted alphabetically. That can affect their behavior. Rules should occur in the order you want them processed.

Often you can combine rules. For example, if you want to target messages from @domain1.com, @domain2.com, and @domain3.com, create a rule "with specific words in the sender's address" and include "domain1" or "domain2" or "domain3" as the specific words.
