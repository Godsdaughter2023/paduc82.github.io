uberkull say:

Sure...

Guilds in ESO hold pretty much the same function as guilds in other games. A social, mini-community of players that align with a faction to play together in the games content. Forming parties to do dungeons, questing, end game trails, etc. And of course to meet new friends and be part of a common goal setup by the guilds leaders.

The reason you can join up to 5 guilds is ESO has no global Auction House. This introduces the concept of Trade Guilds. The way ZOS handled their ESO game economy is by leaving it in the players hands. Each guild can hold 500 players, and in the beginning of ESO you could only buy and sell you goods through a guilds 'store'. You would only be buying and selling what could be posted up on your own guild store, by its 500 members(if you filled the guild). Of course on PC, you could also sell through text chat by linking your items in chat or the common 'WTS Sword of Doom' but that's not on console.

Later, ZOS added npcs scattered around the many towns and world of Tamriel that had the ability to sell items from your guild, called Guild Traders. This changed the game. Now guilds had the ability to have a public marketplace that allowed the Guild to expose their wares to the public. By simply posting your items on the Guild Store, as you did before to your 500 guild members, those same items now could be searched and bought through the NPC guild traders.

Seems simple enough right? Well there is a twist, these Guild Traders have to be bought through a bidding system. Each Guild can bid on the same Guild Trader over the week, each trying to outbid the other for the prime location of the Guild Trader. The bidding ends on each Monday morning. Now, for the prime guild trader locations the price is steep. Powerful Trade Guilds, and even regular pve / pvp Guilds put up over a million gold EACH WEEK to try and win the bid on a guild trader. But of course some guild traders located out in the middle of nowhere go for only 100g, but they barely get any buyer traffic because their not located in towns where people collect to use their bank, craft, sell junk, etc.

Any guild can have a Guild Store, a normal pve guild can run and maintain a guild store. The only requirement is to have 50 people in the guild. Where the power of a dedicated Trade Guild comes in is that their primary focus is buying and selling goods, and to make big money you need to hold prime location guild traders costing an lot of gold. Sure some Pve guilds are powerful enough to grab top guild trader locations, but they also focus on pve, pvp, and events created by the guild leaders. Trade Guilds focus on buying and selling. You can still ask guild members in a trade guild to group for pve, pvp, questing....no different, but there normally is no planned pve/pvp events in a good Trade Guild. Their focus is buying and selling.

Finally, why 5 guilds? Simple, more options to buy and sell your goods and build the games economy. At 500 members per guild, that's 2500 people potentially buying and selling wares. You can only search one guilds wares at a time through its guild store, but can quickly switch between the guilds to see what's in other stores. Then of course, you can browse other guilds wares through npc guild traders. This basically creates a player controlled economy.

lemp say:

First, get your terminology correct.  DVD-ROM (or CD-ROM) are types of Read-only compact disks.  What you have is an "Internal 20X Super multi DVD Writer."

DVD/CD writers use separate lasers for DVDs and CDs.  It is not uncommon for one to fail before the other.  That is, just because your device can read and write DVDs does not mean that it still can read and write CDs.

I have no idea what it means to "perform a restart on" a CD/DVD writer, whether using Device Manager or not.  However, for future reference, you can get to Device Manager in at least the following ways:

  • Right-click "My Computer" and select Properties; click "Hardware" tab; click "Device Manager" button
  • Press the "Windows" key and the "Pause/Break" key simultaneously; click "Hardware" tab; click "Device Manager" button (obviously only works for keyboards with a "Windows" key)
  • Start > Control Panel > System [or, if using Control Panel's "Category" view, "Performance and Maintenance" then "System"]; click "Hardware" tab; click "Device Manager"
I don't know where one buys computer hardware in Oz, but in general a replacement CD/DVD writer shouldn't be very expensive.  In the U.S., e.g.,this one from NewEgg looks like a pretty close replacement to what you have for US$22.  Be aware that your current device uses an IDE interface rather than the newer (and now more common) SATA interface.

igor leyko say:
Hi,
SCSI is a type of interface. It was used for hard drives, scanners, printers and so on and in most applications it was replaced with USB.
If you are talking about disks installed in ordinary home PC this means the SATA hard drive use NCQ technology adopt from SCSI technology. So drivers often show SATA with NCQ drives as SCSI. NCQ increases hard drive performance so SCSI\Disk is a good sign for home computer.
