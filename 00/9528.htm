Saved a document in Word - it saved a blank page with it - Microsoft Community

erinlink say:

Saved a document in Word - it saved a blank page with it

I saved a document in Word and for some reason it saved a blank page with it, How do I delete it?
john mcghie say:

The answer depends on the "reason" why the blank page is there.

 

Turn on your Show/Hide button to show your non-printing characters so you can see what is going on.  The button looks like a backwards capital P in your Standard toolbar.

 

You may find some empty paragraphs or an empty graphic at the end of the document, forcing the creation of another page.  Or you may find a Section Break or Page Break at the end.

 

Word cannot actually "have" a "blank page".  A Word file is actually completely empty until you put something in it.  When you do, Word lays it out from top to bottom, creating as many pages as it needs to do that.

 

My guess is that you have set a section break at the end of the document, and set that section break to start on a "Right Page".  Word then assumes that the first page you produced is a right page (if you are working in English), it fills that page, then realises that to get the next section to start on the next "Right Page", it must insert a blank page after the first to be the "Left Page".

 

Remove the section break, and the document will automatically contract to be one page.

 

Or you may find a heap of empty paragraphs at the bottom of the document.  Delete them, and all will be well.

 

Turn on your Show/Hide and you will immediately see what's wrong.

 

Cheers

rob koch say:

Though Windows 10 itself had enough issues due to its early release, the larger issue for many is the type of thing you are describing as "turning into Apple", since over the years that has become the most common type of customer that Microsoft now serves.

In the past the Windows operating system was designed for programmers, primarily because it was running on a general purpose computer with an open interface which allowed anyone to develop an application to do whatever they required.  Over time though, Windows has become more of a platform for the operation of certain commonly provided applications, with fewer people doing their own development or providing software to small groups of people, whether free or not.

The other thing that has changed is the massive amount of malware and other software trickery that now exists, most of which is beyond what a typical person can recognize.  This has forced Microsoft to close many of the easy to access API and other interface access points within Windows in order to protect the user, in many cases from themselves.  In doing this though, they make things more difficult for the individual home or small developer, since it's now virtually required that they use items like certificates or other more technically difficult security practices in order to make even their simple programs operate.

This is what you are running up against, since the simple situation you wish to have in using your self-developed application on a couple systems is now far more difficult, even though it specifically does nothing to harm anyone else or their PC use.  It's another undesired side effect of the heightened security required to protect Windows from the potential attack by malicious developers, which of course affects everyone.
