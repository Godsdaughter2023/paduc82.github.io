lemp say:
Actually, your problems started when you renamed the user account that the computer manufacturer created for you (called "Owner") to rgbatt.  You should have simply created a new account named rgbatt.  But that event is long in the past. 

The problem is that although Control Panel > User Accounts allows you to "Change my name" for your user account, the only thing that is changed is the time.

In Windows XP, the path to the location for the user's documents is

                      C:\Documents and Settings\<username>\My Documents.

And what shows in the directory displayed in your Explorer1.jpg is

                      C:\Documents and Settings\Owner\My Documents

which is why I said that the computer manufacturer created a user account for you named Owner. 

When you renamed that account rgbatt, the directory names stated the same.  But you never noticed it, likely because whenever you looked at Windows Explorer, you went right to "My Documents" without bothering to start at C:\Documents and Settings.

User profiles in My Computer:
YOUR-0D330DFBAF\Administrator\9.29MB\Local\Local\Modified 8/22/2011
YOUR-0D330DFBAF\rgbatt\1.76GB\Local\Local\Modified 9/24/2011

What's your basis for the above statements?  Where did you look for this information?  I didn't see any directory named C:\YOUR-0D330DFBAF in the screenshots you posted.

What you need to understand is that although the directory structure in Windows XP is a lot simpler than in Windows 7, not everything you see in Windows explorer is actually a real "folder" or directory.

In particular, "My Documents" (and the other "My" directories) are two different things, depending on what you are looking at.  In addition to being "real" directories (as in C:\Documents and Settings\Owner\My Documents), these are "Special Folders" or "virtual" folders.  See the discussion here --> http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_Folders

The way things are supposed to work is that the "My Documents" folder that belongs to the currently-logged in user is shown in Windows Explorer as My Documents, but the other My Documents folders (whose actual path actually is something like C:\Documents and Settings\usertwo\My Documents) will be shown as "Usertwo's Documents".

What seems to have happened is that you have created a copy of your entire "My Documents" directory structure and placed it into a subfolder of your My Documents folder.  In terms of path names, the copies are located in

            C:\Documents and Settings\Owner\My Documents\rgbatt's Documents

Compounding the confusion of this recursive arrangement is that the registry pointers have become confused.

If you delete that directory, including all subdirectories and files, all of your original files should remain in

             C:\Documents and Settings\Owner\My Documents

It's not clear to me, however, whether the registry entries will self-correct after you do this deletion.

This is the kind of situation that is best handled on-site, rather than through a forum.  I suggest that you take the computer to a local, reputable, independent computer repair shop (not a BigBoxStoreUSA or GeekSquad type of place).  It should not be very difficult or take very long to straighten out things.

Good luck.

•Type takeown /U %username% /F %USERPROFILE% /R /SKIPSL (You may also need the /P <password>

•Once that completes type cacls %USERPROFILE% /T /E /G %USERNAME%:F

Method 2

  1. Press WinKey+ Q, type user accountsand click on the result.

  4. Now we have to create a local account user. So after clicking Add an account, click Sign in without a Microsoft account (Not recommended), then clickLocal account. Fill the details on the screen so appeared for adding local account. Click Finish when done.

  8. Moving on, change the account status from Standard to Administrator. Click Change Account Type.
