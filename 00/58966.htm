ken blake say:
Back in the days of DOS, the PrtScn key used to print the screen. But in all versions of Windows, this works differently, and the name of the key is now an anachronism. The key does *not* print the screen. PrtScrn captures the whole screen, and Alt-PrtScrn captures the active window.

Either one captures the image to the Windows clipboard. Once it's in the clipboard you can paste (Ctrl-V) it into any application that supports graphics (Windows Paint, other graphics programs, even your favorite word processor). You can edit or add to the image as you wish, then print it.

This ability to manipulate the image in a program before printing it is an improvement over the original DOS method of just printing it. But if you'd like that old facility back, there are several third-party freeware/shareware programs that can do this, such as PrintKey2000 at http://www.sharewarejunkies.com/00zwd2/printkey2000.htm

te gi master say:

4GB of RAM is plenty for a standard computer that doesn't do highly demanding tasks (IE 10+ web pages open, Photoshop, video editing, etc). Your computer MIGHT be able to handle 8, but it would be highly dependent upon the model and type of RAM it has. Since you only have a LGA775 Pentium D, I'm thinking you either have DDR or DDR2. If you have DDR, no upgrade is possible, 4GB is it. With DDR2, you MIGHT be able to hit 8GB (again, depending on the mode) but 8GB of DDR2 can be pricy.

The BIOS is an update you would get off the manufacture support website (Drivers & Downloads | Dell US, HP Customer Support  - Software and Driver Downloads, etc), under the page for your specific model, which the website would ask you for when you visited the page. Updating your BIOS is something you should be very careful doing, and make sure you are using the newest one (most support pages list when the update was uploaded to the site, so you can easily tell which one is the newest), and make sure you're looking at the updates for your model. Some model numbers are only 1 or 2 numbers different from each other.

90% of desktops do have a little button cell battery on the motherboard, which ensures the internal clock for the BIOS keeps going, even when unplugged. If that battery were in need of replacing, the computer would let you know in some form or another, either by telling you the battery is dying, or by constantly asking you to reset the clock. Replacing the battery is easy, and can be done any time. Those batteries are also expensive as far as batteries go (~$8). If you want to do it anyways, the battery number is CR2032, and you can get them almost anywhere you can get batteries. Once you get the door open, the battery will be quite easy to spot, so, again, replacing it is very easy, you can do it anytime. If you're not constantly getting messages saying your battery is dead, or to reset the clock, I wouldn't bother with it.

And lastly, upgrading your CPU. On the support website for your specific model of desktop, along with updates for your drivers and BIOS, somewhere else on that page should be a CPU Support list. It's different for every manufacture, but most manufactures do publish the list. The CPU Support list will tell you every CPU your computer can run. Once you find newest, most powerful CPU you can run (at least a Pentium D 900 series, 945, 950, etc), you can order it online, and once you have it on hand, you can get it installed. To install it, unplug everything from your desktop, and lay it down on a table. Pull the door off, and on the motherboard, you should see the heatsink. It'll be right on the top center of the motherboard, with a fan on it, and probably 4 screws or some other kind of device to hold it down securely to the motherboard. The type of heatsink it has will be dependent on the model of computer you have. If you need help, YouTube and a search engine will be your friend. Once you get it off, you'll see your processor. It'll have some old thermal paste on it. A clean rag will take that off of both the old processor and the heatsink. Then you can get the processor out, CAREFULLY. There will be 775 pins on the motherboard that the processor was pressed up against. YouTube will be your friend again if you need any help getting the processor out. BUT, if you bend any of those pins, you'll need to straighten them out, without making it worse. VERY DELICATE. I don't think many shops would take on a job like that anymore, because of the age of your machine. So, CAREFULLY remove the processor, and then do everything you just did backwards to reassemble it. New processor in, clean both the surface of the new processor and your heatsink with alcohol, apply some new thermal paste (I mentioned what that is and where you get it in a previous post), reattach the heatsink to the motherboard, make sure your fan is plugged in (the one on the heatsink), door back on, hook it back up, and you should be good to go. When you first boot it back up, it'll ask to go into BIOS so it can configure the new CPU, so you'll go into BIOS, then you can just save and exit. It did everything it needed to do automatically.

I should have covered everything, but there's always a chance I missed a step. I've done so many jobs like this, everything just becomes automatic for me, and I can skip things that most other people need mentioned in steps like this. If you have any questions, or need any more input from me, don't hesitate to ask. If you want, you could also have me double check what you'll be upgrading, you can reply to this post with your specific Desktop Model Number, the model number of the CPU that is currently installed, and what you think you want to upgrade do. I'll double check everything, and let you know if everything will work before you make any purchases.
