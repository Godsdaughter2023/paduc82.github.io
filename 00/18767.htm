john w. vinson say:

Sorry I wasn't clear! 

A properly normalized database might or might not require splitting the tables. YOU know what real life entities these tables represent, and how they're related - I don't. If each record in BillsDb corresponds to one and only one record in BillsDbAddress, and vice versa, then you do NOT need two tables; you could do as well or better with just one. I honestly don't understand why two forms are necessary if they're both about bills (unless it's Bill's database :-{)  ).

If one record in BillsDb can correspond to zero, one, or multiple records in BillsDbAddress, then you do need two tables in a one to many relationship; and the usual tool for keeping them in synch would be to use a Form for the "one" side table, and a Subform for the "many", using the primary key of the "one" linked to a foreign key in the "many" as the master/child link fields of the subform. VBA would be possible in unusual situations, but if you can do it with normal queries or form design I'd go there first.

But - I cannot see your database, and I'm giving advice in the dark here. It certainly is valid advice for some databases (a lot of which I've developed, or learned from wiser developers); but it might be total baloney for your database - I just don't know!
