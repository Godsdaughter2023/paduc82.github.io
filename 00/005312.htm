zorvek say:
When comparing the results of a numeric calculation to a possible constant outcome, if the result of the comparison is unexpected, the problem might not be with the formulas but rather with how Excel handles numbers. Excel is known to introduce a very small amount of floating point noise into calculations which, while not affecting the number's significant digits, can create problems when comparing the results of calculations with constants. Use the following formula to remove insignificant digits or floating point noise from any number:

   =ROUND(A1,SignificantDigits-INT(LOG(A1)))

where SignificantDigits is the number of significant digits applicable to the problem being solved.

Why is it necessary to remove insignificant digits? Excel uses the IEEE 64-bit floating point representation of numbers to store numeric values. This format works very well as long as all values are integers. But when values have decimal parts (digits to the right of the decimal point) then very small inaccuracies or floating point noise can creep into formula results. Consider the following task: remove the whole part and first decimal digit from a decimal number using simple math. Depending on what the original number is and the formula used, the final result may or may not be exactly what is expected. Here are three different formulas that produce the same result (almost):

   =A1-INT(A1*10)/10
   =(A1*100-INT(A1*10)*10)/100
   =A1-TRUNC(A1,1)

The most straightforward way to solve this problem is to first decide on how many significant digits are relevant and then round all results to that number of significant digits. The first formula introduced above illustrates how to do this rounding. Note that the number of significant digits in any number are not the number of non-zero digits to the left and/or right of the decimal point, but the total number digits starting with the highest order digit. The formula works by considering the number of digits in the whole part (digits to the left of the decimal point) and rounding n digits to the right of the left-most digit, whole or decimal. joeu2004 say: Rajesh wrote:
=IF(17.45-16.55=0.9,"TRUE","FALSE") and result comes as "FALSE"!!!
Reason is Excel doesn't calculate 17.45-16.55 correctly upto 15 decimal places!!

You are correct:  Excel does not store or limit the precision of calculations to 15 significant digits.  That it "does" is an oft-repeated misunderstanding or over-simplification, even in Microsoft documentation.

Excel does truncate data entry to 15 significant digits.  And Excel does round displayed values to up to 15 significant digits.

However, numeric values are stored and calculated to the precision supported by 64-bit binary floating point.  That is an industry standard that represents numbers as the sum of 53 consecutive powers of two.

Consequently, most non-integers (and integers larger than 2^53) must be represented by an approximation.  For example, 1/10 cannot be represented exactly as the sum of any finite number of powers of 2.  Try it with paper and pencil, starting with 1/16:  1/16 + 1/32 + 0/64 + 0/128 + 1/256 + 1/512 etc.

Even though Excel performs calculations correctly with these approximations, the differences can accumulate and multiply, sometimes with suprising results.

For example, the following shows the actual exact internal representations (I use comma to demarcate 15 significant digits):
17.45           17.4499999999999,99289457264239899814128875732421875
16.55           16.5500000000000,00710542735760100185871124267578125
17.45-16.55  0.899999999999998,57891452847979962825775146484375
0.9              0.900000000000000,02220446049250313080847263336181640625

If you use paper and pencil to substract the internal respresentations of 17.45 and 16.55 above, you will find that Excel's result is correct [1].

However, you can also see that the binary result of the arithmetic does not exactly match the internal representation of the constant 0.9.

In some contexts, like your IF() expression, that difference is noticable.  In other contexts, Excel might hide the difference by changing ("correcting") the binary result arbitrarily.

But the latter heuristic is dubious because it is implemented inconsistently.  For example, =(1.333-1.225=0.108) returns TRUE.  But =(1.333-1.225-0.108=0) returns FALSE.

As Vijay wrote, the general remedy is to explicitly round calculations with non-integers to the required degree of precision, if any.


-----
[1] The binary result of arithmetic does not always match a paper-and-pencil calculation, and sometimes the binary result depends on the order of the arithmetic operations.  These are due to other anomalies of the nature of the implementation of 64-bit binary floating-point arithmetic, particularly in Intel-compatible CPUs.

[EDIT] To illustrate the latter (order dependency), you might note that =IF(17.45-0.9=16.55,TRUE) returns TRUE, even though your =IF(17.45-16.55=0.9,TRUE) returns FALSE.

zorvek say:
This is not an issue of Excel rounding unnecessarily or in error. This is simply a matter of how the IEE internal representation of a non-integer number in binary is not directly translatable into a base 10 number. Some noise can be introduced in the lowest order digit. So, effectively, Excel, in using 64-bit internal representation, supports about 15 base-10 digits give or take one decimal digit. 10, 11, and 12 digits are transformed to and from the IEEE format without any degradation of accuracy.

More importantly, when working with numbers, it is infinitely more important to consider how many significant digits with which you are working. And, as joeu2004 pointed out, significant digits are not the same as decimal digits. Your measurements are only as accurate as you measuring instruments. I have not found one situation where 15 significant digits are required. Not even when plotting the recent Mars missions. For most situations, 5 or 6 significant digits are about all you need to consider.

But back to your original question, why does Excel occasionally not consider relatively simple base-10 mathematical comparisons to not be equal? The answer, now that we have discussed how Excel stores numbers, is simply that the comparison is made using the IEEE 64-bit representation which can sometimes be off by one single bit based on the math used to derive the value. One bit in the lowest order is all it takes.

We have presented this issue to Microsoft and proposed a better solution than forcing users to use ROUND and other tricks or using the misleading and problematic Precision as Displayed option to fix the problem. Our suggestion is to simply recognize that the lowest order one or two bits of the internal value can be off and to use the base-10 form of the number with rounding to 14 significant digits in order to determine if two decimal values are equal - or exclude the lowest order bits from the comparison. This simple change would save a lot of people a lot of grief.

Remember your example above?

    17.45-16.55=0.9 = FALSE

Really? We are not talking about 15, 12, 10, or even 5 significant digits. Only four significant digits, some very simple math, and a comparison. And the answer is not correct.

(And of course, the problem is with calculations, not just comparisons.)

For example, consider =128.01-128 in A1 and 0.01 in A2.  =ROUND(A1,14)=A2 is still FALSE.  Moreover, the binary representations in A1 and A2 differ in the lower-order 16 bits, not just "the lowest one or two bits".

The problem arises because the integral part of 128.01 (i.e. 128) leaves so few bits for representing the fractional part (46) that when the 128 is subtracted, the inaccuracy in the representation of the fractional part becomes significant.  Specifically:
128.01

Excel vba has a currency data type that works at a much nicer level which is good for financial math additions.  It gets rid of the rounding issues out to 15 digits.  One can get fancy, but here's just a very simple vba function that works with these types of problems.

 

Function ZeroQ(n) As Boolean
    ZeroQ = (CCur(n) = 0)
End Function


Function EqualQ(n1, n2) As Boolean
    EqualQ = ZeroQ(n1 - n2)
End Function

 

=EqualQ(17.45-16.55, 0.9)  -> True

nemanja pantelic msft say:

Hi Hassan,

 

POP3 account  will save messages on server for 14 days by the default Outlook settings.

 

The easiest way to set the server to delete all messages as the users receive would be to uncheck Leave a copy of messages on the server, or change Remove from server after to one or two days. This way, you will have less messages and more space on the server.

 

If you uncheck Leave a copy of messages on the server, please keep in mind that this will affect both sent and receive messages. That means that the messages will be downloaded just from one machine. If you try to download the message again from the different machine, you will not succeed because the message will be deleted from the server. That’s why you could leave message on the server for a few days, depending on the needs of your organization.

 

To find the feature, please open your Outlook and go to File> Account settings> Account settings> Email, select your POP3 email account and click on Change. After you uncheck the option and save the changes, your server will automatically delete every downloaded message.

    Regarding the POP/ SMTP related behavior, I’ll have to elaborate my test results which are, by the way, the same as yours. POP and IMAP are, as you said, related to inbound communication. This means that POP and IMAP receive and sync (or not) any incoming massage. As a newer protocol, IMAP will sync server and client. For older versions of POP, server didn’t have the option to save the messages. Today, you can set POP server to keep received messages for the time you set. For both POP and IMAP, we are using SMTP protocol to send messages. SMTP settings are the same for POP and IMAP which means that the behavior will be same too. So both for POP and IMAP, the outbound communication will be saved on the server. That’s why, the best solution in your case (from my point of view) would be to set and implement Retention policy.
