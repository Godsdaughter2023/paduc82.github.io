techie bear say:

Hello,

The problem is simple and not resolvable:  the game was designed to run on a 16 / 32 Bit system, and you are now running a 64 Bit system. 

 

As a transition from 16 Bit applications, Windows XP and Vista allowed 16 Bit apps to 'pretend' they were 32 Bit apps by Thunking - basically taking 2, 16 Bit pieces of code and combining them into a piece of code that could be 'thunked' into acting like a 32 Bit piece of code. But this was a smoke and mirrors trick that didn't actually produce 32 Bit code.

 

But the 64 Bit architecture can't do that, it needs 'real' 32 Bit code to work, and the old method doesn't produce that, only simulated 32 Bit code. Hence, your game crashes. And of course if you were to go back to a 32 Bit version of Windows you'd lose half your installed RAM (32 Bit can only use 3.25 GB of RAM, maximum).

 

Sorry, this is not the news you wanted to hear.

Regards,

BearPup

a. user say:

You've got the right idea, except that you can't move only the operating system. I'll show you what you can do instead.

First: Having your software on the SSD while leaving your personal files on the HHD is a smart move for two reasons (at least.) Your software will benefit from the extra speed afforded by the SSD, whereas your personal files don't benefit as much. And now you can backup your software and your personal files separately, which is a real benefit if you ever have to restore a backup of your software. With your personal files on a separate drive, they won't go back in time when you restore your software.

Here's how to get the deed done:

1- Move everything to the SSD. You can easily do this with the image backup software that comes with new SSDs. The software and complete directions will be found on the SSD manufacturer's website.

2- [Once you're sure that everything on the SSD is working well,] delete all your personal files from the SSD.

3- Delete everything except your personal files from the HDD.

Not done yet ...

4- Change the default location of your personal files from the SSD to the HDD. I'll use your Documents folder as an example:

a) Right-click on the C:\Users\<your user account name>\Documents and choose Properties from the context menu.

b) Open the Location tab - that's where you change the default location for all your Documents going forward.

That's basically all there is to it. I've done this myself on several occasions, so I know it works. There are details, of course - many of them. That's why you need to do plenty of research on the internet, just like I did, before you'll be ready to start. A community forum isn't a good place to learn this.

This is a major procedure, so you don't want to make a mistake. Take your time, prepare carefully and you'll get it done perfectly the first time, which is what you want.
