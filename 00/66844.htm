Changing the burning speed will not allow to put 6 hours on one DVD instead of 2 hours. Butlower resolution means the same length video clip takes up less space, so you can fit more on the disk. It's the same result in the end, a lower resolution, allows for more "video" or recording time, but at a lower quality. A higher resolution will hold less "video" or recording time, but at a higher quality.

The easy way or a workaround to this is to buy dual layer disks. They're usually marked something like DVD+R DL and will hold 8.4 GB of data, compared to 4.7 GB on a standard DVD-R disk. benjaminpayton say:

Aspect Ratio for videos on WMM 2012

Hi, 

I've recently downloaded the 2012 version of Windows Movie Maker on my Windows 10 laptop. I've tried to convert a video what was originally stretched to a 16:9 ratio from an original 4:3 ratio - basically I want to restore this to the original aspect ratio - I hope you can understand me. When I try to select 4:3 on the project menu, I don't get the standard 4:3 with black pillars on each side of the video, but letterboxes on the op and bottom. I hope someone can understand where I'm coming from with this and an answer would greatly appreciated - happy holidays to everyone by the way! :) 

considerate_guy say:

Movie Maker won't squash or stretch video to fit in a different aspect ratio. Instead it just adds letterboxes or pillar boxes to keep the original aspect ratio of the video.

In your case, what you have is really 16:9 video. Movie Maker doesn't know it was originally 4:3. So all it does is try and fit a 16:9 (widescreen) rectangle into a 4:3 project size and the result is it has to put letterboxes on top and bottom.

You'll want to find a separate program for converting the video to the correct aspect ratio. Once it is 4:3 video again, then if you put that in a 4:3 project, it will have no black bars. Or if you put it in a 16:9 aspect project, you'll get the pillar boxes on the sides.
