a. user say:

Uhm, that *is* RAID. Or, at least, it can be: "striped" is software RAID 0. "dynamic" is a pre-requisite of software RAID on Windows. If your layout is "simple" you are not taking any advantage of a dynamic disk other than it is a little bit easier to change the size. But as you leave no space for expansion it's not that much easier as with "real" partitions. Also, with the size of the disk and the partitions I'd consider it unlikely that you would be going to have to increase one of them one day. You didn't reply to my implied question about this. It looks to me like you created a dynamic disk because "you could".

Now, this doesn't explicitely answer your question, but it gives you a possible solution. I guess that this change is by design (but I don't know, so go ahead and report it as a bug as I suggested). After all, it's the nature of a dynamic disk that it can have data on several disks or have a partition span over several *non-contingent* parts of a disk. "Defragmenting" it becomes somewhat moot at that point I guess. Defragmenting has been the holy grail of disk care in the 90s and maybe early 2000s. Nowadays you really don't have to care. It also makes no sense to switch "automatic" off. Have it run when your system is idle, that's just perfect for a mostly unnecessary task.
