joeu2004 say:

Honestly, I'm not following the details.  I think you need to clarify.  But some general observations might help.

1. Be sure to use an XY Scatter chart, not a Line Chart, to draw the trendline and get the trendline coefficients.  Line Chart trendlines are based on x={1,2,...} regardless of your actual x-data.

2. If you copy the coefficients from the trendline label, be sure to change the trendline format to increase the number of decimal places.  Right-click Format Trendline Label > Number and select a numeric format with many decimal places.  I choose Scientific and 14 decimal places because that displays the most digits (15) that Excel will format, regardless of the magnitude of the coefficients.

3. Even better:  use LINEST to generate the coefficients directly in Excel.  For a 4th degree polynomial, select 5 horizontal cells and ctrl+shift+Enter instead of just Enter) a formula of the form =LINEST(ydata,xdata^{1,2,3,4}) .  It is possible that LINEST generates different coefficients.  Usually, that is okay, as long as the derived y-data matches the chart trendline.  But sometimes, LINEST fails, or it generates invalid coefficients.  In that case, it is prudent to resort to copying the trendline coefficients in the chart.

4. Unless your data truly is related by a 4th degree polynomial (unlikely), note that while a polynomial trendline might be useful for interpolating between the endpoints of known data, usually it is dubious to extrapolate beyond the endpoints.  Polynomial trendlines have a tendously to "blow up" and oscillate wildly outside the range of the known data.
