How To Obtain A Replacement Product Key

If you have a lost or unreadable product key for Microsoft software that came with your computer, please contact the manufacturer of your computer to obtain a replacement. Contact information for the manufacturer of your computer can be found here:

http://support.microsoft.com/gp/oemphone/

If you have lost a product key for software that you purchased separately from your computer, or if your product key is not working, please call Microsoft. To locate the appropriate telephone number, visit the following Microsoft Web site: http://support.microsoft.com/default.aspx?scid=fh;EN-US;CNTACTMS

A fee may apply for lost keys.







Product Key - Where To Find Product Key 

If you bought a computer with Windows preinstalled there should be a Certificate of Authenticity (COA) sticker somewhere on your computer, on some laptops it’s in the battery bay. It will have a 25 character alphanumeric code on it - that is your product key. If it's not on your computer look in your paperwork or on any CD/DVD cases that were included with the computer.

If you bought a retail copy of Windows 7 the key should be included in the box.

If you bought Windows 7 online and downloaded it the key should have been sent to you through email. If you bought from the MS Store you can log in to your account to view your key.






How To Order A Replacement Disk:

http://answers.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/forum/windows_7-windows_install/replacement-cd/fb8230d4-000f-4ee7-b732-c9f8fff28bfd






OEM computer manufacturers are required to provide at least one way for you to reinstall Windows if you buy a computer with Windows preinstalled - your computer should have included either a restore partition on your hard drive, or Windows install/restore disks. ﻿Most computer manufacturers also have a feature to make your own install disks.

If the restore partition is still there and was not removed, it will reinstall Windows. There is usually a prompt when you first start the computer telling you what key to press to start the restore﻿ - check your manual if you're not sure how to. Or if you have install disks - insert them, restart and boot from them to start the reinstall process.

If you restore it to factory condition, it will be like it was when you first bought it - all preinstalled programs will be installed. Anything you installed after buying it must be reinstalled. This will remove any personal files on the computer, back them up first.


Contact the OEM computer manufacturer and ask them to send you an install disk for your computer - they may or may not do it. If they agree to send you one they may charge a small fee. Contact information for the manufacturer of your computer can be found here:

http://support.microsoft.com/gp/oemphone/

Hi Frederik,

Yes, it is true that the tools could be better and user friendlier.  After trying several times and different ways, I was able to transfer the system image from a large hard drive to a smaller hard drive with success. 

But first I must apologize for my rant.  My lack of understanding of how partitions can affect a system image restoration to a new smaller drive caused my failures.  The transfer problem of a system image from a larger drive to a smaller drive also  exists in Linux and MacOs worlds, not just MS.

Here is how I did it and what I used to do it:
- an external USB drive (500gb) ---> stores my system images
- MS' Disk Management (search for "partition" at Windows Start button)
- my 1T drive (current home for my OS)
- my SSD 250gb (my new home for my OS)
- a Windows recovery disk (mine is Windows 7 Recovery for EFI); the BIOS version of Windows 7 Recovery disk doesn't work on the EFI based system images and vice versa.

0> Your system is OFF.

1> leave only your OS hard drive connected and disconnect the rest of your hard drives.
--In my case, I have two 1T HDD: one has the OS and the other one has files.  I disconnected my 1T file drive.

2> boot up and run MS' Disk Management app.
-- I found it by typing "partition" in the Start button's search box.  You should see only one disk which is disk 0.  If you see anyother disks, shutdown and remove them.

3> on disk 0, keep the following two paritions and remove/drop/delete the rest of the partitions.
   partition 1 --> 100 mb EFI (no drive letter assigned to this)
   partition 2 --> your OS partition "c:" drive

-- In my case, my disk 0 has four partitions:
   partition 1 --> 100 MB EFI (no drive letter assigned to this)
   partition 2 --> my OS "c:" drive (500gb allocated here but used only 86gb)
   partition 3 --> "d:" drive 200+gb allocated here
   partition 4 --> "e:" drive 290+gb allocated here

   I backed up my partition 3 and 4 and then dropped my "D:" and "E:" drives.  DO NOT FORMAT YOUR UNALLOCATED block of space.  Leave it as "UNALLOCATED".  It is VERY important that there must NOT be any active or accessible partitions on the right side of your "C:" drive, your OS drive.  If you were to create a system image with an active partition on the right side of your OS "C:" drive, your transfer to a smaller drive will fail.

   My disk 0 looks like this after dropping D: and E: drives:
   partition 1 --> 100 MB EFI (no drive letter assigned to this)
   partition 2 --> my OS "c:" drive (500gb allocated here but used only 86gb)

   A HUGE UNALLOCATED BLOCK

4> shrink your OS "c:" drive to the size which will fit your smaller drive.
-- My new SSD is 250gb; therefore, I shrunk my 500gb "C:" drive to 150gb.  Now, I ended up with more UNALLOCATED block of space on my disk 0.

   After shrinking my OS "C:" drive, my partitions looks like this on disk 0:
   partition 1 --> 100 MB EFI (no drive letter assigned to this)
   partition 2 --> my OS "c:" drive (150gb allocated here but used only 86gb)

   A HUGE UNALLOCATED BLOCK

5> reboot your PC to clear up the old/removed partitions

6> run Disk Management and check your partitions.
--I found one hard drive (disk 0) with two active partitions and one HUGE unallocated block of space.  There should not be any other hard drives.

   disk 0 has:
   partition 1 --> 100 MB EFI (no drive letter assigned to this)
   partition 2 --> my OS "c:" drive (150gb allocated here but used only 86gb)
   A HUGE UNALLOCATED BLOCK

7> Now, connect your external USB hard drive and create your system image
--Windows 7 automatically selects two partitions "EFI" and "Windows (system)" drives for me.

NOTE: At the system image back up screen, where EFI and Windows System drives are preselected for you.  You MUST make sure that there isn't any other drive/drives in between EFI and Windows System.  For example at the "Confirm your backup setting" screen, this is good:

        EFI Systems Partition
        Windows7 (c:)(System)

On the other hand, this one below is NOT GOOD--your system image tranfer to a smaller drive will fail because it failed for me:

        EFI Systems Partition
        <some drive name> <some drive letter>
        Windows7 (c:)(System)

However, if you have only one drive (disk 0) connected to your PC and followed step 0 to step 6, this will not happen.

8> when asks you to create your Windows Recovery Disk, make one.

9> shutdown and connect your new smaller drive (do not make it the bootable/master drive; add it as an extra drive). Your new smaller drive should be DISK 1.

10> reboot and run Disk Management
--At this point, I have two disks: the disk 0 with my OS and the new drive (disk 1).  If your new disk is offline, make it online by right clicking on it and select "Online"

11> format your NEW smaller drive (disk 1)
--If your system is GPT, it will format your new smaller drive to GPT; otherwise, if MBR then it will format to MBR.  You should be able to access your smaller drive.

NOTE: ONCE AGAIN, DO NOT format the HUGE unallocated block of space on the right side of your "c:" partition on disk 0.  LEAVE it as UNALLOCATED.  If, for some reasons, you would need to recreate another system image because something had gone wrong, you could create one again.

12> shutdown your PC

13> DISCONNECT YOUR OLD OS BOOTABLE HARD DRIVE

14> MAKE YOUR NEW SMALLER hard drive the MASTER/bootable drive (disk 0)
--In my case, I disconnected my old SATA bootable OS and then connected my NEW smaller drive to the SAME SATA port where my OLD drive used to be.  At this point, my PC is connected to two hard drives:

   1> my new smaller drive (disk 0)
   2> an USB external drive. 

15> Insert your Windows 7 Recovery Disk (your external drive is still connected)

16> Go through the usual recovery steps; The Recovery program will detect your USB drive and find your latest system image.

17> At this point, automatically, the Recovery program will start transferring your system image to your new smaller drive.

18> If everything had gone well, the transfer would finish and the recovery program would automatically reboot your PC off of your NEW smaller drive.

NOTE: At step 17, if you were to get an error message saying something in regard to "...No disk can be used for recovering the system disk can be found..."  You may try one of the following two options:

a> click "cancel" which takes you to the menu; select "Startup Repair"--the first choice; and reboot and go back to step 16
or
b> click "cancel" and shutdown; you would need to reformat your new smaller drive by reconnecting your OLD OS drive so that you can boot (disk 0); and reconnect your new smaller drive as an additional drive by connecting it as a non-bootable hard drive (disk 1); AND THEN go back to STEP 10 and try the rest of the steps.


These steps worked for me each time I tried.  And I have tried in many different ways.  However, I cannot claim that these steps are the bullet proof since there exists so many different PC set ups.

I hope this could help you.
