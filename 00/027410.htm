vincentchoy say:

There really are only 2 ways to do this - 1. Single Tenant or 2. Multi-Tenant.

Single Tenant means one subscription, probably paid for by HQ. Company A,B,C,D,E are all on the same tenant, and all with separate domain names. So all in one big family. Each access by their own domain, but all on the same Office365 subscription tenant, so easy to share and collaborate. Everyone can talk to everyone.

Downside : Someone must volunteer to manage the tenant. Its only one. In global multinationals, there is usually a joint service desk or outsource vendor that does this. This is good if the companies can be run together as a close knit group.

Multi Tenant means each subsidiary has their own Office365 tenant and subscription. Each tenant is distinct and apart from the other.

Office365 of companyA will view the users on the Office365 of company B,C,D &E as outside entities. Sharing of calendars, SharePoint and OneDrive folders will be treated as sharing with external parties, and come with associated limitations. 

Upside : It is easy to sell off a subsidiary together with their subscription. Each subsidary also manages their own Office365 and buys their own licenses without cross charging.   

Based on what you wrote, a single tenant model would be the way to go.
