Don't concern yourself with the actual number of records reported by .RecordCount.  That value only counts the number of records traversed so far, since the recordset was opened, and on a form that traversal is being carried out asynchronously.  All you can really be sure of telling (without actually moving to the end of the recordset, is whether there are any records at all (.RecordCount > 0) or not (.RecordCount = 0).

sven johannsen say:

Take a look at the plugs on the various headsets you use. With a mic, there should be 4 distinct contacts, the tip, two rings, and a longer part that is close to the body and wires. Compare the positioning of the insulators (small spaces between metal parts). It they look pretty much the same, things should work.

The tip and one ring, normally closest to the tip, are the left and right earphones. The longer part, near the body, is common to both earphones and the mic, if it exists. If you plug in just stereo earphones, with three contacts, normally that will short out the mic as it's contact in the jack will hit the sleeve (the long, common connector). That's fine, it should short out that mic input.

Most important is the relative positioning. I found that boith Nokias sleeve and bumper for the Lumia 900, have just enough material in the earphone hole to raise the earphone connector up enough to misalign the connections. You don't have a case or bumper do you?

BTW Don, my Lumia did not come with any headset, so I have had to play a little to find compatible stuff. The compatibility is more in the button features on headsets. These are definately not a standard, and diferent manufacturers have different options, both in the headsets, and in what the phone responds to. Play/pause is pretty standard as it just shorts a couple of the contacts, and the phone needs to recognize that and react accordingly. Some phones just do play/pause, or answer/end. Others can do other actions based on double or triple taps. That is phone specific.

Volume up down buttons are real touchy. Apple uses a very proprietary mechanism that pretty much only works on iDevices. earphones with volume sliders, like Zaggs, work on anything. They don't turn the device volume up and down, they just attenuate what is coming out of the device.

For a point of interest, the Samsung earphones that came with my Galaxy tab (7"), do everything just fine on my Lumia 900. I can pause/play, double tap to skip forward, triple to skip back. And the volume +/- actually changes the volume on the phone.
