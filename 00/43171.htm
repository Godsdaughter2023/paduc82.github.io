Okay, so if you're using only a Gmail account in Outlook, your calendar and contacts are probably LOCAL to Outlook only.

In order to be able to sync your contacts and calendars across all devices and the Metro Mail, People and Calendar apps in Windows 8, you need to put your contacts and calendars into an email account. The best one for this purpose is obviously your Microsoft account.

Start by adding a new account to Outlook 2013. When asked for credentials, do this:

  1. Choose "Manual setup or additional server types" and click Next
  2. Choose "Outlook.com or Exchange ActiveSync compatible service" and click Next.
  3. Fill out your name and the email address of your Microsoft account
  4. Input the server as m.hotmail.com
  5. Input the email address of your MIcrosoft account as the User Name
  6. Input the password of your Microsoft account as the password.
  7. Click Next

Now you have your Microsoft account syncing with Outlook 2013. If you already have your contacts and your calendar in your Microsoft account, great! That's a lot less work to do. If you need to move this info into your Microsoft account, follow this guide. While this guide was designed to help Windows Phone users, the steps are perfectly valid for anyone moving their data to a Microsoft account. Just skip down to the section called "Copy Contact & Calendar data to the Windows Live folders"

It takes a bit of setup as you can see, but once your data is in your Microsoft account, it will sync across all devices you sign into your Microsoft account with. You can also use your Microsoft account across Windows, Windows Phone, iPhone, Android, BlackBerry, etc. to get your same data on any device.

barkoosh say:

You can do nothing about it. Since the "zero" of the numerals used in Arabic has the form of a dot, Windows automatically changes the display of the full-stop to a comma-shaped character when it's between two digits of the numerals used in Arabic. Format settings can't change that.

By the way, if you look closer, you'll notice that the decimal separator is not exactly the same as the thousand separator (which is rarely used in Arabic).
