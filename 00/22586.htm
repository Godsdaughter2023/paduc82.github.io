This macro might help:

Public Sub TasksDueToday()
  Dim Items As Outlook.Items
  Dim Find As String
  Dim obj As Object
  Dim i As Long
  Dim dtDate As Date
  Dim Folder As Outlook.Folder
  Dim Result As Outlook.Items
  
  Set Folder = Application.Session.GetDefaultFolder(olFolderTasks)
  Set Items = Folder.Items

  Items.Sort "[StartDate]", False
  Items.IncludeRecurrences = False
  Find = Format(dtDate, "ddddd")
  
  dtDate = Date
  Find = Format(dtDate, "ddddd")
  
  Find = "[DueDate] = " & Chr(34) & Find & Chr(34)

  Set Result = Items.Restrict(Find)
  For Each obj In Result
    i = i + 1
    'Debug.Print i, obj.Subject
  Next
  MsgBox "Task due today: " & i
End Sub

Here are two methods you can use to pause your loop...

Dim WAIT As Double

WAIT = Timer
While Timer < WAIT + 2
   DoEvents  'do nothing
Wend

...and then adding WAIT + *the number of seconds you want to pause the code*.  I would also put it outside the loop because you are looping one record at a time.

OR

Private Sub (KillSomeTime)
'Posted by Austin Myers 8.15.2007

Dim PauseTime, Start
PauseTime = 10 ' Set duration in seconds
Start = Timer ' Set start time.
Do While Timer < Start + PauseTime
DoEvents ' Yield to other processes.
Loop

End Sub

displayname_848 say:

I spent many hours on the phone and chat with Microsoft trying to solve this problem, and it is finally solved, so I thought I'd share the results, in case anyone has similar problems.  The main points are:

1.  Either the business or personal versions of Outlook can add both business and personal email accounts, even exchange accounts, even Office 365 accounts. There should be no reason that you need to de-install the Business version of Outlook and re-install the personal version of Outlook just to add your hotmail or Outlook.com account.

A lot of Microsoft employees will tell you otherwise, but this is false.  Microsoft has divided themselves up politically into business and consumer divisions of Office 365, and the first thing they do is to check to see which version of Outlook you are running, but it doesn't make a difference.

In fact, if you already have the Office 365 apps through your work, then you might not need to purchase Office 365 Home or Personal at all.  The only reason you might do that would be to get the additional space in OneDrive or Outlook.com that a subscription gives you.  But you certainly don't need to buy a subscription to Office 365 in order to use the apps if you have them through work.

2.   If you have two factor authentication turned on in your Microsoft account, you will need to use app passwords for Outlook.   You cannot use your main password for your Microsoft account.  Read about this here: https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/help/12409/microsoft-account-app-passwords-and-two-step-verification

3.  Let's say that your Microsoft account is under your school email address that you got when you graduated:   foo at alum.school.edu.  Let's say that your hotmail address is foo at hotmail.com.    The way Microsoft deals with this is that you can log into hotmail.com with either email address.  They are aliases of each other.  But one is primary.

Here's what happens when you try to add foo at hotmail.com to Outlook:  Outlook gets very confused and some superbad things happen.   If your school email address is primary, then Outlook will go to your school's Exchange server and try to add that information to Outlook.  Clearly, this is not what you want.  God forbid, your school might even have an exchange server.

You need to make the hotmail.com alias the primary email address before you try to add your account to Outlook.  This you can do here:  https://account.live.com/names/Manage?mkt=en-US&refd=account.microsoft.com&refp=profile

If you do this, then Outlook will query hotmail.com for the Exchange server information, which is what you want.

4.  Regarding OneDrive, this is much less problematic.  You should not need to download the OneDrive app if you are running Windows, because it is pre-installed.  Each account that you run OneDrive for (say your business and personal OneDrives) gets its own cloud.  This is why you will have a blue cloud and a white cloud.  And there is no conflict if you choose to log in with either email address: foo at alum.school.edu or foo at hotmail.com, because there is no Exchange server to confuse OneDrive.

I might be mistaken about not needing to download the Business version of OneDrive to access your company files, but this caused much fewer problems for me. Anyway, after days of struggling, everything seems to be working.  Feel free to follow up with any thoughts.
