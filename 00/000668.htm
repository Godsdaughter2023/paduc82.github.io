Hi

Both answers are right, sort of.

When you have a perpetual license, Office checks the hardware it is installed on to verify that the original owner is using the software. A change in hardware, changing the startup volume hardware in particular, signifies that the install is no longer running as licensed. If Office sees such a change, it prompts for activation with a new product key.

If you had used Apple's Migration assistant before you uninstalled from the old device, I think your license would have been automatically migrated to your new device.

How to move your content to a new Mac - Apple Support

When Office 2011 was still in service, you could call Microsoft Support and plead with them for a new product key in a situation such as yours. Most often, they would provide the key. These days, support rarely provides a new key in this situation. After all, you could have used Migration Assistant to move your installation to the new device. It's worth a call to Microsoft Support and ask them, but don't be surprised if they turn you down.

If you get turned down, it's probably not the end of the world. Office 2016 is nearing the end of its life. Office 2019 has already been announced, although an exact release date has not been set.

I think you would be doing yourself a favor by simply getting an Office 365 subscription. You'll automatically get most of the new features of Office 2019 right now, and when Office 2019 is officially released later this year, you'll get it as part of your subscription without paying any extra fee. At this point in time, I think purchasing a perpetual Office 2016 license is a waste of money. Get the subscription. Also, Office 2019 will be subscription only. I can't see any reason to get a 2016 perpetual license.

¡firedog say:

550 5.7.0 (COL004-MC3F44) Unfortunately, messages from (209.85.214.176) on behalf of (yahoo.co.uk) could not be delivered due to domain owner policy restrictions. 

As the error message says, the domain owner (Yahoo) has a policy in place that instructs email services (e.g. Outlook.com) to reject any message apparently From a Yahoo account (e.g. name @yahoo.co.uk) that is received from a different domain (e.g. google.com). Because Gmail is forwarding your messages but leaving the From: header intact, Outlook.com is bound to reject them.

That said, I just set up a similar arrangement: forward to me @outlook.com mail sent from an account @yahoo.com and addressed to me @gmail.com. My test messages made it.

If you or someone you know has an account @yahoo.com, can you send a test message from this account to see if that is also rejected? This might indicate that Yahoo have made some changes to their DMARC configuration for @yahoo.com accounts but not @yahoo.co.uk accounts.

Cookies can enrich your browsing experience by allowing sites to learn your preferences or allowing you to avoid signing in every time you visit certain sites. However, some cookies might put your privacy at risk by tracking sites that you visit.

 

You have the option to check the cookies alone in 'Delete browsing history' tab.


To delete cookies manually in Internet Explorer

 

1. Open Internet Explorer for the desktop.

 

2. Tap or click the Tools button, under the “General” tab look for “Browsing History.” Under this heading you can click “Delete” to have the computer delete all cookies for you automatically however to delete the cookies manually click on the “Settings” tab

 

3. Click on the “View Files” tab under the “Temporary Internet Files and History Settings” box that pops up. When you do a second box will pop up with a list of cookie containing files. This may take a few seconds to load so be patient while waiting for the cookie files to load.

 

4. Highlight any cookie file that you would like to delete. You can highlight more than one at a time by holding down the ‘Ctrl” button on your keypad.

 

5. Delete the files that are highlighted by clicking on the “delete this item” located to the left in the same box. As soon as you do you will see the selected files disappear. When you are done just “X” off; this completes your task of manually deleting cookies.

Hello

You should be able to use the import feature of the Photos app to do this. You also might want to configure AutoPlay to automatically import your photos when you plug in your digital camera.

To import in Photos app:

1) Plug in your digital camera to USB

2) Press Windows Key

3) Select "All apps"

4) Scroll down the list to Photos, click to launch

5) Click on "..." app menu

6) Select "Import" and follow the wizard

To configure Auto Play:

1) Press Window key on your keyboard + S

2) In Cortana search, type AutoPlay

3) In results select "AutoPlay settings"

4) In "Choose AutoPlay" defaults, you should have something like "Removable drive" or "Memory card"

5) From the "Choose a default" menu, select "Import photos and videos (Photos)"

Once you configure you should automatically see the Photos app launch to import when you plug in your digital camera.
