rich michaels - mvp (greatcirclelearning.com) say:

What is the format of the document? Doc or Docx? The feature is only available in docx files and that's probably why it's grayed out.

I also have a few other questions/comments and hopefully they help: What is the dpi of the original image? It must be between 240 - 300 dpi. The png format should be fine to use.

What are the dimensions, height and width, of the original? You'll get better results if your image is large and you reduce its dimensions by scaling it once you get it into the document. BTW, scaling is not cropping. You scale by using Format Picture and adjusting either the absolute size or the percent factor on the dialog. Once the image is in the document don't run the compress function, it reduces the quality.

And for my TMI moment. :-)

I find a lot of people confused about image quality when printing. They clip something off of the internet and it looks fine in the document on the screen but turns to **** (that's a professional term) when they go to print. Why does this happen?

Digital images are dots of either ink or luminous dots (pixels) on the screen. Screen resolution is 96 ppi (pixels per inch) whereas ink dots on a piece of paper in order to look proper when printed, need to be up around 300 dpi (dots per inch). So if you clip a 3.75" H x 5" W image from a web site what you get is a 360 X 480 pixel image (96 pixels per inch on each dimension.)

In order for an image, clipped from the screen, to print clearly and it not appear "pixelated" when you print it, you would need to clip an image that was 11.75" x 15" at 96 ppi and then scale it back to the 3.75' x 5" size.

How does this translate to creating an image in a graphics program or taking a picture with a digital camera and using it as an image in your printed Word document? Well, the simple answer is, "Pixels Matter!"

In other words, draw or shoot the largest image you can and then scale it down when you insert it into your document. Reducing the scale maintains quality and conversely, once you surpass the dimension in pixels of the original source image, enlarging will reduce quality.
