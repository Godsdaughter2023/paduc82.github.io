Try this..

Private Sub Worksheet_Change(ByVal Target As Range) 
    If ActiveCell.Value = "Add 5 Rows" Then 
    i = ActiveCell.Row 
    Range("A" & i & ":A" & i + 4).EntireRow.Insert Shift:=xlDown 
    ActiveCell.Offset(5, 0).Value = "Ready..." 
    ElseIf ActiveCell.Value = "Add 10 Rows" Then 
    i = ActiveCell.Row 
    Range("A" & i & ":A" & i + 9).EntireRow.Insert Shift:=xlDown 
    ActiveCell.Offset(10, 0).Value = "Ready..." 
    ElseIf ActiveCell.Value = "Add 20 Rows" Then 
    i = ActiveCell.Row 
    Range("A" & i & ":A" & i + 19).EntireRow.Insert Shift:=xlDown 
    ActiveCell.Offset(20, 0).Value = "Ready..."

End Sub

You could, certainly. But... ONLY IF THE UPDATE SUCCEEDS. And just THAT'S what's NOT assured when you change the product code. Besides, if you DO have adverse effects and claim warranty, then Nokia can very easily find out that you've had your product code changed, because of the version mismatch. Suppose you originally had a (57.02) model (PC: 0539338), and you change the product code so as to reflect a (02)-model. You then apply the update, it succeeds, but you notice adverse effects, such as a malfunction of any sort (e.g. not being able to hear a caller). After the update you changed the PC back to what it was. You then visit an NSP to tell them about your problem. STANDARD procedure at ANY NSP will be for them to check your phone's firmware version. THAT'S where the red alert will come round, as they'll find out that you have firmware version 3.0368.0.0.1, which isn't available for your phone... So, you'll lose the warranty.
And should you not change back the product code, then you're still in a bucket of horse manure, because they'll simply compare the product code read out by the software with the product code under the battery compartment.
Then there's a third issue: SHOULD you forge the sticker on the phone to match the product code you used, then you automatically lose any of the privileges offered by the warranty, as ANY changes to these stickers also void the warranty. So, to go short, for the service points, there is NO WAY to avoid losing the warranty if you did indeed change the product code. Or, at least, not until the software version is also made officially available for the (57.02)-model...
After all this judicial gibberish, there's the technical bit which involves the update going wrong for no particular reason at all, in which case YOU wouldn't be able to boot your phone. The reason I capitalised the word "you" is, that IF you take it to an official NSP, they most probably WILL be able to boot the phone, or at least read out the Boot ROM BLOCK, which contains the product code, and they'll find out that you messed with it. Please note that I'm not here to police this board, that's what the moderators are for. But believe me: IF you change the product code, Nokia CAN find it out, if you LIKE IT OR NOT!

    I shall clarify the PURPOSE of localisation ONE more time. ALL of the phones in the NSeries contain Bluetooth, and the N91 even contains WLAN. Bluetooth and WLAN involve RF signals, more specifically on the 2.4Ghz-band (or, for WLAN, 5 GHz in the USA). Different countries have different regulations for use of devices using the 2.4 GHz band. For example, France PROHIBITS the use of the channels below 6 on this band. If you set your WLAN device to comply with French regulation, you'd only be able to select channels 6 and above for wireless communication. The French version of this phone (or, these phones) will of course HAVE to comply with these regulations, and, are also set NOT to use below-6 channels for WLAN/BT.
    Another stringent rule in France is that audio devices (such as MP3-players) are allowed to have A MAXIMUM Volume of 100dBA. So, Nokia set the French version to comply with this rule as well. Some of those settings may have been easily applied via the software; others may have been applied by altering the HARDWARE of the phone, which would make it INCOMPATIBLE with different versions of the software, as they DON'T recognise the modules...
    I can imagine that some people find it awkward that I only gave examples applicable in France, but that's because France has the MOST STRINGENT laws in this area IN THE WORLD! However, EACH LOCALISED VERSION (whether branded or unbranded) is set to comply with eventual rules in power in the area where the specific phone is MEANT TO BE SOLD.

And, generally, governments will NOT punish anyone who owns a device they might have acquired in another country. However, if Nokia (or any other manufacturer, for that matter) would market a large quantity of devices that are not compliant to local rules and laws, THEY would CERTAINLY be prone to law suits.
