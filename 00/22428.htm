rob koch say:

You seem a bit confused about the meaning and management of personal identity protection, Internet Protocol (IP) addresses and the security of the various accounts used with your devices.

Identity protection is a fallacy, these products are often provided by questionably capable companies like Equifax (whom you'll note from the news was recently breached itself), which are simply monitoring and notification services that let you know when your personal information has already apparently been breached.

That's not a bad thing to know, but unless they also include the service of fixing whatever malicious things might have been done using that exposed information, the value of that knowledge is usually just finding out that your information has been breached a little earlier, giving you more time to try and get the problems it creates fixed.

An IP address is nothing more then the Internet identification number for a location from which a device (i.e. PC, smartphone, etc.) is being used.  What would be most important to your bank is whether the access being made is actually related to you or someone else attempting to illicitly access your account.

What would typically be most important here is whether this IP address was registered as used in your local area or rather somewhere else in the country or world and especially whether you might actually have been in that location when the attempt occurred.  Since you told us noting about these details, it's not possible to provide anymore information than that.

I'm assuming when you mention the debit card, you mean that you've added this card as a "payment option" within the Microsoft Account you use with your PC or other devices to access the Outlook email associated with that same account?  If so, the best way to secure that is to include two-step verification or app passwords for any purposes where that account is used, though this should at least be done for making any changes to the account and payment information itself, which I believe is now required.

About two-step verification

Asking such a broad range of security questions in the same thread can result in confusing answers, so it's best to focus on a single issue such as account access or the IP address question in a single thread.

< EDIT > I just noticed I forgot to mention that none of the above has anything directly to do with Defender, since that security application is instead trying to aid in protecting your PC device from malicious software or other types of attack.  Any of the other items above can or will be involved with malware, but that doesn't mean they specifically have anything to do with Defender itself.

Also, all of the above items are either the same (IP addresses) or have equivalent items like personal accounts or security applications in the Apple world.  The names and operation of these will simply be different, though similar.  Obviously, your personal identity is exactly the same, but how the various security tools used with each might attempt to protect this information may be different as well.

Yes, there's a lot of overlap, but also a lot of differences, which is why working in two environments such as Apple's and Microsoft's can be challenging to a non-technical person.

doug robbins - mvp office apps & services (word) say:

When using Content Control, you should not be applying Protection for Filling in Forms as that is intended for use with legacy FormFields.

If you do what to prevent the text and some of the content controls from being (easily) modified, select that text and the Content Controls and use the Grouping facility in the Controls section of the Developer tab.

You will not of course want to include the Picture Content Controls in such Grouping and you will need to make sure that in the Properties dialog for them that the "Content control cannot be deleted" box is unchecked (but that is the default setting).
