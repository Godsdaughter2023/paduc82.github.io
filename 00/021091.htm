the WACC is i found is 17.6%

Do you still have a question for us?

I believe I answered your original question, to wit:  why does Excel IRR of the series of FCFFs and FCFEs alone sometimes return a #NUM error?

The answer is:  (a) because a valid IRR > -100% is not computable for that series (FCFE); and (b) in general, the IRR of the FCFFs and FCFEs alone is meaningless because it is not computable for all-positive cash flows, the desirable, if not most common situation.

Is that clear?

-----

However, with the new information above, we can demonstrate a valid use of Excel IRR with the formulas that calculate the NPV of the intrinisic values of the firm and of equity.

As I mentioned previously, the IRR paradigm can be used with those intrinsic values to determine WACC and ROE (return on equity; aka cost of equity).

Normally, expected WACC and ROE are determined directly by other means, as you did.

But the following demonstrates the use of Excel IRR to determine average WACC and ROE when multiple expected rates apply in a multi-stage model.

You can download the example Excel file "noli fcff.xls" (click here) [1].

[EDIT] I don't know if "average WACC" and "average ROE" is the right terminology.  I chose it in the same vein as CAGR.  On second thought, perhaps "effective WACC" and "effective ROE" would be better.  TBD.



In summary, assume a two-stage model with different expected WACC and ROE for each stage:  17.60% and 27.50% for the first 4 periods, and 8.08% and 13.75% for the last 8 periods.

(Note:  17.60% and 27.50% are the WACC and ROE rates that you provide.  8.08% and 13.75% are completely hypothetical.  They might not make sense in your model.  They are provided just for numerical examples.)

The FCFFs and FCFEs that you provide are in C2:N2 and C3:N3.  The corresponding hypothetical expected WACC and ROE are in C4:N4 and C5:N5.

The NPV of the intrinsic firm value is calculated in B12 with the formula =SUMPRODUCT(C10:M10/C11:M11).  It is the sum of the cash flows in C10:M10 divided by the corresponding discount rates in C11:M11.  (See the detail explanation below.)

The average WACC is calculated in B13 with the formula =IRR(B10:M10).

Similarly, the NPV of the intrinsic equity value is calculated in B19 with the formula =SUMPRODUCT(C17:M17/C18:M18).  It is the sum of the cash flows in C17:M17 divided by the corresponding discount rates in C18:M18.

The average ROE is calculated in B20 with the formula =IRR(B17:M17).

Is this what the CFO that you work with has in mind?

-----

Note that the usefulness of the IRR in this paradigm hinges on the hypothesis that more than one pair of expected WACC and ROE applies to the FCFF and FCFE models, which is typical for a multi-stage model.  You might want to revisit the assumptions in your model.

The IRR is computable even if there is only one pair of WACC and ROE, as you provide.  But the average WACC and ROE -- the IRR -- is the same as the expected WACC and ROE.

-----

Details .....

As I noted previously (with errata), the NPV of the intrinsic values of the firm and of equity can be calculated as follows when there is only one expected WACC and ROE.

firmValue = Sigma(FCFF[t]/(1+WACC)^t, t=1,...,n-1) + FCFF[n]/(WACC-gFCFF)/(1+WACC)^(n-1)

equityValue = Sigma(FCFE[t]/(1+ROE)^t, t=1,...,n-1) + FCFE[n]/(ROE-gFCFE)/(1+ROE)^(n-1)

The following more-general formula is needed when there are multiple expected WACC and ROE.

firmValue = Sigma(FCFF[t]/Product(1+WACC[i], i=1,...,t), t=1,...,n-1)
+ FCFF[n]/(WACC[n]-gFCFF)/Product(1+WACC[i], i=1,...,n-1)

equityValue = Sigma(FCFE[t]/Product(1+ROE[i], i=1,...,t), t=1,...,n-1)
+ FCFE[n]/(ROE[n]-gFCFE)/Product(1+ROE[i], i=1,...,n-1)

The firm-value discount rates are in C11:M11.  The discount rate for period t is the product of the WACC for periods 1 through t.  Thus, C11 is the formula =1+C4, and D11 is =C11*(1+D4), copied into E11:M11 so that E11 is =D11*(1+E4), F11 is =E11*(1+F4), etc.

Similarly, the equity-value discount rates are in C18:M18.  The discount rate for period t is the product of the ROE for periods 1 through t.  Thus, C18 is the formula =1+C5, and D18 is =C18*(1+D5), copied into E18:M18 so that E18 is =D18*(1+E5), F18 is =E18*(1+F5), etc.

The NPV cash flows in C10:L10 and C17:L17 are the same as the FCFFs and FCFEs that you provide in C2:L2 and C3:L3.

The firm-value NPV cash flow in M10 is the formula =M2+N2/(N4-B6), combining FCFF[11] in M2 with the terminal value FCFF[12]/(WACC[12]-gFCFF)

Similarly, the equity-value NPV cash flow in M17 is the formula =M3+N3/(N5-B7), combining FCFE[11] in M3 with the terminal value FCFE[12]/(ROE[12]-gFCFE).

Note that gFCFF (B6) and gFCFE (B7) are the same, since you provide only one terminal growth rate.  Usually, they are different, according to the previously-cited lecture notes (click here) [2].  You might want to revisit the assumptions in your model.

The IRR cash flows in B10:L10 and B17:L17 are essentially the same, with the addition of CF[0] in B10 and B17.  Since the IRR is the discount rate that causes the sum of all discounted cash flows to be zero, B10 is =-B12, the NPV of the firm value, and B17 is =-B19, the NPV of the equity value.

Note that the IRR cash flows change signs more than once.  Consequently, there might be multiple valid IRR solutions.  Also, Excel IRR might return a misleading value instead of a #NUM error due to defects in the internal implementation [3].

Therefore, it is prudent to confirm that the rates returned by Excel IRR in B13 and B20 do indeed cause the NPV to be close to zero.  To that end, the "check NPV" formula in B14 is =B10+NPV(B13,C10:M10), and the formula in B21 is =B17+NPV(B20,C17:M17).  Note that the result in both B14 and B21 are indeed infinitesimally close to zero.

(In fact, I determined that the rates returned in B13 and B20 are the only valid IRRs between -100% and 1000%.)
