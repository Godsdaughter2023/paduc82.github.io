john w. vinson say:

What you're doing wrong is the very design of your table. You're "encoding data as fieldnames", also known as "committing spreadsheet". Rather than having separate FIELDS for attendance, you should have separate RECORDS - one record per month. I'd use a simple table with an Autonumber primary key, a date/time field for the date of the meeting, a field for the ID of the attendee, and likely some other fields.

What is the current structure of your table? Can you consider normalizing this into a proper database rather than your current non-normalized spreadsheet?

scottgem say:

As John said, what you are doing wrong is the way you designed your database. 

You should have one table for members and another table for the annual meetings. What are these 12 Yes/No fields you have to add?

john w. vinson say:

It's a very common design mistake - you're neither the first nor the last to make it!

The attendance data needs three tables: your members table (from which you can delete the many yes/no fields AFTER you migrate the data into the normalized structure); a table of Meetings, with meeting date, location, comments, etc.; and an Attendance table related one-to-many to each of these. You may or may not need other information in this table (I can imagine a Text or Memo field to record comments by or about this member at this meeting, for example).

It would not be at all hard to take your existing table and create Append queries to migrate the memberID and the meeting date into the new table.

Access' refusal to let you add fields is because deleting a field doesn't really delete it - there is information hanging on in the hidden systems tables. It might be resolved by Compacting the database, or by creating a new database, creating a copy of your table with the desired new yes/no fields, and Appending the data into it; but this is no easier than creating and filling the new normalized attendance table.

And... thanks to you and your members for your service!

scottgem say:

I agree with Karl about the Compact and Repair.

You need a table like:

tblAttendance

You only create a record when member attends a meeting. With this structure you only have to add a Meeting in the Meetings table. instead of modifying a table. Your reports will be easier too.

albert d. kallal say:

Actually, this issue can come up even with a well-designed database.

While you are likely nowhere near close to 255 field, the limit can crop up over the LIFETIME edits on that form. As you edit, the control count is not re-set. So as you add/delete and edit the form, the control count goes up.

And a compact + repair will not fix this issue.

Simply do a save as for the form (different name of course). Now rename (or delete) the original form, and re-name your existing form to the original name. You should be good to go.

Regards,

albert d. kallal say:

You are correct - it is in the 700 range - but the tip and suggestion still applies.

Not aware this "ever increasing" control count issue ever applied to tables. However if one is using fixed size columns, then one can hit the record limit size before you hit the max number of columns (255).

So my bad - the form control limit is in the 700 range. However that "build up" over the life time of controls on the form still remains - and a C+R will not fix this - but a save-as form will fix (re-set) the control count.
