rob koch say:

Though Windows Defender is capable of detecting and blocking most malware that affects a Windows PC regardless of which browser is being used, the issue is actually more one of the exploitability of the browser itself in order to deliver the malware in the first place.

What I mean by this is that the core protections that Microsoft includes within the various versions of Internet Explorer have improved over time, both those provided within the browser and with Windows itself.  This means that even the different revisions of browser on a particular version of Windows can differ in their ability to protect from browser based exploits.

This can be particularly important whenever a new vulnerability is found within the browser which has yet to be patched, since in some cases these protections can successfully block the exploit from working via other mitigations.

The issue here is that the methods and design of each individual browser, even different versions of the same browser, may not provide for or allow the additional protections within Windows or Defender to work effectively.  This doesn't mean they aren't working to protect Internet Explorer, but they may do little or nothing to aid in blocking malware for the less integrated 3rd-party browsers.

The result is the high level of confusion you've seen in most Internet forums and blogs about the protection provided by Defender, since to most users that's the only protection that a Windows system contains.  In truth as with all anti-malware products, the final malware payload and its detection by anti-malware are simply the last resort, after all other methods have failed.

These include features such as URL blocking (SmartScreen Filter), Protected Mode (if enabled in Internet Explorer itself) and other built-in Windows protections such as ASLR and DEP (see Kernel Improvements in the following)

http://technet.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/jj983723.aspx

http://blogs.msdn.com/b/ie/archive/2012/03/12/enhanced-memory-protections-in-ie10.aspx

So now that you hopefully understand that the core issue isn't with Defender itself, here's a link to some better information about the various browsers and their operation and protection with Windows.  Note that the protections provided within the browsers themselves are often the most important factor, though there are various add-on protections available for some that might aid in reducing their susceptibility to particular types of attack.

https://www.nsslabs.com/reports/categories/test-reports/browser-security

Not the simple answer you wanted huh?  Unfortunately if security were that simple you wouldn't be here in the first place.

Rob

When you move a header in your document that is part of the Table of Contents, you can make the Table of Contents show the new location or page number of that header by updating it.
