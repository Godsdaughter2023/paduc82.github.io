ken sheridan say:

You'll find an example of the use of correlated combo boxes for drilling down through a hierarchy in ComboDemo.zip in my public databases folder at:

https://onedrive.live.com/?cid=44CC60D7FEA42912&id=44CC60D7FEA42912!169

Note that if you are using an earlier version of Access you might find that the colour of some form objects such as buttons shows incorrectly and you will need to  amend the form design accordingly.  

If you have difficulty opening the link copy its text (NB, not the link location) and paste it into your browser's address bar.

In this little demo file the combo boxes relate to a tree-structured hierarchy, whereas in your case I'm assuming that will not be so, as I would guess there is a many-to-many relationship type between fiscal years and states.  In essence this does not make a difference to the methodology, but it means the correlation between the combo boxes would not be a simple one of a restriction on a column in a single table RowSource of a States combo box by referencing the Fiscal Years combo box.  In your case the RowSource query would need to include not only a States table, but also whatever table models the relationship type between States and Fiscal Years and the restriction would be on a FiscalYear or similar column in the latter.

In my demo the rows returned are restricted by the use of a query which references the combo boxes as parameters.  Each combo box allows an 'All ….' selection to be made, represented by a zero in the bound column, in addition to selecting a specific items from the list, so entry into the hierarchy at any level is possible.  This is reflected in the form's query, which is:

SELECT Counties.County, Districts.District, Parishes.Parish, Locations.Location, Parishes.ParishID

FROM ((Counties INNER JOIN Districts ON Counties.CountyID = Districts.CountyID)
INNER JOIN Parishes ON Districts.DistrictID = Parishes.DistrictID)
INNER JOIN Locations ON Parishes.ParishID = Locations.ParishID
WHERE (((Counties.CountyID)=[Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoCounty])
AND ((Districts.DistrictID)=[Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoDistrict])
AND ((Parishes.ParishID)=[Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoParish]))
OR (((Districts.DistrictID)=[Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoDistrict])
AND ((Parishes.ParishID)=[Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoParish])
AND (([Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoCounty])=0))
OR (((Counties.CountyID)=[Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoCounty])
AND ((Parishes.ParishID)=[Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoParish])
AND (([Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoDistrict])=0))
OR (((Parishes.ParishID)=[Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoParish])
AND (([Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoCounty])=0)
AND (([Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoDistrict])=0))
OR (((Counties.CountyID)=[Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoCounty])
AND ((Districts.DistrictID)=[Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoDistrict])
AND (([Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoParish])=0))
OR (((Districts.DistrictID)=[Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoDistrict])
AND (([Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoCounty])=0)
AND (([Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoParish])=0))
OR (((Counties.CountyID)=[Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoCounty])
AND (([Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoDistrict])=0)
AND (([Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoParish])=0))
OR ((([Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoCounty])=0)
AND (([Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoDistrict])=0)
AND (([Forms]![frmDrillDown]![cboGotoParish])=0))
ORDER BY Counties.County, Districts.District, Parishes.Parish;

It is then simply a case of requerying the form when a selection is made in each combo box.

While the underlying logic behind a query like that above is actually quite simple, it does take care to put it together.  The parentheses are very important as these force the OR and AND operations to evaluate independently of each other.  It's a big ask for somebody new to Access to attempt this and, while I cannot but admire you for trying, I do wonder whether you are quite far enough up that learning curve just yet.  In particular, I'm not convinced from the SQL statement to posted earlier that your database has a correctly structured logical model in the form of a set of normalized related tables which would enable you to correlate the combo boxes.  For instance, the fact that you have columns for Loc Id and State in a single referencing table, Tbl Go Memo, points to redundancy, as the former determines the latter via intervening levels in the hierarchical relationship between locations and states.  The key feature of my demo is that it is based on a set of correctly normalized tables in which there is no redundancy of the sort which appears to be evident in yours.  The elimination of redundancy through normalization is of course very important in itself, protecting the integrity of the database by the elimination of the risk of update anomalies, but it is also the basis for the building of a coherent interface which allows for the sort of functionality which you are attempting.
