    • Load more results from your message search if you can't find your messages in the first 20 returned. Once you reach the bottom of the results, click "more" to load more messages [released in 8.33]

    As you know, this is in reference to search out on the main screen, not searching within a conversation (Ctrl-F). The latter is still capped, though at 30, and there's no equivalent to "More" there.

    However, much more important than that: the search out on the main screen--even when searching on the Messages category specifically--is a radically different experience than searching within a conversation when it comes to the amount of time that it takes to return and then progress through results.

    I'm not talking about a small difference but a major one, perhaps a 20x difference (of course, you need some months of history to notice this). Searching within a conversation is stunningly slow by comparison.

    Why?  I thought search was fatally flawed in Skype 8, but this gives me some hope. If one part of the same program doesn't show a problem, there's a chance the other part can be fixed.

    Note that I'm talking about the latest Skype 8 (but it applies to any version of Skype 8 that I've used, which are all recent versions) on Win10. (Not the UWP version, which may or may not have the same issue, but I assume that it does.)

    Another bonus of using search on the main screen: it's much easier to see WHEN a message is from, since each result has a date under it. When using Ctrl-F, it's not easy at all, since you need to look for the nearest date demarcation, and one is not necessarily nearby. And to make it worse, floating the mouse over a timestamp doesn't reveal the date--as Skype 7 does.

    So, as long as one ignores Ctrl-F, search in S8 isn't bad at all. This also ignores the fact that you only get 6 months worth of results (main search is well fast enough to support more), and that there are only 2 years total of history, but that's for other threads.

    Hi.  I have been having exactly the same issue.  Doesn't matter what operating system you want to install, whether it is Windows XP or some version of Linux, the 4gb drive isn't big enough - it gets full too quickly and then there is no room for updates.

    Anyway I have just done some searching and come across various articles and posts that explain how to partition your TWO drives, so that part of the operating system is on the small 4gb drive and part of it is on the bigger 16gb drive (you need the boot up parts on the 4gb drive because it is faster than the 16gb drive).

    You don't have two partitions - what you have is two separate ssd drives.  The 4gb drive (the faster one) is soldered onto the Motherboard, and can't be replaced.  It is intended for the operating system, with the 16gb drive (slower and replaceable!) intended for storing your personal files and some programs.  But Operating Systems have got bigger in the last few years, and 4gb isn't big enough.

    You have a couple of options.  One costs time and faffing about.  The other costs a lot less time but some money!

    I have two Asus Eee 900 PC's.  One of them I have replaced the 16gb drive (this is easy - you just unscrew the back, take two screws out, unslot the old one and slot in the new one - just lean against the radiator when you do it, to avoid static, which might possibly affect the drives.).  I have replaced it with a 64gb faster ssd drive - it's great!  It cost about £60, but now I can run anything I like on this great, tiny little machine.
    This is probably the best option if you want to stick with Windows.  Bear in mind that XP will technically become vulnerable after April 2014 when Microsoft stop supporting it.  Hence I upgraded to the 64gb and installed Windows 7, which is running nicely (although I do prefer XP).

    My second Eee Pc I am going to sell, so don't want to buy a new SSD, but can't find anything small enough to install!  So -

    OPTION 1:  Have a look at this page, where it explains how to set partitions so the operating system is spread out across both drives - so you don't run out of space.  These posts are all with regard to Linux - I don't know if it would work with Windows.

    http://forums.auroraos.org/viewtopic.php?f=42&t=3896

    OPTION 2:

    To do this upgrading the disc option, you need to do a couple of geeky things, but they're not difficult when you know how.  I know nothing about computers, and was scared of tinkering, but read up a lot and the scary monsters are just little mice really.  And it's a lot easier than the partitioning options above, but means spending some money on a bigger ssd.

    Firstly - you need to upgrade the bios.  Don't ask me what bios are, I don't know, I just followed the instructions.  This is very easy if you have XP on it already.  You need to download the latest bios file for the Asus 900.  The easiest way to find them is if you register your netbook on the Asus site (you need the serial number).  It's quite quick and easy.  Once your netbook is registered with them, you click on the 'Support' button on the Asus page, and there is a list of downloads - all the latest drivers, bios etc for the 900.  You will have most of the drivers already - they are really just for people who have lost their recovery disc.  So the only thing you need is the bios file.  There is a list of them.  The only one you need is the one at the top of the list - this is the latest one, and it will instal some software on your netbook, that allows it to accept a faster ssd.  If you can't or don't want to register with Asus, then google 'Asus Eee PC 900 bios' and you will probably find links to them anyway, but I find the Asus site is easy and simple (and trustworthy).

    So click to download the bios file - it takes seconds.  It will download as a zip file which needs extracting.  Click on 'extract' and you will be asked where you want the extracted files to be stored.  Select desktop.  Or if you can't find 'desktop' in the list, save it to 'My Documents' - just remember where you saved it to.

    Now go to the start menu on the netbook - programs - asus - a side menu will open from the asus folder with 'asus update'.  Click on this and the asus update utility will open.  There are about four options to select, like download bios, upload bios etc.  Select the one that says 'from file' (can't remember if it says instal from file, download from file or whatever, but it's the file option you select.  Then 'next' and it will ask you where to find the file, and open up a box for you to select the location - select 'desktop' if that is where you have saved it, then follow the instructions (ok, or continue or whatever) and it does it all for you.  It will say something like it is 'flashing the bios' - don't use the computer or turn it off while this is happening.  It only takes a minute or two - screen might flash on and off a few times.  Then it will say you need to restart the computer.  When you restart, a load of text comes up and at the end of it it says power off your computer now.  So you press the off button and turn it off.  All done.  Your bios have been updated.

    If you want to go into the bios menu and see the 'ssd master' option now showing, then press F2 repeatedly as soon as you press the on button and the bios menu will open - you just move around it using the up down left and right arrows on your keyboard.  But it's not necessary to go into the bios menu unless you are installing a new operating system from usb or usb dvd drive.

    So - with bios updated, you can install a new, bigger, faster ssd to replace the 16gb one.  Before you do this, you need to make sure you have your XP disc to reinstall afterwards.  Or you need to have made a system image onto a dvd.  Personally I wouldn't bother reinstalling XP when it runs out in 6 months, so if you don't have an XP recovery disc and haven't made a system image disc, then you will either need a copy of Windows 7 or instal Linux.  Personally I prefer to stick with Windows.

    Anyway - let's assume you have your XP disc.  You need a Supertalent Mini PCI-E MLC sata ssd - they cost about £60 and might take 10 days to come in the post as they come from Germany - not sure if I'm allowed to say where you can buy them, but the big online shopping store has them.  Just enter "Supertalent Mini PCi-E MLC sata ssd for asus eee pc" (without the quotes) in the search box.

    When it arrives, do the job standing next to a radiator (a cold one!) - maybe have a little table there with everything you need on it.  You need - the netbook, the supertalent drive, with the packet already open, and a small posidrive screwdriver, plus a small container (eg an eggcup) to put the little screws in so you don't lose them.  Lean against the radiator until you have finished, to ground yourself and avoid static.  Remove the battery and make sure the netbook is not plugged into the mains.  Take the screws out of the back panel and remove the back panel.  There are two chip type things in there.  The smaller one, nearest you (with the battery end farthest from you) is the RAM.  The bigger one, furthest from you - is the 16gb ssd drive.  There are two small screws at one end.  Remove these.  Then gently pull the ssd sideways - it is slotted in - and it will slot out.  Now put the new one in - slot it in in the same way, giving it a slightly firm push into place to make sure it is slotted into the connectors.  Put the two small screws into the new ssd drive that you took out of the old drive. Then put the back panel back on and screw it in again.  Job done.  You can now put the battery back in.

    If you looked in the bios menu now, you would see the new SSD listed there in white.

    Now you are ready to put XP back on (or your chosen operating system).  You will need a usb dvd drive or to have the os on a usb stick (microsoft have a tool to let you do this, but I've never done it - I use a usb disc drive).  Plug in the usb stick or usb disc drive, with the disc in it. Press the power button on and immediately press F2 repeatedly, over and over again, until the bios menu appears.  You need to disable the original 4gb drive on the motherboard, so the operating system goes onto the new 64gb drive.  This is too long winded to explain (this post is long enough already!) so google it - most of the instructions on the bios menu are quite easy - you just use the arrow keys on the keyboard to move from menu to menu, and the escape button to go back to the main menu, then when you have finished what you want to do, you press F10 on the keyboard and the system starts to boot up.  So, using the right arrow key, go to 'boot' (I think it's the third one along).  There is a list of options - down arrow key to go down the list, enter key to open that item on the list, escape key to go back to the list.  When you see the 4gb ssd, hit enter and arrow key down to 'disabled' then hit return.  This will stop the 4gb drive from being used, so your new 64gb drive will become the main and only drive.  Then hit F10 - select 'Ok' and hit return - the system will start to boot.  Immediately start pressing the Escape key repeatedly and a list of boot options will come up. Down arrow key to usb drive, or disc drive and hit return.  The netbook will now boot the operating system from the disc or usb drive.   When it says - hit any key to boot (or similar), hit any key on the keyboard.  Windows will now start installing.  It's very simple - it does most of it itself, just follow any instructions that come up, like, Ok to restart system, or remove disc from drive.  it might reboot a couple of times.  If it asks you about partitioning, just opt for the simplest option, like use whole disc.

    When it's all done, you'll be at the set up - like when you first bought the computer and switched it on.  By the time you have finished this, if you open 'My Computer' and right click on C drive - properties, it will show you have a 64gb drive with, say 15gb used.  Nice!

    There are youtube videos that show you how to replace the ssd drive - it's simpler than it sounds.

    If you do decide to install Windows 7, it does everything it should, and downloads the drivers you need automatically, but you need to find two drivers - one for the screen resolution, one so it recognises a piece of hardware.  There are places on the internet that have links to them - just needs googling.  It works fine without them, but everything looks a bit stretched on the screen.
