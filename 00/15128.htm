a. user say:

So what if I newly became owner of the phone and I don't know the reset protection recovery key? Is there any way to get around this? I don't have contact with the original owner at all

Hello, IbleedInk.

To know about your phone's Reset Protection Status, you may click here. On the Reset Protection Status page, type the IMEI or MEID. Find it by dialing *#06# on the phone.

However, if the phone is set up but locked, the only way to get the recovery key is to get in touch with the previous owner to turn off the Reset Protection.

How to Turn off Reset Protection

1. Go to account.microsoft.com/devices.

2. Next to the phone you no longer own, select Remove phone.

3. Select the I'm ready to remove my phone check box, note the recovery key, and then select Remove. The recovery key will display on the page and will also be emailed to you.

4. When you're certain that you have your recovery key saved, select the I've kept a copy of the recovery key check box, and then select Okay.

5. Forward the recovery key email to your phone recipient.

Your phone is no longer listed on account.microsoft.com/devices, and the recipient can remove Reset Protection during phone set up using the Disable using recovery key option.

Let us know if you run into any more questions, we'll be here to help out.

In the answer is utterly simple and the solution nearly as easy to implement as well.  The point is that most of these popups today are just that, browser popup pages and nothing more, so there's nothing malicious to detect, since the only thing malicious about them is the content they contain.. Though some security apps have attempted to deal with this by detecting the content itself, that's even more ridiculous, since an inherent trait of these pages is that everything about them including phone numbers or other contact information changes within days or even hours.

So what can we do to stop them?  The answer can be gleaned by actually reading and realizing the key trigger sequence exposed in Greg's ridiculously complex descriptions of the common attack method used today.  His own focus is on the last step in the process, the page redirection used to actually display the final popup itself.

The error here is that everyone including Greg is focused on trying to trap these last steps in the process, while the real answer is to instead focus on stopping the initial trigger, along with doing your best to block the selection of your PC (and yourself) as a target in the first place.  By this I mean the advertising systems that are tracking your every move and collecting the information about you that allows them to determine that you're a potential mark and thus worth being targeted for the display of the popups used to dupe unsuspecting consumers into calling the con men themselves.

If you haven't figured it out yet, the key is to stop the tracking used by the advertising delivery systems, in effect crippling not only the trigger mechanism, but also possibly the data collection systems themselves to some extent.  To do this, we need to learn how Windows settings related to privacy in both the browser(s) as well as Windows itself truly work. 

The key here is the Windows 10 (and 8.1) Privacy setting titled; "Let apps use my advertising ID for experiences across apps".

By setting this to "Off", we both remove much of the ability for the advertising networks to track us as well as resting the exiting ID, which should remove the link to any history of this item on that particular PC.  In effect, this should cripple the ability for the malicious popup purveyors to track you via either the machine or your own personal Microsoft Account on that specific PC.

The other settings worth trying are those related to Tracking [protection] such as "Send Do Not Track requests to sites you visit [in Internet Explorer].

By enabling this, you request that the websites you visit directly not collect the tracking information that makes you a target for the ads, effectively crippling their ability to identify you.  Of course this depends upon the primary website honoring these requests, since the Tracking protection system is voluntary, but most of the reputable major name websites will do this.

Though this system isn't guaranteed, it stands a better chance of working to try and block the malicious pages than most other methods.
