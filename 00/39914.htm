a. user say:

By design, existing restore points are automatically deleted after major updates. You wouldn't want to restore a restore point made before a major update - that would almost certainly damage your operating system.

For the same reason, restore points are automatically deleted after X number of days. In Windows 7 that was 90 days; it's earlier in Windows 10 but I'm afraid I don't know how long X is. You wouldn't want to restore a very old restore point

System restore is widely misunderstood and often confused with backing up. Since Windows 10 includes both file backup and disk image backup - and since third-party software that does this is widely available (and superior to Windows' own tools, in my view) it's no surprise that System Restore is turned off by default. Wouldn't surprise me if it was removed altogether in a future upgrade.

Instead of System Restore, you should learn about making disk images. Briefly, an image is an identical copy of your hard drive. If your computer gets messed up, just restore an image and you've gone back in time like nothing ever happened. With third-party imaging software you can schedule images to occur automatically, overnight, so you're never more than 24 hours from a working copy of your computer.

a. user say:

No, you don't have to buy third-party software. You can use Windows' own backup and imaging utilities, just as you can use Windows' own media player, antivirus, PDF reader, web browser etc. if you want to. But often third-party software has better features and is easier to use. Windows can't be all things to all people.

Backing up is one of the most basic skills that a computer user has, regardless of what operating system they use. And computer journalists, bloggers, authors and teachers have been teaching people to backup since forever. Today's backup software makes backing up effortless. There's no excuse for not backing up.

That "feature that was working absolutely fine" was actually not working fine, which is why it's turned off by default in Windows 10. There are much better solutions today.

Good things to know for your Surface Pro 2 - tips, accessories, backup - Microsoft Community

elaland say:

Good things to know for your Surface Pro 2 - tips, accessories, backup and restore

I am an engineer, and decided to convert from a Panasonic toughbook laptop to the Surface Pro 2.  I wanted to carry a lighter load, and discover some of the ins and outs of tablets and Windows 8 in anticipation of supporting Windows 8 machines at our location in the future.  In order to make the tablet a fully functional laptop equivalent for my needs I purchased some additional hardware: a keyboard cover, USB mouse, docking station, display port to vga cable adapter, USB to 9-pin serial cable adapter, and a 3.0 USB four port hub.

I've listed below some tips I found useful, and other things that I had to learn the hard way with a lot of internet searching.  I did run into a problem where my SP2 would not boot reliably after a few weeks.  It seemed to boot usually after 3 or 4 tries if I was connected to my docking station, but sometimes a half hour of reboots would not get it to come up if I was using it without the docking station.  I finally had the unit exchanged which led to some of the items listed below regarding backup and restore.  I can tell you that Acronis Backup and Recovery 11.5 will successfully make backups, but restoring the System volume it has an issue with due to their proprietary "Active Restore" feature which only works with MBR disks, not GPT volumes such as the SP2 uses.  I still have an active case with Acronis, but in the meantime I was able to use the Powershell approach to successfully transfer everything from my old SP2 to the new one (even with an error message saying it failed).

I hope the following items are found useful for those who are looking for some answers in one place.  A couple of them I've copied from help files.

-------------
Create a Recovery USB drive:

Step 1:     Connect the power supply to Surface and plug into an electrical outlet.
Step 2:     Insert your USB drive into the USB port on your Surface.
Step 3:     Swipe in from the right edge of the screen, and then tap Search.
(If you're using a mouse, point to the upper-right corner of the screen, move the mouse pointer down, and then click Search.)
Step 4:     In the search box, enter recovery, and in the search results, tap or click Recovery, and then tap or click Create a recovery drive.
Step 5:     In the User Account Control dialog box, tap or click Yes.
Step 6:     Tap or click to select Copy the recovery partition from the PC to the recovery drive, and then, tap or click Next.
Step 7:     Tap or click the USB drive you want to use, and then tap or click Next.
Step 8:     Tap or click Create.

The recovery image and necessary recovery tools will be copied to your USB drive. Your Surface must stay awake during the copy process, which will take 10 to 15 minutes.
Step 9:     After the recovery tools are copied, do one of the following:

    Tap or click Finish if you want to keep the recovery tools on your Surface.

    or
    Tap or click Delete the recovery partition if you want to remove the recovery tools from Surface and free up disk space. To confirm, tap or click Delete, and when removal is complete, tap or click Finish.

    Important
    If you choose to delete your recovery partition, you will need your USB recovery drive if you ever need to refresh or reset your Surface in the future.

Step 10:     Remove your USB drive from Surface and keep it in a safe place.

It's best not to use the recovery drive to store other files or data.

--------------

Booting from a USB into the UEFI firmware menu:
    Hold volume up button
    Press and release the power button
    When Surface logo appears release the volume up button
    
In UEFI firmware menu consider disabling the "Secure Boot" feature.  It is designed to protect against rootkits, and the like but also prevents booting from USB sticks and I'm not sure about other potential issues.

--------------

A red background on the "Windows" boot screen indicates the boot keys are missing.

1.    With the Surface powered off, press and held the Volume Up button.
2.    Power the Surface on, holding the Volume Up button pressed the entire time.
3.    In the BIOS screen choose “Install Default Secure Boot Keys” and selected “Yes” to cause the Surface to load the default secure variables.
4.    Ensure the “Secure Boot” option is enabled, exit setup, choosing “Yes” to save the configuration changes.

NOTE: In order to boot from a USB drive Secure Boot MUST be DISABLED.

--------------

Boot from a USB
    Hold volume down button
    Press and release the power button
    When Surface logo appears release the volume down button
  NOTE: If using a USB hub to access external devices along with the boot USB drive ensure that the boot USB drive is in the 1st port of the USB hub or it may not be recognized.  I used the following:
  Port 1 - USB boot drive
  Port 2 - USB drive containing backup to recover from
  Port 3 - USB keyboard
  Port 4 - USB mouse

Backup and Restore using windows Powershell.
BACKUP:
  Search for "Windows Powershell" on Metro, right click result and "Run as administrator"
  Enter the following command:
    WbAdmin start backup -backupTarget:D: -inlcude:C: -allCritical -quiet
      Note: :D: reference is the hard drive letter for the USB drive used to store the backup
RESTORE:
  1. Boot from Microsoft Recovery USB Flash (hopefully created after receipt of unit)
    or using machine's recovery partition: PC Settings, General, Advanced Startup, Restart Now
      NOTE: Secure boot must be disabled via the UEFI firmware - See "Red background" text above.

  2. AFter booting from from Recovery USB or the Recovery Partition Select
       Troubleshoot, Advanced Options, System Image Recovery
         If default backup file is correct use it, else select correct location of backup file

  3. Rather than getting a "restore complete" type of message I got an error message box stating "the system image restore failed", and "Error details: The parameter is incorrect (0x80070057).

  4. Ignore the error message, and reboot the tablet; the restore worked.

Two button shutdown/startup process:
  Power Down
    Hold power button in for 10 secs (forces shutdown instead of sleep)
    Hold volumue Up button and power button at same time for 15 seconds, and then release both
    Wait 10 seconds
    Press and release the power up button to turn it on.

Booting into Safe Mode:
  The F8 key to get into safe mode in prior Windows versions is disabled in version 8/8.1.
  The most direct way to enter Safe Mode assuming your Surface Pro 2 is active and can boot is as follows:
  1. Pull up the Charms menu (swiping from right or use "Windows key + C")
  2. Select the following: Settings, Change PC Settings, Update and Recovery, Recovery, Under "Advanced Settings" select the Reboot button.
  3. Wait - screen may go blank for a little; be patient.
  4. Select Troubleshoot, Startup Settings, Restart
  5. Press a number key corresponding to the type of Safe Mode you wish to boot into.

I used Safe Mode to remove a Trojan virus that my antvirus software detected, but couldn't remove.  My antivirus includes a command line executable version and fortuntely by entering Safe Mode and running the antivirus executable I was able to remove that particular trojan.

    Change font size to 12x16 in properties for the command prompt.

    Click on "change Settings that are currently not available"
      Uncheck "turn on fast startup (recommended)"
Note: I also changed power button settings to "shut down" instead of default "Sleep"

Help alleviate the periodic loss of WIFI connection:

Uncheck the option to turn off the power to the network adapter to save power.  The setting is found from Device Manager - Network Adapter - Power Management.

Also -

Windows 8.1 may have drivers failing to execute the command that wakes your WIFI receiver from selective suspension thereby causing limited connectivity issues.  Disabling it should therefore provide a more stable connection.  You can get to it by the following steps:

1. Go to "Control Panel"

2. Select "Hardware and Sound"

3. Select "Power Options"

On your selected power plans, select "Change Plan Settings"

Select "Advanced Power Settings"

Expand "USB settings"

Expand "USB selective suspend setting"

Disable selective suspend

Thunderbird email sort order Descending
