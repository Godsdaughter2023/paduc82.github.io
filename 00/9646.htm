I assume you mean the values in A2 and A3 are 0.15 and 0.04 formatted as Percentage with 1 decimal place.  If you formatted A2 and A3 first, you might mean that you typed 15 and 4, and Excel interpreted them as 15% and 4%.

And I assume that B1:B3 are formatted as Accounting with 0 decimal places before entering the value and formulas.

I encounter similar misbehavior, although it is inconsistent.  Sometimes, it behaves exactly as you describe.  Sometimes, the format change occurs immediately when I enter the formula.  Sometimes, it happens in B2 instead of B3.  Sometimes it happens the second time, not the first time using your procedure after changing the format to Account with 0 decimal places

The misbehavior also seems to depend on where the formulas are entered (in B2 and B3, immediately adjacent to A2 and A3, v. in C2 and C3).  And it seems to depend on the syntax of the formulas, for example =A3*(B2+B1) v. =$A$3*(B2+B1) v. =$A$3*B2+$A$3*B1.

I say "seems to" because the inconsistent nature of the misbehavior makes it difficult to predict when it should happen -- and when we succeeded to avoid it.

In any case, I suspect there little we can do about this misbehavior.

I believe this misbehavior is different from the problem I observed some years ago [1].  In my case, it was sufficient to click on Format Cells just to look at the formula, and the cell format might change automagically.

In your case, I believe it is due to misguided heuristics in Excel that try to do us a "favor" and choose the "best" format, based on characteristics of referenced cells and perhaps context (e.g. surrounding formulas).  I often see this when I use financial functions like PMT in some contexts.

IMHO, this is a design defect insofar as I do not believe Excel should change formats other than perhaps General.  But Microsoft might disagree.

You might try "reporting" the problem by submitting feedback at http://mymfe.microsoft.com/Office/feedback.aspx?formID=375.

I suspect that is a "black hole" and waste of effort.  But that might be the best any of plebes can do.

If you have a support contract with Microsoft, you could call tech support or submit an official defect report.  Also, an accommodating MVP might submit the defect report through their special channels to Microsoft development teams.

aristarco---01 say:

It is not an intermitent error and the SIM is not guilty of it. It is a problem of firmware, I guess. The problem presents when you receive the "Inbox Full" flashing envelope icon and proceed to empty it. After that, you won't be able to send an SMS. What you need is to do the equivalent of Ctrl-Alt-Del in PCs: take the battery out, wait some seconds, then put it back in and turn the phone on. After setting the time and date, send a test SMS to someone. It'll work just right again.

 

Try not to run out of inbox space, emptying it periodically. If you get the inbox full message again, you know what to do.
