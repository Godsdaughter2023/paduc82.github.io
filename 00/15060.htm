Hello there,

After looking at your Mini Tool Box log, it seems as if you have Vipre Antivirus installed, it's highly possible this is conflicting with Microsoft Security Essentials.

In most situations, having more than one of something can be useful and also ideal, such as two cars or two computers. However, not the case with antivirus programs.

It is generally not a good idea to install more than one anti-virus on your operating system. Many users in their opinion suspect this will provide better protection for them and make their computer more secure in general, but this is not the case. As well as that, it can also cause quite a lot of conflicts between the two competitors.

You may also notice a slower performance of your system. This is due to the fact that both antiviruses will be monitoring your system - when there's really only the need for one to be doing the job. In some antivirus programs, they will automatically disable themselves if another antivirus program is offering real-time protection to your device.

Many users install an additional antivirus to Windows 8 - this is due to the fact that they don't think it has an antivirus program, because in the previous versions of Windows (Windows 7, Windows Vista), Windows Defender has been an anti-spyware program, not a real-time antivirus program. But in Windows 8, Microsoft Security Essentials was renamed to Windows Defender.

Furthermore, you may wish to check out the articles below about having more than one antivirus installed on your computer.

Say we want to link to Sheet2 if A1=2 and to Sheet3 if A1=3.

Without the above condition, just to get to Sheet2, B9 we would use:

=HYPERLINK("#Sheet2!B9","sheet 2 destination")

to make this conditional, we put the conditions in the hyperlink function:

 

=HYPERLINK(IF(A1=2,"#Sheet2!B9","#Sheet3!B9"),IF(A1=2,"sheet 2 destination","sheet3 destination"))

ken sheridan say:
In the upgrade lesson from 2003 to 2010 I did learn about splitting the database at the tables but was unaware that it would prevent deletion. 

It won't.  The back end file on the server can still be deleted.  A copy of the front end on the user's local machine or in their 'personal' location on the system can also be deleted, but that would not be a problem as you, as the developer, would hold a copy of the file.

The real solution is a regular and efficient back-up routine, putting copies of the files in at least two separate secure locations, preferably off-site.  One back-up is not enough as back-ups can be corrupted.

scottgem say:

As noted, encryption prevents viewing data, has nothing to do with deleting files. And splitting doesn't prevent deletion, but it does protect from it happening.
