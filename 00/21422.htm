a. user say:

The trouble with Footnote Citations is that they have only ever been implemented on one version of Word, namely Mac Word 2008.

I have been chasing this up ever since you first posted this question. AFAICS because of a comment on one of Yves Dhondt's "FootNote" referencing styles that says "Word 2008 and later", people not unreasonably believe that Word currently has the facility to do citations in footnotes differently.

But the trouble is that the facility only actually exists in Mac Word 2008 and was omitted after that. It isn't in Windows Word 2010 or later and it isn't even in Mac Word 2011 (the successor to Mac Word 2008).

Word inserts Bibliography material by sending "commands" to the current stylesheet (an XSL transform) and owrking with whatever is sent back. Some commands are "b:Version", "b:Citation" and "b:Bibliography", Word provides a block of XML containing the information about the relevant work(s) to be cited, and some relevant locale information, but it does not provide any information (AFAIK) that would allow the transform to know where a citation was being inserted.

In Word 2008, when you insert a citation in a footnote, Word sends a different "command", "b:FootnoteCitation", so any XSL can respond with different formatting in that version.

Unfortunately, that idea seems to have been dropped immediately, even on the Mac side. In Word 2011, the .XSL transforms still have code to respond to "b:FootnoteCitation", but nothing is different. The obvious assumption is that Word only ever asks for "b:Citation". The styles provided with Windows Word 2010 and later, and Mac Word 2011 do not appear to contain any code to deal with b:FootnoteCitation, although I have only looked at a couple of the .xsl files so am not certain. However, I have tried adding the relevant code to a Word 2010 Style and it does nothing.

While we're here, even the Word 2008 feature did not do what Windows Word currently does. Windows Word always inserts a citation as a { CITATION } field within an SDT (a structured data tag - the thing that has a little title and menu), whether it's in the body text or a footnote. When Word 2008 inserts a citation in a footnote, it just inserts the text result of the citation, not even a { CITATION } field. Not very useful if you ever update the details. And I think that's the same on the Mac side even in 2016.

sofspra say:

@cbarnhorst asks a great question.  We just need more info to help a lot.

In my opinion, dual processor (the proper term is likely dual core) is a term used to describe a generation of processors.  It was single core, dual core, quad core and who knows what is the latest term.  I think it is now described more accurately as 5th gen, 6th gen and latest may be 8th gem.

I always thought of a dual core, when compared to a single core, as a processor that could do more than 1 task at a time and screw up twice as fast as a single core.

A PC is usually bound by the speed of the processor and the amount of RAM available to do a task.  A PC with limited RAM will lose speed just waiting for resources.  A slower processor will simply take long to do a task.

I have a herd of dual core HP notebooks.  They run at about 1.70 Ghz which at best is likely mid range.  Some have 4 GB of RAM and some have 16 GB of RAM and I can't tell the difference in speed.  Some have SDD drives and my workhouse still has a HDD and I can't detect a change in speed.  I have a brand new SDD on the shelf with no big desire to install just for a speed difference.  It would likely improve the speed somewhat.

It is my understanding that Windows only accesses less than 4GB of RAM so more RAM may not speed up Windows that much.  More RAM should allow other processes to run faster since it does provide more resources.

Just never saw a PC with separate processors.   Dual core, for sure, but this is just 1 processor with more capabilities than an older single core processor.

If I was buying, I would likely look at a 8th Gen Intel with 16 RAM with a SDD.   However, my current fleet does everything I ask of it, just a tad slower.   My Insider build is running at 17107 and I have the ISO for 17107.   I can just leave the PC on at bedtime and I wake up with the new build.  Matters not much if it takes an hour or most of the night.  Don't really know.

If you want more speed, replace what you have with PC with a faster processor, larger RAM and a faster disk drive.  A dual core has its limitations in speed.  You can upgrade 1 part only to be blocked by a built in bottle neck some place else.
