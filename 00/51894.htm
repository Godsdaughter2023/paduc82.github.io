spiritx say:

Hi,

Are you working in a home environment or a full featured repair shop? Consider if you
diagnose a driver issue down to the actual problem however do not have what is needed
to rewrite the driver. Same goes for hardware in that do you really need to know a specific
component has failed or that a voltage is incorrect, say in a network card? Or do you only
need enough information to lead to replacing the card?

Well much of hardware diagnosis is intuitive. If the hardware does not operate properly then
drivers and other software might be the cause. Once those have been ruled out as much as
possible then the hardware itself is suspect. In a shop hardware repair is often done by the
"Black Box" method which means to replace the suspect hardware, with a known properly
functioning piece when possible. One real issue is that when the hardware is replaced the
software, drivers, and sometimes even the operating system are replaced or updated which
might have been the issue all along.

Device Manager will provide a lot of information about hardware or drivers that are not
properly operating and includes some methods for possible software/driver corrections.
However it does fall short of being an all inclusive diagnosis program and certainly does
not offer methods even remotely close to self healing.

And there are some hardware diagnosis programs which usually display the type of information
available from Device Manager, System Info, and other sources in a much more readable format.
However even though some such programs claim the ability to perform repairs the reality is
that most do not even come close to their claims. Be leery of Registry Cleaners, System Optimizers,

and other general do-it-all repair programs. Those will usually cause issues while fixing nothing
and should be thought of as Snake-Oil. Today's hardware diagnosis programs can help with
some memory, hard drive, and other hardware issues however they fall short of actually helping
to find and repair most other hardware or software failures.

Unfortunately "Self Healing" computers are still in the distant future though steps, tiny steps,
are being made in that direction.

Bottom line is that if you find a program that actually helps you solve the issues you come across
then that is a plus. If not then it is a waste of time and money. If considering a program do not
believe the hype. Find those actually using the program and inquire about their findings and
results.

Hope this helps.

spiritx say:
Hi,

Beep codes during startup are from the BIOS and do usually indicate hardware
issues. To see what the Beep Codes mean check with the System Maker or Mother-
board Maker for custom systems or the actual BIOS maker.

The beeping sounds are error codes which describe an issue with your machine.
One constant beep can mean keyboard (loose cord or bad), loose card, power, a
short or other issue depending on System/BIOS maker. Other beep patterns have
other meanings depending on the maker of the BIOS.

Bootup and select SETUP/SETTINGS (BIOS/CMOS) - usually F2 as you start up -
look to see BIOS creator then refer here :
