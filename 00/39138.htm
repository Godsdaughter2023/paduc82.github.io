bobby mikkelson say:

Hello Christi,

 

Windows may incorrectly store the DC brightness level in the AC brightness setting. When this happens you may see the display is set to brightness level set for the battery after booting into Windows 7. To resolve the issue you need to adjust the AC brightness setting in power options plan. To do this follow the steps listed below:

 

1. Click Start, click Control Panel

2. Open Hardware and Sound

3. Select Power Options

4. Choose Edit Plan Settings

5. Under AC section Adjust Plan Brightness to the level you wish.

It's actually pretty common for banks and credit card companies to accept payments from outdated cards, as long as the subscription was established prior to the card expiring or becoming otherwise invalid.  This is called "grandfathering".  When Xbox detected that your credit card was going to expire soon, they sent you emails, asking you to update your details on the off chance that it does cause a service interruption.

-

If you did not get those emails, or chose not to update your credit card details, this does not indicate to Xbox in any way that you do not want to continue your membership, so they submitted the payment request to the bank using the same credit card information you provided to them.  Xbox was contractually obligated to submit this payment request to the bank, because you had indicated from the beginning that you wanted this renewing service until you notified them otherwise.  And your bank, understanding that the membership was a grandfathered subscription, honored the charge.

-

If Xbox had attempted to begin a new membership, or initiate a point of purchase sale using an outdated card, it would have been immediately declined, and you would have been notified of the failure to continue the purchase.  Some parents use this feature to their advantage, paying for a subscription on an expired credit card, secure in the knowledge that their child can't make any extra purchases without first updating the credit card details.
