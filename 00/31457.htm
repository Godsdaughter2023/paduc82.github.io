About MSDN or TechNet Product Keys
They are genuine Microsoft product keys, they are actually retail licenses, but it is intended for a particular product channel either the Microsoft Software Developer Network (MSDN) or TechNet for IT Professionals who pay a subscription fee. The main purpose is for evaluation purposes. The great thing about them, unlike trial Microsoft software, MSDN or TechNet keys don't expire. Because the agreement under which the subscription is provided is a single license, none of the software should be distributed outside of it. Even though its $50,000 worth of licenses, it is for one person only to use and no one else. Unfortunately, regardless of the licensing terms, persons still abuse the program, either giveaway product keys or resell it on auction sites. Microsoft licenses it in good faith that customers won't do so, but I guess human nature wins out.

In your case, what probably happened is, you bought an MSDN licensed key, which carries up to 10 activations unlike full packaged retail licenses which only carry 1 activation. The person who sold it to you probably sold it to 10 other persons. Somewhere along the way, one of those persons might have installed it on a second system, activated it, because it went past 10 activation threshold, Microsoft detected it that it was being abused and blocked the key from further use.

arminatorx say:

Unfortunately, there is nothing you can do besides creating a new account for your son. 

Even if you add a new sign in alias email for your son, the account will stay an adult account that can not be restricted anymore by a parent account. 

Also, if the current Microsoft Account email you have on your son's account is removed as a login alias, your Microsoft Account will be deleted, causing you to lose access to your OneDrive, and if it's a Hotmail/Outlook address also access to your E-Mails. 

So I'm afraid that creating a new account for your son correctly will enable you to control your son's child account. 

However, this also means, that your son will lose all game progress, as save games and unlocked game items are bound to your account only.

Since your son is playing on an adult account, that account can't be controlled and can't be monitored. 

While you could set up certain online and privacy restrictions, your kid can simply set them back, as the system considers the person using the account to be an adult. So every suggestion about setting up restrictions and limits can also be undone by your kid himself without your control. 

You could "manually" control his playtime, by creating a new account for yourself, and adding the account your kid uses as a "friend" and turning on notifications whenever that account gets online. 

Then install the Xbox App on your smartphone, and you should get notifications when the kid goes online. 

But that has several drawbacks: You do not get a notification when the kid stops playing, and the kid could simply block your account or remove it from the friend list, and you would then not get any notifications at all. Or they could decide to play "offline", and you wouldn't be any wiser about his playtime as well. 

So if you want a reliable automatic system that restricts your kid's play time and what they are allowed to do online, there is no way around giving them a proper, new child account. 

You might also have a look at https://www.thekitchensafe.com/ which sells smalls containers with a time lock on them. 

You can put the Xbox controller into such a container and set a timer. The box unlocks when the timer is up. 

However, as soon as the kid has access to the controller, they can again play an unlimited amount of time, as there is no control over when the kid has to return the controller to the container.
