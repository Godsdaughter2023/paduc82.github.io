a. user say:

Resolution: there's a long standing "bug" having to do with a limit to number of files stored in your OneDrive account. OneNote scans your first 5000 files and then stops; so, if your OneNOte notebooks aren't discovered within those first 5000, you won't see them in the drop-down when setting email preferences or when the email process is trying to find your notebooks either.

if you reduce to below 5000, all is great.

MS is aware and it's on their backlog but no date has been provided.

    hansv mvp say:

    Let's say the dates start in A2. Select the dates; I will assume that A2 is the active cell within the selection.

    On the Home tab of the ribbon, click Conditional Formatting > New Rule...

    Select 'Use a formula to determine which cells to format'.

    Enter the formula


    =A2<=TODAY()


    Click Format...

    Activate the Fill tab.

    Select green.

    Click OK twice.

    Repeat the above steps, but with

    =A2<=TODAY()-350

    as formula and yellow as fill color.

    Also repeat them with

    =A2<=TODAY()-365

    as formula and red as fill color.

    greginmich say:

    Is it safe to click anywhere on these messages (the ones that pop up and say a virus has been detected and you should call a number for removal) or not? The reason I ask is I'm seeing people here, including Insiders, advising others to click them.

    These Tech Support Scam sites lock up your browser by looping a modal alert dialog – and in any of the several browsers that support Dialog Loop Protection (including Microsoft Edge), you actually have to click on the OK button that’s located in the fake alert dialog in order for the browser to detect the dialog loop and then respond by adding the Dialog Loop Protection checkbox – which allows you to escape from the lockup and close the page. Since the content of the alert dialog is actually under the control of both the malicious website and the browser's protection mechanism, the user gets put in the unacceptable position of having to untangle the mess and decide what's legitimate and what's not.

    The latest variants of the Tech Support Scam take full advantage of this confusion by spoofing both the address bar URL and the Dialog Loop Protection checkbox in the fake alert dialog:

    https://blog.malwarebytes.com/cybercrime/social-engineering-cybercrime/2016/08/tech-support-scams-and-google-chrome-tricks/

    https://www.bleepingcomputer.com/news/security/microsoft-edge-smartscreen-security-feature-can-be-abused-for-tech-support-scams/  

    There’s a great real-world example here:  

    https://answers.microsoft.com/en-us/protect/forum/protect_other-protect_scanning/web-popup-from-httpssupportmicrosoftcom-claims-im/7d8c8938-d761-4a99-8195-04a10201475b

    There’s hardly any question that this is the most prolific and long-lasting scam ever perpetrated on the internet, so it’s pretty discouraging to see such a belated and confusing response on the part of browser developers. If you’re as unhappy as I am about this whole mess, then you can at least help raise the level of awareness by reporting these scam sites:
