In Access parlance a database is a container file containing several objects; tables, forms, reports, queries, etc. A database may encompass one or more containers (I.e. front end and back end). adam vero say:

In Excel 2007 go to the Insert ribbon and choose to Insert a header. You might be able to reproduce something like you need here (dependent on the sort of formatting you need), and this would not have gridline printed through it.

On the other hand, I would suggest that printing gridlines is just adding more ink to boggle the eyes instead of making things more readable and should be avoided.

Use borders where they are appropriate, for example to separate titles or totals from data, or to group data together (maybe every third column to group quarters visually). Use the thinest, palest line that still does the job. (50% grey or lighter is a good start). Separation by using slightly more white space is also helfpul to read columns of data (by making the columns a tiny bit wider), and again to separate titles or totals (increase the row height)

I often use conditonal formatting to apply these kind of borders, especially if I want to have a line under (say) every fifth row to aid the eye following across the ppage (5 is usually about optimum, most people can track the row above or below the line, the middle of the group or the other two positions very easily without needing a line under every row). It also means I only get lines where there is data, not on blank areas.

If you don't want to go that far, you could apply borders where you need them as far down as you think the data will ever need to go, and then simply use filters to make it easy to hide the rows with no data so you don't print ugly lines all the way down the page.

So, if you can talk yourself out of using gridlines, you can put pretty much anything you like for your title at the top of the page and format it any way you choose.

Adam Vero MCT, MMI, MBMSS:CRM 4, MCSA:Messaging Meteor IT - Training and Consulting Services

shane devenshire say:

Suppose you layout your data as follows:

Date Dept1 Dept2 Depty Deptx
1/1/2010 55 45    
1/2/2010 96 6    
1/3/2010 65 38    
1/4/2010 1 29    
1/5/2010 27 75

1/7/2010 2 32    
1/8/2010 12 5    
1/9/2010 7 73    
1/10/2010 79 67    
1/11/2010 12 44    

This is in the range A1:E12.  There are no values under Depty or Deptx

1. Create your cluster column chart.

2. Right-click the Dept1 series and choose Format Data Series, and on the first tab choose Secondary Axis

3. Choose Chart Tools, Format, and open the Chart Element dropdown (the very first tool on the top left of the ribbon) and choose Series Depty.

4. Click Format Selection and choose Secondary Axis.

5. In the chart click Series 2 the Dept2  series and note the series formula - the last argument says 2 edit this to read 3 and press Enter.

=SERIES(Sheet2!$C$1,Sheet2!$A$2:$A$12,Sheet2!$C$2:$C$12,3)

6. On the chart select the Legend.  Then select the Legend Entry for Depty and press Del.  Repeat for Deptx.
