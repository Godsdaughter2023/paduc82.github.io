I believe the key difference between this statement "From an unidentified Microsoft spokesperson." and the other article is that this person expected to be quoted, so the quotes appear complete and in sequence rather than chopped up and mis-used.

The real point in all of this isn't the wording semantics, it's the high level of risk to security that the situation creates.  The reality is that no antimalware no matter how effective can protect a system that has significant unpatched vulnerabilities.  So only a fool would continue to use such a system for anything important or to contain and protect important personal or business data.

 

Using such a system as a toy or to simply browse the web for fun may be less of a risk, but if other systems exist on the same network which contain important data, an unprotected system can be easily turned into a launching platform from which to attack these other systems within the network behind your router/firewall or even other Internet systems.  In other words, turning the PC into a bot.

 

The biggest issue I have with those who state that keeping an unsupported system of any sort operating is a financial decision, is simply that it's a completely foolish one.  If anything of a financial nature is being performed on such a machine, the potential risk of compromise of that financial information becomes the true cost if such an incident occurs.  So a simple computation of what's at risk of compromise (bank accounts, personal data and possible identity theft) vs. the cost of a new device to perform whatever activities are really needed is the cost comparison that should be made.

 

The thing that most consumers need to do is to determine if what they currently use their PC for really requires a PC today, since for many something like a tablet or even smartphone may be sufficient.  This is the evaluation and research anyone with a Windows XP system should be doing right now to be prepared to take advantage of the discounted sales that typically occur both before and after the holidays to acquire something current that will fulfill their true needs.

 

Worrying about whether a particular security application will be "supported" with Windows XP after April 8th, 2014 is pointless effort, since it simply doesn't matter.  The only effort that makes any sense is figuring out how to get rid of that outdated piece of junk before April 8th.

There are several ways on how you can block spam emails in your mailbox. Here are some options that you can set up in your account:

  • Junk mail filter - Selecting Exclusive option. This feature will let you receive emails from your contact list and safe senders list.
  • Email rules - You can set up email rules to filter incoming emails to automatically delete them and not getting them in your Inbox.
  • Blocked senders list - You can add email address/es or domain/s in this list to stop receiving emails from them. You also need to delete them from your contact list and safe senders list as well.
  • Sweep features - Sweeping emails for deletion.

To set up a junk mail filter to Exclusive, please follow these steps:

  1. Sign in to your Microsoft account.
  2. Click the gear icon located on the upper right panel of your mailbox, just beside your username.
  3. Select More mail settings.
  4. Under Preventing junk email, click on Filters and reporting option.
  5. Under Choose a junk email filter, select Exclusive.
  6. Click the Save button.

If you want to create an email rule to delete emails coming from an email address, kindly refer to these steps:

  1. Sign in to your Microsoft account.
  2. Click the gear icon located on the upper right panel of your mailbox, just beside your username.
  3. Select More mail settings.
  4. Under Customizing Outlook, click the Rules for sorting new messages option.
  5. Click the New button.
  6. Under Step 1: Which messages do you want this rule to apply to?, on the first box with drop down list, select the Sender's address and in the second box with drop down list, select yes.
  7. On the third box, type sender's email address. You may select any option that you want to use at first, second and third box depending on your preference.
  8. Under Step 2: What action do you want to apply?, select Delete these messages.
  9. Click the Save button.

You can also add the sender's email address to your Blocked sender list to stop receiving emails from that contact. Here's how: 

  1. Sign in to your Microsoft account.
  2. Click the gear icon located on the upper right panel of your mailbox, just beside your username.
  3. Select More mail settings.
  4. Under “Preventing junk email”, click the Safe and blocked senders option.
  5. Click Blocked senders.
  6. In the Blocked email address or domain box, type the email address of the sender or the domain that you want to block, and then click Add to list.

Once you add an email address in your blocked senders list, you may also need to delete the account details from your contact list.
