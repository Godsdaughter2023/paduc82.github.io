frederik long say: Before you go ahead with the RAID array, you need to consider the benefit (if any) of such a configuration. Here are a few key points:
  • Backup. Many people think that a RAID array protects them against loss of data. This is incorrect. RAID arrays are almost useless as a backup solution.
  • File system corruption, virus infections. Again RAID arrays afford no protection against these events. Whatever happens to one disk in the array will happen to the other disks too.
  • Easy recovery from a disk crash. Theoretically a RAID array allows a system to continue after a disk has crashed (which is very rare these days). In practice few owners of RAID arrays take the time to test if their system will boot with just one or the other disk connected, nor do they take the time to find out in advance how to rebuild a RAID array in case of a disk crash.
For most private installations a solid backup solution plus a disk imaging process is cheaper, more effective and technically much less demanding than a RAID solution.

frederik long say:

I run a monthly System Image backup. 

I am not sure what you mean by "a solid backup solution".

A solid backup solution consists of two elements:
  • It must be performed whenever the loss of your PC would result in a substantial data loss. In some cases the backup must be performed daily. In other cases once a month is sufficient.
  • The backup medium must be external and must be kept well away from your PC for most of the time. Storing your monthly image backup on the internal disk is far from ideal because there are several scenarios that would affect all internal disks. The human element is the greatest risk, e.g. cleaning out the wrong drive to create more space . . .
