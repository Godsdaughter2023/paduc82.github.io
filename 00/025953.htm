Some of those items are always available to use, but others only exist because of the type of shapes that you have on your page....

Autodiscovery Shape - forget this because Autodiscovery was killed over 15 years ago

Displayed Text - the text that you see on the shape

Height - the height of a shape

Layer Name - the name of a layer that the shape is on

Master Name - the name of the master in the stencil that the shape is an instance of

Shape ID - the unique ID of a shape on a page

Shape Name - the unique name of a shape on a page

Width - The width of a shape

X Location - the X co-ordinate of a shape on a page measured from the left edge of the page

Y Location- the Y co-ordinate of a shape on a page measured from the bottom edge of the page

The rest of these items are because you have specific types of shapes on the page (these look like Process shapes):

Antiscale - the value in User-defined cell named AntiScale that sometimes exists in shapes

Cost - A Shape Data row

Default Height - A User-defined Cell

Default Width - A User-defined Cell

Duration- A Shape Data row

Function- A Shape Data row

Process Number- A Shape Data row

Three experiments:

Hi Toni,

 

This forum is only for users of Microsoft Security Essentials.  If you are using Norton, you need to contact them for assistance with malware starting here: http://us.norton.com/support/.

 

To be honest, it sounds more to me like an issue I'd direct to your e-mail provider's support group.  They can assist you with finding out what is really happening with your account and help you take measures to stop it or deal with it properly.  No matter what Norton says, you need to do this ASAP anyway as your account is compromised.  I find it much more likely that the problem is with the provider and not with Outlook itself (or even your computer).  While it can and does happen sometimes, over 90-95% of this problems are resolved with the e-mail provider (or providers if your mobile has a different one and you need to then contact them just as much as your regular computer email provider).

 

In the meantime, change your passwords immediately (and I'd also change any Password Reset security information as well to the extent it is possible).  Check your Outlook and Mobile sent items folders and see if these emails that are being sent are there.  If so, your account is sending them, but we still don't know from where. 

 

Then go online to your accounts (instead of through Outlook) and see if the emails are showing in your sent items folder online.  If so, the urgency of changing passwords and talking to their support people just jumped several magnitudes.  If not, then chances are the damage is already done and the information is "out there" on some server in the Ukraine being used by people with masked computers and TCP/IP addresses rerouted by proxy servers so many times they're impossible to find or trace and they're just using your information to make it look like they're coming from you but they really aren't (and an expert can probably tell the difference by looking at the email source information).

 

If the source of the information cannot be identified or is identified as your computer, then after dealing with the email providers to try to minimize the damage if that's still possible or stop others from using your account if it is being used, then you need to contact Norton to get help cleaning your computer.  Again, I don't think it's your computer but if I'm wrong or there's any chance it could be true, then clean it because if they got access to that through your computer, there's no telling what else they accessed or still are accessing until the system is free from malware.  Then depending on what malware it was and what Norton advises, you need to decide how much other change you need to do (other passwords, logins, account information, banking information, PINS, credit card numbers and account info and who knows what - and some malware is so hard to remove you may need to consider a clean install).  But this is all hypothetical and again, it's unlikely - but you need to check and understand and make decisions based on what you discover.  I don't mean to panic you because this really isn't very likely - but I'm trying to impress upon you the importance of doing it so you'll know the situation for certain.

 

I hope this helps.

 

Good luck!

 

Kosh

Exchange Online - Outlook 2013 - Cached mode

Hi all,

i'm checking limits and configurations in Outlook 2013 with Exchange Online.

The reccomended configuration is to set Outlook 2013 in cached mode. This will create a .ost cache file on Windows client.

The default limit of this .ost file is 50 Gb: this is not a problem using just your personal mailbox. Also you can configure Outlook to cached a specific period, like 12 months, to reduce the size of .ost file.

But what happen when a user have online archive and/or one or more shared mailbox access?

Online archive and shared mailboxes will be full cached locally in the same .ost file. If you configure Outlook 2013 to download just a limited period, this will be valid for the personal mailbox only, shared mailboxes will be fully downloaded.

So it's easy to reach the .ost file size.

Someone know if Outlook 2016 will have improvments regarding this topic?

alexsun_2012 say:

Hi Loreman,

Yes, additional mailboxes including the shared mailbox and archive mailbox are added to the .OST file. By default, the overall size of .OST files has a preconfigured limit of 50 GB. The .OST file size limit of 50 GB can be increased or decreased by making changes to the Windows registry. Here is the official article for your reference: https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/kb/832925.

However, it is not suggested to use cached mode for set up when mailboxes greater than 25 GB. Large .OST files can often cause performance issues like Outlook doesn’t respond correctly when you read or move messages. In this situation, we suggest you backup your emails regularly by exporting them to local .PST files. You can save the .PST files on anywhere and import the messages from the files to Outlook at any time.

https://support.office.com/en-ca/article/Export-or-back-up-messages-calendar-tasks-and-contacts-d791fe88-5c67-44c5-a3f6-a484508082a1

https://support.office.com/en-CA/article/Import-Outlook-items-from-an-Outlook-Data-File-pst-431a8e9a-f99f-4d5f-ae48-ded54b3440ac

As for Outlook 2016, based on my research consult, there is no official article about the .OST file size limit. Considering our forum mainly focuses on Office 365 Online services, if you have further concern about the local Outlook .OST file, we suggest you contact our specialized Outlook Client Support Forum. Thanks for your understanding.
