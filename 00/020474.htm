rohn007 say:

Make it Fit - Page Layout tuning in MS Word

Technical Level : Intermediate

Summary

MS Word is not intended for very fussy, controlled page layout.  But every now and then you may want to tweak a document to fit on a specific number of pages. Usually this happens when you have a 1 page document that spills a couple of lines over on to a second page. 

The Print dialog has a "make it fit" option that will automatically tweak the document for you. But if you are a control freak, here is a collection of actions you can select to apply to the document yourself.


Details

Intro

Every so often you'll find that a document takes up too many pages. For a document you plan to photocopy 1,000 times, for example, the difference between one page and two is 1,000 extra copies. To that, add hours of collating and stapling (plus a tree or two), and what should have been a simple job becomes a nightmare.

Even if your needs aren't as extreme as this, there will be occasions when you'll want to make a document or article a little shorter to fill the available space. This month we look at a variety of tips and tricks for reducing the space your text takes up on the page. Most of these options will also work in reverse if you ever need to expand a piece of text to fill up a space. 

Many of these techniques require you to select the text you want to format before you select the options themselves. The paragraph options will apply to the paragraph the cursor is currently in and, to format more than one paragraph at a time, you'll need to select each of them before you begin.

Most of these options can also be incorporated into document Styles. You'll find that you'll have a more consistent “look and feel” and also get more control over your document if you apply all/most of your formatting using paragraph and character styles rather than applying it directly. That way the changes can be made applied consistently thru the whole document almost instantly.

NOTE: Be discrete and consistent when you apply the following techniques. Apply the techniques to large areas of the document.  For example apply the changes to a whole page or chapter rather than only the last paragraph. This approach has 2 advantages: first, the changes do not have to be as drastic to make up the required space and second because the changes are not as extreme they are less visually detectable, so you can “sneak” the change in without providing a visual distraction to your reader.

1.           Edit Tightly

Before you look at typographical ‘tricks’ and techniques, always look at the document itself and see if you can edit out extra words and spaces.

2.           Margins

A simple way to make extra space is to decrease the size of the margins.  The default margins Word uses are 1 inch top and bottom and 1.25 inch left and right.  That is much more than you need.  In Word 97 use File / Page Setup / Margins (tab). Reduce the size of the margins and select OK.

Note:      if you try to reduce margin too far, you will be notified that “one or more margins are outside of the printable area …”, simply click on the “Fix” button and the minimum margin will be set for that printer.

3.           Fine Tune Styles

If you use styles, such as Word's default styles, you can edit them to decrease the spacing above and below the headings. For example, the default spacing for the Heading 1 style is 12 points before the paragraph and three points after. These can easily be altered to nine points before and zero points after. To alter a style, select Format / Style and from the Styles list select the style to modify (in this case Heading 1). Select the Modify button and then select Format, Paragraph and alter the Before setting to nine points and the After setting to zero points and select OK, OK and Close.

4.           Line Height

Reduce the line height--the amount of vertical space between lines of text. You can do this within a paragraph by selecting Format / Paragraph / Indents and Spacing (tab) and set the Line Spacing to Multiple and type ".95" (no quotes) in the At text box. This reduces the line height to 95 percent of the default spacing.

5.           Font Size

Decreasing font size can dramatically increase the amount of text that fits on a page. Although Word 97 appears to only offer certain font sizes, you can type sizes that aren't in the list and you can use half measures. For example, 11.5 point type is visually indistinguishable from 12 point type but takes slightly less room.

12 point type but takes slightly less room.

Note:      some True Type fonts will allow you select font sizes other than 0.5 pt increments; you’ll have to test the font to see if this is an option

Just another pretty face? Nothing wrong with that. If you’re editing in Microsoft Word, why not use a typeface that you’re comfortable reading and that makes editing easy? (Unless, of course, you’re editing documents that have already been carefully formatted for typesetting.) You can always apply the final typeface and formatting after your editing is finished (probably just by attaching a different template to the document).

Some typefaces lend themselves better to editing than others. Here are some things to look for in a typeface to use while you're editing:

  1. Legibility. Are the characters clear and easy to read?
  2. Universality. Is the typeface readily available at no cost or low cost, and on other computers as well as your own?
  3. Are special characters easy to distinguish? These include the hyphen, the en dash, the em dash, and opening and closing quotation marks.

After considerable testing and exploration, I've found three typefaces that seem to me to work especially well for editing:

  1. Times Roman (yep, that old chestnut), some variation of which you almost certainly have on your computer already.
  2. Verdana.
  3. Georgia.

Times Roman is actually a bit small and condensed (designed to fit lots of type into a newspaper column), but you probably won’t find a face with more easily distinguishable quotation marks and dashes. The em dash is nice and long, the hyphen is tiny, and the en dash falls comfortably in between. You just have to make sure that you get the point size big enough so you can read it comfortably.

Verdana is a Microsoft typeface that was designed for viewing on-screen, so it looks especially clean and legible on a computer monitor. Its quotation marks aren’t as clearly distinguishable as those in Times Roman, but they’re not bad, either. Verdana’s main drawback is that it’s a sans serif font, so the eye doesn’t glide from letter to letter. If you’re using Verdana for editing, however, that’s not necessarily a bad thing, as you may have a tendency to slow down a little as you read.

Georgia is another Microsoft typeface that was designed for viewing on-screen, so, like Verdana, it looks great on a computer monitor.  Unlike Verdana, it has serifs, making it a little smoother to read.

7.           Font Character Spacing

You can also reduce the character spacing (the space between letters, numbers and symbols) to fit more words on a page. Alter the spacing between characters by selecting the text to alter and choose Format / Font / Character Spacing (tab). From the Spacing list box select Condensed and in the By text box scroll to find the best setting. You can reduce spacing in 0.1 point increments. You'll find most 12 point fonts can be reduced by 0.2 points and still remain quite legible.

8.           Font Justification

If you are using Word 00 or higher, using full justification and turn on Tools / Options / Compatibility (tab) / “Do full justification like WordPerfect 6.x for Windows”.  MS Word’s default full justification method is to simply insert full spaces between words.  True ‘typographic’ full justification (as done by WordPerfect) slightly alters spacing between letters and word (by less than full space character).  This works better on long paragraphs than short ones.

9.           Font Kerning

Kerning is when spacing between selected pairs of letters is reduced, ie between “L” and “T”.  Kerning is normally used only for larger font sizes in Titles and heading, say 16pt and up, but it may be enough to squeeze that last letter onto a line to get rid of an extra line.  Format / Font / Character Spacing (tab) click on “Kerning for Fonts” and set font size to 10 or 12 pt (what ever size you use in normal style).

10.       Inter Paragraph Spacing

Most users add a blank line between paragraphs by pressing the Enter key twice at the end of a paragraph. The more professional approach is to use a half space (generally around six points) instead of a full space (12 points). To add a half space between paragraphs select Format / Paragraph / Indents and Spacing (tab) and in the Spacing After list box select six points and click OK. After doing this you'll only need to press the Enter key once between paragraphs.

11.       Cheat on Double Spacing

To give the impression of double spacing without wasting space, set line spacing to 1.5 or 1.75 spacing. To adjust the line spacing use Format, Paragraph, and in the Line Spacing list box select 1.5 to get one and a half lines of space between each line of text. Alternately, use 1.25 or 1.75 spacing by selecting Multiple and in the At text box type 1.25 or 1.75.

Double spacing has its roots in typewriter days when you could only single or double space a document. Today's word processors offer more line spacing options so, if a document doesn't really need to be double spaced, then use something smaller instead.

12.       Use Tabs Instead of Blank Lines Between Paragraphs

Avoid adding extra space between paragraphs entirely by indenting the first line of each paragraph. To do this simply press the Tab key before you type a paragraph and press the Enter key only once at the end of each paragraph. You can set Word 97 to automatically indent the first line by selecting Format, Paragraph and select the Indents and Spacing tab. From the Special list box select First line and in the By text box type one centimetre and click OK.

13.       Hyphenation

Using hyphenation will save space and even out ragged right margins. Turn on automatic hyphenation by selecting Tools / Language / Hyphenation and select the Automatically Hyphenate document checkbox. You can alter the hyphenation zone to give more hyphens (decrease the zone width) or less (increase the zone width) and you can limit the number of consecutive hyphens by specifying a value in the Limit Consecutive Hyphens To text box. Click OK to finish.

14.       Use Columns

Text that comprises short lines, such as step-by-step instructions, will take up less space and look better if set in two newspaper-style columns. To create columns, highlight the text to place in columns, select Format, Columns and select Two Columns and click OK.

15.       Narrow Column Gutters

If you're using columns you can narrow the width of the gutter (the space between columns), which increases the width of the columns and the amount of text that fits in them. To do this, place the cursor in the column and, with the ruler visible (View, Ruler), select the column marker on the ruler line and drag it to make the columns wider and the gutter narrower.

16.       Use Tables instead of Columns

If you are not comfortable with columns, you can use tables to get a similar effect with more direct control.  Table / Insert / Table, then specify number of rows and columns. 

17.       Reduce the Table Column Margins

Reduce left and right margin within the cells (default 0.08 inches may be too rather wide).  Select all the cells in the table, Table / Properties / Table (tab) / Options button, then reduce the left and right margin values.

18.       Table Paragraph Line Spacing

When MS introduced the Ribbon UI the also changed default fonts and spacings. These changes make tables very “airy”. Select the table, launch the Paragraph dialog (Home tab > Paragraph group > Dialog Launcher button). In the “Indents and Spacing” tab, Spacing section, set the Space Before and After to “0” and line spacing to “Single”.

19.       Eliminate Grid Lines in Table

To get rid of the lines in and around the table when printing use Table / Properties / Table (tab) / Borders and Shading button and then select the “NONE” option.

20.       Reduce lines in a Table row

Study the table to see if there is a row that uses more than one line.  You may be able to reduce the number of lines used by adjusting the column widths, or by applying one of the other various font and paragraph techniques listed above.

21.       Fine Tune Graphics

If you're using graphics, you can crop or resize them to reduce the space they occupy. To resize a graphic, select it and drag one of the sizing handles on the corners of the graphic to make it smaller. To crop a graphic, select it and, using the crop tool on the Picture toolbar (display it using View, Toolbars, Picture), drag a sizing handle to cut part of the graphic off.

In Word 97 you can flow text closely around a graphic by selecting the graphic, right click and select Format Picture from the menu. Select the Wrapping tab and select Wrapping Style, then in the Tight and Wrap To box, select Both sides and Ok.

22.       Headers and Footers

You can make significant savings over a large document by reducing the space a header or footer takes up. The distance between the edge of the paper and the top of a header and the edge of the page and the bottom of the footer is controlled using File, Page Setup, and the Margins tab. The From Edge Header and Footer settings control this distance. Decreasing them moves the header or footer closer to the page edge and increases the space on the page. However, be careful not to decrease this amount too much or your header or footer will no longer print.

You can also reduce the font size of the header and footer text to reduce the amount of space they take up on each page. Remember that text as small as five to seven points is still legible and ideal for file references and in-house information. Keep text that your reader will use slightly larger than this.

23.       Better Bullet Options

Bulleted points break up a document making it more readable. If you use bullets, consider reducing the default spacing between the bullet and the text to save room. To do this, select Format / Bullets and Numbering, select your bullet style and select Customize. In the Text Position, Indent At text box, reduce the distance the text is indented from the left margin or current column and select OK. You may need to add a tab to your document in this position by selecting Format, Tabs and in the Tab Stop Position text box type the new tab position, select Set and click OK.

24.       Alter the Tab Stops

The default Word 97 tab stops are set at 1.27 cm (.5 in.). If you use a lot of tabs consider reducing them. To do this select Format / Tabs and in the Default Tab Stops text box select 1 cm or a reduced amount and select OK.

25.       Shrink to Fit

If you're using Word 97 or later, you may use the File / Print Preview and select the Shrink to Fit button on the Print Preview toolbar, which automatically reduces the size of all fonts used in a document.

This solution is quick and easy, it's also fairly unsophisticated and won't work in all instances. It is probably a good idea to try this on a copy of the doc file so that you don’t have to worry about backing the change out.  I prefer to use it as a last resort.  For example, it won't allow you to size down a short article for a newsletter or to stretch a document to better fit a page.

26.       Blank Last Paragraph

Sometimes the only thing you see is a blank page. There appears to be nothing on it. If you turn on the Show/Hide feature you will probably see one or more a blank paragraph marks and or spaces.

The simplest fix is can the previous paragraph mark. Sometimes that is not possible, for example if the last thing in the document is a table. In that case you can select the paragraph mark and set it to 1pt size and make sure there is no space before or after it and it is set to single spacing.

26.1      Blank Para after a Column

A variation of the last page blank is extra space after columns.  You need to use section breaks to setup columns.  Section breaks are associated with paragraph marks.  If you have “Space After” turned on, that may be enough to create a new page. Try turning Space After off on that last paragraph mark.

27.       Paper Size

In some cases you may be able to change the paper size, depending on what paper is available and more importantly, what paper sizes your printer supports.  In North America the common paper sizes are:

  • Letter - 8.5” x 11”
  • Legal – 8.5” x 14”
  • 11 x 17

As well there is “A4”- 8.27” x 11.69” in Europe.

So if all of the above techniques didn’t work and you still have to fit it onto 1 side or 1 page 2 sided, consider changing from Letter to Legal.  Many people will accept a page of legal paper as legitimate substitute for a letter page, but moving to 11x17 is usually too much of a stretch.

28.       Centering Short doc Vertically: File / Page Setup / Layout (tab)

If the doc is less than a full page you may want to center it vertically on the page. By default documents are usually “top” justified on the page, ie text starts at the top of the page below the header area. In the “Vertical Alignment” drop down list, select “Centered”.

Hi Elliot,

Based on your requirement, I suggest you using team site instead of OneDrive for Business.

You can use team site to store documents in your organization. In a team site, your members can create, edit and sync documents with at any times via Office Online or Office client with right permission. Once a document is created or modified, other users will see the change instantly. A team site library can also be synced to local via using OneDrive for Business on different computers. The storage base per tenant is: 10 GB + 500 MB per subscribed user. You can also purchase additional storage for your sites.

Here are some articles for your reference:

Start using your team site

Work with sites, Organize documents and sites in Office 365

Document collaboration and co-authoring

For your queries about OneDrive for Business, please check the information below:

1. Does One Drive do auto synching on the connected devices?

Yes, the OneDrive for Business syncing process is instantaneous. When we make a change to a local file offline, it will be saved. Once we are online, the change will be synced to cloud automatically. If we make a change to a cloud file, it will be synced to local automatically too.

2. Are these all combinable or is it necessary to use one of the 1TB allowances as the central depository of files?

OneDrive for Business provides each user 1 TB storage for their personal site (the OneDrive for Business storage), it is independent. But it isn’t included your tenant overall storage.

3. Is the share setting on a OneDrive account opt out or do you set the share for every directory or file (latter preferred)?

In OneDrive for Business, you can set individual permission for every directory or file. For more information, you can refer to Edit permissions for a list, library, or individual item.

4. I want to be sure that what I load up on my computer to the OneDrive (as shared) is then synched to my employees devices and vice versa - with a minimum of manual effort. As if we are all working off the same hard drive.

Yes, if your employees have right permission to access your OneDrive for Business library, they can use OneDrive for Business application sync it to their computers. And vice versa.

Enter those values into two cells - let's just say E2 and E3


The CF all the cells using the formula option with three levels, with the formulas

=B2<$E$2     (red fill)

=AND(B2>=$E$2,B2<=$E$3)   (yellow fill)
