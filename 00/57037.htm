bossdweeb say:

"my fan (GPU I think) spins to 100%"


If the fan is running at 100% all the time the gpu, and cpu may be overheating.

Run a temperature monitor, if possible, to verify if this is so.

It may be the gpu has been running too hot for sometime now, particularly if the fan has been

running maxed out.

Laptops ( 'gaming' laptops too), even with a cooling pad, don't cool well when running under

full load.

While the TDR error always points toward the gpu drivers, it can come from many things,

including software.

- Disabling TRD errors altogether is not advisable as TDR is meant to protect the system from damage (as well as reflect damage that has already happened).

- The better option, if you want to avoid 'random' TDR errors happening is to use the time change option.

I find  TdrDelay of 5-8 seconds is plenty long for preventing seemingly random TDR errors. However, if the gpu is borked even the default 2 sec will cause TDR or BSOD.


Some other examples of causes of TDR -

Hardware:
- RAM. Run  memtest to verify the RAM is not producing errors
- HDD. Run  chkdsk to test the HDD for errors


Software:

Did you download the drivers from Nvidia or MSI ? With laptops it is best to download the

drivers from the mfg of the laptop, not Nvidia.

- When TDR occurs when using the internet it is sometimes due to browser add-ons, or the
version of the browser you are using.
- Anti-Virus software, particularly AV suites that run a number of programs in the background, or
running more than one AV program in resident (always on), are another possibility.
- Malware, Spyware or Virus are possible. Run a full boot-time scan with your AV.

A virus called iehighutil.dll  that come as an added bonus from some torrent downloads is one example.

- System 'optimising' software is another possibility.
- Games often cause TDR.  In some cases this is due to using too high graphics settings, which may

seem OK, but which can overwhelm the system at various times and cause a processing lag which

leads to TDR.
- Did you install anything around the time the crashes started ?
- Conflicts with other drivers can also cause TDR. One such driver comes with Daemon Tools.

Sometimes there can be conflicts with integrated audio eg. - Realtek, and the Nvidia HD Audio

drivers. Install only the main driver and physics if you don't use HDMI for audio or Nvidia 3D.

- If you have and mini dump files, up load them to your OneDrive for others here to analyse.

"I ran a Furmark test to see if heat was the problem, the answer is no. After around 3 to 4 minute, I hit a max temp of 83 degrees celcius. After 10 to 11 minute I get blue screen. "

I believe 83C is fairly common for a laptop under load. With PC mid 70's is more common because

of better cooling. Long sessions over 80C will shorten the life of a gpu though.

With a  laptop the cpu may also be suffering from high temps if the gpu is.

More so if they share the same cooler, as is common with laptops.

- You said 83C max. So, the temp was still at 83C a minute (seconds would be a better judge)

before the BSOD ?

If so, the temps should not be the source of the BSOD or TDR.

-If not, the BSOD may be from a 'thermal shut-down', which is suppose to protect the gpu (or cpu) from damage (as does a  TDR when hardware is faulting) . Not much benefit if the gpu  has been running at high temps for a long period of time though, as the damage may already be done.

The thermal shut-down is commonly around 100C +\-  on PC, but I don't know if  laptops shut down

that late. It may be more around the 95C +\- mark ?

[EDIT: Your i7 3610qm has a similar thermal threashold ]

Many Cpu's have a much lower threshold, eg.- my AMD Phenom965 has a threshold around 62-65C.

Do avoid running Furmark again. Furmark forces too much stress on the system.

Far more than any game. Furmark has killed many a card (I killed a gpu in '05 running Furmark),

particularly if used more than a few times.

Safer to stick with simple diagnostic tools that just measure the temps, fps, etc. while playing a game.

 - mini-dump files from the BSOD , and  Windows Error reports may reveal something other than

temp to those on the forums who a better at interpreting them than I .

    aruban say:

    Windows 10 problem with Airplane Mode/WiFi will not turn on.

    My wife has a Toshiba Satellite P755, which is about 4.5 years old.  The WiFi hardware is an Intel Centrino-N 1000.  The laptop was upgraded to Windows 10 from Windows 7. After the upgrade, everything was fine, except she no longer has access to Microsoft card games.  But Chrome Apps has all the games she wants, so she switched to them.  

    The problem came later when Airplane Mode came on and I was not able to turn on WiFi Mode. I was able to turn off Airplane Mode, but could not turn on WiFi.  I searched the Web and tried everything suggested, with no success.  I went to the Intel support site and downloaded the latest driver, and installed it.  Nope.  So, I uninstalled the driver (I did not rollback to the previous driver.), and did a full power down, not just a Restart. On reboot, Airplane Mode was gone, and the WiFi Mode was working.  I had to re-enter my WiFi password, but it all works again.  Unfortunately, the problem recurs every few weeks, but I just repeat the process to get it to work.  The alternative is to restore the image copy of Windows 7, which I would rather not do.  Hope this helps others who have encountered this problem.  I also hope Microsoft will fix this problem.
