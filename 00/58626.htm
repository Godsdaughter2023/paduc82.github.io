a. user say:

It takes more than switching to an SSD to make things go faster, as you have discovered.

The addition of an SSD only increases the speed of reading from and writing to the drive. There are many other factors that influence how 'quick' your computer feels - factors involving both hardware and software. Here are some examples:

I switched my HDD to an SSD and saw only a barely perceptible increase in speed. That's because the SSD was SATA III while the data bus in my older computer was SATA II. In addition, the chipset on my computer's motherboard wasn't optimized for SATA speeds. For me the major benefit of switching to an SSD was a quieter, cooler computer and no worries about mechanical failures.

Software that does frequent reads and writes will get more benefit from an SSD than software that makes longer sequential reads and writes. VLC, for example, won't benefit at all since it's mostly doing extended sequential reads. Same for PDF readers.

The ironic thing is that in the future, faster hard drive technology won't mean all that much for consumers. As computing increasingly moves to the cloud you're going to want the fastest and most reliable connection to the internet.

With this feature, a Windows computer can use a single physical wireless adapter to connect as a client to a hardware access point (AP), while at the same time acting as a software AP allowing other wireless-capable devices to connect to it.

david. f say: The likely cause is either noisy input / too much gain, or RF interference.

If it's a consistent noise that doesn't fluctuate, it's probably not RF interference.

If you get noise when recording with the cable only plugged into the computer and not your player, it is RF interference in which case your options are limited - make sure you use shielded cables.
