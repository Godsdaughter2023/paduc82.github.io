It is sounding very much to me that mail merge was probably not the right tool to be using for this application.

 

That notwithstanding, the following code will unlink all of the mergefields in the document from the datasource and convert the document to an ordinary (not a merge document) so that when it is re-opened, that SQL message will not appear.

 

Dim afield As Field
With ActiveDocument
    For Each afield In .Fields
        If afield.Type = wdFieldMergeField Then
            afield.Unlink
        End If
    Next afield
    .MailMerge.MainDocumentType = wdNotAMergeDocument
End With

Do you use outdented headings ("offset layout")? - Microsoft Community

graham hannington say:

Do you use outdented headings ("offset layout")?

I know of two methods in Word to outdent headings:
  1. Set zero left indent on headings and a positive left indent on most other paragraph styles (Body Text et al).
  2. Set a wide left page margin, a negative left indent on headings, and a zero left indent on most other paragraph styles.
In practice, I have found that both methods are blighted by fiddly formatting issues around that qualifier "most".

For example, for method 1 (which I have used for many years):
  • If you want to control formatting strictly via styles without applying formatting "manually" to specific paragraphs, you end up with two "flavors" of many paragraphs: one for paragraphs that are "column width" (aligned with the indented body text column), and another for paragraphs that are "page width" (aligned with the headings). For example, "Caption" for narrow (column-width) figures and "Caption Wide" for page-width figures.
  • A similar issue occurs inside tables. If all of your Body Text et al (List Bullet, List Number, etc) paragraph styles have a left indent, then you cannot use them as-is inside tables. So, you must either live with a limited selection of table-specific styles for use in tables (such as Table Cell), or define corresponding styles without that left indent.
Although method 2 does not suffer from that "styles in tables" issue, it introduces its own wrinkles, such as outdenting running headers and footers, and explaining to other users (authors) about negative indents, and what that wide left margin is all about (and why headings fall outside of the "text boundary").

I'm about to give up on offset layout in Word, and "devolve" various templates to a layout without outdented headings. Which is a shame, because I've been outdenting headings in printed (and PDF) documentation since about 1988 (around about the time I bought a copy of Xerox Publishing Standards: A Manual of Style and Design... and these days, I sometimes specify CSS media rules to outdent headings on wide displays).

Before I do, though, I thought I'd ask: what experiences do other users have with outdented headings in Word? Am I missing some technique that would make offset layout easier to live with in Word?

I should admit that I have other reasons for no longer outdenting headings (in some contexts), independent of any issues with Word. On a printed piece of paper, outdenting headings as eyecatchers makes sense to me. Again, on a printed piece of paper (that is inherently a fixed size), indenting body text to limit the measure (line length, for readability) also makes sense to me. But when you are viewing a PDF on-screen, where the left-hand pane shows headings as bookmarks, outdented headings can start to seem like a waste of screen real estate (although it's not that simple: there are issues of "density" of information and the deliberate use of white space as an aid to comprehension). And "reflowing" a PDF easily subverts the original measure. (As mentioned previously, when designing web pages, I do sometimes specify CSS media rules that apply outdenting to headings when I judge the display to be sufficiently wide, so I've not completely given up on the notion of outdenting headings.)
