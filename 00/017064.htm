suzanne s. barnhill say:

Without reading all the details, let me suggest that you look first at http://www.word.mvps.org/FAQs/Formatting/front_matter_2007.htm. Basic to understanding that article are these:

http://WordFAQs.mvps.org/HeaderFooter.htm

http://word.mvps.org/FAQs/Formatting/WorkWithSections.htm

and perhaps http://word.mvps.org/FAQs/Numbering/PageNumbering.htm

 

In general, when you just need a new page, a page break will suffice; when you need to change the header or footer (beyond what can be accomplished with a StyleRef field) or restart numbering, or begin a chapter with the First Page Header/Footer, then you need a section break, and if the new page should start on a recto page, then you need an Odd Page section break, which will generate a blank even page as required.

 

In any given section, you can select Top, Center, Bottom, or Justified vertical alignment. I prefer to use Top for everything, even pages that should be "centered." If you experiment, I think you'll find that mathematical centering results in text appearing to be too low on the page. To be pleasing to the eye, it actually needs to be a little above the mathematical center of the page. Consequently, I use Spacing Before to place the title, copyright info, or whatever, where I want it. I define chapter numbers or titles (whatever style is the first in the chapter) with the desired amount of Spacing Before. The last style before the chapter text (chapter number, chapter title, or occasionally an epigraph) has the desired amount of Spacing After to separate it from the chapter text. If every chapter has some sort of opening quote of variable length, then I sometimes put all of that preliminary stuff (chapter number, title, epigraph) in a single-cell table with Exact row height so that the text of each chapter will start at the same point regardless of the length of the preliminary stuff.

 

There's a lot of trial and error involved in book design, and you just have to decide what looks right to you.

IncludeText field - Microsoft Community

donwoolery say:

IncludeText field

When I use IncludeText to insert text from another file, the text is inserted as 11 pt even though the text in the file from which it is taken is 10 pt and the text at the point of insertion is 10 pt. What gives?
macropod say:
That will be because the Style definition for the content you're inserting is defined as being 11pt in the destination document, whose definition is overriding that of the source document. The behaviour is normal. The solution is to change the Style definition in the destination document to 10pt. You may need to define a new Style for this in both documents. Alternatively, if it's just boilerplate text all with the same formatting, you could add a Charformat switch to the INCLUDETEXT field and format at least the 'I' of INCLUDETEXT with the desired font attributes. See: https://support.office.com/en-us/article/Insert-fields-in-Word-c429bbb0-8669-48a7-bd24-bab6ba6b06bb
