bill manville say:

In the case of the long formula which does not work in conditional formatting there is a missing ", underlined below: 

=O($D$69="Minisito / Landing, App, Progettazione UX/IA";$D$69="Minisito / Landing, Progettazione UX/IA, App";$D$69="App, Minisito / Landing, Progettazione UX/IA";$D$69="Progettazione UX/IA, Minisito / Landing, App";$D$69="Progettazione UX/IA, App, Minisito / Landing").

In the cases where you are using cell references, they should not be enclosed in quotes - the formulas are looking for the strings, e.g. "Foglio3!$N$6, Foglio3!$N$7".  What you want to look for is the contents of those cells separated by ", " .  Like this:

=O($D$69=Foglio3!$N$6 & ", " & Foglio3!$N$7;$D$69=Foglio3!$N$7 & ", " & Foglio3!$N$6)

Why are some features only available to Office 365 subscribers? - Microsoft Community

mark_a_b say:

Why are some features only available to Office 365 subscribers?

I have a retail licence for Mac Office 2016. Will the Office 365 subscriber features be ported to retail licence users?

And when?

jim gordon mvp say:

The short answer is obvious: to give subscribers something for their money.

There are two main ways to purchase now: subscription (office 365) or stand-alone.

Subscribers get new features as they are introduced into the suite. Stand-alone has a fixed feature set (for the most part).

Large IT shops prefer stand-alone. They typically build a disc image of the apps and features they want to support and don't want to have to re-do this every few weeks. They often prefer Microsoft and other vendors to never introduce new features.

The new features introduced over time will be rolled into a new stand-alone version periodically. The time frame has been from 2 to 6 years. When Microsoft starts feeling the pressure from their large IT customers to have a new feature release is when you're likely to see the next roll-up version.

I've figured it out, thank the gods. This is for the reference of anyone who may search for it in the future, assuming that these threads are indexed somewhere.

 

You need to create two icons as .bmp images. One is the standard icon, the other is a mask. The mask is exactly the same as the standard icon except that the transparent parts need to be painted white, and the opaque parts need to be painted black. Doing this on a 16*16 icon isn't that arduous, fortunately; it's just a case of load the original image, save it as a copy, then paint over the corresponding pixels with either white or black. Both can be added into the ImageList.

 

You assign the standard icon to the .Picture property of the command button from the corresponding .Picture property of the ImageList's ListImages collection. (FormName.ImageListName.ListImages("Tag").Picture)

 

After that, you assign the mask image .Mask = FormName.ImageListName.ListImages("TagMask").Picture

 

Thus far, this has seemed to work on each PC that it's been tested on.
