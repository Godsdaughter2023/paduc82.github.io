mike hall... always say:

Intel 5000x is the chipset, not the model type.

Go to the Crucial website and do their 'system scan' of your memory..

http://www.crucial.com/

This will show what is installed and being recognised..

If you are running 32-bit Windows 7, your computer will never be able to use all 4gb anyway. The 32-bit Windows operating systems can address a total of 4gb, but some of that is taken up by motherboard resources and video cards. The resuult is that you will be able to use between 2.8 and 3.2 gb, assuming that 4gb RAM is installed.

A 64-bit operating system will generally be able to use 3.7gb RAM, assuming that 4gb RAM is installed 

 

If your computer is only showing 2gb, this could indicate that half of the installed memory is either not working or is mismatched.

 

Run a memory test. You can do this by running MEMTEST86 http://www.memtest.org/

or run the memory test in your BIOS, assuming that the function exists.

Is my PC running the 32-bit or 64-bit version of Windows?

http://windows.microsoft.com/en-us/windows7/find-out-32-or-64-bit

 

New computers that come preinstalled with Windows 7 often have what is called a recovery partition. This is used to reinstall the operating system in the event of a system crash. To access it, you will need to boot into when you start your computer by pressing a function key. This can be either F1, F2, F9, F10, F11, F12 or even DEL or Tab key. Consult the owners manual that came with your PC for instructions about how to reinstall Windows.

　

- Samsung uses ESC on most of its systems

- HP uses F9 on most of its systems

- Fujitsu uses F12 on most of its systems

- Acer uses F12 on most of its systems, but you often need to go into the BIOS to enable it

- Dell uses F12

　

In general, look at the screen during POST for a hint about what is possible.

　

If your recovery partition is not available or damaged, you should contact the manufacturer of your computer and request a recovery disc set you can use to reinstall Windows 7. They might charge a small shipping and handling fee.

a. user say:

Any encryption software that uses an industry standard algorithm will protect the privacy of your data (documents, music, pictures and videos,) as long as you have an unguessable password, because no one will be able to view those files without the password.

No encryption software will protect your PC from hackers, because your PC can be hacked just as easily whether or not your data files are encrypted.

The news you heard is likely about the WPA2 network security technology which itself is not an encryption algorithm but a method to generate encryption keys.

Here's how to protect your computer against hacks:

1- Keep up-to-date on the latest threats affecting your security and privacy.

2- Always exercise caution whenever you are online and be alert to situations where you could be attacked. Be vigilant.

3- Make sure that your operating system and application software is up-to-date, particularly for security updates.

4- Backup every day and store your backups in a location that is not in your computer or attached to your computer.

5- Password-protect your router with an unguessable password. If you have a WiFi network, use an industry standard encryption technique with an unguessable network password.

The above 5 steps are the most important. Here is your second line of defense:

6- Always have anti-malware and firewall software running in the background.

7- Occasionally run an on-demand scan with a different brand of anti-malware software.

8- Avoid risky online behavior.

9- Password-protect your user accounts, and use a non-privileged account for your day-to-day computing.

There are other steps you can also take and everyone has their own approach to security and privacy. But one thing is for sure: If you leave your security and privacy totally in the hands of software, without becoming personally involved, you are not secure.

garethgadd7785 say:

thanks for your help, I think I found the issue.

Machines that were unable to connect to Skype for business had IPv6 enabled in the network properties.  

unticking this option allows these computers to immediately connect ( asks for the password and then logs straight in ) 

The local router performing DHCP does have IPv6 enabled .. the domain controller in the remote office running DHCP has IPv6 enabled but has no options configured for it.

So the work around is to disable IPv6 on the troublesome computers network properties.
