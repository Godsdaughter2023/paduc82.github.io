¡firedog say:

The Death of Windows Live Mail is not surprising,  

Windows Live Mail is not dead, and nothing that has happened recently is trying to kill it. Nor has it 'stopped working'.

The program has offered the following functions for the past six years and continues to do so:

  • Newsgroup support using NNTP
  • Feed synchronization with support for both RSS and Atom 
  • Integrated contacts lists, also allowing for synchronization of contacts lists with online ones at contacts.live.com, and support for contacts directories using LDAP
  • Integrated calendar, also allowing for synchronization of calendars with online ones at calendar.live.com
  • Multiple email accounts, with support for POP3, IMAP, DeltaSync (for Microsoft Email accounts that haven't been upgraded to Outlook Mail) and Exchange ActiveSync (for Microsoft Email accounts that have been upgraded to Outlook Mail - requires update KB3093594)
  • Photo email, which avoids the problems of sending large image files by email
  • Saving mail messages individually or in bulk, both within the program and externally

This is a more feature-rich program than any other free one I know of. It works on Windows XP, Vista, 7, 8, 8.1 and 10, although the XP and Vista versions don't support all of the above and may not work much longer as support for these OSs is phased out.

The change at Outlook.com is no more world-shattering than, say, the decision by AOL and other email service providers to phase out support for POP3 in favour of IMAP. Although IMAP is more prone to error, it is better suited to a world where more and more people access their email from more than one device.

I think your post arises from widespread misunderstandings, but sadly this forum is not an ideal vehicle for correcting those misunderstandings. Anyone can post here with their misguided views, often spreading alarm and despondency where none is justified.

No connection could be made because the target computer actively refused it. This usually results from trying to connect to a service that is inactive on the foreign host—that is, one with no server application running.


Either you went to the wrong host, or the server application you're trying to contact isn't executing. Check the destination address you are using. If you used a hostname, did it resolve to the correct address? If the hostname resolution uses a local hosttable, it's possible you resolved to an old obsolete address. It's also possible that the local services file has an incorrect port number (although it's unlikely).
