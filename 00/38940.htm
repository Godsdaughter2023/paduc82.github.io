1) Change BIOS boot order - The most successful fix for this issue is to set the optical (DVD) drive as the first boot device in the BIOS. To do this, first enter BIOS mode as follows:

This is accomplished by pressing a specific key repeatedly as soon as your computer is turned on (or right after it is restarted). For many computers, the key is  F1, F2, F10, the Delete (del) key or the Escape (ESC) key. Consult your computer or motherboard?s manual or try different keys until you find the right one.

3) Update ITE ATA driver - Failing the above, try booting back into Windows Vista or XP. Launch Windows Update and install the ITE ATA driver update before attempting the upgrade. The upgrade advisor may not find this driver.
 
4) Switch from SATA to AHCI - Follow the aforementioned instructions for booting into your computer?s BIOS, then check your HDD or storage settings. Turn off SATA, switching to AHCI, and reboot normally, then re-attempt installation of Windows 7. Also try switching to IDE.

wruss say:

If you are using an external microphone with WSR, but your computer ALSO has a built-in microphone, make sure the built-in microphone is set to Disabled in the Recording tab of the Sound control panel. This will make sure that the built-in microphone isn't inadvertently affecting WSR.

Could there be a short in the wire connecting your microphone to the computer? To check, use the Sound Recorder program to record yourself speaking into the microphone for 20 or 30 seconds. Then play it back, listening carefully for any static, which if present could be firing off macros even when the microphone is muted. (You could also try recording silence and listen for static.)

When you say "mic is muted," I'm assuming you mean you have pressed a mute button on your microphone or USB sound card, and that WSR is still in "Listening" mode. If macros are running while WSR is set to "Sleeping" or "Off ," then...well, that's pretty bizarre.

If none of this helps, please post more information about the computer, Windows OS, microphone, and sound card that you're using. Thanks.

-Russ

wruss say:

Thanks for providing additional details about your computer setup. Your gaming microphone has both a USB connector and a 3.5mm connector, and based on looking at the manual, it appears that the microphone input is going into the computer through the USB connector. This would mean there is a mini sound card built into the module on the cord, and there should be a listing in the Recording tab of the Sound control panel for your Turtle Beach microphone.

The manual isn’t clear about how audio output gets into the headphones. It’s clear that audio output comes through the 3.5mm connector, but the manual also seems to say that this output might be coming through the USB connection as well.

Also, the headset has its own microphone volume control and a "chat boost" feature, which are not common on microphones designed for use with speech recognition, and could be causing fits for Windows Speech Recognition. When you first set up Windows Speech Recognition, you may remember running through a “Set up microphone” routine, which sets a specific microphone volume and SNR for WSR. If after doing this you later make adjustments to the microphone volume with the headset controls, then the volume going into WSR is more or less than it is expecting. This could be bad.

So here are some things to check:

  • If the 3.5mm connector is plugged into the microphone jack by mistake, unplug it and plug it into the speaker/headphone jack. Not all computers color code their ports, so it can be an easy mistake to make.
  • If the 3.5mm connector is plugged into the correct jack, try disconnecting it. On the Playback tab of the Sound control panel, you may be able to set the headset to receive the sound through USB by selecting the Turtle Beach sound card.
  • If the headset came with any software that permits disabling the microphone volume and chat boost features, try that. If not, try lowering the headset microphone volume control and see if that helps. Also, try repeating the WSR “Set up microphone” option in the Speech control panel, and don’t change the Turtle Beach microphone volume features after that. Experiment with low and high headset volume settings, making sure to repeat the WSR "Set up microphone" routine after each change.
  • Even though you can't hear it when you make recordings with your microphone, something about your system may be causing noise to be generated that is triggering the macros. If your headset is plugged into ports on the front of the computer, try switching to ports on the back. Maybe that port location will be less prone to interference. (The ports on the back of the computer are generally believed to be less susceptible to interference because the ports are usually on the logic board. Front panel ports have long wires to logic board connectors, and those wires are susceptible to interference from other internal components.) Heck, if your headset has always been plugged into the back, it can’t hurt to try switching it to the front.

Give these a shot and let me know how it goes. I have more ideas if these don't work.

wruss say:

You could also try using a process of elimination to determine if certain macros are causing the problem. Start by taking half the macros out of your Speech Macros folder and see if you still have a problem. If you still do, swap the macros you removed with the ones you kept. If the problem stops, then one of the macros you removed may be the culprit. Add back the macros you removed in small batches until you identify the problem macro (or macros).

Any problems with macros would likely occur in the the <listenFor> statements because that's what Windows Speech Recognition is listening for to trigger them.
