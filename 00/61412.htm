andre da costa say:
If you are reinstalling Windows 10, then you should skip entering a key.

Windows 10 Digital License
When you upgraded from a previous version of Windows or receive a new computer preinstalled with Windows 10, what happened is the hardware (your PC) will get a digital entitlement, where a unique signature of the computer will be stored on Microsoft Activation Servers. The Windows 7 or Windows 8 genuine license you were previously running will be exchanged for a diagnostics key.

Anytime you need to reinstall Windows 10 on that machine, just proceed to reinstall Windows 10. It will automatically reactivate. You will prompted to enter a product key a couple times through the installation, click I don't have a key and Do this later.

There is no need to know or get a new key, but if you have issues with Digital Licensing or the Activation Troubleshooter, you can utilize an existing Windows 7 or Windows 8/8.1 product key you already own to reactivate if necessary. or use the reset function in Windows 10.

wolfpup3 say:

If nothing about your copy of Windows changed, if this is the same OS, I'd wonder if the drive has errors...I'd try running Spinright on it.

Note that the November update (1511) of Windows 10 supports a new/better type of encryption which isn't backwards compatible with older versions of Windows.  The default for internal drives after 1511 is the new type of encryption, and external is the old encryption. 

If you switched to the new encryption, started encrypting it, and connected to a system running the older Windows 10 or earlier, it wouldn't be able to deal with it.

Otherwise, if you ejected the disc first before removing it, it should be able to pick back up where it was, and you should be able to manage bitlocker to remove bitlocker and that should work fine.  If it's not, I'd have to wonder if the drive is defective. 

Also, you'd want to have backups of all your data regardless, and especially before doing anything like this.  I've got multiple copies of my data, and turned bitlocker on one drive at a  time, making sure it worked first before doing the next one, until all my data was encrypted.  I haven't had any problems like you describe.

Tips on Obsidian mining - Microsoft Community

wildattorney say:

Tips on Obsidian mining

As most people know by now, you need Obsidian blocks to build the Nether Portal.  Obsidian blocks are generated when lava and water meet.  When deep in a dungeon, you may find yourself frustrated with your attempts to mine Obsidian, because often there is active lava right below the block, so as soon as it mines (takes about 15 seconds per block to mine, assuming you have a diamond pickaxe, which is the only thing that will work), it drops into the lava and is destroyed.  If there is an active supply of water around (and chances are there is, because as noted above, Obsidian forms from the combination of lava and water), make it so that the water actively flows over the blocks you are mining while you are mining them (by either destroying blocks that are blocking the water or adding blocks to redirect the water).  Then, when you mine the Obsidian revealing lava underneath, the water will turn that underlying lava into more Obsidian before the Obsidian piece you mined drops into the lava.  Then just drop into the hole you created and pick up the piece.  Works like a charm.

If I may inquire, how do you drop gravel on lava to prevent the Obsidian piece from dropping into the lava beneath?  If there is lava below the layer of Obsidian, the Obsidian piece is destroyed by the lava the moment you finish mining it (unless covered by water).  You could drop gravel on that block of lava once it is exposed, but you still lost the Obsidian piece.  You can't drop gravel on lava before you mine the Obsidian piece, because the lava is blocked by that very Obsidian piece.  The goal here is not just to avoid the lava, but to actually acquire the Obsidian piece.
