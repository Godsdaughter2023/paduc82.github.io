greginmich say:

If you’re having problems that you think might be malware related, there’s a natural inclination to look at what’s starting up in msconfig.exe and what’s running in Task Manager or Process Explorer. But using these tools to identify the presence of malware based on the suspicious behavior of system processes isn’t really a productive approach. The normal behavior of system processes is very complex to begin with, and it’s also highly dependent on the specific program files that are currently running on the system. One or two instances of dllhost.exe will normally open briefly when some (but not all) Windows utilities or programs are started; and the behavior that you’re describing for clicking on a file is normal. System32 files are duplicated in the SysWOW64 folder, and both folders contain dllhost.exe and dllhst3g.exe files.

In Task Manager you can right-click on the file/process, select “Properties”, and then click on the “Details” tab in the Properties window to see if the system files that are running actually belong to the OS. Then you can confirm the legitimacy and integrity of your system files by running the System File Checker:

http://support.microsoft.com/kb/929833

I think it’s very unlikely that the system files themselves would be infected, although they might be exhibiting unusual behavior as a consequence of potential malware (ntdll.exe).

http://www.bleepingcomputer.com/startups/ntdll.exe-3784.html

Since the behavior of system files often appears to be “suspicious” when in fact it’s perfectly normal; and since abnormal behavior doesn’t necessarily mean that the system files themselves are infected, the best strategy is probably to just ignore what you see happening with system processes. The classical use of msconfig.exe and Task Manager in malware removal was pretty much limited to using those tools to identify and disable an unrecognized (non-system) file/process that might be a malware executable. While that approach might still have some limited viability for people who’ve completely familiarized themselves with what’s normally running on their PC, the best approach for most of us is to just get busy scanning with the free versions of our recommended third-party malware-removal tools when things look suspicious. That’s the best way to detect, identify, and remove the malware that your primary AV product doesn’t detect.

http://www.surfright.nl/en/hitmanpro

https://www.malwarebytes.org/free/

http://support.kaspersky.com/viruses/disinfection/5350
