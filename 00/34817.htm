murrayjoe say:

Message came up with, "Windows has detected another computer with same IP address on this netework."

A message came up that said, Windows have detected another computer with the same IP address."... or similar  message to that, could that just be the laptop working of the wireless? 
langware say: MurrayJoe:


You mentioned a wireless laptop. Do you get this message at home (when your laptop is communicating with your own wireless router), or do you get this message when in a public hotspot?

If at home, then I would not be too concerned (what security options have you enabled in your home router?).

If you get the message while using a public hotspot, it does not necessarily mean your computer has been hacked ... but it is a warning that you should not ignore. In general, I would not access any site that has my financial data, or any other sensitive data, from a public hotspot. There are too many ways that a public hotspot can be compromised.

Your original post stated that "A message came up that said, Windows have detected another computer with the same IP address"

There are many reasons this could happen ... some innocent, and some not so innocent.

If your home network is configured in the most common way, then your router is using a feature called Dynamic Host Configuration Protocol (DHCP). You can read about DHCP here ...

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamic_Host_Configuration_Protocol

Basically, when a device on your home network needs an IP address, it asks your router, and your router assigns an IP address ... technically, the router "leases" the address for a specific period (say a day). When that period elapses, the device must "renew the lease".

DHCP saves you from the hassle of having to assign a static IP address to every device in your network, and then remember to recover that address if/when a device is removed from your network.

Sometimes, the DHCP process can get confused and assign the same address to multiple devices. If/when that happens, communications among devices in your home network can get confusing. Windows is smart enough to detect that another device on your home network has the same address as the computer where Windows is running .... but Windows cannot fix the problem.

You could determine the IP address of the computer that is running Windows (and posted the message you saw) .... by entering "ipconfig" (without the quotes) into a command line prompt. Then, you could check the IP addresses of all other devices in your network until you find the other device with the same IP address. Then you could either tell each device to get a new IP address from the router (or reboot the device). Alternatively, you could just try telling the computer that is running Windows to get a new IP address (by first entering "ipconfig  /release" and then "ipconfig /renew" at a command line prompt ... all commands without the quotes).

But since you stated that you are not very Techno savvy, my first suggestion was the simplest ... to just shut down everything in your network and then restart them one at a time starting with your ISP device, router, then other devices. If the problem continues to occur after that, then more troubleshooting (as described in the previous paragraph) would be needed.

I hope this helps to clarify the issue for you. If you still have questions, please post them and I'll try to provide an answer.

arminatorx say:

Green pixels and flickering lines are usually a sign for bad or loose contacts in a HDMI cable or the connectors. 

However, as you already said, you changed cables and tried it on a different TV, which makes this case a bit curious. 

Since HDMI is a digital connection and transmits digital data, it either works, or doesn't work. If for some reason the digital transmission for the data of a pixel fails, you will see those flickering green pixels or blinking lines. 

Maybe because you removed and plugged in different cables to your Xbox and moved it to a different TV, the contacts were "scrubbed" clean by removing and re-attaching the HDMI cable several times. 

Or maybe your console is in a TV shelf, and the HDMI cord is bent in such a way, that you get a loose contact, whereas on the other TV, you have more free room, and the HDMI cord isn't bent as much, causing the loose contact to work.

And the other way around: If you connect the cable from the Xbox from the flickering TV to another HDMI device (e.g. BluRay player), you also move the cable in a way, that the loose contact gets a proper contact, and the cable works without flickering.

If you say the console works flawlessly on the other TV, and other devices work on the flickering TV, I'd assume that the console works, and it's an issue with the cabling or contacts.
