Hello,

if the values are real time values, i.e. the underlying value is numeric, not text, then you can use a simple subtraction formula; End time minus start time equals elapsed time. So use

=B2-A2

and format the result as a time value with custom format [hh]:mm:ss

If you use square brackets around the hh part, Excel will allow numbers greater than 24 for the hours and not roll that into a day.

braintrove.com say: Hi Joseph,

Your issue is your approach. While you can manually type in options for a combo box or drop-down list, that is highly undesirable because it makes maintenance much more difficult, and as you've seen also makes copy-paste much more difficult.

The better approach is to have some kind of a FormData.xml file that you place all your options in, such as:

<FormData><ComboOptions><Option display="One" value="1"/><Option display="Two" value="2"/> .... </ComboOptions></FormData>

Then, you add this XML file into your form, and you bind your combo boxes to whatever fields you want, but you have them pull their options from this FormData.xml file rather than from a hard-coded hand-typed list. If you ever need to update the list, you do it in the XML file, and all of the boxes update automatically in one shot.

Beware of suggestions of the following form, usually attributed to McGimpsey (click here) [1]:

=SUMPRODUCT(--(X1>$A$30:$A$41), X1-$A$30:$A$41, $F$30:$F$41)

where X1 contains the amount of taxable income.

Your table does not conform to assumptions implicit in that formula.

In fact, your table does not make sense to me.  I will explain below.  Moreover, your terminology is ambiguous.  What does "between ... to" mean exactly?  10% of what, for example?

Please provide a URL for an official (government?) online reference that includes the source of the table and how to interpret it properly, ideally with some numerical examples.

The following is one interpretation of your table and the formula to use with it.



(Note:  I use a comma for the 1000s separator.)

For example, the tax on income of 400,000 to 750,000 is 5% of the entire income.

So, if the taxable income is in X1, the tax is:

=VLOOKUP(X1,$A$2:$D$13,3) + X1*VLOOKUP(X1,$A$2:$D$13,4)

For the VLOOKUP to work, A3:A13 must be "a little" larger than B2:B12.  So A3 is =B2+1E-8, copied down through A13. [2]

The VLOOKUP formula was used in columns F and G to compute the minimum and maximum tax for each "slab".  For example:

F3:   =VLOOKUP(A3,$A$2:$D$13,3) + A3*VLOOKUP(A3,$A$2:$D$13,4)
G3:  =VLOOKUP(B3,$A$2:$D$13,3) + B3*VLOOKUP(B3,$A$2:$D$13,4)

Note the huge differences between the tax range for each "slabs".  For example, tax on 750,000 is 37,500, but tax on 750,010 is 92,501 (10%*750,010 + 17,500).

That does not make sense to me.

The following table is a more common interpretation of such "slabs" (marginal tax brackets).  This is the interpretation that McGimpsey's SUMPRODUCT formula is based on.



For example, the tax on income of 400,000 to 750,000 is 5% of the amount over 400,000.

And the tax on income of 750,000 to 1,400,000 is 10% of the income over 750,000 plus the amount in column C ("Tax is...").

So, if the taxable income is in X1, the tax is:

=VLOOKUP(X1,$A$16:$E$27,3)
+ (X1 - VLOOKUP(X1,$A$16:$E$27,5)) * VLOOKUP(X1,$A$16:$E$27,4)

But again, note the huge differences between the tax range for each "slabs" in columns G and H.  For example, tax on 1,400,000 is 82,500, but tax on 1,400,010 is 38,001 (12.5%*(1,400,010-1,400,000) + 38,000).

Of course, that makes even less sense.

The following is the "correct" table to use with McGimpsey's SUMPRODUCT formula and the second VLOOKUP formula immediately above.  It is the most common design of marginal tax tables ("slabs").



The key difference is:  the value in column C ("Tax Is...") is the maximum tax of the previous "slab".

So, for example, the tax on 750,000 is 17,500, and the tax on 750,010 is 17,501.

So if the taxable income is in X1, the tax is computed by either of the following formulas:

=VLOOKUP(X1,$A$30:$E$41,3)
+ (X1 - VLOOKUP(X1,$A$30:$E$41,5)) * VLOOKUP(X1,$A$30:$E$41,4)

or

=SUMPRODUCT(--(X1>$A$30:$A$41), X1-$A$30:$A$41, $F$30:$F$41)

Column F is needed only for McGimpsey's SUMPRODUCT formula.  F31 is =D31-D30, copied down through F41.

Also note that because of the continuity from one "slab" to the next, column A can be simplified.  A31 is =B30, copied down through A41.

However, we do not have the authority to "correct" the numbers in column C.  Either you or your instructor entered them incorrectly, or the governing body is calculating "slab" tax in a very different way.
