Two mistakes.

First, navDate and matDate cannot be part of the Evaluate string.

Second, as I wrote in my footnote:  "Another way to avoid the misinterpretation of dates is to surround them with double-quotes."

Assuming that Excel recognizes text of the form yyyy-mm-dd as dates on your computer, the Evaluate expression should be:

Evaluate("Yield(""" & navDate & """,""" & matDate & """, 0.0416, 102.16, 100, 2, 3)")

That will work if you copy-and-paste it.  But the following is more readable:

Evaluate("Yield(" & Chr(34) & navDate & Chr(34) & "," & Chr(34) & _
        matDate & Chr(34) & ", 0.0416, 102.16, 100, 2, 3)")

Chr(34) returns the double-quote character.

If you might share the Excel file with others, it would be better to use DateSerial to represent dates.  For example:

Dim navDate As Double, matDate As Double
navDate = DateSerial(2012, 3, 31)
matDate = DateSerial(2017, 2, 15)
MsgBox Evaluate("Yield(" & navDate & "," & matDate & ", 0.0416, 102.16, 100, 2, 3)")

I've tried this Evaluate as well, still not working though. >_<


It certainly can be made to work.  But without a concrete example, we might not be able to help you.

If the following does not help, please provide an example of the VBA code that demonstrates how you are creating the parameters to Evaluate("yield(...)").  If you reference Excel cells, also provide the values and formulas in those cells.

Also, please explain what you mean by "not working":  (1) producing a compile-time or run-time error (which, and what error); or (2) producing an unexpected result?

To address #2, be sure that your VBA parameters work with the YIELD function in Excel.  One difficulty (for me) in using YIELD is that the price and redemption value must be specified "per $100 face value".  That means something very different to me than what is intended. [2]

Here is an example, given:

B1, purchase date:        =DATE(2012,4,9)
B2, maturity date:           =DATE(2017,4,9)
B3, coupon rate:             8.63%
B4, price:                         10,810
B5, redemption:             11,500
B6, coupon frequency:  2
B7:  =YIELD(B1,B2,B3,B4/(B5/100),100,B6,0)    [about 10.19%]

The simplest VBA implementation would be:

Dim yld as Double
yld = Evaluate("YIELD(B1,B2,B3,B4/(B5/100),100,B6,0)")

But your situation might be more complicated, perhaps something closer to the following example.

Sub testit()
Dim s As String, yld As Double
s = "yield(" & CDbl(Range("b1")) & _
            "," & CDbl(Range("b2")) & _
            "," & Range("b3") & _
            "," & Range("b4") / (Range("b5") / 100) & _
            ",100," & Range("b6") & ",0)"
yld = Evaluate(s)
MsgBox s & vbNewLine & Format(yld, "0.00%")
End Sub

Note the use of CDbl(Range("b1")).  If we had entered Range("b1"), we would get a runtime error.  The reason is that for type Date variables, VBA substitutes the formatted date representation, e.g. 4/9/2012.  That would be interpreted by Excel as 4 divided by 9 divided by 2012 [1].

Also note that it is prudent to construct the Evaluate parameter in a String variable, e.g. "s" above.  That makes it easy to use MsgBox or Debug.Print to verify what we are passing to Evaluate.

[1] Another way to avoid the misinterpretation of dates is to surround them with double-quotes.  For example:
s = "yield(""" & Range("b1") &  """,""" & Range("b2") &  """," & ...."
But CDbl(Range("b1")) is more readable and less error-prone, IMHO.


[2] Because of my confusion with the "per $100 face value" requirement, I double-check my YIELD usage by computing the IRR of the cash flows some other way.  It is straight-forward for this example, namely:  =2*RATE(10,B5*B3/B6,-B4,B5).

Want cell to fill color if date is more than 1 year past - Microsoft Community

lori_bigler say:

Want cell to fill color if date is more than 1 year past

How can I get the cells in a workbook to fill in once a date is over 1 year expired? Thank you.

hansv mvp say:

Select the cell or cells with dates.

On the Home tab of the ribbon, click Conditional Formatting > New Rule...

Select 'Format only cells that contain'.

Leave the first dropdown set to 'Cell Value'.

Select 'less than' from the second dropdown.

In the box next to it, enter the formula   =EDATE(TODAY(),-12)

Click Format...

Activate the Fill tab.

Select a color.

Click OK, then click OK again.

Hi,

Based on Hans' formula: =EDATE(TODAY();-12)*(A1>0)

Excel is considering an empty cell compared to 0 as equal and dates are internally just numbers and a 0 is in this case equal to 01/0/1900 or 12/31/1899.

By adding *(A1>0) only "valid" cells are taken in consideration.
