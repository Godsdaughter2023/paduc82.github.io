grschinon say: As others have pointed out, this is a newtork feature.

The GSM standard allows for ring times from 5 to 30 seconds in 5 second increments. You can set it by going into your phone's call divert settings, more precisely the "call divert if no answer" setting. Set it to divert to voicemail, and you will be asked to select a delay from 5 to 30 seconds. Once you've done that, the phone sends a command to your network telling it to wait however many seconds before shunting inbound calls to voicemail.

Note that some networks override the setting you give here. If this is the case here you'll need to phone your operator and ask them if there's anything they can do.

rob koch say:

I'd heard of the Sysinternals tools many times over the years including once when Mark Russinovich sat in on a Security session at an annual MVP Summit.  However, I'd never actually used any myself since typical scanning tools have always sufficed for my needs.

I downloaded the latest Sigcheck V2.42 to learn what you might be seeing and found what I expected.  Though I received no indications of malware while checking the primary C:\Windows or System folders, the results where quite different during a check of my personal Downloads folder.  Though nothing in the folder is truly dangerous and only one of the files displayed via VirusTotal reports has ever been detected by any 3rd-party security scanner before, the results didn't really surprise me.

The reason is that while VirusTotal reports are often useful to identify potential malware, they must always be taken with a grain of salt.  That's because they are only the results of checking that specific file using the scanning engines of roughly 50+ 3rd-party antivirus applications and so are the opinions of those scanners and nothing more.

In my case the 4 files detected included; a copy of CCleaner setup, detected as a Trojan/Generic by Antiy-AVL; 2 different copies of MBAM setup detected as different Adware versions by Zillya; and an old Codec pack detected as various PUP/Toolbar/Adware by 4 scanners including ESET and MBAM, as the latter has in the past.

Note that only the last file above is detected by more than one scanner, so this immediately makes the results of the others suspect, especially if the detected files themselves are older.  Only very newly downloaded files are likely to be missed by the major name scanners, so individual detections, especially by the lesser name antivirus are likely to be false positive or at least questionable results.

The other key question in your case is where were the 50 files actually detected on your PC?  If they are in important folders such as Windows or System I'd be more concerned than if they were found in say the Downloads or some temporary folder as was true in my case.  This means they were likely simply downloaded to the system sometime in the past, but not actually installed or operating on the system and may be simply deleted if not desired.

So you truly need to individually inspect each of the VirusTotal reports for these types of issues and then also look at the other tabs it includes such as the File Detail, which in my case indicated the first 3 files were all signed by the valid companies which produced them.  In fact, there was also a green highlighted notice at the bottom of the initial analysis pane stating; "Probably harmless! There are strong indicators suggesting that this file is safe to use. "

So the main point here is to actually read the reports which are presented, since they are really the best source of information describing these files.  If desired for something more in question, then use an internet search for the exact detection results listed by a particular scanner to see whether there might be further information available.  That's why they are provided and the value of these will depend upon the effectiveness of the additional information they might provide.

Sigcheck itself does none of this analysis and only determines which files to submit to the VirusTotal service based upon the particular switch selections you used during your scan.  To me your choice to scan the entire C:\ drive makes little sense, since this is always likely to find files of questionable origin.  This action virtually always causes false positive detections by most scanners and is why unskilled users are more dangerous to themselves and their own systems than helpful when using such highly technical tools.
