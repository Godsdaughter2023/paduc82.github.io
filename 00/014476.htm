jeeped say:
It is almost always better to format the number at the destination rather than the source. In your merged Word document, press Alt+F9 to expose the field codes and modify your merge field by adding number formatting in the form of,

{ MERGEFIELD myfld \# "#0.00" }

Note the addition of \# "#.0.00" to force the merged number to at least one integer and two decimal places. Currency symbols and/or thousands separators can likewise be added.

Press Alt+F9 to toggle the field codes back to field display then press Ctrl+A (e.g. Select All) and tap F9 to update all fields.

Multiplication bug in Excel?

hansv mvp say:

Displayed values in Excel are accurate only up to 15 significant digits.

5,629,499,534,213.12 has the maximum of 15 significant digits.

11,258,999,068,426.24 has 16 significant digits, i.e. one too many. Excel rounds the 16th digit to 0.

Internally, however, the value is stored with greater precision, so if you multiply by 2 again, the result 22517998136852.48 is displayed as 22517998136852.50 - the display value is rounded to 15 significant digits, and the 16th becomes 0.

jllatham say:

Because of the way the VBA engine works, you have do double up on the double-quote marks - it was getting that correct..

Take a very simple formula that you want assigned to a cell:

=""

If you record a macro while doing that, you get this:

ActiveCell.FormulaR1C1 = "="""""

That's a double-quote, equal mark then 5 more double-quote marks.

The first double-quote acts as you expect it to, it marks the = mark as part of a text string, then the two pairs of double-quote marks following the = symbol are translated as being internal to the text string and reduced to two individual double-quote marks, then the 6th double-quote mark is used as you expected again - to terminate the text string =""  But it is sure hard to visualize at times, and easy to lose count.

You can learn more by searching for a phrase like

how excel vba handles double-quote marks

and reading up on it a little.  You'll find that several places recommend taking the same path that I did: when you want a double-quote actually contained in the final text string (or formula), use something like

& Chr(34) &

Which would put a single double-quote mark in the final string, or even

& Chr(34) & Chr(34) & to get a pair of back-to-back double-quote marks - and make what's happening in the VBA code more visible when being read by a person.  Thus my original simple formula would look something like this

ActiveCell.FormulaR1C1 = "=" & Chr(34) & Chr(34)
