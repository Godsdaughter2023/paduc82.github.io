Hi Ginny:

Yes, there is a way to do this, but first I need to understand what the benefit of hiding the "Chapter" text is, because I suspect you will end up doing a whole lot of work you do not need to do.  I do long complex documents in Word all day for a living (I am a technical writer).

For example:  There is no need to show the Chapter number in the page numbers any longer: this is an old technique that hangs over from the days when books were made with a typewriter and paste pot.  Readers REALLY hate it, because they can't find their way around the book (from either the TOC or the Index) and in terms of updating, it's much cheaper to re-print the whole book.  Sending "replacement pages" these days is an exercise in futility: the users NEVER put them in, and if you send someone to do it for them, you have spent far more than the extra paper would cost to print the whole book.  It also encourages the users to keep extra "notes" in the book, so no two copies of the book contain the same information -- it's an information management nightmare.

Similarly, the Master Document is blissfully unaware of the presence of "Chapters".  It doesn't care: it takes its view of the document from the "Outline Level" property of the styles applied.  If you use the built-in "Heading..." series of styles, these will be level 1, level 2, level 3,...Level 9, corresponding to the Heading Number in the style name.

So: if you want to begin each subdocument with a Heading 2 (Level 2 style) you go right ahead, the master document doesn't care.

So you can force the operation you want by simply setting your Insert>Page Numbers>Format>Include Chapter Number>Chapter Starts With to "Heading 2".  That will generate the numbering in the page number from the level 2 heading instead of the level 1 heading.

But again: Your readers will hate you and you make the TOC and Index unuseable for them.

I would also question the use of a master document.  Master documents require every editor of the book to be highly skilled in Word, otherwise they will corrupt the master document.  And when you corrupt a master document, you often lose the entire document.

I would say that if your book is projected to be less than 2,000 pages in total, and you are using Word 2011 on a reasonably-specified Mac, then there is no benefit in using master documents and quite a substantial risk.

If you have some other reasons for needing this approach, let's discuss those -- because I see a risk that this will end in tears :-)

jay freedman say:

When you copy a form field, such as the dropdowns, and paste it into another cell or elsewhere, the pasted field has no bookmark name. That is, if you double-click the pasted field to open its Properties dialog, the highlighted box is empty:

Unless you manually named each of the pasted dropdowns, the macro has no way to address them to change their lists.

macropod say:

For working demonstrations of Dependent & Hierarchical Dropdown Content Controls, see:
 
Dependent Dropdown Content Controls
http://www.msofficeforums.com/word-vba/24661-help-cascading-dropdown-list.html#post77762
 
Hierarchical Dropdown Content Controls
http://www.msofficeforums.com/word-vba/29617-creating-reducing-drop-down-list.html#post94603

Note how the list is not replicated in the code for each of the Hierarchical Dropdown Content Controls. If you need to work with formfields instead of content controls, that could be done, too.

Jay is correct about the formfield bookmark issue - and content controls don't even have a bookmark property - but your use of a table means you can address your formfields (or content controls) by cell address and, if you have more than one in a single cell, by its relative position within the cell. For example:

Activedocument.Tables(1).Cell(2,3).Range.FormFields(1)

would address the first formfield in cell C2 of the first table in the document.

Hi Ashok,

Thanks for your update.

The storage limits in SharePoint Online is stated as below in Office 365 Enterprise plan: 1T + 500MB per subscribed user + additional storage purchased. For example: if we have 35 users with the SharePoint Online licenses(From your description, I noticed you have 35 Office 365 Enterprise E3 licenses ), the base storage allocation is approximately 1T(1T + 500MB * 35 licensed users). We can purchase an unlimited amount of additional storage via the article: Change storage space for your subscription.

More information can be found here: SharePoint Online and OneDrive for Business software boundaries and limits.

In addition, all SharePoint Online for Office 365 plans include OneDrive for Business. This storage allocation for OneDrive for Business is separate from SharePoint Online pooled storage mentioned above. Each OneDrive for Business user gets 5TB for personal storage space. Please note: we still get 1T storage for OneDrive for Business now and 5T storage allocation is rolling out. Please see the Office 365 roadmap.

If anything is misunderstood or unclear, please let us know and we will continue to discuss the situation based on the update.
