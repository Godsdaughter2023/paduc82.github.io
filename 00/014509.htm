The short answer is: this is a typical issue with binary computer arithmetic that uses 64-bit binary floating-point to represent numeric values, as Excel and most applications do.

The work-around is: when you expect a calculation with non-integers (or division of integers) to be accurate to some number of decimal places (e.g. 2 in your case), explicitly round to that number of decimal places.

In other words, change your formula to =ROUND(1610.2+SUM(Current!M6:Current!M500000), 2) .

(Note: The EXACT function compares strings, not numeric values. You did not need to use it to see the floating-point anomaly. Simply temporarily format the cell to display 15 significant digits, or use a formula of the form =A1&"".)

-----

The longer answer is.... 64-bit binary floating-point cannot represent most decimal fractions exactly. Moreover, the approximation of a decimal fraction varies depending on the magnitude of the integer part.

Consequently, for example, IF(10.12 - 10 = 0.12, TRUE) returns FALSE(!).

(In contrast, IF(10.23 - 10 = 0.23, TRUE) returns TRUE as expected, but only by coincidence and due to a dubious heuristic in Excel.)

This is complicated by the fact that Excel displays (formats) only up to 15 significant digits (rounded).

Consequently, the constant 0.12 appears to be 0.1200000000000000000, even when formatted with 19 decimal places. But in fact, the exact decimal presentation of the internal binary representation of 0.12 is 0.119999999999999,99555910790149937383830547332763671875 .

In contrast, 10.12 is exactly 10.1199999999999,992184029906638897955417633056640625 , so 10.12 - 10 is exactly 0.119999999999999,2184029906638897955417633056640625 .

(I use period for the decimal point and comma to demarcate the first 15 significant digits.)

bill manville say:

I can't help you with why it happens, or with a way to stop it happening.

But you could use a macro such as this to put the comments back in position

Sub RePositionComments()
  Dim WS As Worksheet
  Dim C As Comment
  Dim iSave As Integer
 
  iSave = Application.DisplayCommentIndicator
  Application.DisplayCommentIndicator = xlCommentAndIndicator
  For Each WS In ActiveWorkbook.Worksheets
    For Each C In WS.Comments
      C.Shape.Left = C.Parent.Offset(0, 1).Left + 10
      C.Shape.Top = C.Parent.Offset(0, 1).Top - 10
    Next
  Next
  Application.DisplayCommentIndicator = iSave
End Sub

conditional formatting if divisible by 4 = whole number - Microsoft Community

hawaiiham2000 say:

conditional formatting if divisible by 4 = whole number

How would I go about assigning conditional formatting (ex. fill with red) if a cell is divisible by 4 (= a whole number)?
hansv mvp say:

On the Home tab of the ribbon, click Conditional Formatting > New Rule...

Select "Use a formula to determine which cells to format".

Enter the formula =MOD(A1,4)=0 where A1 is the cell you want to format (or the top left cell in the range of cells that you want to format).

Click Format...

Activate the Fill tab and select red.

Click OK twice.
