rohn007 say:

Lots of big pictures will tend to slow your document down.

DO NOT “Upgrade” to 2013 or 2016 in hope of improving performance!

You will be massively disappointed!

There are several things you can try after following Macropod's suggestions.

Try using "Draft" view for "normal" editing. This suppresses most formatting and so it displays faster.

Why are you using 600 DPI images?  Take a look at the specifications of your printer to see the maximum DPI it can support.  Large DPI, like 600 is mainly good for images you plan to blow up to poster size.  Letter size documents are typically printed in 300 DPI, or less.

In much earlier times, with older versions of Office and less powerful computers I too used to split my documents very much along the same parameters you are using.  But, for the last 10+ years when I got my Vista class laptop (with only 3GB of RAM) and Office 2003-2010 I found I could move away from that strategy. 

How much RAM do you have on the work machine?

20-30 MB is an eminently manageable file size for Word 2010.  I currently have a 36MB document that I edit daily. It has some images, not as many as you have and it works out to roughly 1700 pages.  Once you figure out the cause of your performance problem, there should be no reason why you couldn't combine all of the files into one large one to make editing easier.

Go into File menu > Options command > Advanced option:

  • In "Editing Options" section, turn off "Keep track of Formatting" option
  • In "Image Size and Quality" section, turn ON "Discard Editing Data" option. This eliminates the "undo" option, making image smaller
  • In "Image Size and Quality" section turn off "Do not compress images in file". By turning this option Off, if you crop an image, the 'cropped' pieces are discarded, making the image size smaller and therefore making the file size smaller
  • In "Show Document Content" section, I like to turn on "Show text wrapped within document Window" option. It works best with draft and web views to show you more content on screen if have a widescreen monitor
  • In "Show Document Content" section, definitely do try turning ON "Show Picture Placeholders".  This replaces pictures with a blank frame "placeholder". This will speed up scrolling and moving around in the document.  You could record a macro toggling this option so that you can put it on the QAT to make it easier to change when you want to look at the pictures.
  • In the "General" section, turn OFF "provide feedback with animation". This should be a trivial effect, but it may help.

At work in the Windows Control Panel look for the "Performance Options" dialog.  In there you can tell windows to "Adjust for best performance". This will turn off "fancy" features that use some CPU, which could slow down performance in your documents.

Finally, there is a relatively "old school" technique (I haven't used one since my DOS days!) you can try, define and use a "RAM disk". 

The idea is you allocate some RAM to use as a "virtual disk drive". Since RAM is infinitely faster than spinning HD, it eliminates that delay.  The idea is you define the RAM disk, copy a file to it then edit the file. When you are done editing it save it back to the HD.  Do a google search for RAM disk to get specific instructions.  NOTE that RAM disks are "volatile", they go away when the PC is shut down or the power goes out.   In theory you should not have to use this sort of extreme measure, but it may help.

Actually, since the new year SSD's have come down to very reasonable prices. Cheap enough that on Amazon they list 32 to 120GB drives for a relatively affordable price of under US$50.  If the issue is causing you enough grief it would be worth while (for the company or even you) to make this small investment to improve your performance.
