gokul t say:

Hi Ken,

 

Volume leveling is available only with files that are in Windows Media Audio (WMA) or MP3 format and contain a volume-leveling value.

 

To add the volume-leveling value to a file, play the entire file with auto-leveling turned on. The Player will then level the volume for that file whenever you play it in the future.

 

Apparently, volume-leveling is not turned on when adding files to the library.

 

If you wish to apply volume-leveling to all the files in the music library without having to play all the songs individually, I’d suggest you to first to remove the items from theWindows Media Player library, turn on volume-leveling and then re-add items to the Windows Media Player library.

 

Note: Ensure NOT to remove/delete the items from the computer. Remove only from the library.

 

Also see:

 

Change volume settings in Windows Media Player

 

Windows Media Player frequently asked questions

First, driver updating is something you do to fix a problem.  A lot of people worry about up to date drivers and the result is a lot of problems. 

Second, NEVER use drive update sites that proliferate the Internet.  The vast majority of them are in fact malware and either have no drivers for you or have bad ones.  Your principle choice should be the OEM of the device itself. 

Third, keyboard drivers rarely, if ever change.

Drivers do not update themselves.  You MAY be offered driver updates in Windows Updates IF you have selected and other Microsoft software in Windows Update.

Now, I gather you have a problem with your current keyboard????

Keep in mind that keyboards (on desktop style computers???)  are inexpensive and easy to replace.

Try a driver rollback (if offered). Device manager, expand the keyboard item. Right-click choose Properties, Driver tab.

Try uninstalling the driver.  Device manager, expand the keyboard item. Right-click choose uninstall, restart.

In most cases, if you have a keyboard problem, it will be the result of failing hardware, not the driver.

Why doesn't a DVD play? 

A DVD may not play for one or more reasons. The following is a list of possible reasons and some steps you can take to try to solve this issue:

·   Another DVD player application has been installed on your computer that prevents Windows Media Center from playing the DVD. Try closing or uninstalling the other DVD player application.

·   Your DVD drive has been disabled. Verify that your DVD-ROM drive is enabled.

Follow this step to do the same:

To turn on your drive:

1. Open the DVD drive properties by clicking the Start button , clicking Computer, right-clicking the DVD drive, and then clicking Properties.
2. Click the Hardware tab, and then click the DVD drive.
3. Under Device Properties, click Properties, click the Driver tab, click Enable, and then click OK.

·   Your display resolution or connection type does not support the copy protection technology that is required to play the DVD. You may need to change your display resolution to 640x480 or 720x480. Alternatively, you can change the cable connection between your display and your computer to use a DVI or VGA cable.

For more information about the different types of cable connections see:

Connect your computer to a TV
http://windows.microsoft.com/en-US/windows7/Connect-your-computer-to-a-TV

·   Your DVD-ROM drive cannot play back that type of DVD. For example, some DVD-ROM drives can only read DVD+R or DVD-R discs. If you burn a video DVD to a type of recordable DVD that the DVD-ROM drive does not support, the DVD will not play back.

Also try updating the driver for your CD/DVD drive to the latest by visiting the manufacturer’s website and check if you can fix the issue.
