suzanne s. barnhill say:

Assuming that the text in question is not all in one paragraph (and if it's copied from a blog, that is definitely a consideration), then see http://word.mvps.org/FAQs/Formatting/WholeDocumentReformatted.htm.

 

Before you do anything else, though, click on the Show/Hide ¶ button in the Paragraph group on the Home tab to display nonprinting characters. If what you see at the end of a "paragraph" is a bent arrow (or probably two bent arrows) rather than a paragraph mark (¶), then all the text is a single paragraph, and any formatting changes you make will apply to the whole thing. You'll need to replace the line breaks (or two line breaks, as the case may be) with paragraph breaks. Then you can change the indent for a single paragraph without affecting others.

 

Keep in mind, though, that indents are paragraph formatting; you can't apply an indent (except for a first-line indent) to a single line of a paragraph.

rob koch say:

The issue of the emails is completely separate from both the initial breach and the question of whether someone still has access to any individual personal accounts.

Bruce has mentioned the potential that the PC itself may not be secure, which in part depends upon the original attack profile as he is asking, so I'll leave that question to him.

In terms of the emails themselves, the only thing required is that they have your email address and in your specific case the information relating to where you have your bank accounts.  If you've already secured these accounts by changing any passwords and insured that your PC is safe and secure as well, then the only way the scammers can regain control is to attempt to get you to give up this current information yourself.

This is where phishing messages come in and there are many tricks to try to convince (con) you into believing these are both real and something is truly wrong.  For example, using both the logo and other cues like formatting of the message to look as much like the actual bank or other entity is a common method.

The other common trick is to use annoying or strange visual or audible displays in order to intimidate the target into believing that something is drastically wrong and needs to immediately be dealt with.  This is the same as the classic "act now" technique used in advertising, but more blatantly displayed in ways that purposefully aggravate and scare you into inappropriate action.

So the difficult, but highly important thing you need to do is to stop, think and determine whether any individual situation makes sense, since the hope of the scammers is that you'll add each of these individual instances within your mind, allowing them to confuse and fluster you into providing them exactly the types of new information that you shouldn't.

Your other most effective method to counter these types of attack is to only use communication methods that you are comfortable with as insuring you are truly in contact with the correct people.  For example, if for your bank this is by phone, then only trust that method when you've personally called them using a known accurate number to verify any such questionable email or other contact.

The key concept you should see here is "divide and conquer", since it's your potential confusion from information overload that the scammers depend upon.
