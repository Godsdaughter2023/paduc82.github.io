john mcghie say:
Hi Tossa:

Yes, I know what you are talking about: the Chinese characters adopted into Korean.

Assuming that you have the characters appearing on your screen, this means the characters are being expressed in a font that contains those characters.

Internally, the writing is not "characters", it's numbers -- integers normally expressed as two hexadecimal characters. Take the character for "Army".  Internally this appears as two hexadecimal digits: 8E CD or 36557 if you are working in Decimal notation.

If you are working in a Unicode font, in theory the numbers all line up across all of the fonts in the world, so that 8ECD stands for 軍 in any font on any computer.

So no "conversion" should be necessary.  If you were to change from a Chinese font to a Korean font, the character may change appearance slightly for artistic reasons, but the shape and number of strokes would remain the same.

So normally all you would have to do is select the text and apply a Korean font.

However, the rot sets in if you are dealing with text that was created with old fonts designed for the era before Unicode became common.  To get a particular character to appear in those fonts, you needed three pieces of information: the "number", the "Shift", and the "Font".  In those days, numbers could not exceed 256 (8 bits).  To get characters outside the ASCII range (a to Z) the font manufacturer would use a combination of the number plus a "shift" that would tell the computer which of the 255-character "pages" the character belonged to.  Font foundries could suit themselves as to where they placed characters and on which page, because the "rules" needed to apply only to their own font.  And of course you needed to have that particular font installed on your computer.

If that's what you are dealing with, I am not aware of any "easy" way to "convert" these characters, other than to re-type them.  The character codes do not normally match the codes in a modern Unicode font.  If you change the font, you will get random characters.

There:  That was a fairly lengthy "don't know", wasn't it?

If this involves a POP3 account, your contacts would be located in the PST that was associated with the account. If you don't have a backup of that, you've lost your contact data.

If this involves an IMAP account and your contacts were stored in a folder marked with the words "This Computer Only" which means exactly that, then if you did not manually backup (export) that data to a PST file, you've lost your contact data.

If this involves an Exchange account, the contact data would be stored on the server and will be sync'd when you configure the email account.
