jimwynne say:

I mean do I need to buy an additional program such as McAfee Total protection.  I want to ensure that my computer is protected against malware.

I asked you "in addition to what?" and you didn't answer the question.  What you need for malware protection depends in large measure on your web-surfing habits.  There is no anti-malware solution that will protect you if you're not careful about clicking on links, especially links in email messages.  Don't click on a link unless you're sure of where it's going to take you.  When a new malware species is released into the wild, there will always be a delay in antivirus software catching up, so don't ever think that you're completely protected.   I've been using the web since the beginning and I've never had a virus or trojan infection and can count on the fingers of one hand the number of times that antivirus software has prevented it, and even in those cases there were doubtlessly false positives.

For myself, I use Windows Defender and the Windows firewall.  Your mileage may vary, as they say, but if you feel the need for a different solution, get a standalone antivirus program.   As I suggested, if you do feel the need for extra protection, you need to take a look at your own web-surfing habits.

For example, the header row text in Table Grid is defined as Times, 12 pt, bold, centered, with 3 pts before and after.  But, if I'm in Body Text (or Normal) in the document (as most would be when inserting a table) and click Insert>Table, the before/after spacing is overridden with Body Text's 12 before/3 after.  This happens with any other style: the bold, centering, and shading seems to stay; everything else in the Table Grid is overridden, for headers and regular cells.

Shauna Kelly's article at http://www.shaunakelly.com/word/styles/custom-table-styles-2002-2003.html explains this problem in Word 2002 and 2003. Sadly, this was not an article that Shauna chose to use her time updating before her untimely death last year. Changes in Word 2007 were supposed to alleviate the problem provided users made changes to the document defaults instead of to Normal style, but this is poorly understood (I myself don't really understand it despite the explanation at http://blogs.office.com/b/microsoft-word/archive/2008/10/16/behind-the-curtain-styles-order-of-operations.aspx). I suspect that the bottom line is that table styles are still not really a total solution.
