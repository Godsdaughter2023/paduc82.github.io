john korchok say:

PowerPoint links only the data, so changing the link will change the appearance of the chart. The best long-term strategy for handling this is to create a PowerPoint theme and master slide that includes as much of the formatting as possible, so that later updates don't destroy so much of your local formatting.

When you're ready to create a new link:

  1. Delete the chart from PowerPoint.
  2. In Excel, select the chart and copy it.
  3. In PowerPoint, click on the down-pointing arrow on the right side of the Paste button and choose Use Destination Theme & Link Data

john mcghie say:

Hi Jasmine:

I think we may be confusing you a little.

To begin with, the Exclusion Dictionary does not work in Word for Mac.  You can make it work in Word 2011 but it's a long, complex process and not very reliable.

Sadly, it is the Exclusion Dictionary function you need in order to remove the American spellings from Word, and you will never get it in Word 2008.

However: I should explain a few things.  The "language" you set on a custom dictionary controls only which dialect of English that custom dictionary works with.  It does not control the language of the spelling checker.  Word accepts 13 dialects of English. As soon as you set a language on your custom dictionary, it will accept additions ONLY from text market in that dialect of English.

So if you have text that is a mixture of English US and English Canadian, and you set your custom dictionary to English UK, it will not accept additions from that document.  Nor will the words already in there be available when spellchecking that document.

You COULD create 13 different custom dictionaries, one for each language.  However, that's far too much work, maintaining them all.  What we recommend is to set your custom dictionary to "no language".  That means it will be working in any text you encounter, and will accept words from all languages.

However, adding an English English spelling to a custom dictionary will not stop Word from accepting the American spellings, or from offering American corrections; because the Main Dictionary contains both American and UK spellings.

First, you need to ensure that all of the text in your document is marked with the English UK language. Especially text you may have copied from the Internet: that is often marked as English US.  Use Tools>Language to do that. Also use Format>Style to correct the languages used in your styles, otherwise your text will keep reverting to the wrong language.

If you get the language marked on the text correct, Word should rarely accept or offer an American spelling.

If you buy the later version of Word for Mac, come back and we can tell you how to get the Exclude Dictionary working in that.  It can't be done in Word 2008.

Hope this helps

szhosain say:

FIGURED OUT A CAUSE/EFFECT and now have a better work-around!

It was getting tiresome to be careful in what Windows Update would continually try to do with updating Office 2013. Particularly since I accidentally did not deselect the checkbox this afternoon for the SP1 update of Office 2013 and it did the update even though I did not want it!

Then, Outlook, Word, Excel and PowerPoint promptly broke as described earlier! Argh! :(

Apparently, one Office update interacts badly with the NVIDIA Optimus drivers for the NVIDIA graphics chip in my Lenovo W530. These drivers are provided by Lenovo ... and cannot be updated from the NVIDIA site apparently!

(I.e., the WiFi - used while undocked - was a red herring here basically ...)

The work-around is to disable graphics hardware acceleration in Office - the Intel HD 4000 is fast enough to run these office applications without any issues.

To do this workaround:

  1. Open Word 2013 (while docked, or after booting into Safe Mode perhaps).
  2. Start a blank document.
  3. Click on menu item File->Options->Advanced and  look for the checkbox labelled "Disable hardware graphics acceleration" and select it.
  4. Exit Word and check that all is now well with Word, Excel, Outlook, and Powerpoint!

This selection in Word also disables graphics acceleration for Outlook, and prevents all the Office apps from using the NVIDIA Optimus drivers for the NVIDIA K1000M chip in my laptop. And forces Office to only use the Intel HD 4000 graphics chip in there.

Whew, a workable solution so that Office SP1 (and later Office updates that were also causing problems) is now usable again.

Clearly, Lenovo needs to look at their version of the NVIDIA Optimus drivers to see what may be awry here.
