harry stevens say:

I think for a company's standpoint, it's like this -

To improve account security for millions of accounts worldwide, the system of authenticating users if they login to another country is a very great way to deter hackers. There is a good chance that a hacker whose able to phish login credentials from someone may not live in the same country, hence cannot be logged in really easily.

Of course, there are people going to different places, different countries, moving, and so on. But that's number's really low compared to those who will be staying to their own place for good. Let's say for every 1 million accounts, only 45,000 are those people who will be moving around. This is just a sample, mind you, but for a company's perspective, it's better to have better protection for those 955,000 accounts even though the other 45,000 accounts will have some problems logging in.

Pretty much the 'best' solution to get around this is to use an Authenticator App, easy to setup and secure a Microsoft Account. Nowadays, even two-step verification is a viable protection even for other email services, which also uses an authenticator app. So it's best to move on and use such services in order to protect your account.

I'm not sure if new changes have been made - I no longer have in touch with MS personnel who handles Microsoft Accounts, so you may want to Contact Support for further information about this.

EDIT: If you're not aware, phishing is a really big problem for email providers like Microsoft. Nowadays, scammers, or hackers can send out thousands of emails per day that look like it came from a legitimate company, asking you for credentials. You can browse through these blog posts here for these phishing emails, some of which are quite complicated that even experts may be victims of these phishing attempts.

https://blogs.msdn.microsoft.com/tzink/2016/11/23/where-email-authentication-is-not-so-great-at-stopping-phishing-random-it-phishing-scams/

https://blogs.technet.microsoft.com/mmpc/2017/01/26/phishers-unleash-simple-but-effective-social-engineering-techniques-using-pdf-attachments/

The second one is the scary one. Since these emails are not linking you to a suspicious website, email filters for junk or spam doesn't work. It contains a legitimate file that's not infected at all. But the file contains partial information, and you have to 'login' to open the rest. Note that none of these services require you to login, but not all of us are familiar with these services, thus following the steps on how to view the file by logging in with your own credentials.

All in all, Microsoft is protecting your accounts and everyone else's, whether you want it or not.
