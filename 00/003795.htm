a. user say:

Can you spell out

 (a) what type of Excel file you have (.xlsx, .xls)

 (b) how you are protecting it? 

As far as I know, if you password protect an Excel file, only Excel can open it - in other words, there is no way for any other application to open it directly, even though it is "read only". In your case, Access would have to start Excel, get Excel to open the file, and then get the data from the Excel application. And that's not actually how Access works - it makes either an ODBC or OLE DB connection which would only get the data via Excel if the sheet was actually already open in Excel.

However, if that is correct, if you managed to create an Access linked table from your Excel sheet,  you must have done it before you protected the worksheet/workbook. You could probably tell by trying to open the linked table and see if Access can actually open it.

Check your Blocked sender list:

1. In your Outlook.com Inbox, click the cog icon beside your Profile name.

5. In the Blocked senders list, select the name or domain (the part of an e-mail address that follows the @ sign, such as hotmail.com) that you now want to receive mail from, and then click "<< Remove from list".

Add the email address to your Safe senders list

1. In your Outlook.com Inbox, click the cog icon beside your Profile name.
2. Click "Options".
3. Under Junk email, click "Safe senders".
4. Click "Safe Senders".
5. In the text box, type in the email address or domain you’d like to always receive email from, and then click "Add to list >>".

Add the email address to your Safe mailing lists

1. In your Outlook.com Inbox, click the cog icon beside your Profile name.
2. Click "Options".
3. Under Junk email, click "Safe senders".
4. Click "Safe mailing lists".
5. In the text box, type in the email address or domain you’d like to always receive email from, and then click "Add to list >>".

Check your Email Rules

1. In your Outlook.com Inbox, click the cog icon beside your Profile name.
2. Click "Options".
3. Under Mail, click "Inbox and sweep rules".

Or

Note: Not all international characters are supported as filters.

Formula for Projected Costs

I am trying to come up with a formula that can give a projected cost based on percentage completed.

 

i.e.:  If I have a cumulative costs of $213,955.00 and am at 56.18% completed at this phase of the project, what would be the estimated/projected costs when completed at 100%.

 

 

 

 

joeu2004 say: Lise wrote:
If I have a cumulative costs of $213,955.00 and am at 56.18% completed at this phase of the project, what would be the estimated/projected costs when completed at 100%.

=213955 / 56.18%

[EDIT] Since we are talking about dollars-and-cents, it would be prudent to explicitly round in some way.  For example:

=ROUND(213955 / 56.18%, 2)
or
=ROUND(213955 / 56.18%, 0)

You might also consider ROUNDUP instead of ROUND.

[EDIT] Explanation....

Algebraically, you have:

total * 56.18% = 213955

So:

total = 213955 / 56.18%
