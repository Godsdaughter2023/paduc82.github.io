a. user say:

Well, you should be able to switch startup modes back and forth...

Whenever anybody "thinks" they want to switch from Selective startup to Normal startup, I ask them to
first look at the msconfig Services and Startup tabs.  Probably lots of things listed and probably many things unchecked which means they will not be starting/running on your system and it is reasonable to think that the fewer things you have starting/running the faster your system will be.  Since it could be possible to uncheck some things that are necessary, you have to be careful and keep track of things.

Then switch msconfig to Normal startup (don't click OK or Apply since you are just looking) and check the Services and Startup tabs again and holy cow - now everything is checked and everything is going to be starting/running the next time you restart your system.  So maybe Normal startup isn't such a good idea anymore.

Don't forget that msconfig is not a startup manager - msconfig is a troubleshooting tool that often gets used as a startup manager (I do it too :-) though).  So you can use msconfig as a quasi startup manager if you want too but some folks will overuse it (I think).

There used to be a problem with some HP printer installations that would cause that msconfig issue but I have not seen that in a long time.  The solution from HP:  reinstall your XP from scratch which is some advice that will not come from this keyboard (except once).  In spite of the error, the changes still seemed to work but the error was annoying, maybe they have since fixed it.

I figured there was a Norton involved somewhere and besides this known issue with Norton (hence the links I already knew about), Norton is a known CPU and memory hog so if you did not pay for it or are not being forced to use it in some corporate environment, you might consider replacing it with something with a smaller footprint and fewer issues - like Microsoft Security Essentials, at least to test.  If you go that route, just be sure you use the special Norton Removal Tools and not Add/Remove Programs.  You can always reinstall your Norton again later if you are compelled to do so, right?

Norton is also notorious for interfering with System Restore in such a way that they even wrote an article about it:

https://support.norton.com/sp/en/us/home/current/solutions/v51118464_EndUserProfile_en_us?product=home&version=1


It still will not hurt to run MBAM and SAS and you might be surprised at what it turns up that Norton missed.  No single AV seems to know about everything and when one reports your system is "clean" I just interpret that to mean that it just didn't find anything that is knows about, so it is prudent to use more than one reputable scanner to get the best coverage.
