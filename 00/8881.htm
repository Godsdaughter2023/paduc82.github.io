rohn007 say:
If removing references to other styles does not fix the problem then I would take it as a sign that there is at least some document corruption in the file.

First thing to try is repairing the file. Make a copy of it. Then from inside Word, use the file open dialog to open the copy. When you are opening it click on the down arrow beside Open button and select Open and Repair.

If that doesn't work I would transfer the text to a new file. There are a few options.

First is open a blank document, and copy the text from the bad file into the new file.  I suspect this won't work because it is likely to carry over the style corruption.

If that doesnt work next thing to try is SAVE AS to RTF format. That will keep most of the formatting. Then SAVE AS from the RTF back to DOC format.  Recreate the required styles.

IF that doesn't work, SAVE AS to TXT format to get rid of all formatting and graphics, then copy the text into a new DOC file. Recreate the required styles.


There are couple of options to recreate the styles. Either use the organizer to copy them from another file (Perferably not the damaged one) or simply manually recreate them.

rob koch say:

Malicious actors always seem to target those who spend time reading the popular news of the day, no matter what that might be.

As previous threads here have discussed, the malicious advertisers have found a method of creating their own ad networks that allows them to gain a good reputation, waiting until an opportunity such as this one comes along where they can expect large numbers of people to read certain articles.  Then they start randomly redirecting only those individuals deemed to be susceptible by their chosen criteria to the malicious ads along with the otherwise normal ads they've used to build their good reputation.

So in truth it's not MSN that's displaying the ad, it's one of the many advertising networks they've contracted who's displaying the ads from another advertising network that also delivers occasional redirections to specially designed websites that contain the malicious popups.

If this sounds messy and difficult to understand, it is and that's exactly why it works so well, since it's nearly impossible to trace or vet properly, since the entire online advertising system is designed as sets of ad networks with multiple layers selling slots to advertisers whom may act as agents for others as well.

This is why as I've stated multiple times in other threads, even the ad blocker programs are really just an afterthought trying to block the ads that are being displayed once they reach the PC.

The best method is to turn off the "Let apps use my advertising ID..." in the Windows 10 privacy settings, since this also resets the advertising ID itself, removing the connection to your previous browsing history for that PC and in theory the targeting of that specific PC as well.  Unfortunately, this also removes the ability for you to see the targeted advertising from otherwise "good" advertisers, so you won't see those timely ads about that car or other product you've been searching for recently.

There are other tracking systems within the browsers themselves like cookies and the option in some browsers to set "Send Do Not Track requests..." to sites you visit, which can also help to reduce what the advertising networks know about you.  However, if you also tend to frequent any of the major social networks, then all bets are off, since the typically draconian tracking policies of these sites tend to make those of the general ad networks look like amateurs.
