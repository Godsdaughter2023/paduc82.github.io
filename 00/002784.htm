joeu2004 say: Markstu50 wrote:
I'm getting an ex-boss of mine, an experienced estate tax and estate planning attorney, to try to unravel this mystery.

Regarding the 7.2 percent bracket for NY estate tax, if you look at the tax table, that marginal bracket covers a range of about $500k. The change in the taxable estate due to a decreae in the $1 million charitable deduction should be in the tens of thousands of dollars, well within that $500k range, so I'm virtually certain there will be no change in bracket.

I think it is wise for you to enlist the aid of an experienced (and hopefully knowledgeable) tax  specialist.

It does not matter how broad the 7.2% tax bracket.  What matters is how close to the lower end the numbers are.  My hypothetical numbers demonstrate that.  But my numbers might differ from yours, which are reality after all.

First, there are two marginal tax tables to consider in NYS ET-706:  Table A and Table B.

When we figure the NYS estate tax based on zero charitable deductions, we use $1.95M (less $60K for Table B) as the taxable estate.  That is in the 45% and 7.2% tax brackets respectively.

But note that $1.89M ($1.95M less $60K) is within $350K of the lower limit of the 7.2% tax bracket in Table B.

When we figure the NYS estate tax based on hypothetical charitable deductions of $755K, nearly the maximum allowable based on my hypothetical requirements (not reality), that is significantly more than $350K.  So it should not be surprising that we drop in tax brackets.

In fact, we use $1.195M (less $60K for Table B) as the taxable estate.  That is in the 41% and 6.4% brackets respectively.

As I repeatedly emphasize, my calculations are based on hypothetical constraints in the trust document, which I have no knowledge of.  They are provided for demonstration purposes only -- that is, to demonstrate the procedure of finding the maximum allowable charitable deductions.

I am not saying that the taxable estate will always drop out of the 7.2% (and 45%) tax brackets when you apply real numbers to the problem.  I am merely point out that it is theoretically possible that they might.

Therefore, I think I have demonstrated that it is wrong for you to work with only the 7.2% in figuring the change in estate tax due to the charitable deductions.  Instead, I think you need to work with the real marginal tax rate schedules.

PS:  While you are applying the actual trust document specifications to this problem, I hope you and your expert will pay close attention to Line 4 of Sched O for the federal Form 706.  I did not incorporate that into my Excel model of the estate tax calculations.  Frankly, I do not understand it; that is, it seems like a circular calculation, based on my uneducated interpretation.  I would be surprised if that is, in fact, the case.

echo s say:

Click off the edge of the slide so nothing's selected. Press and hold CTRL. Now click the chart.

 

Now you can nudge it with the keyboard arrows. (Don't shoot the messenger. In fact, you can thank me and another MVP that we can even do THAT much!)

 

If you want your charts to have absolute formatting, then right-click the data series, choose Format Data Series, go to the Fill tab. There, click Solid fill and chen click the Color paintbucket. Click More Colors at the bottom of that dropdown. Any color you apply from here is not themed -- so it won't change when you change themes or color theme sets.

 

Alternatively you can use chart templates. These also are not themed, so colors stay the same.
