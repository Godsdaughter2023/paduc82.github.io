<Configuration>

<Add SourcePath=”D:\Archive\Office2016\” OfficeClientEdition=”64” >

<Product ID=”proplusretail”>

<Language ID=”en-us” />  

<ExcludeApp ID=”Access” />

<ExcludeApp ID=”Publisher” />

<ExcludeApp ID=”Grove” />

<ExcludeApp ID=”Lync” />

<ExcludeApp ID=”skypeforbusiness” />

</Product>

</Add>

 

<Logging Name=”OfficeSetup.txt” Path=”%temp%” />

</Configuration>

NOTE: you can setup the deployment to include multiple languages ie:

<language ID= ‘en-us’ ‘es-es’  />

Using group policy, there are over 2100 settings you can control via the deployment tool

D:\Archive\Office2016\setup.exe /download configuration.xml

that will create the sub directories and download the files necessary for your desired configuration

5. Now, whether this is from the ISO or the downloaded files, run:

D:\Archive\Office2016\setup.exe /configure configuration.xml

bingo, bango, bongo, you’ve got a customized install.

Worksheet Function DATEDIF gives inconsistent resulsts. eg DATEDIF - Microsoft Community

smoothstream_bi say:

Worksheet Function DATEDIF gives inconsistent resulsts. eg DATEDIF between 28 Feb 2010 or 31 Jan 2010 and 30 Jun 2010 both give the result of 4 months.

Worksheet Function DATEDIF gives inconsistent resulsts. eg DATEDIF between 28 Feb 2010 or 31 Jan 2010 and 30 Jun 2010 both give the result of 4 months.

What is the simplest workaround?

hansv mvp say:

The result isn't "wrong", even though it may not be what you want.

I think you'll agree that the difference between 16 Jan 2010 to 15 Jun 2010 is not yet 5 months, so DATEDIF correctly returns 4.

The same holds for 30 Jan 2010 and 29 Jun 2010.

So why not for 31 Jan 2010 and 30 Jun 2010? Just because they are the last day of the month?

Added later:

If you want to make an exception for the last day of the month, use this (with the first date in A1 and the second date in B1):

=DATEDIF(A1,B1,"m")+(A1=EOMONTH(A1,0))*(B1=EOMONTH(B1,0))*(DAY(A1)>DAY(B1))

joeu2004 say:

HansV wrote:

I think you'll agree that the difference between 16 Jan 2010 to 15 Jun 2010 is not yet 5 months, so DATEDIF correctly returns 4.
[....]
So why not for 31 Jan 2010 and 30 Jun 2010? Just because they are the last day of the month?

Actually, yes.  Note that EDATE(DATE(2010,1,31),5) is 30 Jun 2010.

These anomalies are not necessarily "defects" per se in either DATEDIF or EDATE.  They are simply two different perspectives on date computation.

In legal and financial circles, in the US at least, the monthly anniversary of an initial date is the same date of each anniversary month.  But if the anniversary month does not include that date, the end of the anniversary month is used.

So, for example, 30 Jun 2010 is 5 months after both 30 Jan and 31 Jan 2010.  28 Feb 2009 is 12 months after both 28 Feb and 29 Feb 2008.


hansv mvp say:

Thanks. I think the formula from my previous reply should be

=DATEDIF(A1,B1,"m")+(B1=EOMONTH(B1,0))*(DAY(A1)>DAY(B1))

to handle February correctly.

joeu2004 say: Smoothstream_BI wrote:
Worksheet Function DATEDIF gives inconsistent resulsts. eg DATEDIF between 28 Feb 2010 or 31 Jan 2010 and 30 Jun 2010 both give the result of 4 months.  What is the simplest workaround?

Simplest?   I don't know.  But one way might be:

=DATEDIF(A1,A2,"m")+(EDATE(A1,DATEDIF(A1,A2,"m")+1)=A2)

That assumes the start date is in A1 and end date is in A2.

Arguably, it would be "simpler" (certainly more efficient) to compute DATEDIF one time in a helper cell, then reference the helper cell twice in the formula above.

Caveat:  I have not fully (re)tested that formula.  I will have more time later.

PS:  Some MVPs point out that DATEDIF has become unreliable for one particular option ("md"?) starting in a particular XL2007 SP, I think.  Someone has provided a VBA alternative.  But as I recall, it still computes months the same way that DATEDIF does.  So I think the correction above will still be needed, if/when that person posts his standard recommendation not to use DATEDIF at all.  Just a heads-up.
