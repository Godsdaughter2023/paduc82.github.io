chacking say:

@Jim Gordon:

You ever used a mobile device (or the "Outlook Mail & Calendar" app on Win10) to sign into a Google or Yahoo mail account? Instead of just prompting you for a username and password, you get taken to a special web page (often displayed inside the email client app, but still, hosted by the email service provider) and on that page, you will enter your credentials; after doing so, the page will ask if you want to trust <name of email client> to access your emails and such. If you approve this access, *then* the page closes, returning you to the app.

This is more secure than just using password auth from the app every time, because the app uses a unique authentication token instead of a human-memorable password, and the token is *only* used by that app - every client gets its own token - so you can tell which clients are accessing your account and, if needed, cut one off without breaking anything. The app doesn't need to store your password (and risk leaking it if an attacker steals your device), and indeed the app need not ever *see* the password; it's entered into a web view and sent directly to the service provider.

Every non-trivial email client I know of supports OAuth for at least Google (and usually Yahoo), *EXCEPT* for (so far as I know) Microsoft Office Outlook. It's quite surprising (and kind of shameful) for a professional product to not support this, thus the questions in the hope that there's some less-than-obvious way to use OAuth built into Outlook.

(For the record, I'm on Windows, not Mac, but I can't find any way to use OAuth there either.)
