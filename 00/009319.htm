myall_blues say:

  1. How does it know to remain anchored at AF2 after the formatting is swapped from standard to R1C1 type formatting?
  2. What does the "RC[32] mean in the range end-point and comparison value? (Standard formatting has it as AF2 ... AF15236 for both the range end-point and the comparison value.)
  3. Am I looking at FALSE-POSITIVES in the column after the formula is replicated to the bottom of the table or is the R1C1 format of the formula actually working and valid results? (The results actually appear valid and correct, though checking 15K of rows is way out of my time available window.)

1. In R1C1 style, the square brackets ( [ and ] ) determine the difference between absolute cell references ($A$1 in normal form) and relative cell references (A1). Square brackets make it a relative cell reference. Thus in your formula once converted, R2C36 (without square brackets) refers explicitly to row 2, column 36 (AF2 presumably).

2. Similarly, RC[32] is a relative cell reference and refers to  the cell that is in the same row as the current formula, and 32 columns to the right.

3. No - it should work as expected. If it was correct and valid as an A1 style, then it will work as an R1C1 style.

See also the explanation at this link: https://tduhameau.wordpress.com/2012/09/27/the-beauty-of-the-r1c1-reference-style/

International access to my Hotmail/Outlook account - Microsoft Community

johnbphd say:

International access to my Hotmail/Outlook account

I recently went to the UK and Hotmail/Outlook did not allow access to my account without having an active cell phone for confirmation.  

I will soon be going to Romania and Spain. How can I ensure I will have access to my Outlook email account while out of the USA?

Thanks.

zigzag3143 (ms -mvp) say:
We have implemented a security update called Familiar location protection to keep our customers’ accounts secure. The message "You haven't signed in from this location recently." will occur if you are signing in from a location more than 300 miles from your normal location. The Microsoft account service will prompt you for an additional challenge to complete the log in. Codes can be the date of birth, or receiving a code sent to your SMS number or alternate email if configured.
 

To prevent this you will need to configure your computer/mobile device as a trusted device 

Given the new security measures, Account Settings requires an extra security, through the use of a security code.  Also known as 2 step authentication

If you mark your computer, phone or tablet as a Trusted Device, you won't be asked for any codes when you access these settings in the future.  To do this

1. Login your account here.

2.  Under Security & Privacy  Click on "Manage Advanced Security" 

3. You will see the "protect your account" page. Select any option to get a code, and request for it.

4. You will see a checkbox that says : "I sign in frequently on this device. Don't ask me for a code". Tick the checkbox, and enter the code.

Your computer/device is now Trusted. Whenever you use your account on that specific computer/device, you will no longer asked for a code.
