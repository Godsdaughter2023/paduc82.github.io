Things to take away from this:

1) The 'age' of the device drivers do not matter - functionality of the devices does.  If everything is working to your liking/as it always has/properly with a given device and you don't need something more out of it (some feature of the device only the newer driver grants access to, some tweak that allows for a bit more performance out of said device, etc.) - do not worry about device drivers.

2) If you need to update a device driver - go to the source.  First try the assembler (OEM) of the system - in this case Dell.  OEMs like that almost always have a web page devoted to the support (and driver downloads for) of their systems for years after they actually stop supporting them for free - leaving the device drivers and such up there for people to obtain.  The OptiPlex 380 is a system I think came out in February of 2010 - so it is 5 years old - Dell supports their OptiPlex lines for at least 5 years in every case I know of (this is not about *warranty*, just about a product line and keeping it 'alive'.) Second try the manufacturer(s) of the individual components.  Intel chipset?  nVidia video card?  Realtek sound device?  Broadcom network card?  Each of those manufacturers do, although some will tell you to go to the OEM site, often have device drivers (sometimes newer than the OEMs) for their products.  Do not go with the third-party solution.  If it was plausible for anyone to successfully track all the hardware/software combinations out there - it would have been down to a single product long ago. heh

3) Take a system image before you start ANYTHING like this.  Things can (and often do) go wrong and computer software, thank goodness, is one of the few places where it is entirely possible and fairly simplistic to 'go back in time when everything was working' - all thanks to backups/system images.  Sure - it takes a little effort and some external drive space someplace, but the hours of frustration it saves is WELL worth the effort.

4) Patience and step-by-step effort pays off.  Don't go installing all the new drivers you can find all at one time on a system you haven't had experience with prior.  Install one, reboot, feel it out and make sure it didn't break anything and see if the difference you hoped for exists.  Once you are sure everything seems fine - do the next... then the next, etc.  I'm not talking hours of testing - just a nice reboot or two with some 'normal opening and closing of common applications for you' before continuing on.

 

 

I take it - from what I have read and read between the lines - you merely wanted to make sure this system was 'tuned up' and working in 'peak condition'; there wasn't anything 'wrong' with it (the issue with the 'not genuine' message didn't exist prior to the device driver updates...)?

 

Come back once you get things going and/or if something doesn't work out.  Sometimes there are ways out of a difficult situation you just aren't seeing while in the midst of it - and most people here are more than willing to share!

OEM version of Windows is tied to the motherboard (computer) that it was first installed onto.  Acer has provided a "bios locked" version of Windows that will self activate using the information in the computer's bios.  When you changed the motherboard you also changed the bios and the operating system can no longer activate.
 
With the different "hardware" on the new motherboard the boot programs can't find the proper drivers for the new hardware and that is why you are having a failed boot.
 
The only option is to purchase a retail FULL install operating system, OEM or retail, (you technically have no previous OS to upgrade now as that license died with the motherboard) and do a clean install.  You need to also download and install the motherboard chipset drivers (unless supplied on a disk with the motherboard) as well as drivers for all the peripheral equipment for the operating system you decide on purchasing.
