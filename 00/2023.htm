Sub Example_Intersect()
  Dim R As Range, C As Range
  Dim Msg As String
  If Not TypeOf Selection Is Range Then
    MsgBox "You did not select a range"
  Else
    Set R = Intersect(Selection, Rows(10))

    If R Is Nothing Then
      Msg = Msg & "Your selection intersects not Row 10"
    Else
      Msg = Msg & "Your selection intersects Row 10 at " & R.Address(0, 0)

Private Sub Worksheet_Change(ByVal Target As Range)
  Dim R As Range, C As Range
  Dim FirstDate As Date, LastDate As Date
  Dim StartDate As Date, EndDate As Date
  Dim ClearLine As Boolean
  'If nothing of our interest is changed we're done
  Set Target = Intersect(Target, Range("F6:AQ20,D6:D20,E6:E20"))
  If Target Is Nothing Then Exit Sub
  'Don't flicker with the screen
  Application.ScreenUpdating = False
  'Walk trough the first column
  For Each R In Range("F6:F20")
    'Get the dates from this row
    FirstDate = R
    LastDate = Range("AQ" & R.Row)

    'Walk trough the dates
    For Each C In Range("D6:D20")
      'Get the start date from column D
      StartDate = C
      'Get the end date from column E same row as in Column D
      EndDate = C.Offset(0, 1)
      'Create the ganttline
      CreateGanttLine Range(R, Range("AQ" & R.Row)), _
          StartDate, EndDate, FirstDate, LastDate, C.Interior.ColorIndex, ClearLine
      'Overwrite the colored cells next time
      ClearLine = False
    Next
  Next
  'Show the result
  Application.ScreenUpdating = True
End Sub

rob koch say:

Ward.[Xmodem],

Are you really that Ward (C.) who originally attended a small college in WI?  If so, I went to the same school a few years later and also used the software you developed with my IMSAI 8080 and various modems for many years.

As for your issue with the SSD writes by Defender, I'd also have been concerned when seeing such numbers.  But after reading the following thread, especially the excerpt below, I'm much less concerned about my own SSD now.

https://social.technet.microsoft.com/Forums/en-US/c85722e0-126c-4180-b391-415b3523c55d/any-news-about-optimization-for-ssd-in-windows-10?forum=WinPreview2014General

"Virtually all drives provide SMART monitoring of how much data has been written, as well as projected lifespan, and information on expected program/erase cycles is available online. 

The Samsung 850 Pro, for example, should last 6,000 program/erase cycles.  Being conservative and assuming a really bad 3:1 write amplification (which is an overestimate), imagine being able to write the entire SSD and erase it 2,000 times.

For a single 512 GB SSD, that would be 1 terabyte of write load per day for almost 3 years.  Remember, you can read it as many times as you like; NAND only wears on erase cycles, which happen during re-writes.

Most people do not write anywhere near 1 terabyte of data per day.  I use my system heavily and in my case (including it being an SVN server) and I don't even reach 20% of that.  I'm spreading the write load across 4 drives with the RAID array so the load on any one device is divided further."

What I gather is that along with the fact that my own SanDISK SSD Dashboard utility indicates no issues, the SSD drive itself also contains significantly more hidden capacity than older drives, reducing both the risk of a "full" drive capacity causing the types of issues discussed by several in that thread and the premature failure of the drive due to heavy write activity.

Since it appears that your own SSD drive might be any of a few different models used in that system, you'd need to determine the exact drive to know whether it's new enough to provide this same sort of capacity and protection from failures.

However, if it's at least a relatively recent model, according to the above the quantity of writing necessary to "wear out" a drive is much larger than I'd even thought, so you may be unnecessarily concerned with something that's lees likely than the other more typical problems that mobile devices experience.  For example, it's probably more likely that you'll have keyboard, touchpad or other physical issues due to either spilling liquid or dropping items on it, or even the laptop itself on a hard surface.

In my own case, I'd killed two regular hard drives in this same laptop, as well as spilled a sugary liquid that caused keys and the touchpad to stick or operate badly.  Though I managed to fix the keyboard by flushing it with water, I finally chose to switch to the SSD to avoid losing a future HDD to the same abusive way I tend to handle the system during operation.

So I really think you may be worrying about nothing here and should simply concentrate on the things you can control like the settings for defrag and possibly others.  Though I see another poster in that same thread suggesting to disable both hibernation and page file, I see little reason for this since the number of times these will actually write significant quantities to disk is relatively small.  As the poster I quoted above also mentioned, "most have an inflated idea of how much data their systems write."

I've actually stopped using sleep or other "quick start" power modes when shutting down for the night though, since unless I'm going to use the same open screens later, the system actually starts faster from a full shut down.

Once you figure out which SSD model you have, you might want to get the manufacturers utility if you haven't already in order to see whether it's reporting any lifespan or temperature issues.  Mine is still indicating 100% life remaining and I'd allowed it to perform several defrag operations over the last few months with Windows 8.1 before I realized this wasn't automatically disabled.  Personally, I suspect the $65 - 256GB SSD drive will far outlast the laptop itself.
