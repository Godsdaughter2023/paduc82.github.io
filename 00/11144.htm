Your old documents are just fine. They are not "blocked." Your computer just doesn't remember what to do with them. If you start Word through the Start menu, you should be able to open and edit any of them.

When you installed the new version of Word you told Windows you wanted to use that program for Word documents. It "registered" as the application for your computer to use to open and edit all Word documents. When you uninstalled the new one, the uninstaller is not smart enough to fix this. It knows you uninstalled Word. It does not know that you still have your previous version of Word. You need to re-register your version of Word with your computer.

Hold down the Windows key and press R. (then release both)

This will open a small box you can type in. Paste the following line in that box and press Enter:

winword.exe /r

Note the space before the /.

What happens when you do that?

If it can't find the program, try the following (with quotation marks) in the Run box for Word 97:

"C:\Program Files (x86)\Microsoft Office\Office\winword.exe /r"

If it can't find the program, your previous version of Office may have been uninstalled and you would have to reinstall it.

MS Project as a Contractor - Microsoft Community

scott_6929 say:

MS Project as a Contractor

I am familiar with MS project but ran into a new challenge that I'm struggling with.  One of the business I'm working with provides their services for a fixed fee.  All of the "resources" have different hourly rates.  The client will only ever pay that fixed fee but I still need to add up the resources hours x their rate to figure out if money was lost or made on that fixed fee.  In addition, I'm trying to determine how many hours can be spent on the project before they go over the fixed fee....and start to loose money.  Thanks in advance! 

trevor rabey say:

So, the business that you are working with is a subcontractor with their own resources, and they perform a number of the tasks in your project?

And the fixed fee is what it costs you for those tasks to be performed, and you pass this on to your client "at cost" ie no margin?

Do you know who the resources are, and each of their rates, and which tasks each resource will be assigned to?

If so, then just list the resources in the resource sheet with their rates and assign them to tasks as if they were your resources.

However, it may be that you don't know who they are or what their rates are, or which tasks they will be assigned to.

In that case, you just have to estimate it all.

You will need to create individual generic resources like "sub1", "sub2" etc, because you are going to assign whoever you think will perform each task to the appropriate task.

Alternatively (lazier), just have a resource called "subcontractor" and assign that resource to all of the tasks at various units so that subcontractor assigned at 100% represents one person, subcontractor assigned at 200% represents two persons etc. However, this shortcut comes at a cost because you will end up with over-allocation but will not be able to level.

As a first step, I would spread the fixed fee uniformly across all of their tasks, as a fixed cost (see the cost table), so that the cost accrues to the tasks and the project without any resource assignments.

This would establish an average cost for each task.

Whether that is an "accurate" estimated cost for each task will depend on the nature of the tasks.

Then these fixed costs could be adjusted up or down depending on the relative estimate of the cost of each task, until you are happy that you have established an approximate "budget" for each task.

Then I would assign whatever I thought the resources would be for the first task until the cost of the resources was the same as the budget for the task (so the task cost apparently doubles) and then zero out the fixed cost.

Do this for each task until the fixed costs have all been replaced with resource costs.

You then have a combination of tasks, task durations, resource assignments, and resource rates, all of which are estimates but at least they add up to this sacred target fixed fee.

If any of the inputs are changed anywhere, the change will have to be compensated for elsewhere to maintain the budget. If one task gets longer, another will have to get shorter, or the assignments will have to be adjusted and/or the rates will have to be adjusted.

Any help so far?

trevor rabey say:

Scott,

To answer your particular question about "Is there a way to "level" the project(by resource) to make it stay within budget", I guess the answer is no, because that is the part where the human being who is making the decisions comes in.

The whole point of breaking the project into specific tasks and assigning specific resources to each of them is so that you can apply the necessary degree of oversight and control that you are trying to achieve.

If you cannot break the project into specific tasks and assign specific resources to each of them, then you simply will not have that control.

What you seem to have at the moment is a bunch of guys doing a bunch of tasks, and you will only find out that it all cost more than you thought it would cost after the tasks have been done, or, even worse, after all of the hours have been burned but the tasks have not all been done.

Essentially, you don't know (precisely) what has to be done or who will be doing it, or rather which specific people will do which specific things, all of which is the definition of not having a plan. The direct consequence of not having a plan is that you also don't know how long it will take or how much it will cost. You also don't have a cash flow forecast which would show how the forecast cost is, or will be, distributed over the duration of the project. You also don't know if what you are trying to achieve is feasible given the limits on the available resources and the budget limit.

If you go into it with the intention of shuffling people around as you go along, isn't this the same thing as choosing to plan the project as you go along rather than in advance?

It is difficult to see how you can on the one hand embark on the project with this intention, and without answers to the questions about what, when, who, how, how long, how much, and how much when, and on the other hand expect to have much control. It might work. Much human endeavor is undertaken like this and it does produce results, as well as a lot of failure. But better planning up front will shift the odds in your favour.

I would ignore the $100K, and instead work out what it will cost to achieve what has to be done, and then see if if it is more or less than $100K, which is the real question here.

I don't think you should be aiming to track how close you are getting to the budget.

I think you should design the project plan so that you can forecast or predict now how close you will be to the cumulative budget up to each review point (status date).
