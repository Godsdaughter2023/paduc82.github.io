rcpeka say: I recently replaced my Wi-Fi router, then found 'RalinkLinuxClient' (two entries) in my Windows 10 'Network' window.  After a while, they disappear from the list.  They will return if the list is refreshed.  Right-click/properties shows model number RT2860, manufactured by Ralink Technology (company no longer exists), and a MAC address, which is registered to Andon Health Co. (China manufacturer).
>
In the 'Network' window, right-click on the 'Name' at the top of the list, and turn on 'Discovery Method' - the 'RalinkLinuxClient' is found with the 'WCN' method.  'WCN' indicates 'Windows Connect Now' which is Microsoft's version of the Wireless Protected Setup (WPS) protocol. This is used to configure Wireless LAN settings for an Access Point (AP) or a Wireless Device.  In my case, two appear (one each for the 2.4GHz and 5GHz band?).
>
I turned WPS off in my router, as I have no use for it.  To keep the 'RalinkLinuxClient' from being found by my PC, I disabled the 'WCNCSVC' service (listed as 'Windows Connect Now - Config Registrar').  To disable this service, right-click on 'This PC' or 'My PC', select 'Manage', 'Services and Application', 'Services', then right-click on the 'Windows Connect Now - Config Registrar' entry, pick Properties and set the startup type to 'Disable'.
>
I'll speculate that many devices (routers/phones/TVs/etc.) have the 'RalinkLinuxClient' present to enable the MAC address (hardware/chip level) communication that may be needed to establish IP (software level) communication.  Even if the device does not have the actual Ralink chip, it may have the same programming.  Looking up the MAC address may help you to find the manufacturer (or maybe not help, if the nominal device manufacturer has someone else build the unit).  I don't think the 'WCN' devices are a security risk, but I don't actually know enought to bet on it - so, for myself, untill I have need of it, I'll leave the service disabled.
>
Good Luck!
