jllatham say:

There's no definitive answer to the question.  You haven't described the type of data that you're working with and that would be a big consideration.  Some types of data are just natural candidates for storage in a relational database, other data can be a royal pain to try to work with in an RDB.

The key to building a successful RDB is in your design of the tables and their relationships to one another.  One of the basic goals of an RDB is to enter any given data item as few times as possible.  A somewhat trivial example (but one I actually put together in the real world for an insurance company): a physician's directory for an insurance company.  Each physician may have more than one office and more than one specialty; and each office may have more than one phone number.  With an RDB you can enter the physician's name one time, link that to a table listing their specialties, link the name to an office location, and link the office location to a phone numbers table.  From this structure you can quickly query to list the physicians: by name, city, state or specialty.  In Excel you'd very likely have a separate row devoted to at least the combination of specialties and office locations.  And with Excel, if you hadn't planned ahead, then when a physician added a new specialty, or new clinic/office location you might be revising your spreadsheet layout and any underlying analysis formulas or automated features.  With the RDB, you'd simply add in the new specialty or clinic/office location and all of your queries would still work.

On the other hand, if you have some data that consists of pretty much unrelated, individual and unique entries, then Excel is probably the easiest and quickest tool to track them.  Example: I try to track what discussions I participate in various web-sites such as this one.  Each one is pretty much a stand-alone entry and would require one record in an RDB, and does occupy one row on an Excel sheet.  With Excel a little use of auto-filtering quickly gives me "related" records with regards to where I posted, or the general concept/problem related to that discussion.

If you do decide that it would be beneficial to use Access to store your data, sit down and do some planning for its design to give you a tables structure that will be efficient and work for you.  Trust me, this is the critical stage of design for the database because everything else is totally dependent on the table structures and relationships.  If you find out late in the game that you've screwed that up, then you get to rework pretty much everything after revising the tables.

Excel and Access make a great team.  You can actually use Excel as the user interface (front end) to the database very easily through queries or VBA coding.  By using Excel to extract datasets from the database you can build up spreadsheets from the extracted

You might find one pamphlet I put together helpful in your decision making.  I said the key to a successful RDB setup is structuring your tables, and a good part of doing that is having some grasp of data normalizing.  If you'll go to this page:
http://www.jlathamsite.com/LearningPage.htm and look toward the bottom you'll see a couple of links related to Access.  One is to a .pdf file that attempts to teach you a little about what data normalizing is and how to evaluate your data using it to set up your table structures.  The second link is to an Access file that goes along with the .pdf file so you can actually see a small database set up based on the teachings of the pamphlet.
