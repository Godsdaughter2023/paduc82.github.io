dhtampa say:

Okay I got this working across all my platforms now.  Here are the complete steps for anyone else who happens across this thread:

1) Pre-req's

some are supported by default, while others specific to Windows computers, are not.  I was able to connect via Outlook 2016 on Mac, and Outlook on iOS tablet and phone after enabling MFA on a user, did not require anything additional on the 365 Exchange admin side.

A second note; if you're on Outlook 2013, you need a registry key; documented at the bottom of this post.

2) Log in via PowerShell

You must connect a PowerShell instance to Exchange Online.  If you've already enabled MFA on your 365 admin account like I had, you need an additional Power Shell module to permit MFA connections to Exchange Online.  I'll break this out into two login sequences for MFA and non-MFA:

[Option 1, for 365 admin accounts that do not yet have MFA enabled]

Execute the following via power shell:

  • Set-ExecutionPolicy RemoteSigned
  • $UserCredential = Get-Credential

    At this point it should pop up a login and password dialog box to collect your 365 user/pass info
  • $Session = New-PSSession -ConfigurationName Microsoft.Exchange -ConnectionUri https://outlook.office365.com/powershell-liveid/ -Credential $UserCredential -Authentication Basic -AllowRedirection
  • Import-PSSession $Session

[Option 2, for 365 admin accounts that have MFA enabled]

You will require an MFA-enabled PowerShell module, as described on the following page:

https://docs.microsoft.com/en-us/powershell/exchange/exchange-online/connect-to-exchange-online-powershell/mfa-connect-to-exchange-online-powershell?view=exchange-ps

Since those steps and screen shots no longer match up with the current look and feel, here's where to find the module:

  • Log into your 365 admin account; https://portal.office.com/adminportal/home
  • At the very bottom left of the menu, click "Admin centers" and go into Exchange admin center:
  • Again, bottom left of the menu, click 'hybrid' and then you'll be able to download the Exchange Online PowerShell Module with support for MFA.
  • Run the install; it will create an icon on your desktop for launching the MFA-enabled PowerShell.

Launch a PowerShell session using that desktop icon for an MFA-enabled shell, execute the following, replacing the myadminuser... stuff with one of your organization's 365 admin users:

Connect-EXOPSSession -UserPrincipalName *** Email address is removed for privacy ***

It should pop up the familiar 365 login sequence including MFA credential box.  Once you authenticate, it will download hundreds of commands, so this could take a couple minutes on a slow connection, just let it work.

3) Enable Oauth profiles for Office 365

Next, and this is the part that was creating the problem for my Outlook 2016 on Windows 10 devices; you must enable the Oauth profiles feature for Office 365, which is not on by default, or at least was not for my organization (set up in January 2016).  Here's the article where I found this:

https://blogs.technet.microsoft.com/ivfranji/authentication-enabling-modern-auth-oauth2/

Here's all you need to do, via your connected PowerShell session:

Set-OrganizationConfig -OAuth2ClientProfileEnabled $true

4) Enable MFA on your Office 365 users

  • Log into your 365 admin portal as an admin user: https://portal.office.com/adminportal/home#/homepage
  • Click Active Users on your dashboard.
  • Click one of your users, doesn't matter which one
  • Click Manage multi-factor authentication
  • You'll now get a list of all of your sign-in allowed users.  Pick the ones you want, then set MFA to enabled.
  • Those users will need to log into the 365 portal now to complete the setup as it's specific to them.  You can also delete all their app passwords at this level if you want, and even force them to use MFA.

Only note to add about the above is that the Microsoft authenticator is really nothing more than a TOTP code, and you can use any TOTP-compliant generator to generate and store your codes.  So, the users logging in and setting up MFA, if they choose the app generator, as opposed to phone call or text, can use their favorite TOTP app, such as Google Authenticator, Authy, etc.

5) Notes


That was it for me.  After doing the above, I removed and re-added my Exchange Online account from Outlook 2016 on Win 10 and it immediately prompted for the familiar two factor info and things worked great from that point on.  My Mac and iOS devices were unaffected by the above change.

If you're using Outlook 2013, you need to add a registry key described here:

https://blogs.technet.microsoft.com/ivfranji/authentication-enabling-modern-auth-oauth2/

If you're on 2016, you do not need to do that even though they key is not present; it enables MFA / OAuth2 by default.
