  • Se nada acontecer quando você pressionar a tecla do logotipo do Windows  + G, verifique as configurações da Barra de jogo. Abra o aplicativo  + G, verifique as configurações da Barra de jogo. Abra o aplicativo Xbox e selecione Configurações  > Jogos e verifique se Gravar clipes de jogos, capturas de tela e transmissão usando a Barra de jogo está Ligado.

  • Se a Barra de jogo não aparecer para um jogo em tela inteira, tente atalhos de teclado: pressione a tecla do logotipo do Windows  + Alt + R para começar a gravar um clipe, pressione-o novamente para parar. Você verá a tela piscar quando a gravação começar e terminar.

Simplesmente a barra a barra de jogo volta estar desligada.

zacabeb say:

None of the console makers officially endorse third party emulation as far as I know. They may just be lax about it since piracy of current generation content and the second hand market for the same content are their main headaches. They certainly didn't release source code or in depth hardware specs of older systems to the public.

Microsoft is also dependent on other companies' technology and patents when it comes to the version one Xbox. For instance, even though it was DirectX-based, some of the graphics APIs are dependent on functionality that was provided by nVidia in the XGPU, and which Microsoft had to relicense from them for the limited backwards compatibility on the Xbox 360.

On top of that, some of the functionality on the Xbox has been carried over to the Xbox 360 - and even though it's been refined a lot there, would expose code to the public that Microsoft doesn't want exposed. The Xbox was also had the most extensive Internet support of its generation, meaning there is a lot of DRM functionality they cannot expose and which games were fully dependent on.

And finally, even if they were to support emulation they could only endorse running games off the original discs or as Games on Demand titles. Any other means of obtaining the games to actually run under the emulator would, suffice it to say, not be in Microsoft's interest to support.

andre da costa say:
1. Disable any security software before attempting to upgrade or do a clean install.
2. Make sure your computer is updated (devices and applications)
3. Disconnect any external devices before installing.
4. Check your hard disk for any errors:
Click Start
Type: CMD, from the results, right click CMD
Click 'Run as Administrator'
At the Command Prompt, type: chkdsk /r
When you restart your system, your computer will be scanned for errors and attempts will be made to correct them.

Also, run the Windows 7 Upgrade Advisor, it will scan your system and provide a report telling you if your PC can run Windows 7 and if there are any known compatibility issues with your programs or hardware.
