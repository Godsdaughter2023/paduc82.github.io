loveuhateu say: Just take these precautions -

Features that use Bluetooth technology, or allowing such features to run in the background while using other features, increase the demand on battery power. Turn Bluetooth technology off when you do not need it.
 
Features that use Wireless LAN (WLAN), or allowing such features to run in the background while using other features, increase the demand on battery power. WLAN on your Nokia device switches off when you are not trying to connect, not connected to an access point, or not scanning for available networks. To further reduce battery consumption, you can specify that your device does not scan, or scans less often, for available networks in the background. 

If you have set Packet data connection to When available in connection settings, and there is no packet data coverage (GPRS), the device periodically tries to establish a packet data connection. To prolong the operating time of your device, select Packet data connection > When needed. 

The Maps application downloads new map information when you scroll to new areas on the map, which increases the demand on battery power. You can prevent the automatic download of new maps. 

If the signal strength of the cellular network varies much in your area, your device must scan for the available network repeatedly. This increases the demand on battery power.

If the Network mode is set to dual mode in the network settings, the device searches for the UMTS network. To use only the GSM network, select Network mode > GSM. 

The backlight of the display increases the demand on battery power. In the display settings, you can change the time-out after which the backlight is switched off, and adjust the light sensor that observes lighting conditions and adjusts the display brightness. 

Leaving applications running in the background increases the demand on battery power. Close application you do not use.

chulley say:

I had nearly endless trouble with this, but finally got it to work.

 

Firstly, a little overview. There are two things that have to be done before the LD-3W is available and connected. The first is to pair the device, and the second is to connect to the serial port that gets defined. The second step is what starts up the little blue middle light, and assuming the device can see the sky, eventually locks in and lights up the little white one too.

 

I am using Windows XP service pack 3 and the standard Microsoft Bluetooth stack.

 

Opening bluetooth places, devices etc, I was able to search for and find the LD-3W with no problems. Pairing with it was just as easy, with the "0000" pair code. But hte darn thing just sat there, with the little green light blinking and nothing else.

 

Reading the web gave little help, apart from finding 3,000,000 sites offtering to sell me one.

 

I *think* part of the problem may be, it takes the bluetooth adaptors and drivers time to find things. The following may help: wait a few minutes after connecting, then try to discover services. Try rebooting with the device still paired. Open the Bluetooth devices, look for your LD-3w, right click and select properties, then services. If you have "this device offers the following services - serial port", things may be going well. Tick the box, and it may spring to life.

 

If not, various anecdotal evidence suggests the problem is Microsoft's Bluetooth stack. I downloaded the Bluesoleil stack, version 6.4, from their website and installed that. You get a free 24 day trial period. Searching for devices in there, I again found my LD-3W. I tried all the connect stuff, but no joy. Then, I unpaired it, deleted it and searched for it again. This time, when I found it, on right click, it said I had a serial port service. Trying to activate this said I had to reboot again, which I did, and hallelulia the thing worked as expected. I used Google Maps with GPS tracker as a test bed.

 

Knowing how these things go, I then uninstalled Bluesoleil, rebooted and tried the default Microsoft stack. This time, when I found the device, the serial port was present and I can use it. So, Bluesoleil has left some settings behind and all is well.

 

Hope this helps!

ChrisH

chulley say:

... follow-up

 

Unfortunately, on the next reboot, everything was completely stuffed up! I desperately tried to reinstall stuff, but no joy. I had to do a system restore. So, unless you're planning to keep to Bluesoleil 6.4 stack and pay for it, don't do the above!

 

After the restore, I did however finally get the LD-3W working with the default Microsoft Bluetooth stack and Google Maps with GPS tracker. Having discovered and paired the device, I started the app. The problem is, you can't select the port (in my case, COM16, the LD-3W outgoing port, which you can find from Bluetooth places).

 

There was one listed in the dropdown as COM16c, but that didn't work. In desperation, first I set everything else up right in the software (NMEI protocol, baud rate 38,400), saved it, closed it, fired up regedit, found gps (HKEY_CURRENT_USER\Software\gps) and changed the GPSPortName to COM16. Then on restart, ... it worked!

 

So if you have a similar problem with other software, I recommend that - manually overide the COM port in either the registry or the config file, and see how it goes. Or, keep the Bluesoleil stack.

 

Hope this helps again,

ChrisH
