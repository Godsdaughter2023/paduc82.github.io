cmdrkeene say:

The logo is stored in the Temporary Internet Files and used by the icon cache.  If either of these are cleared for any reason (cleanup, new installation, even a normal system reboot), the icon could be lost, resulting in the shortcut displaying simply the generic "webpage shortcut" icon you describe (the earth icon).

Other browsers that store shortcuts with a different method don't usually suffer this issue.  For example, Google Chrome tends to display the correct icon from the website (known as the favicon for "favorite's icon") even after the cache is cleared.

Good news, however:  Microsoft fixed this icon issue with Internet Explorer 9, which I am surprised to say is a great improvement from IE8.  If you are interested in the upgrade, you can get it from http://windows.microsoft.com/ie9. 

 

 

Shawn B Keene | Microsoft Community Contributor

  • Are there accounts showing other than the one you're signed in as and the Guest account?  If so, are they "Standard user" accounts or "Administrator" accounts?
  • Are there any accounts showing other than the one you're signed into and the "Guest" account?

STEP 2:  Create a new account.  I'm making two assumptions here:  (1) the account you're signed in as is an "Administrator" account and (2) there are only that account and the Guest account

  • Click the link below the list of accounts to "Create a new account"
  • On the next screen, give the new account a name
  • Choose either "Standard user" or "Administrator."  The usual recommendation is to use a "Standard user" account for your every day use.  This provides some protection against malware because if the malware attempts to run under your account, it will be limited in the damage it can do.  In practice, I suspect that most Windows 7 users ignore this advice and use an "Administrator" account as their basic account.
  • Complete the process of account creation.

STEP 3:  Create an "Administrator" account

  • If in Step 2 you chose to create your every day account as a "Standard user" account, you need to create a separate "Administrator" account to perform tasks that only computer administrators are permitted to perform.  This includes things like creating new accounts, installing new programs, installing printers, and several other "system" tasks.
  • Follow the steps in Step 2, give the new account a name, choose "Administrator" as the account type.
  • Be sure to create a strong password for the Administrator account even if you elected not to use a password for your Standard user account

STEP 4:  Create an "emergency use only" Administrator account.  If the every day account you created in Step 1 was an Administrator account, then you can consider the Administrator account you created in Step 3 to be your "emergency use only" account.  Otherwise, repeat the process shown in Step 3 and create a second Administrator account.  The idea here is to have an Administrator account that is rarely used -- so it's not very likely to become corrupted -- but will be available should you need to use administrator access in order to recover your system after some problem.

After you have created the accounts, log into each of them at least once.  Then log off all of them except the account you want to use as your everyday account.  You can log on or off an account by clicking the arrow on the right end of the Shut Down button:

Once you have all the new accounts created, you can move any files (documents/pictures/music/videos) that you might have saved in the original account into your new every day account (that you named the way you wanted).  Normally, this is pretty easy, but you may have confused things depending on what you did when you"updated the administrator info and put it in my name."  After you answer that question, I'll give you the appropriate directions.
