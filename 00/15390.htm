You should find your way through the registry in order to make the Language Check appear in the Status Bar.

Here are the steps:

  1. Close Microsoft Word.
  2. Click on Window icon (Start), in Search Bar, type 'regedit' and 'regedit' icon will appear.
  3. Click on the icon. A Registry Editor window will pop up.
  4. On the left panel, select "H_KEY_CURRENT_USER", go to "Software", go to "Microsoft", go to "14.0" and next to "Word". You will find "Status Bar" in the list there.

  7. The default Value data should be "1". Set it to "0", leave the base as "Hexadecimal" and click OK.
  8. Restart Microsoft Word. Check the Status Bar. The Language Check icon should be there.

DMARC reporting when Office 365 is sitting behind Mimecast - Microsoft Community

bobo_14 say:

DMARC reporting when Office 365 is sitting behind Mimecast

I know that Office 365 sends aggregate and forensics DMARC reports, and Mimecast does not support DMARC reporting at the moment.

So what is the case when Mimecast sits in front of Office 365 like shown below.

Office 365 → mimecast (sending email)

Office 365 ← mimecast (receiving email) are DMARC aggregate reports being sent by office 365 in this case or no reports will be sent?


reinto say:

@BOBO_14

TL;DR

Office 365 currently does not send out any DMARC reports. If it was sending out Aggregate reports, being behind a Mimecast would still generate reports for emails not filtered by Mimecast (not SPAM or Phishing). They would probably contain a lot of failures, because, for Office 365, the sending server will be Mimecast, which most likely is not added to the SPF of the sending domain. And, depending on what Mimecast is doing with the emails, the DKIM signature, if present at all, may be broken.

Hi Vincent,

You wrote:

"DMARC is an entry into the DNS, and is an instruction by the sending organization, to the anti-spoof engine of the receiving organization, on what action to take, and where to send reports (back to the sender), if they ever receive a suspected spoof mail from the sending organization."

Aggregate reports are actually sent back to the sending organization regardless of the authentication result, as it is also important to know which parties are successfully impersonating your domain, regardless of whether or not you authorized them to do so.

You continue:

"It is up to the receiving organisation's anti-spoof scanner to send you the reports if they are capable. Thus you get no reports if the receiving organization has no anti-spoof engine, or does not send DMARC reports."

And that is absolutely correct.

But then, and also to answer the original question:

"Conversely when your Office365 finds a spoof mail, and the sending organisation is requesting a DMARC report, Office365 will send the DMARC report based on the DMARC email address the sending organisation has put in the DMARC."

It (currently) doesn't. Office 365 sends forensics nor aggregate reports to (ruf and rua resp.) listed email addresses found in DMARC records, according to this Uservoice article: https://office365.uservoice.com/forums/264636-general/suggestions/11094318-dmarc-aggregate-reports-from-o365-domains and that's a shame.

While forensic reports are resource consuming (1 extensive report per failure) and ignored by most Mailbox hosters, aggregate reports contain statistics on successrate of authentication, per sending IP address. This information is most valuable and should be sent, especially by major mailbox hosters like Microsoft.

So to conclude: Office 365 currently does not send out any DMARC reports. If it was sending out server will be Mimecast, which most likely is not added to the SPF of the sending domain. And, depending on what Mimecast is doing with the emails, the DKIM signature, if present, may be broken.
