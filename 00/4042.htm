joeu2004 say:

Because in Excel, that is interpreted (-0.553708)^0.25, and we cannot the 4th root of a negative number.

Some people try to argue that Excel is "wrong". But really, that is just the way it is.

Write the formula as -(0.553708^0.25).

That is probably effectively how you did it on your pocket calculator. Or at least, that is how the pocket calculator interpreted the formula.

joeu2004 say: In reply to deleted message

Dana wrote:

=IMABS(IMPOWER(-0.553708, 0.25))

Or simply -POWER(0.553708, 0.25).

SIGN(A1) * ABS(A1)^0.25

What you *don't* have, however, is an ability to turn off auto-update (because Microsoft took that out of Skype, too). So every time you restart Skype, it will update with the newest version. 

So you will have to reinstall it every time you use it. Fortunately, it only takes a few seconds to do it, and you can just keep the .dmg on your desktop or somewhere handy so you don't have to re-download it from the website each time.

It's a huge pain in the ****, but it's better than not being able to use a headset or external mic.

Not that long ago, Windows Defender's level of protection was used as a “baseline” for comparing the protection offered by third-party AV apps. That’s no longer the case, and once Windows 10 is updated to the Fall Creators Update, Windows Defender should be sufficient for most users. Specifically, the last two version updates have made a quantum leap in Defender’s level of protection against ransomware and zero-day threats by including these features:

Block at First Seen:

https://docs.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/threat-protection/windows-defender-antivirus/configure-block-at-first-sight-windows-defender-antivirus

https://blogs.technet.microsoft.com/mmpc/2017/07/18/windows-defender-antivirus-cloud-protection-service-advanced-real-time-defense-against-never-before-seen-malware/  

Windows Defender Exploit Guard:

https://docs.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/threat-protection/windows-defender-exploit-guard/exploit-protection-exploit-guard

Attack Surface Reduction:

It looks like we also have the ability to add Attack Surface Reduction rules with PowerShell in Version 1709:

https://docs.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/threat-protection/windows-defender-exploit-guard/enable-attack-surface-reduction

https://blogs.technet.microsoft.com/mmpc/2017/10/23/windows-defender-exploit-guard-reduce-the-attack-surface-against-next-generation-malware/

Controlled Folder Access:

https://docs.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/threat-protection/windows-defender-exploit-guard/controlled-folders-exploit-guard

https://blogs.technet.microsoft.com/mmpc/2017/10/23/stopping-ransomware-where-it-counts-protecting-your-data-with-controlled-folder-access/?ocid=cx-blog-mmpc

PUA Protection:

Windows Defender can also detect and block Potentially Unwanted Applications, although this feature is turned off by default and needs to be enabled with PowerShell:

Run this command line at the elevated PowerShell Prompt:

Set-MpPreference -PUAProtection 1

Then confirm that PUA Protection was enabled by returning the current state for PUAProtection:

$Preferences = Get-MpPreference

$Preferences.PUAProtection

https://docs.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/threat-protection/windows-defender-antivirus/detect-block-potentially-unwanted-apps-windows-defender-antivirus
