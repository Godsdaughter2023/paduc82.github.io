It's worth exploring what is meant when a feature is "supported" by Microsoft. A common misinterpretation is that "supported" describes whether or not some functionality exists. But that interpretation doesn't match how Microsoft tends to use the word. For example, there were a lot of things that developers could do on Windows Mobile that were not supported such as OpenGL graphics,  placing custom icons in the task tray or programmatically controlling the screen brightness. While they were not supported one could still encounter a great deal of success in working with the features. When things went wrong then that's when the meaning of supported (or unsupported) became more clear; when supported functionality failed one could request action from Microsoft to resolve the problem. But when unsupported functionality failed then there was no guarantee of assistance; one was on their own.  

Right now Windows Phone still doesn't have support for the Asian or Arabic languages. Support is coming, but it's not here yet. When Microsoft reaches the point of being prepared to support a language then the update will be made available to the phone manufacturer and the manufacturer and the carrier can decide what languages will be made available to the user.

1. Set up a new partnership between your phone and the computer (by plugging in the USB cable and walking through the wizard when your phone is detected).
2. Check the box for "Contacts" during the partnership. Do not configure an exchange server.
3. It may take 1-2 synchronization cycles to get everything sync'ed from the phone to the device.
