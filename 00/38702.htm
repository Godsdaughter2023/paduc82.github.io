cbarnhorst say:

As HairyFool says, don't worry about the System Reserved partition.  

If you want to move anything, move the pagefile to the HDD.  That improves the durability of the SSD, but not by a lot unless your SSD was manufactured with 19nm technology.  If it was then definitely move the pagefile and turn off System Restore (I assume you have an aggressive backup plan).  

The system changes folks make when using an SSD don't affect performance as much as increase durability since they reduce writes to it.  In general though, durability is not as much of an issue as some people like to say because normally you won't live long enough to wear one out.  For sure, they outlast the OS, applications, and particularly the computer you installed it in.  They last decades.

The rule that bigger is better especially applies to SSDs because of wear-leveling.  A 1TB SSD will last eight times longer than a 128GB one, given the same usage pattern.  But then what does eight times "decades" matter anyway?

cbarnhorst say:

Samsung 850 Pro is the best there is this year.  Yes, it has 40nm architecture.  40nm means the insulating material between the cells is twice as thick as 19nm.  This is the key to durability.  What wears out an SSD is the repeated application of voltage that forces electrons through the insulation into and out of the cells.  The idea is to trap electrons inside the cells or erase by pulling them out again.  As the electons are forced through the walls they "singe" the insulating material slightly and over time burn pathways through it.  Then the cells can no longer keep electrons trapped and the cell is unable to hold a charge.  The thicker the walls the longer it takes to reach this point.  The difference between 40nm and 19nm on this point is 50,000 write cycles for 40nm versus only about 1,000 write cycles for 19nm.  A write cycle is every cell in the SSD written to one time.  Wear balancing (done automatically by the controller) ensures that no cell is written to a second time before all cells have been written to once.  A typical user will write approx. 5gb of data per 50 hours of use.  At that rate, it will take you more than 40 weeks before one write cycle is completed.  With 50,000 write cycles available, the SSD will outlive you.  Don't let anyone tell you SSDs wear out too quickly.  There is no comparison with HDDs, whose mechanical nature guarantees that they will break down much much sooner.  Samsung has several excellent white papers on their site.  Read them.
