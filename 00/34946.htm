OK, this could get complicated.

I am not an expert in drivers but I know a little about displays. If my previous advice did not work then, as you said, it is a problem with your driver - most likely a compatibility issue with Windows 10.

Important: Read through the whole post before continuing!

There are a few things you can try, but they all come with their drawbacks. I will try to help you with each of them now:

Method 2: Fix your driver:

As I said before, I know very little about drivers but I'll do my best. Here it goes...

After doing some research, I have found that with the release of Windows Vista, Microsoft removed the capability to force change your screen resolution other than the recommended sizes.So you would need to do one of two things if you want to keep your current monitor in action.

What to look for:

  • Ideally it should specifically specify that it works with your monitor or laptop display.
  • It must have the capability to display the amount of pixels you need (obviously).
  • It must be from a secure source - when messing around with your display driver, if you get it wrong, your computer may never show anything again if there is a troll that made a false driver.

How to apply the new driver:

  1. Search your computer (using cortana) for 'Device Manager'.
  2. Open it.
  3. Find the 'Monitors' section and expand it.
  4. Right click on your monitor.
  5. Click 'Update Driver Software...'.
  6. Click 'Browse my computer for driver software'.
  7. Select the driver you found.
  8. Follow the steps it gives you.
  9. See if it works!
  10. If not, just update the driver as you would normally and it will revert back.

If this doesn't work, the second option would be to do the same but for your graphics card. To do this, do the same as above, but look for your graphics card driver on the internet (hacked) and on step 3, expand the 'Display adapters' section instead.

If either of the two sections in Device Manager have multiple drivers, do the steps above for all of them.

The error seems to be pointing at a non-Hotmail or non-Live email account configured with Windows Live Mail which attempts to send an email with pictures or photo albums.

When Windows Live Mail sends pictures and albums, it automatically generates a photo album in Windows Live SkyDrive which is another service/feature available to those who signed up an account with Windows Live. This becomes a problem for non-Hotmail or non-Live accounts configured with Live Mail because their account can't connect to SkyDrive for the albums to be created.

I'd recommend that you attach the files using Insert > Single Photo in the ribbon menu. You still select multiple photos using that command but it won't be sent as an album, or an album won't be created in Windows Live SkyDrive.

Another way is to attach the photos. It won't display the photos in the email, but it will be sent as attachments to the email.
