ken sheridan say: I'm not sure how far one can infer a decline in its use from a fall in the demand for training in Access.  Users receiving any formal training at all have always represented a small minority, and the free availability of other resources, as Daniel points out, might well have accentuated this.  It is clear that for many visitors here YouTube is the first port of call.  One thing I am convinced of is that, in the 20 years I have been a respondent in this and other forums or newsgroups, the level of awareness both of the most fundamental principles of relational database development, and of the mechanisms available in Access has declined substantially.  In my early days as a Sysop for CompuServe the user base was undoubtedly more informed, and one could propose solutions with a high degree of confidence that the visitor would have the skill level to implement what was described.  These days I'm surprised when, with anything beyond the trivial, somebody is able to follow and implement what I'm saying straight away.  I've come to expect that my first response will only be the start of a sometimes lengthy process of clarification.  

Another factor is the way Microsoft emphasises the ease of use of Office applications through the use of wizards, templates and the like.  One cannot blame them; they are in the business of selling their products after all.  However, this leads both employers and users to believe that the products can be used out of the box with very little in the way of a learning curve.  Anyone who has even a modest familiarity with relational database development knows this is not the case with Access in particular, it being a development environment to a far greater degree than other members of the Office suite.

It's an inescapable fact, in view of the above factors, that there are very many Access users out there who really don't have much of a clue about what they are doing, and are consequently producing very poor quality database applications, if they can even be called that.  So, while there might have been a decline in the demand for your services, the need for them is self evident.

Can't Add External User to Sharepoint Online Site - Microsoft Community

david h levine say:

Can't Add External User to Sharepoint Online Site

Hi All,

So, I have a team site setup in Sharepoint Online. External sharing is enabled, and I have invited external users.

One of our external users is having a problem, and cannot access the site though. To make things more interesting, this external user is an Office365 user also.

She can log into her company's Office365 tenant at http://login.microsoftonline.com but when she attempts to access our SPO team site, she gets an error stating that she does not exists in the directory.

Since there is not much to do wrong with sending the invitation to an external user, I am at a loss on what the issue is?? I was going to open a support ticket, but figured I would ask here first. I have revoked and resent the invitation a few times...

sky pei msft say:

Hi David,

The error message indicates that the external user JanisiaB may log into your teamsite using an account which is not JanisiaB@XXX first. So currently, he can't use the account JanisiaB@XXX to log into the site again. Generally, the following two reasons may lead to this issue.

1.The user logged into your site using a Microsoft account or another Office 365 account by himself first.

2.The user click the site link in the invitation email, while he may access some services using another Microsoft/Office 365 account on his default browser at the meantime .

For this kind of issue, we need to remove the external user from your tenant completely and re-invite him. Here are the steps:

1. As an Office 365 global admin, go to Office 365 admin center > USERS > Active Users. Find and delete the affected external user account.

2. Go to the Office 365 SharePoint Admin center > user profiles >Manage User Profiles > find the external users and delete the external user.

3. Delete the external user from your site:

a. As a SharePoint administrator, go to the site collection in which the external user is invited.

b. Open a new tab in the browser, and enter <site collection URL>/_layouts/15/people.aspx?MembershipGroupId=0.

c. In the "People and Groups : All People" page, delete the target external user from these site collections.

4. Use SharePoint PowerShell to check whether you can get that external user. If so, delete that user. Here are the steps to use SharePoint PowerShell:

 a. Download the PowerShell and connect it according to this article: http://technet.microsoft.com/en-us/library/fp161372(v=office.15).aspx

 b. Get all external users by running Get-SPOExternalUser -Pagesize 20

 c. Delete that unique external user by running Remove-SPOExternalUser -UniqueIDs <UniqueIds>

 d. Wait for a while, and then run Get-SPOExternalUser -Pagesize 20 to see if the external user has been removed completely.

5. If you use the same browser to share with the external user that you’ve shared with before, please remove the browser cache since the People picker cache will remain even though the external user is fully removed from SharePoint Online.

6.Reinvite the external use.

Thanks,

Sky Pei

Formatting by special character - Microsoft Community

doie say:

Formatting by special character

Hi all,

Is conditional formatting possible using special characters e.g. using '<'. I have a condition formatting the cells of greater than or equal to a number but this picks up cells with any <X.XXX and formats them even though they're in spec. Cells could have different values like <0.01, <0.005, etc but they all start with '<'

hansv mvp say:

Text values are considered to be greater than all numeric values in Excel, so if you want to impose a condition on text, you must place it before the "Greater than or equal to" numeric conditions.

You could use a formula condition of the form =LEFT(A1,1)="<" where A1 must be replaced with the cell address of the active cell within the selection.
