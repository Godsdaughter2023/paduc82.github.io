max, singapore say:

Assuming source data (ie all real numbers, with possible duplicates) are running in A2 down
In B2: =IF(A2="","",IF(COUNTIF(A$2:A2,A2)>1,"",A2))
In C2: =IF(ROWS($1:1)>COUNT(B:B),"",SMALL(B:B,ROWS($1:1)))
In D2: =IF(ROWS($1:1)>COUNT(B:B),"",LARGE(B:B,ROWS($1:1)))
Copy B2:D2 down to cover the max expected extent of source data, eg down to D50? Minimize col B. Cols C and D will return the exact results that you seek, ie the uniques list of numbers, auto-sorted in ascending/descending order. Results will be dynamic to source

ashish mathur say:
Hi,
 
This is not a formula based solution but uses advanced filters
 
1.  Assume data is in range B2:B11 (headings are in row 2);
2.  Copy B2 and paste it in E2;
3.  In E3, enter <120
4.  Click on a blank cell and go to Data > Filter Advanced Filters > Copy to another location
5.  In the list range, specify B2:B11.  In the criteria range, specify E2:E3.  In the copy to box, select any blank cell
6. Check the box for unique records only and click on OK.
 
Sort the output in ascending order.

1.       Did you just install Office from a retail box you just purchased?  If so, go to Control Panel, Programs, Programs and Features and see if there is another copy of Office installed besides the suite you just installed.  Some pre-installed Trial Office 2007 suites can conflict with the product key validation process.  If you see a duplicate copy of Office installed, then uninstall it and try to run your new Office program again.  Chances are your product key will work now.

2.       If you are using the 25-digit product key that you found on a sticker on the bottom of your laptop or on the back of your desktop case, then you are using the wrong key.  This key is the product key for the Windows operating system, and NOT for the Office Suite.  The correct product key for the Office suite will be in one of these places:

a.       Inside the retail Office 2007 box (if you bought Office from a retail store)
b.      Inside an e-mail from the online website where you purchased Office
c.       A sticker on the outside of the CD case
d.       Microsoft Product Identifier Card

john 675 say:

Asterisk in Excel Formula

Hello,

Will someone tell me what the Asterisk does in this formula: SUM((IF(($B$3>$D$6)*($B$3<=$L$6),L26:L26,SUM((IF(($B$3>$D$6)*($B$3<=$M$6),L26:M26,SUM((IF(($B$3>$D$6)*($B$3<=$N$6),L26:N26)))

It works, but I am not for sure I know how I got it to work.

Thank You,

hansv mvp say:

The asterisk is the multiplication operator: 2*3 results in 6.

($B$3>$D$6) is either TRUE or FALSE.

($B$3<=$L$6) is either TRUE or FALSE too.

When used in arithmetic calculations, TRUE is equivalent to 1 and FALSE is equivalent to 0 in Excel. So (($B$3>$D$6)*($B$3<=$L$6) is 1*1 = 1 if both ($B$3>$D$6) and ($B$3<=$L$6) are TRUE. And it is 0 of either ($B$3>$D$6) or ($B$3<=$L$6) is FALSE, or if both are FALSE.

Instead of ($B$3>$D$6)*($B$3<=$L$6), you might as well use AND($B$3>$D$6,$B$3<=$L$6)
