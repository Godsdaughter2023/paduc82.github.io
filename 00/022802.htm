Do any of those websites you were browsing have advertising?

That was a rhetorical question by the way, of course they do, the Internet today is full of them!

Most of these fake browser popups today aren't due to malware that's already on the PC, that was the case a few years back but stopped working well as AV programs began handling these better.  Now the easiest way to find those most susceptible to such attacks are the advertising networks that sell blocks of advertising access on major websites, since these also contain the ability to target the types of people and activity which are known to be most likely to respond to this type of false messaging.

In other words, the malicious actors have decided that you are part of a group that is likely to respond, so they are using the advertising networks in order to track and target you for repeated ads that launch these popups.  Now don't get paranoid, because it isn't you personally they are tracking, but rather the types of sites and/or articles you read and other actions you might take like clicking on other specific ads or articles on those pages.

So one way to stop these is often simply avoiding using the browser to do things like reading news or especially other hot button sites like those driven by advertising links such as "20 pictures of this" or "10 ways to do that" which are often found at the end of various articles.

It's hard to say exactly what specific activity is making you a target without knowing far more about your habits, but in general, this is why the trend recently has been to move to news and other apps rather than the browser for such regular reading.  Unless you configure both Windows and the browser with privacy in mind, including blocking many of the ads that are normally displayed, it's hard to avoid these popups any longer.

To try and determine exactly how these popups are being delivered, try either removing your current home page if you have one or simply avoiding the websites you most commonly visit for a short time.  If that causes the ads to stop at least temporarily, you've at least found which website(s) are delivering the ads, though they're likely to return eventually on other sites that use the same advertising networks, so this typically isn't a true solution.

As I mentioned, there are other methods of configuring Windows and the browser for privacy, as well as using ad blocker add-ons in the browser, but these are relatively extreme.  However, if you don't wish to change your web browsing habits, they may be the only way to dependably reduce the number of popups you see.

Note that major websites like MSN, Yahoo and several others commonly used as home pages had seen significant increases in this type of popup activity earlier this year.  Though some of this has been reduced, the deeper problems with the advertising networks still remains, so I assume we'll continue to see flare-ups and I'm also sure there are some websites that aren't even aware the problem is happening to them, since it isn't their own web pages that are causing the problem.

hansv mvp say:

Let's say the names are in A2:A500 and in B2:B100.

 

Select A2:A500. I'll assume that A2 is the active cell within the selection.

On the Home tab of the ribbon, click Conditional Formatting > New Rule...

Select 'Use a formula to determine which cells to format'.

Enter the formula

 

=ISNUMBER(MATCH(A2,$B$2:$B$50,0))

 

(adjust the range as appropriate).

Click Format...

Activate the Fill tab.

Select a color, then click OK.

Click OK again.

petrib say:

There used to be separate versions of PC Suite for each and every phone model

 

At some point it evolved into a single, "unified" PC Suite version supporting all phone models (relatively current at the time and afterwards, ignoring support for many older models).

 

A few yeara later Nokia branched off parts (the core PC connectivity bits/components) from PC Suite to create a new application (user interface) called Ovi Suite (PC Suite was being kept updated alongside) based on Qt.

 

A couple of years later (last fall) Ovi Suite was renamed to Nokia Suite (as Nokia is gradually moving away from the "Ovi" brand it introduced in 2007).

 

Latest Ovi/Nokia Suite here:

 

http://www.comms.ovi.com/m/p/ovi/suite/English

 

Latest PC Suite here:

 

http://europe.nokia.com/support/product-support/nokia-pc-suite

 

If things haven't changed lately, you can even install both on the same PC, if you wish.

 

For N9 (MeeGo) Nokia also has the Nokia Link:

 

http://europe.nokia.com/support/product-support/nokia-link

 

And for Mac users Nokia has had stuff, too:

 

http://europe.nokia.com/support/product-support/mac-support

 

 

(Note: For Windows application developers Nokia has also released two different SDKs, Software Development Kits. For the phones prior to the "unified" PC Suite times, there is/was something called the "Nokia PC Connectivity SDK", and for the newer PC Suite supported phones there's the "PC Suite API", also known as "Nokia PC Suite Connectivity API".)
