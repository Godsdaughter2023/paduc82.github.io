jmh3143 say:
FYI...
"It's not a true crash, in the sense that the Blue Screen was initiated only because the combination of video driver and video hardware was being unresponsive, and not because of any synchronous processing exception".

Since Vista, the "Timeout Detection and Recovery" (TDR) components of the OS video subsystem have been capable of doing some truly impressive things to try to recover from issues which would have caused earlier OS's like XP to crash.

As a last resort, the TDR subsystem sends the video driver a "please restart yourself now!" command and waits a few seconds.
If there's no response, the OS concludes that the video driver/hardware combo has truly collapsed in a heap, and it fires off that stop 0x116 BSOD.

If playing with video driver versions hasn't helped, make sure the box is not overheating.
 Try removing a side panel and aiming a big mains fan straight at the motherboard and GPU.
 Run it like that for a few hours or days - long enough to ascertain whether cooler temperatures make a difference.

If so, it might be as simple as dust buildup and subsequently inadequate cooling.

bossdweeb say: Yes - 95C is just below the thermal protection cut off point, and is too hot.
Laptops are prone to running hot due to their compact nature, and limited fan options.
A laptop should never be used when resting on a soft surface.

Graphics cards on laptop should not have to run over 80C under load, even with high ambient air temps.
Obviously, the cooler the better.
By way of comparison. A properly cooled PC's graphics card should not have to run much over
low/mid 70's C max, even with ambient air temps up to 35C . Mid 60's under full load is more common.
 
While your Logitech cooling pad fan may be loud at full speed you do really need to run it at full
speed when the machine is under heavy load for it to work most efficiently.

btw - JMH may not have noticed you were using a laptop at first.
There is no side panel to remove on a laptop as with PC, but the case can be opened to allow
for more complete cleaning of dust.  Removing the side panel on a PC and aiming a desk fan into
the case is a good test of a case's cooling efficiency. Unfortunately not an option with laptops.

    steve winograd say:

    The quota being exceeded isn't in Outlook (or even on your computer), and deleting messages from Outlook folders (Sent, Deleted Items, etc) won't help.

    The problem is that your mailbox on your E-mail server is too full.  That can happen if you've configured your E-mail account in Outlook to leave messages on the server.

    The solution is to remove messages from your mailbox on your E-mail server.
