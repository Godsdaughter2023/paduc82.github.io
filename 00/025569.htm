Turn on the display of non printing characters by clicking on the ¶ button on the Home tab of the ribbon and then make sure to include the paragraph mark (¶) at the end of the text that you are copying.   If you are only copying part of a paragraph, you will need to either press Enter in the source document so that you have a ¶ at the end of the text that you need to copy so that you can include it, or copy the whole paragraph and then delete the unwanted part(s) of it after pasting into the target document.

scott mcdaniel say:

You should not use Multi Valued Fields for that. Instead, you should use a Subform or Listbox, and store those choices in a Many-To-Many Join table. In your example, you'd have a table for Company, another for Products, and you'd then have a table to "join" those two:

tCompany

--------------

CompanyID

CompanyName

etc

tProduct

-----------

ProductID

ProductName

etc

tCompany_Products

------------------

Company_ProductID

CompanyID

ProductID

Using this process, you could associate as many Products with a Company as needed. You would represent this by use of Form - Subform, with the Form based on the Company table and the Subform based on the Company_Products table (the subform). The subform would include a combo that would be based on the Product table, and the user would select products to relate to the Company.

scottgem say:
Hi Erik, I'm an independent advisor and will try to help. In the example you used, you need to have a Products table. When you create the Multi-Value field (MVF), you will use The Products table as your Rowsource. However, because an MVF does not allow you to enter text, you cannot use the AutoExpand feature of regular combos. Therefore, I would recommend using an unbound search box to filter the MVF.

But frankly, I would not recommend using an MVF at all. MVFs model a many to many relationship in a non-traditional way. As such they make for some problems in retrieving the data. For example, if you wanted a listing of all companies selling Product B, it would not be as easy.

The traditional way is to use a junction table. Something like this:
tjxCompanyProducts
CompanyProductID (Primary Key Autonumber)
CompanyID (Foreign Key(
ProductID (FK)

You would then use a subform with a regular combobox to select the products associated with a company.

If you need further clarification on this feel free to ask.

ken sheridan say: I would also recommend against the use of a multi-valued field to model a many-to-many relationship type.  A fundamental problem with such fields is that they do not allow for non-key attributes of the relationship type.  Products offered by a supplier is a case in point.  An attribute of the relationship type would normally be the unit price charged for the product by the supplier.  With a multi-valued field this cannot be recorded.  When the relationship type is modelled by a table which resolves it into two one-to-many relationship types, however, it merely needs a UnitPrice column to be included in the table.

When creating a table to model a many-to-many relationship type, such as a CompanyProducts table, note that the CompanyID and ProductID foreign key columns constitute a candidate key of the table.  As such they must be included in a single unique index.  In such a situation I would do this by making the two columns the composite primary key of the table, dispensing with an unnecessary autonumber primary key column.
