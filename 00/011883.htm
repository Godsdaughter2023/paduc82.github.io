Any mid to high end machine "off the shelf" will do the trick

TALK TO HER! She probably has definite ideas of what she wants / needs.
  • ask her if she would prefer a laptop to a desktop! Modern laptops have more than enough "horsepower" to do multiscreen document editing.  My 7 year old laptop has an external monitor connected and only 3GB of RAM and I often have several Word documents that are up to 1500 pages long, with lots of graphics in them (big files). A laptop has the advantage of also being portable when she has to take her work on the road.
  • i5 or i7 chip  (Note: you do NOT have to buy the absolute newest, "best" most expensive CPU chip for word processing! )
  • ask her if she wants Windows 7 or Windows 8.1. You can still get Windows 7 from some builders, and many people prefer the "old style" desktop to the new "touchy-feely" Windows 8 Start Screen.  
  • Consider Win 8.1 Professional. It also gives you the right to "downgrade" to Win 7 at no extra charge if you wish, 2 for the price of one if she wants Win7 now, but expects to need to upgrade to Win 8.1 in the future
  • For Windows 8.1 on a desktop computer you should also strongly consider getting an 8-10" touchpad that allows at least 5 finger input as well as pen input for pen markup of documents. The tiny touchpad on laptops and some new keyboards simply is not adequate to the needs of Win 8.1.
  • 6GB -12GB of RAM
  • mid range video card with 1-2GB of RAM, make sure it has at least 3 video connection ports
  • talk to her and see if she wants a 2 or 3 monitor setup. For a document editor I can see where 3 monitors would work well for her. 2 set up in "Portrait" orientation for side by side document editing and one "landscape" for spreadsheets and some web browsing.  I have seen "3-packs" of monitors sold in a discounted bundle.  They can be stand alone units or attached to a single mounting framework to provide more stability
  • for the snappiest performance get a SSD hard drive, min 240GB, 500 GB would be better.
  • make sure it has 2 or more "USB 3" ports as well as multiple USB 2 ports.
  • talk to her about the "workstation". that is the total combination of the chair and desk.  Anyone who spends a lot of time at the computer needs a good, "ergonomic" workstation setup that allows you to adjust the chair height, angle, adjust armrest height, adjust keyboard height control monitor height.  If you are getting her a new workstation, you may want to consider getting her one that can easily convert to a "standing desk" format, ie mainly able to raise the keyboard high enough
  • talk to her about the keyboard and mouse. The feel of these things can be very important. I prefer to spend a few bucks more to get very a specific "Microsoft Natural Keyboard" with a specific key layout and specific mouse layouts (5 button).
  • Buy her a couple of 1-2GB external USB drives that support USB 3 (they only cost $75-150 each).  These are needed for backups to external device just in case there is a hard drive crash. She doesn't want to lose some one else's work!  Having 2 allows you to rotate one to an Off-site location, just in case there is a disaster at your home. 
  • Does she want to be able to encrypt her whole HD to protect the documents she is editing.  Then she needs Win 8.1 Pro and computer that meets it specific hardware (TPM Chip) and software (a specific new boot protocol) to make "Bitlocker" work automatically. This will require a little research to identify the specific requirements it needs.

cortig say:
Unless you use tricks with Aliases or symlinks, Outlook always uses the identity in ~/Documents/Microsoft User Data/Office 2011 Identities. That’s most probably why it’s not using the copy of your identity on the external drive.

Contacts are in ~/Documents/Microsoft User Data/Office 2011 Identities/Main Identity/Data Records/Contacts as you figured out by yourself. The approach you used should work fine. The warning about corrupted database is expected as Outlook noticed that the data on the drive didn’t match the “index” it had in it’s database file and rebuilt it.
If the process brings the contacts back to life, great, if not……

There is an alternate solution though. Rename the identity folder from the external drive and place it alongside the Main Identity on your boot drive. Relaunch Outlook pressing the Option key down and from the database Utility there, you should be able to see both Identities and launch Outlook on the renamed one.
From there, you should be able to export all your contacts to an .olm file that you will later be able to restore in the Main Identity.

tom ogilvy say:

Sreekanthan Pillai,

it depends on how the data is laid out and what kind of changes can be made. 

If you copied the original sheet and then went into that sheet and made only some numerical changes, you could make a copy the updated sheet, then copy the data from the original sheet, select the same cells in the copy and do a paste special, then choose subtract and values. 

Differences would remain.

--

Regards,

Tom Ogillvy

sreekanth pillai say: The changes done are not only numerical it contains  text also.

tom ogilvy say:

With the same assumption that the original sheet was copied and edited in place (no rows or columns added or inserted), then you could go to a blank sheet and in A1 put in a formula

=Sheet1!A1=Sheet2!A2

then drag fill that down and across. 

(I assume all the sheets are in the same workbook.  You have said separate files, so you would change the references of Sheet1  to something like 

=[MyWorkbook1.xls]!sheet1!A1=[MyWorkbook2.xlsx]CopiedSheet!A1

)

You could get more information (illustrated with the original formula for convenience)

=IF(=Sheet1!A1=Sheet2!A2,"",Sheet1!A1&"<=>"&Sheet2!A1)

Blank cells would return zero.
