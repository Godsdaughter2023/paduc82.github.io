1. You can use Skype anywhere in the world where its services are not blocked. As long as data exists, you can call each other. While you can IM one another Skype to Skype, inbound SMS texting is not supported.  Only outbound SMS texting is supported.

2. People can call your Skype number, they will not be able to SMS text it. They can only IM your Skype name from their Skype client.

3. Skype numbers are actually landlines that only receive inbound calls. All the restrictions or limitations of a US landline, apply to a US Skype number. To call each other over the phone you can settle on an outbound US unlimited subscription calling plan for each account, so that you can call each other's US Skype number and any other US numbers (landline or mobile). If you need to place phone calls to other areas, that will require credit or a landline/mobile subscription that covers the cost of the calls you make. Be aware that many service numbers while travelling are premium rate and would not be covered by an overseas unlimited calling subscription. These subscriptions may be great to call friends and family numbers, but not many business numbers.

You basically need to determine if the cost of:

2 Skype numbers

2 Unlimited US subscriptions

and any extra credit is worth it or not.

All inbound/outbound subscriptions auto-renew. If you want a subscription to only last a single billing cycle, be sure to cancel it anytime before 3 days prior to the end of the cycle. This ensures you will not be billed for the next cycle. Cancelling will not kill your service, it will still finish out its cycle. Cancelling simply stops the auto renew process. Many people forget or are caught unaware and have 1 or more subscriptions renew month after month when they no longer require the service.

david m. connell say:

You would need to set up port forwarding in your router - basically, tell the router which computer on the network  the remote desktop traffic should be sent. Most routers support it, but the instructions vary depending on the make and model of router. It's also a big security risk. RD is great for access within your network, but once you open up a port on your router to the outside world for it you're just inviting the hackers in.

A better option would be to use Teamviewer or Splashtop, which are both more secure.. You install a program on the target/host computer to receive the incoming connection, and access it through the service and its program. Simple setup, no static IPs needed, and no open ports on the router required.

If you're planning light use, Teamviewer would be the better option - it's free for consumer use, but once you use it past a certain amount of time for they want you to pay up. TV is pretty much the industry standard for remote access services, so it may be worth the money to you if you can budget for it.

If you plan to remote in rountinely, especially if it's just you, Splashtop might be better. They don't have a free option for access from other networks but they start out really inexpensive. $60 a year for one person to access up to 10 computers.

Both work very well - I've had no problems with either service.

patrick barker say: Sander, unfortunately, the culprit is still iaStorA.sys. This is not simply a driver we can remove and replace as more than 90% of the time once it's removed, you can no longer boot. You can try one thing, or you can do a clean install of Windows and not install the IRST driver at all and allow Windows to keep the default driver.

First off, let's create a Restore Point - http://www.eightforums.com/tutorials/4690-restore-point-create-windows-8-a.html

Head to Device Manager > IDE ATA/ATAPI controllers, change the drivers for the Intel (also works for AMD/Nvidia - enables TRIM for SSDs and allows SSD software to access the drive for firmware updates, etc.) to the standard AHCI 1.0 Serial ATA Controller. Do this by selecting "Let me pick from a list of device drivers on my computer" and then choose the "Standard AHCI 1.0 Serial ATA Controller" (it's the inbuilt Windows driver for AHCI SATA). Apply/OK your way back out of Device Manager.

Reopen Device Manager and check that the MS driver has 'stuck'. Then, still in Device Manager, select 'View' > 'Show hidden devices' and open the 'Non-plug and play Drivers' section and right-click on iaStore A, select Properties then the Drivers tab; under Startup, change the Type to Disabled, Apply it and then you should be prompted to reboot - do it.

Wait a few moments after the reboot - if Windows prompts that you need to reboot again, it may have re-enabled it - reboot if needed then check that iaStoreA is still disabled.

Or, as I said, run a clean install of Windows and don't install IRST.

Regards,

Patrick
