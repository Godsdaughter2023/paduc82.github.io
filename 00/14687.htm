cbarnhorst say:

Normally build numbers are simply consecutive and there is usually a build a day (overnight compilations), but the majority of builds are judged not suitable for release, apparently even to the inner ring (one blog estimated the inner ring at a few hundred MS folks).  

The dev team determines a build range for a new product.  Major gaps in the range sometimes appear.  Those gaps are based on progress of several related dev programs, such as PC, Mobile, Xbox, and so on.  There are many concurrent programs.  If the programs are not quite in sync at the time MS wants to release a final build for one of the programs, they will leave a gap for the other programs to fill in as they reach their release builds.  This gap allows the programs to all wind up at the same final build number.  

Sometimes MS will make a very large gap by starting a whole new build range.  They did this after the first release of Windows 10 in 2015.  In fact that jump was several thousand skipped numbers, easily the largest of all time.  That's how they got from 10586 (the first general release of W10) to the current 14393 released late last year.  Then they jumped about 600 numbers when they started the current development builds, and that's how they got to this week's 15034.

johnthepilot say:
Many thanks for your detailed reply, although I'm still not sure I understand the logic. If I understand what you've said, was build 15031 released six days after 15025?

cbarnhorst say:

About six days.  The general pattern is successive build numbers for successive overnight compilations.  But MS does what it wants and may skip a build number (or more), obviously for internal reasons.  They do not disclose anything about this and we all wind up guessing at what may have happened.  As far as I can tell we always guess wrong, but it entertains us.  But what we do know is that it is not about just PC builds but includes a group of projects that are proceeding in parallel.  A speed-bump in IoT or Mobile, for example, seems to once in awhile result in a skip ahead in PC build number to give space for catch-up in the lagging program.  In summary build numbers are part of MS's version controls and, while they indicate progress to us, they do change unexpectedly once in a while.  Other than tomorrow's build being numbered higher than today's, nothing is set in concrete.  

Another thing to remember is that the sources we have for tracking these builds that we never see are third-party sources unassociated with MS.  These sources depend on unofficial information involving leaks (probably sanctioned) and sometimes these sources have trouble verifying data.  We have to take them for what they are.  Buildfeed talks about this at the bottom of their main page.

markclavelle say:

How to insert two blank and unnumbered pages in Word, and start the footer numbering on page three.

For the life of me, Word seems autocratic. I cannot insert two blank pages without the numbering starting on the first page. 0 is not a number, if you remember basic math. I need a copyright page and then a blank page, unnumbered. Then to start the document (p.3) as the first page. Any help?
hansv mvp say:

Create a new document.

Create the copyright page

Position the insertion point at the end of the page.

On the Layout tab of the ribbon, click Breaks > Odd Page (under Section Breaks).

On the Insert tab of the ribbon, click Footer > Edit Footer,

This activates the Design tab of the ribbon, under Header & Footer Tools.

By default, Link to Previous will be highlighted in the Navigation group.

Click Link to Previous to deselect it.

In the Header & Footer group, click Page Number > Format Page Numbers...

Select 'Start at' and make sure that the starting number is set to 1, then click OK.

Place the insertion point where you want the page number (press Tab if necessary).

In the Header & Footer group, click Page Number > Current Position > Plain Number.
