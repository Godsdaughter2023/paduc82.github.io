zed z say:

USB-Serial; multiple drivers??

Becoming increasingly difficult to purchase laptops with "real" RS-232 serial ports.....the 9-pin variety.
That said, and we own 300 or more 'applications' from several manufacturers which we use in "field service" of communications equipment, everything in software until recently was written with the real serial port as the connection to the Device Under Test......the DUT.  No problem.  Sometimes we had to edit the Baud rate, or use a null modem cable, but it always worked.

Now we have the USB......and "adapters" to get from USB to the RS-232 protocol.

We own five 'adapters' and five different 'drivers' and find that, (A):  Can't be depended on, and, (B):  Can't always make anything work.........

We own several Win 98 OS laptops, several Win XP laptops.  All use the hard-wired DB-9 serial port and all work very well with apps from several major communications equipment manufacturers.
Recent offerings in equipment now are equipped with USB connection for maintenance, thus use of the USB port on laptops.

Now the problem:  A field service technician must carry TWO laptops; one for serial hookups, the other for USB.
One day soon there won't be any computers with the 9-pin RS-232 port available......what then?

We have found that some adapters work when connected to some particular equipments, but not to others.  
Some of the apps go back to the late 80's....this may be asking too much of the 64-bit OS.  These were real DOS-based programs runnng on 8-bit machines.  This isn't the norm......most apps are very late 90's and newer.
Manufacturers are offering their own versions of 'adapters' and including a 'driver'.  These are 'suggested' for a narrow range of uses.  None of the several we own seems to function as a real RS-232 port......the connected equipment expects to see a wired connection, handshake, etc, ......and it appears this isn't happening.

We must load a given driver a  for a given adapter to run a given application......then 'remove' the driver in order to load another to use with a different adapter with yet another app.  This stinks, but we're still working.....

So........can one load multiple drivers (as long as they don't have the same file name) and expect Win 7 to find the correct driver when one of the adapters is plugged in?  Does this tax the intelligence of the Plug N Play grimlin?

A local fellow who professes to know something about this has told us that the root problem is that the driver is an interface and converter between the USB protocol and the RS-232 protocol, and that a 32-bit or 64-bit machine with a USB is moving data faster and in much larger chunks than the serial-connected  DUT can handle.  This then likely ends up being a "make-believe" RS-232 where the 'settings' (Baud rate, maybe) varies because of how the computer handles the app, how the USB-Serial conversion handles the handshake expected by either/or, or both, the app & the DUT.  

Is this a useless chase?  There are hundreds of thousands of equipments out there that have the 9-pin serial connector and do not have a USB connector.  I don't think all these folks will junk all the equipment just to buy newer stuff with USB ports. 

The field service guys are really good at what they do, they're just not software engineers.  As such, the laptop is just another tool......like screwdrivers and voltmeters.

Somebody jump in here....explain USB-serial on a Dell 64-bit Win 7 .........it worked real good on XP & 9-pin!!

Thanks!!

Hi Divine

You could try removing those fingerprints, restart, check for the latest updates then set them up again.

Check to make sure any third party security software that came with your computer is not enabled. Examples include:

- Encryption software
- Third party facial recognition software, might be conflicting with Windows built in software.
- Examples of such software include HP Client security, Lenovo Fingerprint Software


1. Reset Facial Recognition and/or Fingerprint Options

Press Windows key + I to open Settings.
Click Accounts and select Sign-In options.
Locate Fingerprint or Facial Recognition options and click Remove under them.
After that, click Get Started and follow the on-screen instructions to reset Fingerprint and Facial Recognition.
Reboot your PC if needed.

2. Modify Group Policy Settings - skip this step if you are not running Windows 10 Pro/Enterprise only

Creators Update might have modified some of the crucial settings of Group Policy. Therefore, we would recommend to launch the Group Policy Editor and check/edit the settings related to Windows Hello functionality.
