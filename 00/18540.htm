financial engineer say: To answer your question as to why the IRR for the 3rd data set is higher than the IRR for 2nd data set, you would have to look at your "model" that is generating the 3 data sets. Looking at the numbers you posted as a table, I infer that you may be using time series analysis or similar exponential model to produce the cash flows.

This resulted in the 2nd data set having higher standard deviation of 10.41784 as compared to 10.41705 of the 3rd data set . This means that it will require a higher interest rate to discount the cash flows in 3rd data set to bring their net present value to zero as there are less costs and more benefits. 

Now let us discuss IRR calculation, please note that an internal rate of return is the discount rate that sets an IRR equation to zero. To do this one must at first define this equation and there are a number of ways of doing this as explained here http://finance.thinkanddone.com/irr.html

An IRR equation may be defined in terms of NPV - net present value, NFV - net future value, EAA - equivalent annual annuity, BCR - B/C ratio which then leads to defining a function f(x) whose roots we seek to solve the equation for a particular rate.

Now to solve one of these equations is a different story as there are no closed form formulas to solve these equations when number of cash flows exceed 5. At least I am not aware of any such formulas to find IRR when number of cash flows exceed 5.

For an investment that has two cash flows (outgoing and incoming) then we may use the future value formula to find this interest rate such as

FV = PV (1+i)^N

where we are solving for rate

i = (FV/PV)^(1/N) - 1

If there were only 3 cash flows then that permits us to represent the IRR equation with Quadratic equation of the form

aX^2 + bX + C = 0

where a, b, and c are the respective cash flows and X is the variable of interest that we are seeking to find.

This equation has 2 or less solutions that are possible to find with Quadratic formula These rates may be real or these may turn out to be complex numbers as the solution has a radical in numerator that may encounter finding a square root of a negative number

X = [ -b +/- Sqrt ( b^2 - 4ac) ] / 2a

If the number of cash flows are limited to 4 that permits us to use the cubic equation to solve for rate of the form

aX^3 + bX^2 + cX + d = 0

If the number of cash flows are limited to 5 that permits us to use the quartic (I think its called) equation to solve for rate of the form

aX^4 + bX^3 + cX^2 + dX + e = 0

where a, b, c, d and e are the cash flows in sequence.

Finding solution for IRR using Cubic and Quartic formulas is possible but these formulas are quite complex and are beyond my comprehension.

Now for cash flows whose count exceeds 5, I am not aware of any closed form formulas that find the IRR solutions. May be someone knows but I don't know such a person. In such cases the underlying IRR equation has to be solved using iterative techniques or what are called numerical methods that find a single root (one IRR). These iterative methods require an initial guess called a seed value that is used in repetitive calculations that are checked for convergence. If such iterative calculations converge we have found one of the IRR values. If such iterative calculations do not converge we halt the process and redo them by selecting a different guess rate as a seed value. This is one reason that Excel IRR function permits an optional guess rate.


Over the years a large number of such iterative methods have been discovered some dated a couple of centuries ago such as the Newton-Raphson method named after the two mathematicians who independently discovered it namely Sir. Isaac Newton and Joseph Raphson. Excel's online documentation states that Excel IRR function is programmed using an implementation of NR method however there are doubts about such a claim as there are contradictory statements found on Microsoft's site.


There are many other iterative methods besides the NR method that range in complexity and accuracy of the desired IRR solution. These iterative methods are suited for computers due to the large number of repetitive calculations that are required. Most business professors at 80% of business colleges in the U.S. take a rather trivial approach in teaching their students when it comes to finding an IRR. Look it up on search engines and almost everyone else speaks of linear interpolation as the method of choice in finding the internal rate of return.


From the offset, the idea sounds novel that we have to corner the interest rates at which NPV is at opposing ends (one +ve and the other -ve) arising from the idea of central limit theorem that if a function is continuous over an interval [x0,x1] and f(x0) is negative and the same function f(x1) is positive then there is a point x in between x0 and x1 such that f(x)=0 

But how in this world are you going to find such two odd rates that lead to cornering the IRR in between. That is something those college students fail to ask their professor and the homework assignment that is given to them then gets posted on those AnswerMyQuestionBecauseIhaveToPassTheSubjectToGetMyDegreeToFindAJob websites.

As I stated, the iterative methods may find 1 or zero IRR solutions at a time however there exists some very complex computer algorithms based of numerical methods that find all possible IRR solutions (both real and complex IRR solutions). 

Even if you did find all real IRR solutions, the use and interpretation of the real IRR may still be left undefined.

Take the three set of cash flows that you have posted. There are 3 IRR values for each of your three sets of cash flows. So which one of the three IRR values best serves the purpose? 

Since the IRR solutions were found by seeking roots of the underlying IRR equations, thus the use of the correct IRR value is left to our intuition.

As these are investments that are under evaluation, and we are seeking a break-even point that serves as a yard stick that helps an analyst in finding an investor's ROI - return on investment thus the first question one should ask is

"Did we make money from this investment"?

To answer this question, you would have to look at the cash flows from the bare eyes to see what did we gain from it and one way to do so would be to either find the net worth of investment (the sum of un-discounted cash flows) which if found to be positive would mean we made money. If such a sum is found to be negative then we lost money. If such a sum is zero then we made nothing and lost nothing.

A far better approach is to find the yield on such an investment (an IRR is itself called a Yield and an XIRR is the annualized Yield).

To find the yield on investment, we take sum of benefits and divide it the sum of costs and then to find its Nth root minus one giving us the Yield. This is the same as finding an Modified IRR (MIRR) using 0% as finance and reinvestment rates.

A negative yield will suggest a loss, a positive yield means gains and a zero yield means no return on investment was made or expected.

As I stated in my first reply, the sum of your cash flows for 1st data set is positive whereas the sum of cash flows for both the 2nd and 3rd data sets is negative. Thus the yield on first investment is positive yet the yield is negative for the 2nd and 3rd data set.

If you want it to be 2 paragraphs, make it 2 paragraphs... Place the insertion point before the word One in your example then press the return key. Why do you insist on trying to make a single paragraph behave as though it were 2?

Also, Word is designed to not permit a line within a paragraph to begin with a space. There is no legitimate format that calls for that to occur. A space can precede the first character in a paragraph but I can think of no reason for that to be done. In any event, indentation is not done with the space bar. You are still being lucky and it will not be that way forever.  When you view a table's datasheet you are looking at records in whatever order Access thinks is an efficient way to retrieve the records from storage and this changes as records are added and deleted.

As John said, the ONLY way to see a datasheet with the records in a specific order is to use a query that specifies the table fields that you want to use to sort the records.  If you want to see the records sorted by the primary key, then simply use a query that sorts by the primary key field(s).

john w. vinson say:

I think there may be some communication breakdown due to differing jargon.

As all of us have been trying to emphasize -

Access is NOT A FAILED IMPLEMENTATION OF EXCEL.

It is a different program, with different base assumptions, different techniques, and different advantages and disadvantages. Trying to use Access with concepts like "record numbers" and "right order (in tables)" is not just wrong, it will make your job much much harder than it needs to be.

If your saved imports aren't working right - fix the saved imports! There have been several suggestions in this thread explaining how to do so.

I wonder if you're using the word "form" in the common parlance of "a piece of paper containing information" or in the Access parlance of "An Access user interface object designed to display data on screen and allow user interaction with that data"? The proper tool in Access for transferring data to a sheet of paper is a Report. A Report will be based on a Query which allows you to select which fields and which records to print, and you can use the Sorting and Grouping property of the Report object to print the records in any desired sequential order.

If - as I gather - your Excel spreadsheet contains a number field which defines the desired sort order for the report, I'd recommend that you import the spreadsheet into a table including that field (and it doesn't make any difference whatsoever what order Access stores those records); you could then launch a Report based on the imported table (or a Query based on that table), using the desired field in the report's Sorting and Grouping property to print the records in the desired order.

Seriously: if you want to use Access - and I think it's a very good tool for what you want to do! - then listen to the good advice you're getting here, and put in a little effort to learn how to work with Access in the way it's designed to work, rather than struggling to jam it into your preconceptions of how it ought to work
