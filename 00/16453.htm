jllatham say:

Excel will do the job just fine.  And we will keep things simple here, at least I hope I do.

 

Let's set up 3 columns in row 1 of a sheet, we will name them

DEPOSITS    DEBITS   BALANCE

so now we are down to row 2 for our first entries.  You could set this up in several ways, but how about we really keep it simple and just leave empty cells under DEPOSITS and DEBITS and just type in your starting balance in C2 under BALANCE.  Let's say that's $100.00

 

We do not need any formulas in columns A (deposits) or B (debits) and you will just enter the values there as needed/when they happen.  But we need one formula in cell C3 to give you the running balance that formula is:

=C2+A3-B3

That says: take the balance on the row above this cell, add any entry on the same row in column A to that balance, and subtract any entry on the same row in column B; and all of that gives us our running balance.

 

You can then fill that formula on down for as far as you need to in column C, to deal with as many deposits/debits you need to enter now or in the future.

 

You can use other columns to track things like the date of the deposit/debit, what it was for, maybe check or invoice numbers, any thing you want.  So we might end up with a sheet that looks like this:

 

       A                    B                            C

Effects of disabling hardware acceleration
With hardware acceleration disabled, screen renders (drawing) is done using software rendering (WARP) that relies mainly on the CPU and uses limited or no graphics card resources. You can cause a lower frame rate and increase in the working set. Performance becomes more dependent on CPU characteristics, like memory, number of cores, and on how many apps are open, and can strain the CPU resources.
