Under what conditions would a "Full Scan" detect anything that - Microsoft Community

mooly say:

Under what conditions would a "Full Scan" detect anything that real time scanning and scheduled "Daily Quick Scans" might miss.

The only situation I can think of is if malware got onto a system for which a signature hadn't been released or is that to simple a view to take.
What I am really asking... is it necessary to do full scans or should the real time scanning and "heuristics" monitoring by MSE cover all bases as it were.  
Any "official" guidelines on this, and what do you folk actually do? 
rob koch say: The common 'rule' that developed back in the days when Windows Defender was originally in beta is that a Full scan is normally only required when you believe there might be malware present on the PC, for example after an attack has just occured.

The reason for this is that under normal circumstances, a quick scan should find anything 'known' to be malware, since it already looks in all of the places that active malware are known to be found.

However, it is possible that malware 'installer files' and/or 'droppers' might exist anywhere on a PC, though these are also where most false positives might occur, since they contain the most variance from the operational (installed) malware itself.  For this reason some may wish to scan occasionaly to detect whether any of these might be found, though of course the MSE real-time protection should detect these before they can be executed and also usually during the download process.

So really a Full scan is simply a backup or 'sanity check' scan, so as Jim mentions it's really up to each individual how often they wish to perform these.  I personally have daily Quick scans scheduled for when I'm not using the PC, but only perform a Full scan if I believe malware might be present, though I also ran an initial Full scan right after installation.

IE doesn't have any support for embedded TIFF files.  You could try a third-party extension called AlternaTIFF.  (I'm just aware of it and haven't actually tried it.)  Otherwise you can always open TIFF files in XP's Picture and Fax Viewer.   Tip: one way to do it is to use right-click E-Mail Picture... to OE and then in OE doubleclick on the attachment for the .tif file.
