How much security/privacy do you get with a free Outlook.com - Microsoft Community

jdandrews say:

How much security/privacy do you get with a free Outlook.com e-mail account?

I don't know if you guys are allowed to answer this question directly or not, but I thought it was worth asking.

I know that Microsoft's enterprise offerings are usually pretty solid, they have e-mail services that are good enough for organizations that have to meet FERPA and HIPAA requirements, entire companies use Office 365 Business Premium or an Exchange plan for their internal e-mail services, and in general it has a good reputation in that market. And given how seriously some of those companies take data security, they would probably never use Microsoft if they thought there was some kind of risk in doing so or they weren't satisfied with the security precautions taken.

But I've noticed that the opposite perception exists among many home users. They think big companies are stealing their data and reading their e-mails if they use a free e-mail account, and that their personal information will be compromised. Even when I explained about the enterprise offerings being relatively secure, there was still the perception that ordinary individual users won't have the personal information exposed in their e-mails respected by a big tech company even if business customers can manage to get that sort of deal by signing an annual contract.

So what I'm wondering is, would Microsoft still have most of the same precautions and safeguards in place with personal accounts that they do with enterprise customers to ensure that Microsoft employees don't see the contents of individual e-mails and that any algorithms scanning the e-mails don't send your personal information back to Microsoft? One example of something that freaked people out was the fact that something in Windows 10 helpfully told people when their packages were coming from Amazon after scanning their e-mails, and that made them think Microsoft received and interpreted information about where they shop and what they ordered.

My thinking is that it wouldn't make sense for Microsoft to deliberately make the data centers responsible for Outlook.com less secure or take a significantly more cavalier approach with Outlook.com users, and more than likely just has a uniform data privacy policy everyone is trained on carefully that makes them extremely wary of retaining personal information or looking at anyone's e-mails regardless of what type of account it is because they don't want the liability. Basically, whatever they might gain from having the personal information of home users gathered from e-mails wouldn't be worth the risk to their credibility in enterprise and education when they tried to explain that their data privacy policies and precautions are only lax with regards to home users but that they have much better precautions and policies to protect the people paying to use their enterprise cloud.

john korchok say:

When you select them, does the Picture Tools Format tab appear on the Ribbon, or the Drawing Tools Format tab? If Drawing Tools Format appears, it's not a picture, it's still a text box.
