    a. user say:

    It's easy to see who's on your network at any time. For one thing, you can always sign in to your router's administration console (see how below) and one of the first things you'll see is a network map, which shows you all the devices that are currently connected to the network.

    And then there are apps, like Fing, that will give you complete details on every device currectly attached to your network.

    Do these three things and you can be assured that no one can access your WiFi network:

    1- Password-protect the administration console.

    The administration console - which probably has more than one name - is where you find all your router's settings. It's actually found in your router, not your computer. You get to it by opening a web browser and navigating to your router's IP address, which often (but not always) starts with 192.168 etc. You'll be prompted for a user name and a password - that password has to be un-guessable and un-crackable. Anyone who knows or can figure out that password can reverse all the configuration steps you take below.

    2- Password protect the WiFi network.

    You probably know that your router transmits and receives information via radio signals, which makes it very easy for anyone to listen in. If you password-protect your network - with a different, un-guessable, un-crackable password - then no one can join your network unless they have the password. That means you'll need to manually enter the password to each device that you allow on your network.

    3- Encrypt the WiFi signals.

    Oh, this is so esoteric, you gotta love it. You should encrypt the data flying around in those WiFi signals with a method called WPA2-PSK AES. Don't worry, it's a setting in your router's administration console. (WPA3 will arrive soon.)

    With those three steps you are locked down, my friend, so unless the NSA (or the KGB) has their eyes on you, you're safe.

    "Forgetting" is something you do to yourself, not somebody else. When you tell your device to forget a network, you're telling it to not remember that network's password, which means you'll have to manually enter the password the next time you want to use it.

    spiritx say:

    Hi,

    Low cost 3rd party software firewalls are pretty much problems waiting to happen. They all
    have compatibility issues and offer only a few enhanced controls. In fact their issues often
    actually lower the overall security instead of raising it. The Windows Firewall has no compatibility
    issues and is extremely secure if you know enough to bypass most of the hype. Quality software
    firewalls cost thousands. Most routers have hardware firewalls built in and those are considerably
    better additions for the buck than any cheap/low cost software firewall.

    So I recommend you get rid of ZoneAlarm and stick with the Windows Firewall. Of course you
    should only have one active firewall as they will interfere with each other if 2 or more and that
    really lowers security and causes problems.
