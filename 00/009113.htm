rob koch say:

Looking back at an earlier thread of yours in which you initially had issues setting up MSE for a full scan, my guess would be that your PC is set to sleep after some period of time and is thus going to sleep during the period the scan is running.

This is why GreginMich had suggested setting the full scan to run during a time you were using the PC rather than at night, since there's nothing within MSE scheduler to stop the PC from going to sleep,  This must instead be programmed using a scheduled task to wake the PC as you mentioned, since this will also keep the PC awake during the period that MSE is running.

Since MSE is built as basic protection with minimal features, it appears that you require additional user friendly features to perform the things you'd like your antimalware to do for you.  People such as GreginMich, myself and others helping here either know how to use things like scheduled tasks or simply perform full scans manually, since that's really what the original developers of MSE intended as one of them told me in a meeting several years ago.  I personally only run a full scan manually on a rare occasion, with a quick scan scheduled to run every evening before I turn the PC off.

Near the end of your other thread you responded last August that you were considering a different antimalware to replace MSE.  Based on the above I would recommend that you try to find one that includes the scheduling and other features that you prefer.  Complaining about the way MSE performs these isn't going to change anything at this point, since Microsoft has moved on to develop for Windows 10 and left legacy products like those for older Windows operating system versions behind.

As an example of this, that web page you discovered with the security certificate warning has been "broken" now for at least a few months.  There's really nothing wrong with the page itself at all, the problem is with the configuration of the web server it resides on.  When you click a link that takes you to that page it tries to take you to an HTTP page which is unsecured.  However, once the page is reached something on the server is instead modifying the link and changing it to an HTTPS or secured URL instead.  However, since the server itself doesn't contain an SSL certificate, your browser detects this as a potential security failure and responds instead with the certificate security warning.

In other words, since the help page is simply public text and so has no need to be protected by using an SSL certificate based transport, there's really no reason for the HTTPS URL prefix to be used.  So someone involved in setting up that server simply messed up the configuration and created this entire pointless problem.  However, the simple fact that this issue has existed for months now and no one at Microsoft has bothered to fix it tells me that their focus is elsewhere.

Since the obvious push is on towards Windows 10 which includes Windows Defender that completely replaces MSE, I think it's clear where their attention has gone.  Note that with Win10 Defender though, there's no longer a separate menu item to schedule a scan, only a quick scan is built-in to the Windows Maintenance schedule.  Though you can still create a scheduled task to add a separate full scan, you've already indicated you don't like that solution.

Your other questions regarding Windows 10 and the newer versions of Office are better asked in the forums here created for those.  Most of them are probably already answered there, but since one question often leads to another I'd suggest asking those there instead.

rob koch say:

The best advice is typically from self-employed computer businessmen who've had a background in supporting computers for years, though their individual background may vary widely.  The big box stores are often manned by kids who may or may not know the broader concerns, but occasionally there are some good people there too, it's just hard to insure.

You already seem to have a good recognition of how such a person should talk to you, so I suspect you'd recognize them once you talk to them.  The key is finding someone local to be able to physically aid in initial setup as well as discuss the configuration you desire to the level you wish to support it yourself in the future.  If they aren't interested in doing this, they're not the right person or business.

I get the impression that you've typically done everything yourself to this point, so if you've been successful you already understand much of what you'll need, you just need some guidance to get past the changes that you're unsure about.  Most people who do this professionally would probably recommend this be done on an hourly basis, with some estimate of hours to complete the project.

Hourly rates can vary widely, but I've seen rates from below $50/hr. for simple installation work by techs, up to $150/hr. or more for security analysis done by network engineers.  Your needs should be somewhere in the middle of that range depending on other common hourly rates in your region for professional work.

If you can perform any repetitive work that may save you money, but if it's only you and your own PC, this may only take a couple to a few hours.  Often combining the work with the purchase of a new PC through the same business may help to absorb portions of the cost, but that depends on whether they resell and/or whether you want their hardware and further support.

Any truly effective business will work with you within reason, you just need to be clear up front about what you want so they can indicate what they can provide,  If they give you a bad feeling, walk away before you commit, since a few minute discussion of what you're looking for should be free.  Any sense they're just trying to sell you some hardware or software is a bad sign.

I haven't been doing this type of work recently, though I've considered it, but wouldn't like to try it remotely at least at first.  I feel more comfortable being in front of the person and equipment I'm responsible for even though the trend is moving towards online support as well.  If your location forces you into this situation, I'd examine the major online options carefully, but consider Microsoft itself since they tend to bundle support for the current operating system and major apps like Office.  If your business requires other major application(s), you may want to see whether anyone supports those as well, unless you feel confident in your own ability with them.

There's no single right answer here, since I've done various versions of these for businesses my entire career, either as an in-house employee, contractor or on an hourly basis.  What matters is your own level of confidence and how much support you'd like to have from someone else when your own abilities reach their limit.  Paying someone else makes sense when it saves you time you'd spend learning how to do it yourself and can potentially gain the specific knowledge about your own configuration to provide peace of mind as well.
