violet chen msft support say: Hi Larry,

Thanks for your reply.

Firstly, it’s not feasible to use rules to prevent certain emails from being journaling. Whether an email is being journaling is decided by the journal rule you create. If you apply the journal rule to all messages of a group, then the following emails will be journaling:

a. Emails sent to or received from this group.
b. Emails sent to or received from a member of this group.

Anyone else stumble on a similar issue? Any suggestions are appreciated.

john korchok say:

There are a few things going on here. You're getting the non-existent style Heading2_HC + +Body because of a Word setting. Here's how to turn that off:

  1. In Word, choose FILE, then Options.
  2. Click on Advanced.
  3. In the Editing section, uncheck Keep track of formatting. OK out.

The next thing to check are your style settings. Open Heading2_HC in the Manage Styles>Edit tab and click on Modify. In the 4th field down Style for the following paragraph, what is listed there? If you want a return at the end of Heading2_HC to give you another paragraph of Heading2_HC, then enter that name in the Style for the following paragraph field.

Finally, the fact that Word added +Body (Calibri) is an indication that your styles aren't keyed to your theme. If you intend to create theme-based styles, then first create a font theme that has the major fonts used in your style, Century Gothic being one of them (Themes run off the base font name, not a particular weight, so you can't set it to Century Gothic Bold). Then in the Font dropdown of the Style formatting dialog, scroll to the top and choose Century Gothic (Body) or Century Gothic (Headings). With this approach, it's simple to revise a set of styles at a later date by applying a new theme.

teylyn say:

For what it's worth:

Theme colors are the same across many Office products and can be applied to different elements in the different applications. Not all Office applications have exactly the same elements and not all Office applications have elements where all the aspects of the theme colors can be applied.

The dark/light foreground/background colors don't all apply to specific elements in Excel. One significant place where theme colors (or better: the order of the theme accent colors) really come into play in Excel is "dark 1" for the default text color in cells and  the order of colors in chart series. The first series will be formatted with Accent 1, the second with Accent 2, and so on.

All other color aspects are very straightforward and have been pointed out above. 

As to the things that seem to be missing or not documented in Excel literature:  Other applications that use the theme colors include Word, Powerpoint, Publisher and SharePoint. In SharePoint, certain screen elements are formatted with the dark 1 background color. Other elements are formatted with the dark 2 background color. If you change the dark 2 color in the theme, you will see the difference immediately because the whole look of the site changes dramatically.

If you change the dark 2 in Excel, you may not see any difference, because no element uses that color by default, as far as I know. Unless you have manually formatted elements to use that color, the color change will not be immediately apparent in Excel, but can be dramatic in PowerPoint or SharePoint.

One important aspect of themes is that they can be created in one application and then applied to another office application to ensure a consistent look and feel across all Office products.  Unfortunately, the theme colors and their make-up and application have not been documented very well.

I once put together a PowerPoint file to mock up how theme colors apply to SharePoint elements. There is no Microsoft documentation and I had to use trial and error to arrive at my results. Here is an article about that on my website.
