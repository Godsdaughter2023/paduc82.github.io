    a. user say:

    Bookmarks are not part of the operating system; they're part of a web browser. Each web browser stores bookmarks in its own way.

    For example, in Microsoft Edge your bookmarks are are stored in the so-called hamburger menu in the upper right corner. It's an icon with three horizontal lines. Click on that to open the Hub (it's official name) and once the Hub is open notice the star symbol on the left. That's where Microsoft Edge stores its bookmarks (which Microsoft calls Favorites.)

    Other web browsers store their bookmarks differently.

    If you are asking "where are Edge bookmarks physically stored in windows 10".... then answer is a bit complex.   Microsoft Edge no longer stores its favorites as individual files and folders like you've become accustomed to with Internet Explorer.   Instead they are now stored in a database (and not in the registry)

    %USERPROFILE%\AppData\Local\Packages\Microsoft.MicrosoftEdge_8wekyb3d8bbwe\AC\MicrosoftEdge\User\Default\DataStore\Data\nouser1\120712-0049\DBStore

    a. user say:

    My computer is completely up to date and my internet speed is as fast as it's always been.

    Your internet speed is provided by your internet service provider. My ISP is Comcast (ptui!) Microsoft has no ability whatsoever to make the speed of your internet connection any faster or slower. Your ISP is solely responsible for bringing the internet into your home.

    Once the internet has arrived in your home, your own local area network (LAN) takes over. The speed of your LAN can be (and probably is) very different from the speed of your internet connection.

    We know very little about your LAN (it includes at least one Ethernet cable,) but here's an obvious example just for the sake of illustration: If your internet speed (download) is 200 Mbps and the speed of your LAN is 100 Mbps, websites aren't going to arrive at your computer any faster than 100 Mbps regardless of how quickly your ISP is sending them to you.

    Bear in mind too, that the numbers quoted for network speed, whether the internet or your own LAN, are theoretical maximums measured under laboratory conditions. In real life the speeds are much slower.

    Something else: The speed of a LAN is limited by the speed of the slowest device active on the LAN.

    Your LAN is composed of hardware (routers, network adapters) and software (drivers and router configuration settings) that do not come from Microsoft. In fact, Microsoft has little influence on the speed of your network, and about as much influence on the internet itself, where Linux rules (hooray!)

    To make your network work as fast as it can on Windows 10, you need the fastest available network adapters running supported Windows 10 drivers and a router configured for maximum speed.

    If all this sounds very complicated, that's because it is. It's much simpler just to blame it all on Microsoft, but 'taint so. And if you think I'm just fanboi, let me assure you ... Microsoft is responsible for plenty of scr*w-ups that are much bigger than network speed.
