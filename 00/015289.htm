hansv mvp say:

Let's say the range you name "A1" starts in cell A2, and that the two dates you name "A2" are in cells B2 and B3.

Select the first range.

On the Home tab of the ribbon, click Conditional Formatting > New Rule...

Select 'Use a formula to determine which cells to format'.

Enter the formula

=AND(A2>=$B$2,A2<=$B$3)

Here, A2 is the first cell in the selected range, and as mentioned, B2 and B3 are the cells with the first and last date to look for.

Click Format...

In the Font tab of the Format Cells dialog, tick the Strikethrough check box.

Click OK, then OK again.

jay freedman say:
The macro in the first article you cited, from wordribbon.tips.net, will do what you want. As the article mentions, you may need to change the "Text1" to the actual name of the field in two places in the code (if the field's name isn't Text1).

However, the article assumes you know a few things that apparently you don't know yet:
  • If you're going to distribute the form as a document, you must save it as a Word Macro-Enabled Document (*.docm) by changing the Save As Type dropdown in the Save As dialog. If you will distribute it as a template from which users will create new documents, you must save it as a Word Macro-Enabled Template (*.dotm). The other formats will remove the macro during saving (although you'll get a warning message first).
  • To put the macro from the article into your document or template, follow the instructions at http://www.gmayor.com/installing_macro.htm.
  • To make it so the macro runs when the user exits from the field, (1) while the form protection is turned off, double-click the field to open its Properties dialog, (2) click the dropdown labeled "Exit", (3) select the name of the macro, and (4) click OK. That tells the field that the macro is that field's "On-Exit Macro".

As an aside, the article's method is limited to making only the first form field mandatory. It works because that first field is automatically selected when the document opens, so the user can't do anything else until the cursor exits that field -- and then the macro runs. If the field that's supposed to be mandatory isn't the first one, the user can simply click in any other field without ever entering the field that has the macro, and then the macro never runs. In that case, you have to use what the article calls the "more comprehensive approach" of catching the empty field when the user tries to save or close the document.


The article at support.microsoft.com that you cited has no bearing on the task you want to do.
