ken sheridan say: I'm afraid I don't have time at present to give your model the level of consideration it would need, but I've archived a link to the thread so I can take a longer look at it if an when time permits.  I can't give any promises, though.  It's always tempting to give a quick top-of-the-head response in cases like this, but that can do more harm than good.  Until one has systematically analysed the dependencies, it's not really possible to give a fully considered reply.

You've obviously given considerable time and thought to the model, and you are clearly familiar with the Information Engineering (IE) modelling conventions.  Bear in mind that an Entity Relationship Diagram (ERD) per se does not cover all situations, however.  It is not possible to tell from an ERD whether a table is normalized to Fifth Normal Form (5NF) for instance, as this is dependent on the 'business rules' in the reality being modelled.

You, of course, have the great advantage of an intimate knowledge of the reality being modelled, so are consequently in a far better position than we are to judge the model.  It also means that you can test it by the equivalent of Einstein's 'thought experiments', i.e. by imagining  as many possible real life scenarios as you can think of, and then considering how the model would represent these, and whether the representation could result in any update anomalies which would result in inconsistent information, or in the loss of information.  We, on the other hand, can only second guess what real life situations might arise.  So, put on your thinking cap, and throw as much as you can at the model to see if it stands up.

Here is my small contribution, mostly focused on technical db design.

NOTE: I did not go to school in USA or UK, and some concepts may be country-specific and over my head.

Typos:
StartingStuidentNumber

Naming convention:
Don't use spaces or other funny characters in field names (e.g. EnglishName (first only)).
Don't use unnecessary abbrevs like VNFirstName, GSLSREPUnits, GSPrice, ClsUnitMonths.
PrimaryorSecondary: I'm guessing this is a P or S. I would use boolean IsPrimary myself, and make sure there can be only one per person.
I don't know what a "WardTown" is. Bing doesn't either.
Don't use Reserved Words like Invoices.Date and Comments.DateTime.

Other:
I do like your JoinDate/Dropdate approach. It is very common and should work well.
Not sure I am thrilled with different tables for Students/Trainers/Teachers/Contacts. Someone may be in all 4? Consider Persons, with PersonType, and perhaps a few 1:1 related tables for type-specific fields (e.g. TeachingCertificateExpirationDate (required, datetime)).
The Months table goes a bit far for me. What is the benefit? Same with DaysOfTheWeek.
Schools.AccountManager should probably be AccountManagerID linked to Persons.
ClsUnitMonths: I don't understand what there is to store at the month level, rather than at the Class level. Maybe this is just an awkwardly named table. Consider "MonthlySuppliesDelivery".

None of the above should be construed as "this is a bad db design", in fact I consider it in the top 10% of what I have seen in my 25 year career.
