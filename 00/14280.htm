What is the formula for calculating payment , when i know interest - Microsoft Community

robnicolle say:

What is the formula for calculating payment , when i know interest rate, present value , length of the loan

What is the formula for calculating payment , when i know interest  rate, present value , length of the loan

joeu2004 say:

You also need to know the frequency; typically, monthly.  In that case, if B1 is the loan amount ("present value") as positive value, B2 is the

=PMT(B2, B3, -B1)

Note the sign of the loan amount (-B1).  The key is:  assign a different sign for cash inflow and cash outflows.  The choice of signs is arbitrary.  I choose signs so that the financial function always returns a positive value.  That's just a personal preference.

That formula assumes that the final balance is zero.  If there is a "balloon payment" (final balance), enter it into B4 as a positive value, and use the formula =PMT(B2, B3, -B1, B4).

Those formulas also assume that payments are at the end of the period (i.e. end of month).  That is typical.  However, for car leases and such, the payment is at the beginning of the period.  In that case, use the formula =PMT(B2, B3, -B1, B4, 1).

Finally, the PMT function usually returns an amount that is not rounded, because of course the function does not know how to round the amount.  You can display the result rounded to the precision that you want (typically 2 decimal places).  But bear in mind that the actual value is still not rounded.

Alternative, you use the formula =ROUND(PMT(B2, B3, -B1, B4), 2).  But if you do that, any amortization schedule that refers to that result will not have a final balance of zero or the balloon payment.

joeu2004 say:

PS....  I forgot to discuss the periodic interest rate in B2.

Typically, you are given the annual rate.  But in the PMT formula, you must use a periodic rate that matches the frequency of payments.

There several common ways to convert an annual rate to a monthly rate (for example).  The lender needs to tell you how to do that; or you can rely on regional custom or regulations.  The latter might vary depending on the type of the loan, namely a mortgage vs. other loans.

In the US and many other countries, the annual rate is always divided by 12.

In the UK and Canada, non-mortgage rates are typically divided by 12, I believe.

But in the UK and EU, mortgage rates might be compounded, following EU rules.  So the monthly rate would be

(1+annual)^(1/12) - 1.

In Canada, mortgage rates a compounded semiannually (!).  So the monthly rate would be (1 + annual/2)^(1/6) - 1.

That is why I prefer to put that calculation in one place, namely B2.
