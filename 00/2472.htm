john mcghie say:

Hi Chris:

We are in very early days with development for Office 2016 for Mac.  There is no serious IDE available yet, and the built-in VBA IDE is not really up to speed for commercial purposes.

Much of what works on the PC will not work on the Mac.  You will rapidly become expert at "Conditional compiles":

#If Mac then

   Mac code goes here

#Else

   PC code goes here

#Endif

The # prefix is a compiler direction to conditionally compile code.

There are a variety of built-in constants and variables such as Ver and Version that enable your code to discover what game we are playing.  The Version constants will irritate you endlessly by returning a string you will have to parse :-)

Ron De Bruin has a wealth of information here:

http://www.rondebruin.nl/

Other than that, anything you see published by Rich Michaels is well worth a read: Rich is a commercial ISV operating cross-platform. He also appears in here when he gets time.

For commercial work, you probably can't go past Visual Studio and MSDN.  Serious developers speak very highly of the Visual Studio IDE and you can get "lite" versions that support only Office development.  Probably safe to say that you MUST have Visual Studio as one of your tools if you want to make money.

The entry point for Office development on MSDN is here:

https://msdn.microsoft.com/en-us/library/office/gg264383.aspx?f=255&MSPPError=-2147217396

Microsoft is busy moving a lot of its Office development resources to GitHub.

Sadly, the piecemeal nature of VBA documentation is an historical artefact.  It will drive you crazy with trivial and meaningless examples, some of which don't even work.  It does it to us too...

It is perhaps worth noting that Microsoft Office is a large framework of C++ modules, many of which have multiple entry points, and many of those entry points are publicly documented. If you find any that are not publicly documented, using them is very high-risk: they may suddenly change or disappear.

So each of the development languages are actually pulling the strings on a C++ module entry point. You can write in whichever language lights your fire to achieve the same result, and Visual Studio attempts to make that easy.

Visual Studio treats Office as an object-oriented, multi-threaded, multi-user, multi-bitness environment that richly rewards good design, clear documentation and commenting, and structured programming techniques. And savagely punishes those who attempt to find an easier way :-)

On the Mac, "Sandboxing" adds a whole new level of pain, and it is severe.  Applications are prevented from reading or writing anywhere except a short list of locations they "register" and from talking to each other.  You need to carefully read and understand the Apple documentation on sandboxing, and learn how to enquire which locations your target applications are able to access, and how to use your favourite installer to install components (especially the little AppleScripts you will need...) in their required locations. Unless you get this right, your application will fail, or it will issue prompts to the user requesting access, to which they will say "no" (or their login permissions will deny them) so your application will fail anyway, often in random and perplexing ways depending on which user said "no" to what.

Hope this helps

jim gordon mvp say:

Thanks for the reply.

For the near future (next 5 years or so) I think Visual Basic for Applications is the way to go for cross-platform Office add-ins for Word, Excel, and PowerPoint. VBA offers the largest overlap in terms of write-once work everywhere. That said, there are significant differences between Mac and Windows, as well as within various update versions of Office on Mac and PC. This will drive you crazy, but it is something that can be overcome.

Outlook for Mac can be programmed using Applescript, but not VBA.

VBA gives you the most exposure to the Office object model and file system.

Microsoft is on the path of exposing the Office object model to Javascript. Insanely, they chose to call Javascript add-ins by the same name as VBA add-ins: "Office Add-Ins." This makes finding documentation for both much harder to find and work with.

Javascript add-in support currently exists in Word, Excel, PowerPoint and Outlook in Office 2013 and Office 2016 for Windows. It also works in Outlook 2016 for Mac. During the Office 2016 Preview there was a build or two where Javascript add-in support was turned on in Word, Excel, and PowerPoint on the Mac, but it is currently switched off without explanation. My guess is that Javascript add-in support isn't quite ready. When it was turned on I didn't run into any problems with it, so I hope they turn it back on soon.

Once Javascript add-in support is fully turned on in Office for Mac the write-once work everywhere concept will probably be more true, but I don't think the Office object model will ever be as exposed to Javascript as it is in VBA. Also, Javascript runs at about 1/10th the speed as VBA. From what I can tell, this is not something that can be gotten around and is a drawback to consider.

Active-X support was never on the Mac and is deprecated on Windows, so avoid Active-X altogether.

John already pointed you to Rob DeBruin's site. He has dedicated many hours to overcoming cross-platform differences. John also pointed out that Apple's sandboxing scheme, which I consider to be worse than any virus or malware attack ever launched on the Mac, is something that you will need to contend with.

rich michaels - mvp (greatcirclelearning.com) say:

It's probably more semantics about who is blocking whom but the short answer is, REST calls can be performed but not like they are from any VSTO/Net application, VBA application on the PC or Mac Office 2011 application. On a Mac running Office 2011 they have to be performed from a MacScript command such as:

MyScript = MyScript & "open location " & Chr(34) & fName & Chr(34) & Chr(13)
MacScript (MyScript)

Where "fName" is the URL to the location being opened.

Under a sandboxed Mac Office 2016 application that command must be turned into an AppleScript command and uniquely installed into the special Application Script folder under the user's profile to be executed.

A real pain in the a@# which leads to a business ROI question for all to consider!
