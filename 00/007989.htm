Click Start or tap the Windows key and type default programs then hit <enter>. From here you have two avenues.

Method 1

In the new window click Set your default programs. If your 2007 Office programs are now listed in the Programs list on the left, select one (e.g. Word 2007) and then click Set this program as default at the bottom. Repeat for the other Office 2007 programs.

If your Office programs are not listed, click the Back button in the top left and move to Method 2.

Method 2

In the new window click Associate a file type or protocol with a program. In this new window scroll the list down to .docx and double click it.

%PROGRAMFILES%\Microsoft Office\Office12

... and select winword.exe. Repeat for other Word document and template types (e.g. .docm, .dot, .dotm, .docxml, etc).

Repeat the same process for the Excel file types, (e.g. .xlsx, .xlsm, ,xlt, etc), using excel.exe.

Phones Dead Why Make MY PC RUN LIKE A PHONE? - Microsoft Community

tk-tim say:

Phones Dead Why Make MY PC RUN LIKE A PHONE?

With Microsoft all but admitting defeat with their latest news about getting out of the phone business then why, I ask you, are they still insisting on making my desktop operate like a phone?

Now don't get me wrong, there are a lot of nice little things in Windows 8 and Windows 10 for those that use a device with a touch screen.  I enjoy my Surface while traveling.  But, when I'm back in my office trying to run business applications, I find the OS really gets in the way.  Not so much the OS, but the interface.  Let me explain.

If you read the posts, complaints, and suggestion about Windows 10 you quickly come to the conclusion that it really all boils down to two things.  One, the Start  Menu and two the look of a flat lack luster menu system.  Sure there are bugs and issues where things don't work, but these can be fixed.  But you cannot fix a design issue. So lets look at these two things and what MS should do:

Start Menu:  For any user, this is probably the most critical feature of the OS.  So, why all the issues?  First, it depends on how you're using the system.  If you are using Windows on a touch screen then you probably enjoy many of the features of the new Start Menu.  If you're just reading emails, social media'ng, using the Internet, FB'ng then you're probably happy.  Most likely 90% (or more) of the consumer market probably fit into this.   They run a few applications and many find it easier and better than any other version of Windows.  Fine, no argument there.

The second group of people are the so called power users or business centric users.  These users, for example, will be using their computer for much more.  They might have 100's of applications loaded, operate in a domain, and do much more than the average consumer or simply they may use their PS to operate a business.  This group of users isn't or doesn't want nor require all the social media and apps which Windows 10 offers.  Basically, in these cases the user finds the interface lacking.  I suppose, there are those that will argue how they can do all in Windows 10, but lets be real clear here.  If anyone at MS really looked at this, they'd boil it down to at least two user camps.  Those that want a Metro Apps interface and those that want a Menu Interface (as in Windows 7).

You don't have to take my word for it, if you read the feedback, it doesn't take long to discover exactly that.  So then why doesn't MS offer you a choice?  Offer something that a user can turn on where they can select the new Menu System or a Win32 Traditional Menu?  Why is it so hard to accept this simple fact?  They have the source code to do exactly that and the average intern can see these are the at least the two basic Use Case requirements from reading consumer feedback.

Look & Feel:  Just like the Start Menu, you love it or hate it, but just like with the Start Menu Microsoft can offer more than one style can they not?  Many find the flat appearance tough to read and work with while on the desktop.  Again, it is fine for others like me when I use my Surface, but on my desktop it just doesn't work as well as something like an Aero Theme would.

Summary:  Microsoft has lost the tablet war and phone war, I would argue that tablets are dead anyway because of cell phones.  iPhone and Android devices pretty much have killed the tablet market.  Sure, there are still many tablet users but sales have slowed as users have adopted and embraced their cell phones.  You can do everything on the Smart Phone that you can do on a tablet, just with a smaller screen.

What isn't dead is dual devices, all in ones, and desktops/laptops.  Devices like the Surfaces which acts like a tablet or laptop has a growing place in business and are very popular with consumers.  Sort of like having the best of both worlds.  All in ones, with touch screens have another appeal in the consumer market.  Lastly, the traditional PC or Laptop.  The PC will be around for years.  You cannot run the world on a Smart Phone.  You're still going to have PC's no matter how many cell phones are sold.  You will have PC users and businesses using PC's and these can and will be verify profitable for MS. 

So, if you're MS and you're going to compete, in this market then you can see why you'd want a touch centric menuing system and a flat look that renders on these devices.  But, those rules don't apply to many desktop users.  So why alienate desktop users?  MS has the source code to do both.  I suspect one would argue that it would increase costs, but alienating businesses is costly too.  Much more costly than adding back something that you already have and maintaining it.

I have heard many say, MS should offer a version of Windows for business.  But, that hasn't worked in the past.  Microsoft tried that with Windows 2000 and then they tried Windows Me as a consumer product and most recently the Surface RT.  All failed and I am not suggesting that approach.  What I am suggesting is for MS to make Themes available.  Themes or Styles is really what the requirement is.  Yes, the new Start Menu is an XAML interface whereas prior Start Menu's were compiled C++ programs, but there is not anything saying a Theme or Style cannot also include the menu system. 

If  MS fails to recognize the simple fact they are alienating many PC users then they will continue to have problems converting desktop user that prefer a more traditional interface, or businesses that do too.  You have to remember, many of us in business don't get a free update to Windows 10.   Therefore, if my company wants to upgrade to Windows 10 we have to pay for it.  The way it stands today, we will not upgrade our installed Win7 base.  On the good side, it will save us millions but costs MS millions in sales. 

Without the backing and upgrading form the business customer will be costly for MS, especially when you give away the OS to consumers.  Is there that much money to be made from the Windows Store and Cloud computing?  You could argue that if MS gets a hold of your data, email, pictures, etc. by giving you a free tool to access it then they can control and charge you monthly a fee for maintaining it.  After all, none of us use a computer or cell phone, for that matter, solely for the menu system or style.  We use the device for access to data.  Data about what to buy, where to eat, sales, marketing, etc.  Just like Excel, you don't use it because you like the menu and only use it for that.  Excel is worthless and only becomes useful when you put data in it. 

Is MS looking at the access device now as throwaway with whats being important is the data?  For he that holds the key to the data kingdom will rule the world?  If this is true, they might be ahead of the pack.  After all, if you can make hardware for less than $100 with a free OS, it is hard to make money.  However, if this cheap access device is now disposable then what become valuable and profitable is keeping the data.  If you can secure and wrap this up in one central place then you can charge monthly service fees to access it.   Only time will tell, but meanwhile why abandon your business customer and others when you can simply offer themes?

ronaldoonenil say: You could do it with a macro like this

Sub UpDate_Pivots()
    Dim i As Integer, j As Integer
    
    For i = 1 To Sheets.Count
        For j = 1 To Sheets(i).PivotTables.Count
                Sheets(i).PivotTables(j).PivotCache.Refresh
        Next j
    Next i
End Sub
