    Open your registry and find or create the key below.

    A. Go to start and regedit and press enter.

    B. HKEY_CURRENT_USER\Software\Microsoft\Notepad

    E. Restart Windows for the change to take effect.

    lemp say:
    When you downloaded the zip file and right-click to "Extract all," just pick some handy location in which to put the extracted folder.  I generally download to my Desktop and then the extracted folder will appear on the Desktop, but it doesn't matter ... as long as you remember how to get to that extracted folder.  So, if you downloaded XP_Fax.zip to your Desktop, after you run the extraction wizard, you'll have a folder named XP_Fax on your Desktop (in addition to the original XP_Fax.zip file).

    If you start by going to Control Panel > Add or Remove Programs and then click the icon in the left pane to "Add/Remove Windows Components," you should get to the "Windows Components Wizard" like this -->


    It would be extremely surprising if NONE of the boxes was checked, but if the box for "Fax Services" is not checked, then that probably means that it didn't get installed (because the files were missing).  If you check the box and click the Next button, you should get an "Insert disk" screen, where you click OK and then a "Files needed" screen.




    In the "Files needed" screen, click the Browse button and navigate to the folder you extracted.  Thus, if you saved the extracted folder on your Desktop, you would browse to C:\Documents and Settings\<yourusername>\Desktop\XP_Fax.  When you click the OK button, the Wizard should install Fax Services.  Click the "Finish" button. 

    To configure things, see this article --> http://support.microsoft.com/kb/306550

    Good luck.  Even if you get Fax Services installed, whether you are ultimately able to send and receive faxes depends on whether the notoriously finicky XP Fax Services likes your particular fax modem.  The stock answer for problems at this point was to check and ensure that your fax modem was included in the Windows [XP] Hardware Compatibility List.  Unfortunately, that list is long gone, and as far as I'm aware, you can't even find a copy of it using the Internet Archive (aka the Wayback Machine).  The odds are better if you have a fax modem implemented all in hardware rather than the less expensive "winmodems" that came along later.  But give it a shot.  And don't forget that you must connect your fax modem to a plain old analog telephone line (trying to use VoIP -- Internet phone -- is asking for a whole additional can of worms to deal with).
