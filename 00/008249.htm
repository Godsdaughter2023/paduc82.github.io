suzanne s. barnhill say:

Word 2010 and earlier have a Compatibility Option, "Suppress Space Before after a hard page or column break." By default, this option is not enabled in those versions. Unfortunately, Word 2013 and above enable this option by default and don't give you a choice. So the only (simple) recourse you have if you want this to work (and I ignore kludges such as adding an empty paragraph) is to save the document in Word 2010 format. This is not an option that Word provides in the Save As dialog; it requires a simple one-line macro (courtesy of Jay Freedman):

  1. Press Alt+F11 to open the VBA editor.

  2. Press Ctrl+G to open the Immediate window at the bottom of the editor.

  3. In that window, paste this line and then press Enter:
    ActiveDocument.SetCompatibilityMode wdWord2010

  4. Close the VBA editor.

  5. Go to File | Options | Advanced and scroll to the bottom.

Publisher produces fixed width pages, that are left justified by design. Though we have figured out a workaround to center the pages, there is no reason why you can't produce a good looking left justified page.

Reference: Understanding background padding in a Publisher web (aka white space):
http://msmvps.com/blogs/dbartosik/archive/2006/01/07/80563.aspx
 
I don't quite understand what you mean by the text getting cut off. As a general rule your web publication should be one of the two default widths of 760 or 984 pixels wide as per the web publication templates. Do not make your pages wider in an effort to fill your extra wide local montior screen as most people who view your pages will not want to be forced to scroll horizontally. For example I rarely look at a page in a browser window wider than 800 pixels even though I have a large monitor. Even if I viewed pages in wider windows I wouldn't want the text boxes to be that wide. Why do you suppose a newspaper column is so narrow and doesn't extend the full width of the paper?
 
With the page width set at 760 or no more than 984 pixels then you do not allow your text boxes to overlap into the gray scratch area off the page as that text will not render when you preview the page or when you produce your web site pages. Perhaps that is what you are experiencing. You would probably benefil from running the Design Checker. Go to Tools > Design Checker. This will tell you if you are overlapping into the scratch area or making other fundamental design or layout errors.
 
The page length is variable in Pub 2003 and 2007 and will be as long as the content on the page. Even though the default length of a web publication  is 4,608 pixels in Publisher, and can be made as long as you want, when you do a web page preview or publish your site, Publisher will automaticaly truncate the page immediately after the last design element whether that be a text box, image, etc. This is an advantage as you do not have to force your design or layout to fit a specific page length as you would with a print publication. The background color/shading/image will fill the page length and the background padding as per the article I referenced above.
 
 
It sounds like you would benefit from some additional reading about how web publications are different than print publications. I would start with the article:
 
Prepare, publish, and maintain your Publisher Web site: http://office.microsoft.com/en-us/publisher-help/prepare-publish-and-maintain-your-publisher-web-site-HA010094760.aspx

http://office.microsoft.com/en-us/publisher-help/CH010079337.aspx
 
After you read over those articles and get your basic web publication set up properly if you still want to go the extra step of centering the content, then post back, or read the material in the other message thread about centering your publication. But for now, concentrate on the basics.
