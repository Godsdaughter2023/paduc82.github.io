Use the absolute reference $B$9 to keep B9 "constant" when you to copy across "columns and rows".

However, you might want to use $N76 if you want that to become $N77, $N78 etc as you copy the formula down a column.

jay freedman say:

First you need to know that when you insert a cross-reference, Word creates a hidden bookmark at the location of the thing you're referring to. The name of the bookmark starts with an underscore and "Ref" followed by a long random number. You can see the names of these bookmarks by opening the Insert > Bookmark dialog and checking the box for "Hidden bookmarks".

Apparently, what has happened to some of your figures is that the bookmark covers not just the label and number but the entire figure and caption. I'm not sure how or why that occurs, but here's how to fix it:

  • First make a list of the incorrect cross-references and their bookmarks. Select a reference that shows the whole figure, and press Shift+F9 (or right-click and choose Toggle Field Codes in the menu. Write down the number following "_Ref" in the field code. Do that for each of the other incorrect cross-references.
  • Open the Insert > Bookmark dialog and check the "Hidden bookmarks" box.
  • Click on one of the bookmark names from the list you made, and click the Go To button. That will select the whole figure and caption. Close the Bookmark dialog. (You can skip this step if you can easily identify and locate the figure that corresponds to the cross-reference.)
  • Reduce the selection to just the label and number -- the items you want in the reference.
  • Reopen the Bookmark dialog, click again on the same bookmark name as before, and click the Add button.
  • When you've resized all the incorrect bookmarks in your list, update all the fields by pressing Ctrl+A (select all) and F9 (update fields). The cross-references should now all be correct.

For x = 1 To Worksheets.Count
    For y = x To Worksheets.Count

              Sheets(y).Move before:=Sheets(x)
         End If
    Next
Next
End Sub
