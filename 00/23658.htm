alex chen msft say:

Hi Nachiket Barve,

Thanks for clarifying your concern.

After user sign in his Office 365 account, there will be a token created on his PC/Mac, this is like a key to the Office products. If the PC is connected to the network, the token will be renewed timely.

But if the PC/Mac is disconnect from network 1 month. The token will be expired, and the user won't have access to Office products anymore.

In your scenario, since you have deleted the Office 365 account for that user, his token won't be renewed, and he will lose the access to Office in 1 month.

It won't have any influence on your company. It's just a local activation mechanism for Office 365.

Regards,

Alex Chen

alex chen msft say:

Hi Nachiket Barve,

 

My apologize, I think I didn't make it clear in the last reply.

When the deleted user's PC is online, the token will try to renew itself daily, but since you has already deleted that Office 365 account, it won't be renewed successfully. Thus, that activation token will stayed for a month and after that it will be expired, the user will be prompt to activate his Office 365 for continue working.

 

The point is, no matter his PC is online or off-line, as long as the token couldn't be renewed in 1 month for any reason, he will lose the access to Office 365 desktop application.

hansv mvp say:

Make sure that the ranges - in your example Site, ReviewNum and BMIYN - have the same size. If one of the ranges has more or fewer cells than the others, you'll get #VALUE!

 

Also be alert for relative cell references. If a range is defined as =Sheet1!$G$2:$G$50, it is fixed. But if it is defined as =Sheet1!G2:G50, it is relative to the active cell: when you select a different cell, the range will shift.
