But at face value, it simply implies that your investment resulted in a loss.

Ravins Singh wrote:

Q2: In my case, investment occures in first 3 years, and revenue also started after end of first year in regular interval, plz suggest how to use NPV

An important "feature" of Excel NPV is:  the first cash flow is discounted.  Typically, that is not what we want.  So if your cash flows are in B1:B10, with the initial investment in B1, the formula should be =B1+NPV(R1,B2:B10), not =NPV(R1,B1:B10), where R1 is the discount rate.

Another important requirement for most Excel financial functions:  inflows and outflows should have opposite signs.  For example, B1 (invesment) might be negative, and B2:B10 (revenue) might be positive.

The mathematical IRR is a rate that causes the NPV to be relatively close to zero.  So it is unclear why you are calculating both.

Ravins Singh wrote:

Case-1: Calculate the present vaalue of CAPEX also and use it as initial investment

or

Case-2: First year investment be taken as initial investment, and further investment should be taken care by that year revenue

I don't understand the relevance of that to your question.  Looks like part of a homework assignment, and you have not provided sufficient specifications from the assignment for us to offer any guidance.

But that might answer my question above:  why are you calculating both NPV and IRR?

Are you calculating the NPV of capital expenditures, as suggested in Case 1?

If yes, then ironically, you might indeed want to discount the first cash flow, if the expenditure occurs at the end of year 1, and you want to calculate the initial investment in year 0 (or beginning of year 1).

Bottom line:  You really have not provided sufficient context for us to offer dispositive guidance.
