Option Explicit

Sub Scenario()
  Dim i As Integer, j As Integer
  Dim r As Range
  Dim S As Shape, n As Shape
  Dim d As Series

  'Create some data in each sheet
  For j = 1 To Worksheets.Count
    With Worksheets(j)
      .Range("A1:B1") = Array("X", "Y")
      For i = 2 To 10
        .Cells(i, 1) = i - 1
        .Cells(i, 2) = Int(Rnd * 10)
      Next
      Set r = .Range("A1").CurrentRegion
      Set r = r.Offset(1).Resize(r.Rows.Count - 1)
      .Names.Add .Name & "!XX", Intersect(r, .Columns("A"))
      .Names.Add .Name & "!YY", Intersect(r, .Columns("B"))
    End With
  Next

  'Create a normal chart
  Set S = ActiveSheet.Shapes.AddChart
  S.Left = 0
  With S.Chart
    .ChartType = xlXYScatterSmooth
    .SetSourceData Source:=Range("A1").CurrentRegion
  End With
  Set n = S
 
  'Create a chart with a named range
  Set S = ActiveSheet.Shapes.AddChart
  S.Left = n.Width
  With S.Chart
    .ChartType = xlXYScatterSmooth
    Do While .SeriesCollection.Count > 0
      .SeriesCollection(1).Delete
    Loop
    .SeriesCollection.NewSeries
    With .SeriesCollection(1)
      .Name = "Named"
      .XValues = ActiveSheet.Name & "!XX"
      .Values = ActiveSheet.Name & "!YY"
    End With
  End With
End Sub

Sub RenameSeries()
  Dim CO As ChartObject
  Dim S As Series
  Dim i As Integer, j As Integer

  Set CO = ActiveSheet.ChartObjects(1)
  Set S = CO.Chart.SeriesCollection(1)
  For i = 1 To Worksheets.Count
    If InStr(S.Formula, Worksheets(i).Name) > 0 Then Exit For
  Next

  j = Application.InputBox("Refer to sheet (1.." & Worksheets.Count & ")", _
    Default:=i, Type:=1)

  For Each CO In ActiveSheet.ChartObjects
    With CO.Chart
      For Each S In .SeriesCollection
        S.Formula = Replace(S.Formula, Worksheets(i).Name, Worksheets(j).Name)
      Next
    End With
  Next
End Sub

rohn007 say:
Maintaining printed manuals (that change frequently) with Word is really difficult. I started doing that 20 years ago and it is a PITA.

Suggestion 1: Migrate your users to electronic copies of the document.  Discourage printing of the manual. Can they do their jobs using the manual on screen?  I know that many people find that a hard leap to make. So you have to show then how switching to a e-document can make their job easier:
  • show them how easy it is to find "stuff" in an e-document when they know keywords (add requested keywords).
  • Show them hyperlinking (create hyperlinking in the document to make their jobs). 
  • Show them how to use the Navigation pane
  • Show them how to use Outline View. 
  • show them How they can easily make suggestions to improve the manual, quickly.(ie create a change / suggestion process)
  • show them how quickly those changes can implemented and propagated
  • it's a "green" initiative ("save a tree") to cut down on paper consumption (guilt them into acceptance)
  • it saves the company money

If you decide to go with e-copies you have to decide how they are distributed

  • individual copy to each user
  • central LAN based copy
  • Inter/Intra net

What for Are the copies they get access to:

  • are those copies are editable, bad idea, ie Word docs
  • are those copies static ie individual copies of a PDF (XPS)


Suggestion 2: if you continue with printed documents, move away from continuous page numbering from start to end. Switch to chapter or even section page numbering within chapters.  Or even better yet, get rid of page numbering, just number headings.  Maintaining page numbers in printed manuals that you want to update individual pages in is a MAJOR PITA!  When you edit documents, page numbers change quickly. Unless you have a lot of forced page breaks (and lots of free/white space at the end of sections). You can force page breaks applying the "Next Page" attribute to heading levels.



Suggestion 3: especially for a printed manual (but even for an e-copy) keep and distribute a "change" log that tracks all changes made.  You can use the track Changes "change bars" feature to highlight changes, or even just use the highlighter.   Make sure the document version number is prominently displayed on the cover page, and even on all of the pages to make it easier for people to recognize when they have an out of date copy.  I also like to use date printed in the footer.



Suggestion 4: download a copy of this book. It is useful for editing books:

Macros for Writers, Editors and Proofreaders

by Paul Beverley, LCGI

http://www.archivepub.co.uk/book.html


suzanne s. barnhill say:
While this method of updating manuals is deprecated (see http://word.mvps.org/FAQs/Numbering/ChapterNumber.htm), I would suggest that you save the original manual as a PDF. Acrobat makes it very easy to replace individual pages from one PDF with new/changed/corresponding pages from another PDF. When you have replaced the page, send the entire changed PDF to users, with instructions to print the specific page (or, if they prefer, they can reprint the entire manual). You could, of course, do the same thing with a Word doc, but the risk is that pagination may be slightly different because of differences in printer drivers used by those 50 users.
