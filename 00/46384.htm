gerry c j cornell say:

Most system failures result in a BSOD (Blue Screen of Death) and a dumpfile report is created providing that the computer has been configured to create reports.  In a limited number of system failures no dumpfile is created. A report is generated when the computer is next booted and placed in the System Log in Event Viewer. The report is given an Event ID number 6008 and gives very little helpful information, save that the cause is unknown and that it occurred at the date and time specified. . A further report is placed in the Reliability Monitor with a red orb on the Miscellaneous Failures line and it is recorded as a Critical Failure. Unfortunately the Technical Details for this type of failure reveal no additional information.

 

The more common causes of this type of failure all relate to power failure. These can be because:
1. The user has pressed the Power On / Off button.
2. A power failure or a loss of supply to home or office where the computer is located. If this has happened it will often be obvious because electric lights are other electrical appliances switch off.
 3. A faulty electrical appliance on the same electrical circuit as the computer or the computer trips the contact breaker or blows a fuse and switches off the supply for safety reasons.
 4. The power cable between the mains power socket and the computer becomes disconnected at either end.
 5. A power surge or lightning strike occurs. Your computer should connect to an uninterruptible power supply unit, which then connects to the mains supply. If an incident occurs you will have to reset the uninterruptible power supply unit. Without one of these units in place a lightning strike or power surge can damage the computer beyond repair.
 6. A faulty power supply unit. The best test here is to remove the existing power supply unit and replace with a known good power supply unit having the same capacity. Most home users do not keep a spare power supply unit to hand.
 7. A damaged or disconnected power cable or motherboard connector within the computer. You can check the cable is properly connected but a damaged motherboard is not easily checked.
 8. If the computer is a laptop check whether the battery can be fully charged, holds the charge and was or was not connected to a mains supply when the failure occurred.

jadon paul wilke-heiming say:

HP OMEN X 900

bossdweeb say:

Cheap ? You can build your own for less. OK, you may not get such an unusual case, but building your own

also has the advantage of not being tied in any way to HP (or any other OEM builder).

The HP machine may already have Windows installed, but you still have to set it up to suit your preferences.

- I see they have several permutations of this model. So not knowing which specs you are looking at....

- 32GB of system RAM is a waste for gaming, and pretty much any other non-professional/business user.

Even 16GB will have 8GB sitting there doing noting on the majority of machines.

- While a graphics card with 4GB VRAM may be able to push most games to a 4K resolution 4GB is not really

adequate for true 4K (3840x2160) with some current and very likely some of the up and coming AAA titles.

- Liquid cooling on a graphics card is not really necessary, and when not gaming and doing simple tasks

such as internet, which runs the system pretty close to idle, liquid cooling can make more noise

than air as the  pump still runs at speed regardless of the radiators fan speed (more so with cpu liquid cooling).

- Xfire (dual graphics cards) is not worth the extra trouble unless you like tinkering and tweaking.

Gamers have to do plenty of stuffing around without adding an extra level of potential for issues that

Xfire (or SLI w/ Nvidia) bring.

- The hot swap HDD bays on the case are nice (a must, imo) if you have old HDD's that you can use for

doing backups on.

The case is good looking, and the separate chamber concept is good, but I like to be able to set things on

top when necessary.

Looking at the case in one of the reviews it looks like a mini-ATX or one of the other smaller motherboards.

I don't have enough images to see if there is only one (any ?) fan at the front, and it looks like the radiators

for the liquid cooling are the only exhaust .

Even liquid cooling runs better in a case with good air-flow, and while small form factor desktop PC's are gaining favour, I've yet to try a small case. But I'm certainly not ready to trust my hardware to a case with no input or

exhaust other than that on the gpu/cpu coolers.

- In the past HP and other OEM builders have used cheap low end power supplies which were rated to suit the

original configuration, and which often necessitated psu upgrade when upgrading the graphics card.

Not a likely scenario with this model, but worth pointing out ( It may also have a non-standard size psu ?).

- Building your own is not difficult. Due care, patience and carefully reading the admittedly often vague instructions

(or hieroglyphics) and a screwdriver is about all you need.

I you think you may want to build your own, check out Newegg or similar on line hardware dealer -

http://www.newegg.com/Store/Computer.aspx?name=Computer-Hardware

- Check out the dual chamber Corsair Carbide Air540 (using this myself atm), or the Air 240, if you want

a small form factor.

- Some home builders by low end cheap power supplies. Don't do this. Gaming machines can put a lot of

stress on the psu for long periods of time, so you need a good quality brand psu. A mid range model

is adequate, but avoid the bargain basement.

However, you can also go to high with the Watts/amps. This does no serious harm, but can cause the psu

to generate more heat and waste some mains power.

A psu runs most efficiently between about 25% minimum load and 85% max load, so if you have too much

power it will run below the 25% when the machine is idling along on the internet, etc.
