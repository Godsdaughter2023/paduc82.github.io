"Shared with me" in OneDrive for Business - Microsoft Community

manuela rusu-hack say:

"Shared with me" in OneDrive for Business

Technical Level: Intermediate

                                        Article written by Jose Morais, ODB Support Escalation Engineer

DESCRIPTION

This article will explain the "Shared with me" feature, and how can you work with it.

This feature is only available if you have an Office 365 subscription.

First off all, important to understand is that this is a View (and not a folder) of the files that have been shared with you, meaning you don't own those files. The Shared (iOS, on Windows Mobile or Android,) or Shared with me (on Macintosh or Windows Desktop) view, lets you see the files others have shared with you.

OneDrive for Business now gives you easy access to files and folders shared from external partners.

It allows users who have folders shared with them from other OneDrive for business users, to be able to take those folders offline in the same way they can take their own files and folders offline today.

When a partner shares a file with you from anywhere in Office 365 (including OneDrive, SharePoint, Word, Excel, PowerPoint, OneNote), it appears in your Shared or Shared With Me view, so you've always have your important documents at hand across all your devices.

SUMMARY

  • How to access the "Shared with me" view in ODB?

  • Can I sync "Shared with me" ODB folders to my local computer?

  • Where can I see the files from "Shared with me" on my local computer?

  • From wich app(s) can I access the "Shared with me" files?

  • Can I share the "Shared with me" files from OWA (Outlook Web Access)?

                               ========================================================


How do I access the “shared with me” view in ODB?

  1. Log in to Office 365 portal with your Office account email

  2. On the O365 Home page, click in ODB icon (At the top bar, select the Office 365 app launcher icon, and then select OneDrive.)

  3. On your left side, you will have the "Shared with me" view

  4. When you click it, it will show the files others have shared with you

Can I sync “Shared with me” folders/files to my local computer?

By default, when you turn on the sync client, it will only sync the folders from your own OneDrive for business files/folders.

However, you can also sync folders that other people have shared with you, which gives you offline access to these files in addition to yours.

The only requirement, is that the folder was shared giving you the "Can edit" permission.

  1. Browse to your OneDrive for Business.

  2. Click “Shared with me”, from the left panel.

  3. Click on the folder you want to sync to your computer.

  4. Click “Sync”.

  5. Follow the prompts to complete the process.

Where can I see those files on my local computer?

When synced into your local machine, this folder will show up on your computer under:

  • Windows OS

    Under the path "C:\Users\USERNAME\SharePoint\".

    Note: USERNAME is your local machine user profile name

  • MAC OS

    Under the path "Macintosh HD > Users > USERNAME > Sharepoint Tenant display name"

    Note: Macintosh HD is the default machine name

    USERNAME is your local machine user profile name

With which App(s) can I access the “Shared with me” files?

      • You can access using the OneDrive for Business Sync Client

      • You can also access the "Shared with me files", from Desktop and Mobile Office apps (Word, Excel, PowerPoint, Onenote), going to File > Open > Shared with me.

Example from Desktop Word:

Can I share the “Shared with me” files from OWA (Outlook Web Access)?

Yes you can.

  1. Log in to Office 365 portal with your Office account email

  2. On the O365 Home page, click in “Mail” – OWA icon

  3. Compose new email

  4. Attach a file

  5. The first option is to attach a OneDrive file. Go to “Shared with you”

  6. Select the file(s) you want to send, and then click on "next".

  7. Select the option you want to proceed.

m h g say:

What are the actual conditions? If any conditions involve multiple cell ranges, it's possible you may need to enter the formula as an array formula.

Also, if ConditionA is an equality comparison involving numbers with fractional parts, evaluating the formula step-by-step doesn't exactly match how Excel actually evaluates it. You can often simulate this difference in behavior pressing [F2] to go into Edit mode, selecting portions of formulas, and pressing [F9] to evaluate only the selected portions. For example, =SUM(B3:G3)=X99 could return FALSE, but evaluating the SUM alone gives 123.4567, evaluating X99 alone also gives 123.4567, and =123.4567=123.4567 evaluates TRUE. If you change the formula to =SUM(B3:G3)-X99, Excel may return a very small nonzero value.

If you're dealing with numbers with fractional parts, don't use equality tests A=B. Instead use tests like ABS(A-B)<5E-7 or ABS(A-B)<MIN(ABS(A),ABS(B))/2E12.
