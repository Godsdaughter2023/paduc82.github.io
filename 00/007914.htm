john mcghie say:
Hi Carolyn:

You could in fact say that Office 2011 will work "only" with TrueType fonts.  It will also work with OTF (OpenType) fonts, but only if they contain TrueType outlines.

There can be two further issues:

1)  The fonts will may not appear until after you reboot the computer.  OS X needs to pass the fonts to Mac Office, and that happens only at boot-up.

2)  The fonts won't appear unless they have been coded as Unicode fonts.  Old "Mac" fonts can't be used.

3)  The fonts may appear out of alphabetic order.  Word sorts its font menu by the internal name coded in the font: if that's wrong or absent, the fonts can end up right down the bottom of the list.

4)  Mac Office is not comfortable with "Font Managers".  I strongly suggest that you enable ALL your fonts and leave them that way.  Fonts "enabling" and "disabling" as you switch applications is a recipe for "interesting times" in Mac Office.

I also suggest that you define styles containing the fonts.  That way, you will never have to look at the stupid font menu again.  It is very slightly quicker to customise the built-in styles than to create your own styles, but the difference is only a couple of clicks.

Once you have defined the fonts you want into styles, check the "Add to Template" box that appears on the Modify Style dialog.  That writes the new styles back to your template, so they will be available forever more, and you will never have to find those fonts again.

When you are defining styles, the font list you see shows every font on the computer, it is not using the Word font list.

Hope this helps

jay freedman say:

There is no reason the Summary Information feature should highlight anything in the document. I think these two things are unrelated except that they might have coincidentally happened at or near the same time.

The Summary Information button is a shortcut to the Properties dialog that you can also reach by clicking Office button > Prepare > Properties, then in the top left corner of the pane clicking Document Properties > Advanced Properties and clicking the Summary tab. (This is one of the stupidest paths in the Word 2007 interface, but I digress...) It allows you to enter information about the document that you can also see in the file's Properties dialog in Windows Explorer, or use for searching.

Highlighting is a completely different issue. There are probably a half-dozen ways that text can become visually "highlighted" -- besides the actual Highlighter tool, the text could have background shading in almost any color, or it could be in a field (which is always gray), or the document could have been marked as restricted editing except in the shaded area, or...
