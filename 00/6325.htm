=DATE(YEAR(A1),MONTH(A1)+1,1)-WEEKDAY(DATE(YEAR(A1),MONTH(A1)+1,1)-3)

Hi

You have turned on Intelligent Services. The new grammar checker is now much better at recognizing sentence structure, idioms, etc.

The new version suggests that you get rid of potentially superfluous and idiomatic modifiers. In the cases you cited, I deduce that your qualifiers are designed to explain that your values are approximate. "Mainly reside" suggest that there are a few (an unknown quantity since none was specified), who don't reside in the northern part of the country. That's different from saying simply "reside," which might imply everyone resides there, although your readers may be intelligent enough to know that there are almost certainly exceptions whom you don't have to claim.

The choice is yours. If you think your readers need to be told that not everyone resides in the northern party of the country, keep the qualifier. If you think your readers will understand the concept without having to add the qualifier, then take the grammar checker's suggestion to drop the unneeded word "mainly."

The old grammar checker was not able to understand these nuances in language. The new one does!

"Near to" is a very odd expression. I would ditch it.

"Approximately 35-47%" - The "Approximately" qualifier on a statistic that is already quite wishy-washy yet very specific, makes it seem like you're not very confident of the value. If I read an important statistic and it was qualified with "approximately" I would not be convinced that the statistic is true.

"Almost half" - How much is "almost"? Why not simply say the statistic? This one depends on the audience and the formality of the data and what you are trying to say. I might be happy with "Almost" but it depends.

My point is that the new grammar checker alerts you to expressions you might want to give a second thought to before releasing your document. It's much more thorough than the old one.

a. user say:

Hi Peter,

Since you can't access anything with the phones software menus while trying to update it, you need to perform a hard reset by following these steps:

  1. Press and hold the volume down and Power buttons at the same time until you feel a vibration (about 10–15 seconds).
  2. When you feel the vibration, release the buttons, and then immediately press and hold the volume down button until you see a large exclamation mark.
  3. Once the exclamation mark appears, press the following four buttons in this order: volume up , volume down , Power , volume down . Your phone should now reset and restart itself. (It might take a while for the reset to finish.)

The hard reset operation will take a while to complete and will show spinning gears before it reboots your device to factory settings. Usually it will finish within 30 minutes but can, in some rare cases, take up to a couple of hours. Some older Nokia Lumia handsets have a bug where a reset never seems to finish. If that should happen to you, check this wiki article for a solution.

Let us know how it works.
