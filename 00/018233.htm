geoffbarraclough say:

Yes that is a silly way of creating a letter!!!

However, you can make it easier for yourself

1: Use a "Reference sheet" to hold all your static text

2: Use a new row for each major block of text in excel rather than trying to cram it all into 1 cell

3: char(10) will work to create a line break as long as the cell is formatted to wrap text

By way of an example, if you add a new sheet and call it Ref then in A1 on that sheet enter:

FEE STRUCTURE

In A2 on the Ref sheet enter:

The Standard Fees for a

In A3 on the Ref sheet enter:

Client Consultation

On your original sheet in A1 enter:

=Ref!A1

In A3 enter

=Ref!A2 & Ref!A3 & " are"

hopefully this will give you something to work on that will make the layout and concatenation a little easier than you have been finding it - as you can see - you don't need the concatenate formula - you can just use a & to join either a piece of text in a cell or some hard coded text together

That along with not trying to cram too much into 1 cell will help you out

You could use TEXT to format the number returned into the text string, eg:

Document opens with incorrect formatting. - Microsoft Community

nicoji say:

Document opens with incorrect formatting.

I am using Word 2008 for Mac, version 12..3..6. I am trying to prepare a poetry manuscript; it requires exact line breaks, consistent margins and formatting. But today my document opens with many errors--lines breaking where they should not; margins changed, some shorter some wider. I have corrected these errors, saved everything, closed and re-opened--only to find that some of the corrected copy is, again, formatted incorrectly. Yesterday, everything was formatted correctly, but today it is not. Can anyone please help me with this?
john mcghie say:
The first thing is to turn on your non-printing characters, so you can see what you are doing.

On the Standard toolbar there is a control labelled "Show".  It looks like ¶ Click it until you can see your paragraph marks.  You will also see little dots where the spaces are.  You need to see both.

Next you need to understand that a Word document is not like a sheet of "paper".  There are no strands of fibre to keep the letters apart, so unless you put something there, all of the letters will pile up into each other, which is how word-processors are designed to behave.

Next thing you need to know is how to create line breaks.  In Word there are three kinds but we will consider only two: paragraph marks, and carriage returns.

A paragraph mark ends the line: it also carries all the formatting for the preceding text: so it is responsible for the spacing and margins.

A carriage return simply brings the insertion point back to the left margin (if you are writing in English).  Word adds a "line feed" command to the "carriage return" to give you a new line.  But other than that, the carriage return carries no formatting at all.

In Word, we use styles to apply formatting.  A style is just a collection of formatting to which we assign a name so we can find it again.  In your case, I suggest that you use the built-in style "Body Text" for your poetry.  Word uses the "Normal" style for other things, you do not want to make changes to it if you can avoid doing so.

So customise Body Text to have the formatting you want.  Many poets use a "centred" justification, with a 12 point font, a "Single" line spacing, and 12 points of space after.  Unless you have a different idea, set that to begin with: you easily change it later.  The first benefit of using a style is that you only have to change the formatting once in one place and all the text in the document gets the new formatting without you having to do anything else.

Now to those line-ends.  At the end of each line, hit Shift + Enter to give yourself a carriage return.  This will end the line and give you a new line.

At the end of each verse, simply hit Enter.  That gives you a new paragraph.  It ends the line, and gives you some space between the verse just ended and the verse just about to start.

A couple of points:

1)  Always work with your non-printing characters turned on, otherwise you simply can't see what you are doing.

2)  Never leave a space at the end of a line.  A space at the end of a line is a signal to the display software that if it needs to it can join that with the line below.

3)  Do not attempt to use spaces to lay things out.  Because in modern software a "space" is completely disposable: the software can stretch them, compress them, or remove them altogether to make things fit.  That's probably what has been causing your problems.

Use a style to contain your formatting so it's consistent throughout the document and you only have to set it up once.

Know the difference between a carriage return and a paragraph mark, and use each for their correct purpose.

And no spaces!

Hope this helps
