diane poremsky mvp (slipstick.com) say:

NDR = Non-delivery report, aka, a "bounce message" telling the sender that the message was not delivered.

I, too, have several addresses that are spam magnets and I use a rule to forward mail with one of three subjects to my good address and everything else is deleted as it arrives on the server - Outlook never touches it.

For best results, these bounce rules should be configured on the mailbox (via webmail), not in Outlook. No need for Outlook to download junk - let the server reply and delete it as the mail arrives.

While you may enjoy bouncing messages, your server has to process them and any NDRs that come back from the invalid addresses spammers prefer - bouncing one message can increase web traffic 2 -3 times (more if you include a reply and the original message). Getting Outlook out of the picture is a lot more efficient.

How many potential customers actually go to the web site to place an order? You might do better with a new address and a better spam filter or by instructing people to use a specific subject and then forward those messages to the new address. Letting the server delete the junk as it arrives and using rules to forward mail with certain subjects will reduce server load and improve the experience for your customers.

Basically, an IRR cannot be computed for your example cash flows.

As we increase the guesstimated IRR starting from 0.01%, most of the discounted cash flows approach zero -- all but the first cash flow, of course.  Consequently, the NPV simply approaches the first cash flow (350,000).

To some degree, this is limited by the arithmetic limitations of the computer, due to the wide range of dates for the cash flows.  But a similar trend is apparent even if the cash flows are one day apart.  The NPV approaches 380,000 before we reach the arithmetic limitations of the computer.

As we decrease the guesstimated IRR starting from -0.01%, the NPV increases monotonically.

[EDIT] Note:  Here, the terms IRR and NPV refer to the financial concepts, not the Excel functions.





joeu2004 say: PS....  I wrote:
Basically, an IRR cannot be computed for your example cash flows.


Perhaps the MIRR would be useful to you [1] -- although not the Excel function by that name.

The MIRR is the rate that causes the (negative) NPV of the outflows (persumed to be negative) to equal the NFV of the inflows (presumed to be positive) discounted to present value.  Put another way:  the MIRR is the rate that causes the sum of the NPV of the outflows and the discounted NFV of the inflows to be zero.

That value is computable with your example cash flows [3], netting cash flows that occur on the same day (ergo, there is no net cash flow on 3/15/2007).

However, it requires that you know some "investment rate" for inflows and some "finance rate" for outflows.  Those two rates can be the same.  But usually there is some accounting basis for your choice (e.g. cost of capital).

As an example, if the investment and finance rates are zero, the MIRR of your cash flows is about 15%.  And with your cash flows, the MIRR increases as those rates increase, again assuming the two rates are the same.

If your cash flow dates are in B1:B26 and the net cash flows are in C1:C26, the MIRR can be computed as follows (I think it is good to use helper cells, at least initially, to aid in visualizing the mechanics of the MIRR) [2]:

E1, investment rate
E2, finance rate (=E1)
E4, NFV:    =SUM(IF(C1:C26>0,C1:C26*(1+E1)^((B26-B1:B26)/365)))
E5, NPV:    =SUM(IF(C1:C26<0,C1:C26/(1+E2)^((B1:B26-B1)/365)))
E6, MIRR:  =(E4/-E5)^(365/(B26-B1))-1

Note that the formulas in E4 and E5 are array formulas.  Enter an a mistake, select the cell, press F2, edit as needed, then press ctrl+shift+Enter.

As a check of the MIRR in E6, you can compute the discounted NFV as follows:

=E4/(1+E6)^((B26-B1)/365)

It should be about the same as -E5.

-----
[1] IMHO, the MIRR is not as meaningful as the IRR.  But reasonable people might and do disagree.

[2] [EDIT] The one-line MIRR formula is the following array formula:

=(SUM(IF(C1:C26>0,C1:C26*(1+E1)^((B26-B1:B26)/365)))
/-SUM(IF(C1:C26<0,C1:C26/(1+E2)^((B1:B26-B1)/365))))^(365/(B26-B1))-1

[3] [EDIT2] The MIRR is always computable as long as there is at least one net outflow as defined above.

Yes and no. ;-)

Use FIND to search for the value, but than use INTERSECT to get the intersection of the row and the column.

  With Sheets("Search")
    Set R = .Columns("A").Find(5187, LookIn:=xlValues, LookAt:=xlWhole)
    'Found?
    If Not R Is Nothing Then
      Intersect(R.EntireRow, .Columns("S")) = Sheets("Edit").Range("C17").Value
    End If
  End With
