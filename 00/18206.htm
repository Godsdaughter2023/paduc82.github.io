That NDR means that the recipient hosting gwo3.mbox.net. is detecting a SPF violation in the sender (your) Domain and rejecting the messages.

1-The solution in the “Recipient” Side is: as per this Information:
https://community.mimecast.com/docs/DOC-1369
Code:550
Reason: SPF Sender Invalid - envelope rejected
Description: The inbound message has been rejected because the originated IP address isn't listed in the published SPF records for the sending domain.
Recommended Solution: Ensure all the IP address for your mail servers are listed in your SPF records. Alternatively, create a DNS Authentication Policy with the "Inbound SPF" or "Reject on Hard Fail" option disabled. Messages that fail our SPF checks are subjected to spam and RBL checks, instead of being rejected.
1-The solution in the “Sender” Side (You) is
Setting up a valid SPF. Ensure that all sender servers IPs are listed on your SPF.
You can check and validate your domain SPF in this link.
http://www.kitterman.com/spf/validate.html
Example: a valid SPF record for Exchange Online is this:
v=spf1 ip4:192.168.0.1 ip4:192.168.0.2 include:spf.protection.outlook.com ~all

Where:
-Exchange online: include:spf.protection.outlook.com
-IPv4: ip4:192.168.0.1 ip4:192.168.0.2: are Usually, IP address of the outbound mail server for your organization.

-Doc:How Office 365 uses Sender Policy Framework (SPF) to prevent spoofing

jennifer st. onge say:

This problem occurs when "ISO 19005-1 compliant (PDF/A)" is selected when saving.

To fix, deselect that option when saving. The .png images will display correctly. 

  1. File > Save As PDF
  2. Click Options button
  3. In PDF Options (bottom), uncheck "ISO 19005-1 compliant (PDF/A)" 

Why? Transparency is forbidden in "ISO 19005-1 compliant (PDF/A)" formatted documents, so the transparent portions of .png and .tif files will be filled in.

I've been searching for this answer for years, and I finally found it on another answers.microsoft.com forum.

joeu2004 say: SummerRae wrote:
I tried it with the missing parenthesis and it still did not work. What finally did work was:
=TTEST(IF((EthosData[Strain]="D2")*(EthosData[Genotype]="K"),EthosData[No-Tone Dur]),
IF((EthosData[Strain]="D2")*(EthosData[Genotype]="W"),EthosData[No-Tone Dur]),2,2)

The two forms are functionally equivalent.  The significant difference is that the form above includes the table name EthosData.  That is necessary in some contexts.  The nested IF form would be the following array-entered formula (press ctrl+shift+Enter instead of just Enter):

=TTEST(IF(EthosData[Strain]="D2",IF(EthosData[Genotype]="K",EthosData[No-Tone Dur])),
IF(EthosData[Strain]="D2",IF(EthosData[Genotype]="W",EthosData[No-Tone Dur])),2,2)

Both forms are equally good, IMHO.  The nested IF form seems easier for many people to understand.  But the form above lends itself to being modified to effect OR logic, if the need arises.
