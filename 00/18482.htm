rob koch say:

Yes I can, since most of the normal, non-technical consumers I know can be confused by just about anything technical.

I was in the room at Microsoft (as a consumer MVP) when many similar issues were discussed with the developers back in the days of Windows Live OneCare and later MSE, and though those of us there were all fully capable of understanding any of these things the trend still moved towards either the removal or avoidance of most features that might confuse a significant portion of the user population.

I'd also seen similar issues with consumer confusion caused by advanced features menus in other 3rd-party security products, which though these were often retained, also lost market share as both Microsoft and many other vendors simplified their products.

So though I understand that a small, but vocal portion of the Windows user population has always preferred more advanced features and logging, the greater quantity of consumers have typically been better served by the opposite, which is the direction Microsoft has taken their entire product line the last dozen years.

It seems appropriate to me that the focus has remained on informing the user of the truly important items like recent or unresolved malware events, while reducing the useless "noise" from excessive information relating to quantities of files scanned or scans executed, which provide absolutely nothing in terms of real security value.

I believe that Microsoft's take on this is that it's always possible for those truly interested in such product enhancement (if that's what you believe these features to be) to simply pay for a 3rd-party product to perform them.  Since most consumer users see no added value and so the features only serve to consume additional resources, there's no reason from Microsoft to add and maintain them.
