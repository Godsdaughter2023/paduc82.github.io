When migrating to a new computer, I don't typically use the .olm route.

I use the migration assistant to import the Mac user account, which copies over the identity from the old Mac.

Just so you know, the identity is located in ~/Library/Group Containers/UBF8T346G9.Office/Outlook/Outlook 15 Profiles/Main Profile. When importing the account, the migration assistant also copies over the content of the ~/Library folder so it (in theory) works just fine, but since you didn't select that folder when you migrated, obviously, the data didn't carry over.

That being said, there shouldn't be anything wrong with going through a .olm. The only problem with the .olm is that to doesn't take over account settings, rules, etc. Just the data itself.

Now that you have some data in the identity, copying over the ~/Library/Group Containers/UBF8T346G9.Office/Outlook/Outlook 15 Profiles/Main Profile folder is indeed problematic if the emails are in the On My Computer section.

If I were you, I'd try two things:

  1. Export to the .olm again on the old Mac (making sure you have enough hard drive space for that). Maybe something went wrong the first time around, or you had forgotten to select something.
  2. Do it the other way around: export to a .olm from the new Mac, archive the ~/Library/Group Containers/UBF8T346G9.Office/Outlook/Outlook 15 Profiles/Main Profile identity, then copy over the identity from the old Mac and then re-import whatever new data you have in the .olm.

dan at it associates say:

First, please establish that the computer actually came with an OEM installation of Office Home & Business (H&B) 2010.

Background:  Most computers from major manufacturers are coming with the Office 2010 Single Image installed, which when first started by the initial user, can be activated as (1) the free Office 2010 Starter Edition, (2) a Trial of Office Home and Student 2010 or Office Professional 2010, or (3) in conjunction with the purchase of traditional packaged discs or a product key card, one of the following:  Office H&S 2010, Office H&B 2010, or Office Professional 2010.

 

If she cannot look on her invoice from the computer manufacturer and see a substantial extra charge for an OEM installation of Office H&B 2010, then it's almost a certainty that the computer does not have an OEM license but came with the Office 2010 Single Image that has been activated as a Trial of Office Professional 2010.

 

OTOH, if she can see that extra cost for an OEM license for Office H&B 2010 on her invoice, then what has happened is that the computer manufacturer has installed her Office H&B 2010 license but when activating such, she did not uncheck the box that when checked also installs Trials of the differential programs that make up Office Professional 2010.  So in addition to the programs that make up Office H&B (Word, Excel, PowerPoint, OneNote, and Outlook), which should be now be activated as perpetually OEM licensed product, her computer will have Trial installations of Access and Publisher.

 

You can verify my hypothesis by opening W, X, PP, ON, and OL and they should not have Trial in the title bar; if you open A and P you should see Trial in the titlebar.

 

She can uninstall the Trial and revert back to her OEM licensed H&B by opening Word and clicking on File>Help, and looking for the "Licensing Operations" button to uninstall "Office Professional 2010", which will uninstall the differential programs and leave her Office H&B 2010 intact.

 

See the first part of this article for some screen shots: http://office.microsoft.com/en-us/word-help/remove-or-uninstall-office-2010-trial-HA101972584.aspx

sivakumar7332 say:

You are not too late to discover this. You are right SharePoint is the backbone, whatever you call it. Onedrive for business is just a document library on a user's personal site. Best practice is that Onedrive for Business is kept for personal files, some large organization disabled Onedrive for business for their users.

In SharePoint you can create team site for team collaboration. This is where administrator comes into picture in terms of access control and data integrity.

SharePoint is an open platform, you can use it whatever you can think of, but to do it right, follow the best practice.

appriver_dpetree say:

SivaKumar is correct

Also, to answer your question or statement about the deleted files, you are correct. Only the end user that owns the OneDrive for Business has access to his "MySite". His "MySite" is where the OneDrive for Business is stored.

So, if the user deleted files in his OneDrive for Business, then you need to tell the End user to go and get them our of the trash as they are the only ones that have access to them.

The End user will just go back to the Document Library where the files were located, click on the Gear on top right, go to "Site Contents". On the Site Contents page, they will see the link for "Recycle Bin" on the right hand side (kinda up to the top. Anyways the end user can check the box next to the files and click "Restore". It will then restore those files.

Now, if you are a Global Admin, you can grant yourself permissions to the end users ODFB site. All you need to do is Go to the SharePoint Admin Center, then User Profiles, find the end user, then use the dropdown to "Manage Site collection  Admin". There, you can add yourself as an Admin on there site.

Now you can just browse by URL to the users Site and see everything just as they do.

Hope this helps
