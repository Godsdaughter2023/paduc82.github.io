XL 2010 - Personal Monthly Budget Template - Square Brackets in - Microsoft Community

stecyk say:

XL 2010 - Personal Monthly Budget Template - Square Brackets in Formula

I am looking at the Personal Monthly Budget template that is included with XL 2010.

As part of the template, it has named ranges "Table1" through "Table12". In each of the tables, there are formulas for the variance between different items. For example,

Mortgage or Rent   |   Projected   |  Actual   | Difference

In the Difference column, the formula is "=[Projected Cost]-[Actual Cost]"

As near as I can tell, "Projected Cost" is not a named range. That is, if I search for names, "Projected Cost" does not appear. Similarly, if I highlight "Projected Cost" in the formula bar, I get #Name? error.

Yet, if I highlight, "[Projected Cost]", then I get an array of values.

Can someone please tell me what the brackets mean and how I determine where the variable "Projected Costs" and "Actual Costs" are found?

Or, alternatively, please point me to an appropriate reference where I can understand this notation?

I am reasonably well versed in Excel, though I haven't seen this notation before. It looks interesting, and I would like to understand it.

jllatham say:

Watch this video

http://www.videojug.com/film/how-to-create-tables-in-excel

and I think all of your questions will be answered.  Basically the [Projected Cost] and [Actual Cost] entries are Excel's way of referring to cells in certain columns (those names are the titles for those columns in the table) on the same row.  Square brackets have always been used as a reference to relative addressing in the R1C1 formula formats and that's probably carried over into this way of creating those names.

 

Consider a simple formula in cell D2:  =C2-B2

Both the row and column are "relative" and if you look at the formula in R1C1 format it appears as:
=RC[-1]-RC[-2]

Notice that the column references are relative to column D and are enclosed in square brackets.  The R without any additional information is also relative for "this row".

If we change the formula to = $C2-$B2 then in R1C1 it becomes:
=RC3-RC2

And finally, if we make the entire formula absolute as: =$C$2-$B$2 then the R1C1 form is:
=R2C3-R2C2

Notice that in the absolute R1C1 form formulas, the square brackets are not present.

Although you cannot include other files than pictures in the standard email app, there is another way that does not require OneDrive and is very flexible.   4. Select one of your registered email adresses: a new email with this adress as the sender is created and all documents are already attached
  5. Write/copy the email text, the header and add the address of the recipient (can be done quickly with the +button if the recipient is in your People list), then you are ready to send it.

Although I agree that the email app should include the option to attach any kind of document, the above approach works very well and is very flexible too and centers around the well-implemented sharing process.
