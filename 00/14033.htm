IMHO, the only alternative -- setting the "Precision as displayed" (PAD) calculation option -- is much more error-prone.  And it does not solve some instances of the root cause of the problem.  For example, IF(10.01-10=0.01,TRUE) returns FALSE, even if PAD is set.  The only remedy is IF(ROUND(10.01-10,2)=0.01,TRUE), assuming you expect an accuracy of 2 decimal places (e.g. dollars and cents).

Explicit rounding does not simply "hide the issue" or "hide problems".  It is the correct solution when you expect results with a specific precision (i.e. number of decimal places).

It also allows you to pick and choose when "rounding suits our purposes".  In contrast, PAD applies to all cells that are not formatted as General.  (Technically, it even applies to General cells.  But since it rounds to 15 significant digits in that case, you might not notice.)

You say that rounding does not suit your purposes "in this specific issue".  I presume you mean in this particular instance.  For my edification, please provide a concrete example where a numerical "error" (arithmetic anomaly) of about 5.68E-14 is problematic, but you cannot use explicit rounding to solve it.

If you do not know what precision you require (i.e. it depends on the values involved), PAD probably also will not solve your problem, since that requires that you use a numeric format with a specified number of decimal places in order to be effective (to the degree that it is ;->).

Steve wrote:
In order to retain precise values, we kept going to longer and longer decimal-valued numbers. At a certain point the boss came back and said that we started with 2 decimal places, and he wants to end with two decimal places.

As you "look further into PAD", you might want to look at the comments in the discussion "What are the dangers of using Precision as Displayed?".

I am no fan of PAD, as you can see.  But one advantage:  it is easy to change the precision of a formula simply by changing the cell format.  That is also one of the dangers.

For example, you might compute a daily interest rate, but display it with only 6 percentage decimal places (or fewer).  Your intent might be for calculations to use the more precise underlying value.  But if you set PAD, the underlying value, not just the displayed value, will be rounded to 6 percentage DPs.

If you change your mind later and display it to 4 or 8 percentage DPs, it might have an unintended pervasive effect on other calculations that depend on it.  (On the other hand, that effect might be intentional.)

Often, we do not want interest calculations, in particular, to be rounded even if we display the results to 2 decimal places (dollar and cents).  Rounding the underlying value could have surprising results in amortization schedules, for example.
