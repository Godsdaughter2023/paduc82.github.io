ken sheridan say:

So, I repeat my original question: is there a way to quickly categorise these medicines in a new field?

I do not think you will find any experienced respondent here who is prepared to assist you in continuing a fundamentally flawed model in which the table is open to the risk of update anomalies, resulting in an unreliable database.  The process of normalization is a formal one  which eliminates redundancy by decomposition of a non-normalized table into two or more related tables.  The first three normal forms are formally defined as  follows:

1NF:  First Normal Form: A relvar is in 1NF if and only if, in every legal value of that relvar, every tuple
contains exactly one value for each attribute.

2NF:  Second Normal Form: A relvar is in second normal form if and only if it is in 1NF and every non-key
attribute is irreducibly dependent on the primary key.

3NF:  Third Normal Form: A relvar is in 3NF if and only if it is in 2NF and every non-key attribute is
nontransitively dependent on the primary key.

Loosely speaking, in the language of the relational model, a relvar (relation variable) equates to a table, a tuple to a row (record) and an attribute to a column (field).

There are higher normal forms, whose definitions you can easily find online, but in most cases a table normalized to 3NF will be normalized to the higher normal forms also.  This is not to say that these higher forms can be ignored, however.

The basis of the classical system of normalization (there are others) is that of functional dependency, and anyone attempting to design a relational database should be conversant with this concept.  Essentially it means that where, for the value of one attribute, the value of another attribute will always be the same, the second attribute is functionally determined by the first, e.g.  to give a simple example, the value London of the attribute city functionally determines the value England of the attribute country (I'm assuming for this example, albeit incorrectly in reality, that the values of attribute city are distinct).  Any table other than a Cities table of which city is a candidate key, will not be correctly normalized as the country attribute is determined by the non-key attribute city, not solely by the key of the table.  The table would thus be unreliable as a result of the redundancy arising from the transitive dependency of country on the key via city.  It is this principle which Scott has been attempting to impress upon you.  I strongly urge you to take note of his advice.
