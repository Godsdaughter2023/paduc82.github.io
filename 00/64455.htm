jennifer rowe say:

(edited to show more steps - I deleted an earlier post to ensure that what I sent was accurate :))

Hi JB,

You stated that that both kiddos are set up as Standard users - that's the problem.  Log into the PC and change the user types to Child (i.e. not standard or administrator), and they won't have the ability to disable the feature.  

  1. For Windows 8.1 here are the steps from this page: 
    • Swipe in from the right edge of the screen, tap Settings, and then tap Change PC settings.
      (If you're using a mouse, point to the lower-right corner of the screen, move the mouse pointer up, click Settings, and then click Change PC settings.)
    • Tap or click Accounts, and then tap or click Other accounts. 
    • Select the account you want, and then tap or click Edit.
    • In the Account type list, select Child and then tap or click OK
  2. Once the account on the PC is set correctly, you'll also want to click on the "manage family safety settings online" in that same window.  That will get your WIndows 8.1 PC connected properly to the service.  Clicking on the link will send you on the Microsoft family website on account.microsoft.com/family. 
  3. Once there, look for the account profile that shows your child's name and the PC name (i.e. not the one that shows the MSA - even though your child may use that to log into Windows, the settings changes you make there will *only* be applied to Windows 10 and Windows Phone devices only).  Each device that isn't running Windows 10 is managed as a separate profile on this page. 

One thing you should also know though - as long as they are able to get around it, their activities won't show on the activity report, and they can turn safesearch OFF, so can get to far more content than they probably should.  It might be a good idea to switch it back when you can.   

(and just FYI, child users *cannot* have admin privileges!).

entegy say:
M4A, MP3 and WMA are all standard formats supported by the media players found on Windows (well, except for WMA in iTunes). An attempt to convert any of them would actually result in degraded audio quality of the file. The absolute best is the uncompressed audio from the CD, but that would literally take 700MB or more of space per CD.

Between these three file formats, there really isn't a need to be "uniform". The best thing I can recommend for any music library is to ensure the music is properly tagged with album and artist info so it looks correct in players such as Zune, iTunes, Winamp, the Music app, etc. In terms of these three file formats, you would lose much more time and quality trying to convert them just so the file types are the same.

As to which of these three formats are the best between each other, I leave that to an audiophile. I simply do not know.

Overheating laptop could be due to performance issues. It might also happen if the power plan settings are not appropriate for the system’s processor.

Let’s try to identify the exact cause that is creating the overheat issue. Follow the methods:

Method 1: Please follow the steps to run the system maintenance troubleshooter:

a) Press Windows and X key together and select Control Panel.

b) Click on Troubleshooting.

c) Click on View all on the left side panel.

c) Click on System Maintenance and follow the onscreen instructions.

 

You may also refer to the link below to improve PC performance:

http://windows.microsoft.com/en-US/windows-8/improve-pc-performance 

 

If the issue still persists, move to the next method.

 

Method 2: Let’s change the power plan settings to use minimum processor state to prevent overheating.

Reducing the maximum processor state for your laptop (both when it is on battery or when the power cable is plugged in), reduces the processor’s performance a notch (depending on your settings) and prevents it from being used at optimum potential by an application or game, which will reduce thermal heating. For example, if you are playing a game that is consuming 100% of your processor’s capacity, then it may also result in heating up your system, whereas reducing the battery power state to, say 80%, can resolve this problem, and also result in battery power conservation.

 

Follow the steps to do so:

a) Press Windows and X key together and select Power options.

b) Click on Change plan settings for the power plan that you have set on the laptop.

c) Go to Processor power management. Expand the icon and expand maximum processor state.

d) Reduce the processor state (for both Plugged-in as well as On battery) to a certain level to verify if it makes any difference.

e) Click on Apply and then OK.

 

For reference, you can refer to these link to optimize the computer’s performance:

http://windows.microsoft.com/en-US/windows-8/improve-performance-optimizing-hard-drive
