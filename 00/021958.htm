joeu2004 say:

Vladimir wrote:

Bernard wrote:

has to do with some of the fancy footwork Microsoft incorporates to alleviate binary rounding problem

Not really, at least not with Vladimir's examples.

The order-sensitivity of any arithmetic expression is due, primarily, to the nature of the standard 64-bit binary floating-point representation.

Numeric values are stored as the sum of 53 consecutive powers of 2 ("bits") times an exponential factor.  Consequently, most decimal fractions cannot be represented exactly; instead, they are approximated.

However, because some of the 53 bits must be used to represent the integer part of a numeric value, the approximation of the same decimal fraction might vary depending on the magnitude of the integer part.

That is why IF(10.01-10=0.01,TRUE) returns FALSE(!), for example.  The approximation of x.01 where x=10 is different from the approximation of x.01 where x=0.

That is the primary reason why any arithmetic expression is order-sensitive:  the intermediate results might have very different integer parts, and that might result in very different approximations of the intermediate decimal fraction parts.

Consider the different sums in the following two examples, which differ only by the order of one addend.

The non-zero sum in second ordering is merely a coincidence.

In this example, we can see the difference (rounded to 15 significant digits) between the cumulative sum in B26 and the last addend in A27.

However, sometimes we cannot see the difference because Excel (and VBA) format only the first 15 significant digits, an arbitrary limitation.
