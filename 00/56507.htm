shenan stanley say:

What you have is a  problem with your DSL or with the router.  (Or with the DSL Modem/Router if it is one box.)

The command did not break anything and it certainly did not break all of the computers at once.

So let's be straight about what I think you have...

You say you have DSL - so you have a telephone line plugged into the wall and that telephone line goes to your DSL Modem.  That DSL modem may or may not be a router (the router you speak of.) 

If the DSL Modem is *not* the router, then you have a network cable going from the DSL Modem to the router.

No matter if the DSL Modem is the router - there are two network cables: one going to what you call your "main machine" and another going to a network switch.  Then from the network switch, you have connected all the other machines.

Assuming a default setup of the router and that this router is nothing special - here's what is happening...

Your DSL Modem gets an IP address from the DSL provider.  One IP.  It may be static (you would likely pay extra for that, but being a business, you are paying a lot for the DSL anyway...) or it may be dynamic.  If the DSL Modem is not also a router - then that IP is granted to the box that connects directly to it (whether it is a computer or a router.)  Let's assume that the DSL Modem is *NOT* a router and the device connected directly to the DSL Modem is the router.  That router has the *public* IPand can now hand out *private* IPs it controls to - likely - 255 or so 'clients'.  However - if it is like most SOHO routers (not somehting I would likely use in a business setup, but...) it only has a FEW ports built in to connect devices to (4 maybe.)  So you have connected a switch to one of the few ports on the router - expanding the one port you used on the router to the xx number of ports you have on the switch.  You hooked another cable straight from the router to the 'main computer'.  However, here's the thing... It makes no difference to the router if you hooked that main computer to that port directly on it or to a port on the network switch - unless you have VLAN'd things out - and going by your answers so far, I find it unlikely that you VLAN'd anything. ;-)

So - what does that lead to?  You need to check the configuration of the router to see what Public IP it is giving and if it is acting as a DHCP server for your Local Area Network (LAN) on the other side of that equation - handing out private network IPs to the machines attached to it.

If you are not getting a public IP - that is why you are not getting Internet on any of the computers.  Your running the IPCONFID /FLUSHDNS command on a given computer served no purpose and would not have broken anything in a setup such as the one I assume you have (I presented.)

If that is not like your setup - please - give your setup.  Give name brand/models as well on the equipment - as that will help those here to help narrow down your issue.

What i would suggest doing is (simplified for ease of following):

1) Unplug (from power) your DSL Modem.

2) Unplug (from power) your router.

3) Unplug (from power) the network switch.

4) Turn off all computer equipment.

5) Make sure there is one network cable going from the DSL modem to the router and it goes into the WAN port of the router - not one of the LAN ports.

6) Make sure all network cables connecting to internal equipment from the router (whether that is a switch or a computer) are connected to the LAN ports of the router - not the WAN.

7) Plug in the DSL modem (into power) and wait approximately 3 minutes.

8) Plug in the router (into power) and wait approximately 3 minutes more.

9) Plug in the network swicth (into power) and wait approximately 1 minute more.

10) Turn on *any* computer - one that you really didn't change would be best (the reformatted ones - did you install the drivers for the network cards after formatting them?) - and log into it with an administrative level account.

11) On that computer, check to see if you have Internet.

If you do - visit http://www.whatismyip.com/ to see your public IP address (do *NOT* post that here.)  That IP address will be the same for the other 10 computers.  Start up the other computers one at a time and confirm.

If you do not have Internet connectivity - you need to troubleshoot the router.  Get the private IP address of the computer you are on.  You can do this by pressing the "Windows Logo Key"+R and typing the following command into the "RUN" dialog box and clicking "OK":
  cmd /k ipconfig

That will bring up a command prompt and somewhere in there you should have a part that looks like this:

Ethernet adapter Local Area Connection:

   Connection-specific DNS Suffix  . :
   Link-local IPv6 Address . . . . . : ____::____:____:____:_______
   IPv4 Address. . . . . . . . . . . : ___.___.___.___
   Subnet Mask . . . . . . . . . . . : 255.255.255.0
   Default Gateway . . . . . . . . . : ___.___.___.___

You want to take note of the "Default Gateway" IP Address and open Internet Explorer and connect to that IP address to configure your router.  It could be 192.168.1.1 or 10.10.1.1 or a whole host of other provate IP addresses.  You can post that here safely.

a. user say:

This may help to understand what's going wrong:

After the BIOS or EFI (whichever your computer uses) has finished booting your computer, it hands over control of your computer to the operating system. In order to do that, it has to look for an operating system.

The first place it looks for an operating system is on the first partition of the first hard drive. Normally, the first hard drive is the one connected to the first SATA connector on the motherboard. If there isn't an operating system on the first partition of the first hard drive, the BIOS/EFI checks the next partition of the first hard drive, if there is a next partition, and so on.

If the BIOS/EFI can't find an operating system on the first hard drive, it will check the second hard drive - the one connected to the second SATA connector on the motherboard - and so on, until it finds an operating system.

Therefore, to ensure success Windows should be installed on the first partition of the first hard drive. If that isn't where you installed Windows, you're likely to run into the problem you have encountered.

The message you are seeing is asking you to disconnect hard drives that do not have an operating system - disconnect them from their data cable or remove them from the computer - so that the BIOS/EFI will find the hard drive that has Windows installed. But you don't have to go that far.

If you installed Windows on the second or a subsequent hard drive, you can enter the BIOS or EFI and change the order of the drives it checks for an operating system. So, for example, you can tell the BIOS/EFI to look first in the second hard drive for an operating system. That's easier than physically moving drives around inside your computer, although that would be the best course of action because it maintains the way computers are typically setup at the factory.

To further illustrate I have included an image of the Disk Management utility that I found on the internet. The 'first' hard drive is called Disk 0. If you have a newer computer with EFI, the first partition on Disk 0 is actually the EFI itself and the next partition right after is where Windows is ideally installed. That's what we mean by the 'C: drive.'

andre da costa say:
After installing the February 13, 2018 security update, KB4074588 (OS Build 16299.248), some USB devices and onboard devices, such as a built-in laptop camera, keyboard or mouse, may stop working for some users.

Cause
This issue occurs when the Windows Update servicing stack incorrectly skips installing the newer version of some critical drivers in the cumulative update and uninstalls the currently active drivers during maintenance.

Power on and off your computer three times
On the third time, your computer will boot into the Advanced Recovery environment
Click Advanced Options
Click Troubleshoot
Click Advanced Options

Click Command Prompt (Admin)

Confirm the drive letter for the Windows image. Usually D: –> dir d:

Run the following to view the installed packages which will also show a date of install.

Dism /Image:D:\ /Get-Packages

Find the package(s) that were just installed by date. Run the following command on the last installed package:

dism.exe /image:c:\ /remove-package /packagename:Package_for_RollupFix~31bf3856ad364e35~amd64~~16299.248.1.17

If you are running 32 bit Windows, run this command instead:

dism /online /remove-package /packagename:Package_for_RollupFix~31bf3856ad364e35~x86~~16299.248.1.17

Reboot.
