2. If I have the third value and subtract the second then subtract the first I get 0. (465333.43-314.56-465018.87 = 0)

3. However if I have the third value, subtract the first then subtract the second, I do not receive 0 as the result. (465333.43-465018.87-314.56 = -.00000000000233058017329313000)

What causes excel to add additional decimals and not correctly calculate 0? All cells are formatted the same and no parenthesis are within the formula. Excel version is 2010.

joeu2004 say:

Justin wrote:

1. [....] (465018.87+314.56-465333.43 = 0)

2. [....] (465333.43-314.56-465018.87 = 0)

3. However [....] (465333.43-465018.87-314.56 = -.00000000000233058017329313000)

What causes excel to add additional decimals and not correctly calculate 0?

It's not that Excel adds additional decimal fraction digits.  It's that Excel fails to show us all of the decimal fraction digits that are there in the first place.

-----

First, we can usually avoid such differences by explicitly rounding calculations that you expect to be accurate to n decimal places (n might be 2, in your case) to

ROUND(B1+B2-B3,2), ROUND(B3-B2-B1,2) and ROUND(B3-B1-B2,2) would all yield exactly zero in your example.

-----

The infinitesimal differences are a consequence of the standard binary representation of numbers that Excel and most applications use.  Numbers are represented by the sum of 53 consecutive powers of 2 ("bits") times an exponent.  Most decimal fractions cannot be represented exactly in binary; instead, they can only be approximated.  The accuracy of the approximation depends on the magnitude of the integer part, which uses some of the 53 bits, leaving fewer bits for the approximation of the decimal fraction.  And calculations are order-dependent because of the varying magnitudes of the integer part of intermediate results.

Often, we cannot see the infinitesimal differences because when displaying a worksheet, Excel formats only the first 15 significant digits (rounded) of the decimal representation, which can have many more decimal places.  That is an arbitrary limitation.

The following table explains your example in more detail.

Column C shows the exact decimal representation of the values in column B.  I use period for the decimal point and comma to demarcate the first 15 significant digits.  Column A shows the formulas in column B.

To begin, compare the approximations of the decimal fractions in your numbers in C1:C3 with the more accurate approximations the isolated decimal fractions in C13:C15.

Second, compare the approximations in C9:C11 of the first operation of the formulas in B5:B7 with the corresponding approximations in C1:C3.

Note that by coincidence, C9 and C10 exactly match C3 and C1.  So it is no surprise that the formulas in B5 and B6 result in exactly zero.

In contrast, note the difference between C11 and C2.  That is the root cause of the infinitesimal difference in the result of the formula in B7.

The difference arises because of the "truncated" approximation (sum of powers of 2) of the decimal fractions in B1 and B3, as shown in C1 and C3, compared to the "longer" more-accurate approximation (albeit still "truncated") of the decimal fraction in B2, as shown in C2.
