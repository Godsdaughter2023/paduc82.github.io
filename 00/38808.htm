lozzyho say:

Some additional info:

Examining the Event Viewer immdeiately after trying to hibernate (and failing) reveals that an Event ID 45 was encountered by the volmgr, with the detailed diagnosis reporting:

"The system could not sucessfully load the crash dump driver."

I then  checked the Registry key HKLM/System/CurrentControlSet/Control/CrashControl/DumpFilters which listed SbHiber.sys and dumpfve.sys, both apparently from Microspft so presumably OK. 


This is exactly what happened to me and a colleague. However, I think you are wrong about SbHiber.sys - at least in my case (and my colleague's) SbHiber.sys was from McAfee that was uninstalled while using Windows 7, but the uninstaller did not remove the reference. As a result, SbHiber.sys was failing to load, causing the VolMgr event on starup.

 

When we removed the SbHiber.sys reference from the registry key at HKLM/System/CurrentControlSet/Control/CrashControl/DumpFilters (just remove "sbhiber.sys" and leave the other text in the key value) and re-enabled Hibernation and Fast Startup, our problem with both disappeared.

 

I hope this helps someone else, 'cos it was driving me nuts. I hated not having hibernation!

a. user say:

Hi Willbowden,

Thank you for posting in Microsoft Community. I understand your concern and I'll be glad to assist you.

Generally speaking, you really shouldn't tamper with the AppData folder. Its name suggests what it does, it's the folder your programs use to store their information.

Are you assuming that the deletion of the file in AppData has affected your system?

Windows will not delete the files that are in use, but as these are 'temporary' files
you do not need to worry about those you can delete. This is a common practice that is can be done
via Disk Cleanup (which does not delete all the files),  but if you want to, go ahead and
copy them to a folder somewhere of your choice and compress them if you are desperate for space
on your SSD.
Leaving too much in your TEMP folder will slow Windows.
