At the risk of being flamed, I am going to be a contrarian here. If I understand you correctly your point is that MSFT has a product on the market to create custom database applications, and it doesn't do what you want, and you want them to tell you how to do it, or fix whatever (perceived) bugs you may have discovered. There should be no cost to you.

IMHO that is an unreasonable position to take. Comparing Access to a great plumbing job breaks down in so many different ways, I can't begin to list them.

Access is a product that retails for about $125 and has millions of users. Let's speculate that "the channel" gets about 33% of that for putting it on their shelves, and MSFT gets the rest. Their $84 contributes to their ability to keep the lights on, advertise, pay their employees and investors, etc. Some fraction of that goes towards customer support.

If they were to choose to put skilled customer support people on the phone to talk to you, and these people would cost $60/hr, how many customers would they be able to help before the company ran out of money?
If they put unskilled customer support people from a low wage country on the phone to talk to you, they would still have a losing business model.

MSFT and all other major software vendors have figured this one out at least a decade ago: you cannot give free personal support for such products. Unlike plumbing jobs these products have a truly infinite number of permutations. There is no way a test program before release of the next version can cover all of them. And even if we are aware of them, fixing all known bugs of any severity is impossible as well given time and money constraints. Add to that the fraction of users that are in over their head and should study rather than complain.

So what is MSFT and its cohorts to do? Get free help! They discovered that some people are passionate about their products and are willing to invest their time to help others. Give these volunteers a marketplace (e.g. answers.microsoft.com) and a crown (e.g. MVP status), and problem solved.

As a consumer, you can rail against this model, or you can work with it. The latter is more productive. If you haven't got the response you were hoping for, consider that:
* It may be an unreasonable request (can you download my app and fix it?)
* You may have stated your problem in an unappealing way, so people avoid it  (starting with a rant could be in this category)
* It may be an obscure problem truly nobody knows about or is interested in
* It appears to be a very minor problem, irrelevant for the overall success of the app  (the box appears here, and I want it to appear there)
* You may not have given enough information, or presented it in an unorderly fashion
* You appear to not have spent your own time trying to figure out the problem
* You seem to present a level of skill not commensurate to the level needed to accomplish the task
* It may not be the right question for this forum, or it may have been posed at a bad moment in time (we're off on spring break)
* (I am sure this is just a partial list)

I am NOT saying any of the above fits you to a T, but if some of this rings a bell, I'm hoping these are pointers to improve your success. You could damn Access and go somewhere else, but this is an industry-wide phenomenon. Access has A LOT going for it, not the least a vibrant community willing to help.

john w. vinson say:

I am attempting to delete the records of table one from table two if they are found in table two.  I have other delete queries that compare two tables and I am able to delete records.  Here is the SQL from one of those delete queries.

 

DELETE BOMPART.*, BOMPART.BOMPARTID, BOMPART.PartNumber, ECOPG1.eco
FROM (BOM INNER JOIN BOMPART ON BOM.BOMID = BOMPART.BOMID) INNER JOIN ECOPG1 ON BOM.ECNKey = ECOPG1.ECNKey
WHERE (((ECOPG1.eco)=[ECO#]));

 

Note that your DELETE clause in this query specifies only one table - BOMPART.

 

Your original post has BOTH tables in the DELETE clause. If you only want to delete records from Table1, then you should only specify Table1:

 

DELETE table001.*
FROM table001 INNER JOIN table002 ON table001.partnumber = table002.partnumber;

 

It's not clear to my why you specified the field [misc] in your original query; as noted, you can't delete just a field value, only an entire record (which is the meaning of the asterisk). In practice you should also be able to just specify any field:

 

DELETE table001.partnumber FROM table001 INNER JOIN table002 ON table001.partnumber = table002.partnumber;

 

would give exactly the same result.

 

Both of these queries require that there be a Relationship, with referential integrity enforced, between the two tables on Partnumber. If there isn't, you may need a Subquery similar to the one you posted in your second example.

john w. vinson say:

AGAIN:

 

If you want to (as you say) delete records from table001, then you should - MUST! - specify that table in the DELETE clause. You're telling it to delete from table002, NOT from table001.

 

Try

DELETE table001.*
FROM table001 INNER JOIN table002 ON table001.partnumber = table002.partnumber
WHERE (((table002.partnumber)=[table001].[partnumber]));

If that doesn't work (which it may not if partnumber isn't a primary key) then try

DELETE table001.*
FROM table001 WHERE partnumber IN (SELECT partnumber FROM table002);

BACK UP YOUR DATABASE first of course, as you always should do when running a query like this; deletion is a one-way trapdoor and there is no UNDELETE!
