To the nearest whole percent, what is the yield to maturity - Microsoft Community

ms. parker say:

To the nearest whole percent, what is the yield to maturity (YTM) of the bond today

Help!!!!  I am struggling with this one

 

A corporate bond has a face value of $1,000 and an annual coupon interest rate of 6%. Interest is paid annually.

12 years of the life of the bond remain. The current market price of the bond is $1,027, and it will mature at $1,100.

 

To the nearest whole percent, what is the yield to maturity (YTM) of the bond today?

 

Need percentage answer and formula

joeu2004 say: Marcia wrote:
I cannot get the dates right could you help

Waste of your time.

YIELDMAT is for "a security that pays interest at maturity".

For your problem, "interest is paid annually" and "12 years of the life of the bond remain".

YIELD is for "a security that pays periodic interest".

Specify dates using the DATE function.  For example, DATE(2012,1,1) for settlement date and DATE(2024,1,1) for maturity date, representing the remaining 12 years on the bond.

Marcia wrote:
A corporate bond has a face value of $1,000 and an annual coupon interest rate of 6%. Interest is paid annually.
12 years of the life of the bond remain. The current market price of the bond is $1,027, and it will mature at $1,100.
To the nearest whole percent, what is the yield to maturity (YTM) of the bond today?

=YIELD(DATE(2012,1,1), DATE(2024,1,1), 6%, 1027/10, 1100/10, 1, 0)

formatted as Percent with zero decimal places. [2]

1027/10 and 1100/10 because the YIELD function wants those parameters "per [units of] $100 face value", and the face value is $1000.

There are 10 units of $100 face value ($1000/100).  So, the price is 102.7 and the redemption value is 110.0 for
The answer is 6.25731570275310%.  As a check, we can use the IRR function set up as follows:



B2 is -1027 instead of 1027 to represent an outflow (price of bond).

B14 is 1160:  $60 for the interest coupon that year plus $1100 for the value at maturity.

B3:B13 are the annual interest payments (coupons).  The coupon amount is $60 because, by bond market conventions, the bond coupon rate (6%) is stated as a percentage of the face value ($1000), not the maturity value ($1100).

The IRR formula in C2 is shown in the Formula Bar in the image.

The IRR function returns 6.25731570276749%, compared to 6.25731570275310% returned by the YIELD function.  Small differences are to be expected due to the difference in algorithms (method of calculation).


-----
[1] Note that this answers a question that I posed in a previous YIELD discussion, namely:  would the redemption parameter ever be anything other than 100?  I had never heard of a situation where the value at maturity is not the face value, other than when a bond is sold or "called" (forced to redeem) before maturity.  But by definition, that still is not the value "at maturity", IMHO.  I do not know enough about bond market conventions to say whether or not the terms are misused in the problem statement.  Obviously the bond market has very "interesting" (read:  strange) conventions.

[2] Format as Percentage with zero decimal places to meet the problem requirement, namely "to the nearest whole percent".  IMHO, interest and bond rates should never be rounded to a whole percent.  It causes too much rounding error when applied.  I always display interest and bond rates with at least 2 decimal places, and often with 4 decimal places.  But heck, this is just a training exercise ;-).  And the good news is:  the underlying value in the cell is still more precise, even though the displayed value appears to be rounded.

It's been a long while since I've used Vista, but I think the wording is pretty much the same for uninstalling Vista updates as for Windows 7.

1) In add/remove programs (i.e. Control Panel>Programs and Features)

2) Left-click on "View installed updates" on the left side or pane of the Control Panel's "Programs and Features"  window (you should see it at the top of the pane, just below "Tasks" [Vista] or "Control Panel Home" [Win7]).

3) After all the updates are listed, if necessary scroll down to the section called "Microsoft Windows" - you may want to click on the column labelled "Installed on" (in Win7, not sure if it's labelled the same for Vista or not, but should be similar if not) so that the listing is ordered by date installed to make it easier to locate.

4) You're looking for the update called, "Security Update for Microsoft Windows (KB2699988)". When you spot it, left-click/select it and either right-click and select "Uninstall" or left-click on the "Uninstall" button just above the column headings, to the right of the "Organize" and "Views" buttons.

5) Reboot your machine when it finishes uninstalling the update.

Note that after restarting your machine, the KB2699988 will show up again under Windows Update. You can either choose to select it and then choose to "Hide this update" (I forget the exact wording) or just leave it alone but de-selected so that it doesn't get installed again until you have finally narrowed down what is causing the problem, or MS releases a fix, and you're ready to try it again. It's a critical update so you may want to be sure to check for fixes for the problem so your IE installation isn't left vulnerable to the issue(s) addressed by the update. It'd be quite easy to forget that you uninstalled it.
