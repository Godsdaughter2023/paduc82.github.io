From the Family team: How to decide if your child should be an - Microsoft Community

daniel strommen [msft] say:

From the Family team: How to decide if your child should be an administrator on their device

Technical Level : Intermediate

Summary

In general, we recommend that only parents be administrators in Windows, until your child is responsible enough to manage their own device and keep themselves safe online.  This page helps parents understand when it makes sense to give their kids administrator or standard user access.


Details

(Hi!  I'm Daniel from the Family team.  We want to know what Insiders think about our family features, and how you use Windows with your family.  So I'm starting a series of wiki pages that cover practical issues related to Windows and your family, and would love to get your feedback as Insiders.)

We know that you take your kids' online safety very seriously, so we try to provide tools and resources to help you understand what's right for your family. In general, we recommend that only parents be administrators in Windows, until your child is responsible enough to manage their own device and keep themselves safe online.

When you add a user to your Windows device, you can choose whether to make them a standard user, or an administrator. Administrators have full access to manage the system, including the ability to install software for all users (including unintentional malware), read any user's files on the device, or manage other users. Standard users can use the device, personalize their own profile, and install some kinds of software for themselves.  Learn more about administrator accounts.

What are some specific reasons I might NOT want to make my child an administrator?

  • If you have turned on the family safety features of Windows, including web filtering, activity reporting, screen time limits, app or game blocking, then we do not recommend making your child an administrator. Although these features will seem to work, your child will be able to get around them.
  • Administrators have full access to modify system settings.  A young child may accidentally change something they don't understand, making the device unable to start up, or putting it at greater risk for malware.
  • On a shared family PC, you may not want to give them access to read or delete everyone else's files, or unintentionally install system-wide malware that may steal personal data from other users. (Standard users are also at risk for malware, but only for their own user profile.)

When should I consider making my child an administrator?

  • If your child is responsible for their own device, and can be trusted to take care of it and keep themselves safe online without parental supervision.  Learn more about online safety at the Microsoft YouthSpark Hub.
  • If your child's games or apps, or software provided by their school, only work as administrator. (Are you in this situation? Please let us know.  Go to the Feedback Hub and file feedback under Security, Privacy, and Accounts > Child accounts and family features.)

How can I control which users are administrators?

You can use the Settings app to switch a user between "administrator" and "standard user".  Open the Settings app and go to Accounts > Other people.  Learn more about setting up your family's accounts.

Welcome to the  world of computers.  Don't feel bad.  Everyone knows something about computers and no one knows everything.   You and I are between these 2 goal posts.  You ask a basis question, let's try for a simple answer.  This may not be the correct platform, though.


A computer is a dumb damn thing.  It has to be told how to do every task it does.  A program tells the computer what to do.  The computer has to have a place to storage data.  Think of the computer as a big filing cabinet.

In a filing cabinet, you would likely have folders with a name attached to the folder.  Let's call the folder Household Expense.  In this folder, you would likely put pieces of paper for invoices, etc.  These are files.  Within the main folder, you might have sub-folders to separate major categories within the main folder.

You can use File Explorer to look at the folder set on the computer.  You should see a little "folder" icon beside an actual folder and then files that are just files with the folder.

Here is a shot of my Junk folder.  Junk is the main folder that I might use if I wanted to get data of little or no value but not prepared to delete.  It might have testing or Temporary data.  You will see 3 additional folders that likely contains more data.  you then see a bunch of files under the main junk folder.

A more typical case might be how you store your pictures.  You might have a main folder called Pictures.  you might then want more sub-folders called Trip 2010, Trip 2011, Trip 2015, Dogs, etc.  You can quickly zero in on the picture of interest and not have 1,000s to search through.  Just like a good filing cabinet or photo album.

Hope this helps.  I first worked on computers when they were the size of a house.  I learned something about my computer yesterday and likely today.

mike h.. say:

Hi,

 

To date there is no native function in Excel that does this so we need to write our own.

 

ALT+F11 to open VB editor, right click 'ThisWorkbook' and insert module and paste the code below in. Close VB editor.

 

 

back on the worksheet call with this

 

=CountColour(A1:C6,E1)

 

 

Where A1:C6 is the range to count and E1 is a cell with the same font colour that you want to count.

 

 

 

 

Function CountColour(rng As Range, clr As Range)
Application.Volatile
Dim c As Range
For Each c In rng
    If c.Font.Color = clr.Font.Color Then
        CountColour = CountColour + 1
    End If
Next
End Function
