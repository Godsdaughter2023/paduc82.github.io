Start>Run>msconfig [enter]

This brings up the System Configuration Utility. Look on the Startup tab and find the probable culprit. Uncheck the box next to its name, Apply and OK out. You don't need to restart immediately, but the next time you do you'll get a dialog saying you've used the Utility. Just tick the box that says in effect, "don't bother me about this again".

Important - Do not use the System Configuration Utility to stop processes. Instead, use Start>Run>services.msc [enter] and do not stop any services unless you really, really know what you're doing.

wethotuwasatoad say:

This post contains solutions to this thread and one other issue related to MouseTool.

MouseTool is a free (donationware) mouse-clicking utility created by Jeff Roush (JR Software LLC) in the 1990s.

I have been using MouseTool for close to 20 years and it has been a lifesaver. Due to quadriplegia, I have no movement or sensation in my hands (although I do have some use of my arms from partial function in my shoulders and biceps). As a result, I can use a large trackball but clicking is impossible without taking my eyes off the screen and looking down at the trackball. 

MouseTool enables the mouse to click whenever the cursor stops moving. It is a simple concept really but considering how often mouse clicks are used, it has literally been a godsend for me.

1) Solution to the present thread:

As described above, there are two options for viewing the (ON/OFF) status of MouseTool and accessing it's settings. One is a small floating MouseTool box which by default is always-on-top and displays a green ON-indicator or red OFF-indicator dot. 

The other option eliminates the on-screen box and in its place, displays a small mouse icon in the system tray (which can be hidden or in view like any tray icon). When MouseTool is OFF in this viewing mode, a red circle/bar is superimposed onto the mouse icon.

To switch from the on-screen to tray mode, left-click near the top of the box between* the minimize & restore buttons. If that fails, try holding down the left-click button momentarily in that same location. Alternatively, to switch from the tray mode to the on-screen mode, right-click the mouse icon and select [Restore from Tray].

I have never seen documentation explaining these steps but discovered them through trial and error.

2) Solution to persistent drag or shift-down behavior:

On occasion, MouseTool may act as though the left-click button or shift-key is being held down. This behavior can almost always be eliminated by switching MouseTool to Double-click mode momentarily and then back again to Left-click mode:

• Right-click the MouseTool tray icon
• Select Double click
• Right-click the MouseTool tray icon
• Select Left click

*Be sure that neither the rectangle surrounding the minimize button nor the rectangle surrounding the restore button is visible but that the cursor is directly between the two.

Even then, the 'Get Windows 10' icon did not appear until I had installed a few more essential updates and used the Microsoft automated activation or validation service. The thing that surprises me about this hotfix is that it takes about 40 minutes to run and all the time is seemingly doing nothing, but this time things progressed. As I recall it brings you to 'Update Downloads' and 'Preparing for Installation'.

I again downloaded the Windows 10 files. This took a little over 2 hours. I then went through 'Prepare for Upgrade' and 'Start Upgrade Now'

After going towards shutdown, the screen shows 'Configuring Update for Windows 10'  which takes about 12 minutes. After a restart and the brief loading of files, 'Upgrading Windows' appears with a large % dial count-up dial in the centre showing the progress of the upgrading.

At the bottom of the screen there are three titles i.e Copying Files - Installing Features and Drivers - Configuring Settings. As the installation process performs each action there is an individual % count-up, as well as the overall progress feature.  The Upgrading procedure took about 1 hr 10 mins.
