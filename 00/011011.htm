(VBA = Visual Basic for Applications)
(for kludge, see e.g. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kludge , preferably before proceeding)


1. With an empty document open in Word, go to Word->Preferences->Ribbon and Toolbar, look in the second column for the "Developer" tab, check the box next to it, then click Save. Quit the Preferences Pane.

2. In Word's Ribbon, you should see a new Developer tab. Click it,  then click the "Visual Basic" icon on the left. The "Visual Basic Editor" pane should appear.

3. You should see a dialog box with a list of things in the left-hand column, probably starting with "Normal (Normal.dotm)"

4. Click the little triangle to the left of "Normal", then right-click on "Microsoft Word Objects" and click "Insert", then "Module". A new item called "Modules" should appear under Microsoft Word Objects, and under that, you should see "Module1" (you may have to click the little triangle to the left of "Modules"). Click Module 1.

5. Now you should see two panes to the right of the list of modules etc. Use cmd-C to copy the code - I provide it again below - and cmd-V to paste it into the top pane.

Sub showWordPosition()
Application.StatusBar = "Word: " & ActiveDocument.Range(1, Selection.Start).Words.Count
End Sub

Sub resetStatusBar()
Application.StatusBar = ""
End Sub

6. Close the Visual Basic Editor pane.

7. In Word's top menu, click Tools->Customise Keyboard. Under Categories, locate and select "Macros". You should see resetStatusBar and showWordPosition in the list on the right.

8. Select "showWordPosition" and click in the "Press new keyboard shortcut" box below. The trick now is to press some keyboard combo that (a) Word recognises at all, (b) makes sense to you and (c) has not already been assigned to something in Word or Mac OS, or at least, something in Word that you are actually likely to use. It's not easy. How about command-option-shift-P ? Yes! You need to be a virtuoso pianist or something to do that easily. You can always come back and modify this later, or add other keystrokes. But when you press that combination, you should see the reassuring message "Currently assigned to [unassigned]". Click the Assign button

9. Repeat for the other routine (resetStatusBar). Perhaps use Command-Option-Shift-R as the keystroke, verify it's unassigned, then click Assign, then click OK to close the dialog box.

Now open a document with some text, click somewhere in it, click command-option-shift-P, and see what happens. Then, if you see the status bar change, click command-option-shift-R.

There is just one more thing. Ideally, you don't put important stuff in normal.dotm, because when things go wrong in Word one of the things that people will suggest is that you locate and delete normal.dotm and let Word create a new one. In that case, you would need to go through this rigmarole again. Ideally, you would pu this stuff in a separate .dotm that Word would load as an "Addin", btut that involves even more steps...
