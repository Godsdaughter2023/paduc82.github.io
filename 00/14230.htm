Security issues in distributing fillable MS Word forms - Microsoft Community

markus701 say:

Security issues in distributing fillable MS Word forms

I need to create a fillable form in Word 2010 (Win7) that can be distributed thruout the company.  Users must be able to store this form on their desktop, click on it to open it, fill it out, then email it to a single specific email address.  I'd like to automate the email by adding a macro with the email address hard coded into the .docx distribution file.  Ideally, they fill in the form and click on Submit to email the completed form.

I know how to construct the form.  I can construct the macro for the email process.  My big question is security.  That is, if I distribute such a form, will it not be able to be opened on some computers because it contains a macro?  E.G., those with local admin rights might be able to open it but others would not.

Also, the form will have checkboxes, plain text, Rich text, and comboboxes.  Do any of these controls also carry with them security issues that would prevent us from wide distribution thruout the company?

jay freedman say:

Using an installer is good, as it allows you to sidestep most of the problems. I apologize for raising some red flags; I assumed you were just going to email the empty form with instructions on how to mail it back.

The desktop is definitely not a trusted location by default, and is not likely to be one in most installations. Take a look at this page for background and advice. Then see this page for more detailed information about dealing with trusted locations programmatically. But read on -- you probably don't have to fiddle with trusted locations because you can use one of the default ones.

There are a couple of problems (easily solved) with your proposed scenario.

  • In Word 2007 and later, .docx documents and .dotx templates cannot contain macros. You'll need to save your document as a .docm or .dotm file.
  • Do not plan on filling in the form and saving it to a different name; it's too prone to damaging the basic document, and it isn't how Word is designed to work. The file you install should be a template, not a document; in this case, a .dotm (macro-enabled) template.
  • The template should be installed in the user's Templates folder (typically %appdata%\Microsoft\Templates), which is one of the default trusted locations, and a shortcut to the template can be placed on the desktop. In this setup, you don't have to sign the template, although you can do so. If it's possible that a user has changed the settings in the Trusted Locations dialog, the TechNet page gives information about how the installer can read the registry to verify the default or detect a non-default location.
  • A shortcut to a template automatically opens Word with a document based on the template; the template itself remains unchanged. The completed document can be saved as a .docx file, because it doesn't have to contain the macro -- the macro remains in, and runs from, only the template. Each time the user double-clicks the shortcut, they'll get a new empty form.

jay freedman say:

Yes, you will find many situations where documents containing macros will be blocked, or at least that the macro will be disabled. In some cases this is caused by the user's settings in File > Options > Trust Center > Macros, and in other cases it will be enforced by group policy. The most useful measure you can take to avoid some but not all of those situations is to apply a digital signature (but not one from the selfcert.exe program) to the document. It may also help to instruct users to store the document in a trusted location (according to the settings in their File > Options > Trust Center > Trusted Locations dialog) before opening it.

The alternative is to forego the macro and just place the email address prominently at the top and/or bottom of the form, asking users to attach the completed form to a manually created email.
