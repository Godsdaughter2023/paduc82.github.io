cmdrkeene say:
There's unfortunately not.  In fact, WoW likely shouldn't even behave that way (if you were to pin the running instance, it'd probably take on the "right" functionality).

This was designed to make it easier and avoid duplication.  For example, Outlook used to show an icon on the QuickLaunch, another icon when it's running, and at least one icon by the clock (plus another if you had new mail).  Contrast that with Windows 7.  Today, Outlook gets one button.  If you have new mail, the indicator shows up on the same button.  It doesn't move (catch me if you can) just because you run it.  That gives way to another benefit: if it's the 2nd icon, it's always the 2nd icon. Users are confident about where it lives. And since it never changes, you can press Windows-2 to launch it (or switch to it).   You never have to say "your mail is here. Unless it's running, then it's here. Unless you minimized it, then it's by the clock. Unless it's hidden, then its in this popup. Unless it's not, then it's in the QuickLaunch".  Yikes.

BUT... I understand that you might want to open more than one copy of a program.  If you opened it, and now it's a button, you might think you can't open another fresh one.  You can do that by clicking with your mouse scroll wheel (just like you open a link in a new tab in your browser).  If you don't have a scroll wheel, you can shift-click (same as browsers there too).

This leads to a lot of consistency, plus you don't have 2 (or 4 or 5 if you're Outlook... geez) icons all over the place that all go to the same program.

tricky300 say:

Hi,

The answer is yes.

I am assuming that you want to do this because it avoids you having to by a wireless adapter for your Win 7 PC.

I have replicated your situation on my PCs and it works.

Firstly, it may be that you need a special Ethernet cable called a crossover cable. It may also be that you do not need this cable and that an ordinary ethernet cable will suffice. I am going to assume that your Win 7 PC has a modern network card, most times this means a crossover cable will not be needed. I will come to how to determine this later.

I am making some assumptions here about you having a 'normal' setup, so if something doesn't pan out as I anticipate let me know exactly why and I will help further.

The first thing you might need to do is on the router. You need to make sure that the router is allocating ip addresses in any range other than 192.168.0.y

  • Click Start then click Run, type cmd and click OK
  • In the resultant black window type ipconfig and hit enter
  • If the gateway is listed as 192.168.x.y where x is anything other than 0 then nothing needs to be done
  • If the gateway is 192.168.0.y then you need to alter your router's settings to allocate ip addresses in the range 192.168.1.y
  • Your router is not known to me so I cannot give you specific instructions right now but if you give me the exact make and model I can probably find this out for you.
  • Once you have done this, verify with ipconfig again to make sure the gateway is now 192.168.1.x

Next you must change things on the XP laptop

  • Click Start then click Run, type ncpa.cpl and click OK. This will open Network Connections.
  • Right click your wireless network connection and click Properties. This will open the properties of your wireless connection.
  • Click the Advanced tab and tick 'Allow other network users to connect through this computer's Internet connection' and click OK. Click Yes to the resultant message about ip address 192.168.0.1

Connect the laptop to your Win 7 PC with an Ethernet cable. Your router was probably supplied with one of these.

Now you must change things on the Win 7 PC

  • Click Start then type ncpa.cpl and click OK. This will open Network Connections.
  • Right click your local area connection and click Properties. This will open the properties of your wireless connection.
  • Click Internet Protocol Version 4 (TCP/IPv4) and click Properties. This will open the TCP/IP properties.
  • Click Use the following IP address
  • Enter 192.168.0.2 for the IP address
  • Enter 255.255.255.0 for the Subnet mask
  • Enter 192.168.0.1 for the Default gateway
  • Click Use the following DNS server addresses
  • Enter 192.168.0.1 for the Primary DSN server
  • Click OK
  • Click Close

I think that should do it provided that your Ethernet cable does not need to be of the crossover variety. To determine if your cable is OK

  • On the XP laptop click Start then click Run, type cmd and click OK
  • In the resultant black window type ping 192.168.0.2 and hit enter. You should get 4 successful replies from 192.168.0.2
  • On the Win 7 PC click Start type cmd and click OK
  • In the resultant black window type ping 192.168.0.1 and hit enter. You should get 4 successful replies from 192.168.0.1

Now open a web browser and see if it all works.

Good luck.

Thank you for letting us know about your concern. We suggest that you try the solutions below.

Run the Surface Diagnostic Toolkit.

The diagnosis and repair takes about 15 minutes as it would check for both Windows and driver updates. It will automatically install any missing updates for your device. Click here to learn how to run the toolkit.

Force shutdown your Surface to ensure all updates are applied.   

  1. Press and hold the power button for a full 10 seconds, until the screen turns off, then release the power button.   
  2. Press and release the power button to turn your Surface back on. You should see the Surface logo.If that doesn't work, use this two-button shutdown process to make sure your Surface completely turns off.   
  3. Press and hold the power button on your Surface for 30 seconds and then release it.   
  4. Press and hold the volume-up button and the power button at the same time for at least 15 seconds and then release both.   
  5. The screen may flash the Surface logo, but continue holding the buttons down for at least 15 seconds.   
  6. After you release the buttons, wait 10 seconds.   
  7. Press and release the power button to turn your Surface back on. You should see the Surface logo.

Clean the connectors on your Surface Book.   

  1. Detach the Clipboard from the keyboard.   
  2. Clean the pins on the connector with a pencil eraser. Rub the eraser back and forth across the pins.   
  3. Wet a cotton swab in rubbing alcohol. Clean the pins on the long, narrow part of the charger that you plug into your Surface. Rub the cotton swap back and forth across the pins. After you are done cleaning the connector pins, make sure that the pins are dry, and then reattach the Clipboard to the keyboard.
