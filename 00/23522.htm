Why the waiting period to replace Microsoft Account security - Microsoft Community

a. user say:

Why the waiting period to replace Microsoft Account security information exists

Hi,

I'm not employed by Microsoft in any fashion. Here's my story:

My Live.com account was hacked in July 2009 by automated software. I thought I had a secure password. I didn't.

It took me a month of:

1) repeatedly asking for access from a Microsoft help desk agent, (there actually is a live person I can talk to!)

2) changing my password and resetting my personal and security information,

3) having my password hacked within hours and most of my personal information changed again

before I was able to reset my password with a 14 character long string of garbled text, numbers and other characters that actually stopped the attack process.

I almost didn't gain access the last time. Almost too much of my personal information had been changed by the attackers.

Now, if the attackers were forced to wait a month before they could change the password on an account that they had gained valid access credentials. Would they continue with the attack, or would they go somewhere else?

You can still access your email; you just can't change your security information. Here's a link: http://windows.microsoft.com/en-US/windows-8/delete-security-info. When you do finally reset your account information with Outlook, you will receive a Recovery Code. It looks like all other Microsoft product keys. Print the email out and put it in a safe place.

I tell my customers that if they don't want to accidently access their Microsoft account, they should use an email client, like Windows Essentials. Great for backing up your email, contacts and calendar. An email client provides a consistent non-dynamic interface that works with many different webmail and traditional email providers.

If you still want to use email on your browser, follow both of these steps to ensure you can recover your account:

1) Use a current active mobile phone number in your security information. I can't stress this enough! Keep it current! Once you learn text messaging, getting a verification code via text messaging is the easiest, fastest way to get back in business. If your phone dies before you can change it in your account, then there's step 2:

2) Use an alternate email address as a backup method of validating your account. The only reason for the alternate email account is to verify the primary email account; use the primary account to verify the alternate account. Hopefully, you will always have one active, verified account to send email.

Don't use another email address from the same provider you are using as your primary email address, if at all possible.

Here's why: many browsers will log you in automatically and they get confused if you have multiple accounts with the same provider. (You could use another browser to log in to your alternate account, like Chrome or Firefox to eliminate this hassle, but I don't like to put all of my informational assets in one place. Diversify accounts for stability.)

Hope this clears up the confusion and anger.
