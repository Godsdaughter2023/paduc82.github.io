Based on the symptom that OneNote keeps having you enter the username and password, I would suggest you try the following steps to clear your Office credentials for MacOS.

 

1. Quit from all Mac Office Applications, except for OneNote

2. In OneNote, sign out. This only logs you out, and all the notebooks will still be there.

7. Launch OneNote and sign into your accounts.

greginmich say:

Unfortunately, comparing AV apps on the basis of personal experience just doesn’t work – because long term comparisons involve comparing rough impressions of an older version of one app with rough impressions of a newer version of another app; and because the performance of these apps is also being compared across completely different threat landscapes, and software environments; not to mention differences in a host of other time-dependent extraneous variables. And consequently, all of this timebound, incongruous, and fuzzy information that gets stashed away in your personal experience is quickly rendered irrelevant by ongoing changes in the apps themselves and in their environment – so your personal experience really needs to be tossed out the window if you want to get a clear picture of what’s happening in the here and now.

On the other hand, if you compare several current AV apps in quick succession, this introduces both smaller scale time-dependent discrepancies in the apps and their environment, and also many sequential artifacts, including an increasingly cluttered environment, improper installation or configuration of the AV apps, missed malware or incomplete remediation with a previous app, incomplete removal of the previous app’s components, and so on.

So the information gleaned in home-based sequential AV testing is always both highly impressionistic and degenerate in nature – and the only valid methodology for evaluating AV apps involves parallel testing in an AV lab with periodic side-by-side comparisons: The latest version of each AV app has to be installed on a fresh copy of Windows 10 with a properly controlled and configured third-party software environment that equalizes all of the potentially confounding environmental variables – and then all of these AV apps need to be pitted against exactly the same set of real-world threats (live URLs). The malware sample set also needs to be carefully selected in order to make sure that it’s highly-representative of the threat landscape that's currently being encountered by Windows 10 users.

The AV-Comparatives Real-World Protection Test uses this classical methodology of controlled observation in a realistic and representative environment in order to provide us with a good benchmark for comparing AV apps. And this combination of testing design elements also allows us to do some essential fact-checking with respect to the claims made by AV marketing departments and self-styled “security experts” – and let’s face it, the internet is quickly devolving into one giant hoopla machine, scammer's paradise, and boo-boo blogosphere – where it’s difficult, if not impossible, to come up with any trustable information on AV apps. So while this AV-Comparatives benchmark test might never be as completely accurate and up-to-date as we would like it to be, it is like a breath of fresh air in an online world that seems to be increasingly polluted with the fog of misinformation.

Of course no AV solution can indefinitely sustain a 100% protection rate in this real-world testing, and even when an app scores 100% on this test, it’s still very likely that it’s actually susceptible to some of the new variants or zero-day threats out there that weren’t included in the test’s malware sample set – so a 100% protection rate isn’t actually achievable for any AV solution. If you follow this Real-World Protection Test, you’ll also see that Kaspersky Internet Security (which is more powerful than the free version) frequently misses samples (as it did in the March test cited above), and would undoubtedly miss other emergent threats if we increased the malware sample size sufficiently – so your assumption that Kaspersky offers 100% protection clashes with plenty of evidence to the contrary. Also, as I stated in my reply to your previous version of this discussion, there are several important Defender protection features that are disabled by default, including PUA Protection. This PUA Protection feature, in particular, would need to be enabled in order to test Windows Defender’s actual detection rate, or to accurately determine its effectiveness as a standalone AV solution:

Right-click on the Start button and launch Windows PowerShell (Admin).

Then copy, paste, and enter this command line at the prompt:

Set-MpPreference -PUAProtection 1

https://www.tenforums.com/tutorials/32236-enable-disable-windows-defender-pua-protection-windows-10-a.html
