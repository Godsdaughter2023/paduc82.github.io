Try this from Tom Ogilvy.

 

Sub SortRows()
'Tom Ogilvy macro
Dim r As Long
Dim lRow As Long
Dim lCol As Long

    Application.ScreenUpdating = False
    Application.Calculation = xlCalculationManual
    lRow = ActiveSheet.Cells(Rows.Count, "A").End(xlUp).Row
    lCol = ActiveSheet.Cells(ActiveCell.Row, _
    Columns.Count).End(xlToLeft).Column
    For r = 2 To lRow
        With Cells(r, 1).Resize(1, lCol)
            .Sort Key1:=Cells(r, 2), Order1:=xlAscending, _
            Header:=xlGuess, _
            Orientation:=xlLeftToRight, _
            DataOption1:=xlSortNormal
        End With
    Next r

    Application.ScreenUpdating = True
    Application.Calculation = xlCalculationAutomatic

End Sub

Sorting on the second row will group all the columns which contain occurrences in the second row but are absent from the first row. The top of each block of columns in the table will be a set of consequtively grouped cells regardless of the occurrences in any underlying cells. The example is from the natural world.  It could be observations in a borehole, or depth below high water mark on a beach.  In a census of occurrences, which could be animal, vegetable or mineral, not everything occurs in every place.  That is why there are gaps in the initial table of observations and why there will be gaps in the rows and columns of the sorted data.  The question being answered by the sorted table is - which object(s) occur highest (or closest) which objects occur no higher than the next level, or which objects occur no lower than, and so on. The sorted table shows the distribution of the objects with respect to a defined parameter.  Absence is often just as important as presence in making judgements on the significance of the observations. Which is why the gaps are there - they exist in the real world.

Finding Full With at Half Maximum (on a graph) in Excel? - Microsoft Community

playmannn say:

Finding Full With at Half Maximum (on a graph) in Excel?

Ok, I have this bell-shaped curve and I've got the value at it's maximum.Now I have to find the with of the bell at the level of exactly half the maximum.Is this possible in Excel and how?Or in other words, is it possible to measure the linear distance between two random points of a graph's curve, which is needed in this case?
lars-Åke aspelin say:
Ok, I have this bell-shaped curve and I've got the value at it's maximum.Now I have to find the with of the bell at the level of exactly half the maximum.Is this possible in Excel and how?Or in other words, is it possible to measure the linear distance between two random points of a graph's curve, which is needed in this case?

Assuming that you are talking about a scatter (X-Y) chart, that the X-values are in cells A1:A100, and the Y-values are in cells B1:B100, try this formula:

=MAX(IF(B1:B100>=MAX(B1:B100)/2,A1:A100),MIN(A1:A100))- MIN(IF(B1:B100>=MAX(B1:B100)/2,A1:A100),MAX(A1:A100))

Note: This is an array formula that has to be confirmed with CTRL+SHIFT+ENTER rather than just ENTER.

This gives the difference between the highest and lowest X-value that have Y-values larger than half of the maximum Y-value. That means that you will get a slightly lower value than the "width" that you ask for. If this is not acceptable, the formula can be extended to do a linear interpolation on the X-Y values near the points where Y is Max(Y)/2.


If your chart is not a scatter chart, but a line chart or a column chart, and the Y-values are in cells B1:B100, try this formula:

=MAX(IF(B1:B100>MAX(B1:B100)/2,ROW(B1:B100),0)- MIN(IF(B1:B100>MAX(B1:B100)/2,ROW(B1:B100),ROWS(B1:B100))))

Note: This is an array formula that has to be confirmed with CTRL+SHIFT+ENTER rather than just ENTER.

This gives you the number of data points that have an Y-value larger than the half of the maximum Y-value.
