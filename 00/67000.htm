That 15 Documents minimum is there because some systems do not have the resources to handle opening that many files at one time. Also some printers do not provide a large enough queue to handle that many documents.

 

If you have a system that can has enough resources, you can change this setting.

 

1. Press the WinKey + R to display the Run dialog. Type  regedit.exe and click OK to start the registry editor.

 

2. Navigate to the following Key.

HKEY_CURRENT_USER\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Explorer

 

3. In the right side pane, Right Click and select  New/ DWORD (32-bit) Value.

 

4. Name the Value  MultipleInvokePromptMinimum  (Case sensitive).

 

5. Right click the new Value and select Modify.

 

6. In the Base section, select Decimal.

 

7. In the Value Data box, type the number  16  and Click OK (The number 16 will make the selection of files Unlimited).

 

Press the F5 key to refresh and exit the registry editor.

9f is likely a driver issue. Antivirus/antispyware/security programs can also cause these issues.
Have you added devices lately or updated drivers - be sure to check in Windows Updates to
see if a driver was updated prior to this occurring. This could also be caused by a loose card

That error means a driver is not handling the power state properly. Check in Control Panel -
Device Manager to be sure major drivers are not shut off during sleep - double click Display
Adapter - NIC and WIFI - Sound and others - Power Management tab - uncheck Allow

low level chipset drivers)

description, version, product name, company that created the driver, and more.

Click - Uninstall - REBOOT this will refresh the driver stack.

with their own software and drivers and any other major device drivers you have.

Now go to System Maker's site (Dell, HP, Toshiba as examples) (as rollback) and then Device

each installation.

shows up. This is because some drivers rollback before the latest is installed (sound drivers
particularly do this) so install a driver - reboot - check to be sure it is installed and repeat as
needed.

Dear friends, we gather here today to morn the passing of a great application; cut down in the prime of it's life.  Born in August 29th, 2003 little Skyper (short for Skype Peer to Peer) showed early promise of being a great communications application and quickly impressed everyone Skyper met.  As the application matured, the domain name became the more respectable Skype (due to domain name ownership issues) and Skype became a world wide superstar in the communications world.

Families could visit with video calls, text chat with each other, or play games across the miles.  The number of platforms expanded from laptops and mobile phones to smart televisions.  Yes, Skype became a great product with a wide following around the world.

I personally became acquainted with Skype while looking for work in Austin, Texas.  My mobile phone plan had a very limited number of minutes and I needed a way to make long distance calls economically.  A mutual friend introduced me to Skype and it proved to be just what I needed.  At that time, Skype was allowing outgoing calls at no charge as a promotional opportunity and I appreciated the freedom this gave me.  As I became better acquainted with Skype I found many of the other features were fantastic.  I could play games with my nephew in Michigan (or help with homework), and got to use the video connection with my parents in their later years.  Yes, Skype quickly became a well loved application on my Windows XP system.

While Skype's early years showed great promise, Skype's later years showed it had contracted a bad case of Microsoft.  Early symptoms included deafness (to customer requests), a loss of functionality (games and other features disappeared without warning), and bloating of the software even though functionality was reduced.  Support on smart televisions suddenly stopped working and Skype acquired a foul stench of compulsive updates with fewer and fewer features.

In January 2019, Skype suffered it's final indignity when a virtually useless Skype 8 version (a stripped down, semi-functional not quite alpha-ready release) was forced on an unwilling user community.  Despite months of complaints and efforts to resuscitate Skype 7 (which while ailing was usable), our beloved Skype 7 stopped working and was consigned to the ash heap of history.  Skype, while it's demise was reminiscent of other once great institutions (such as Sears), was finally at peace, hounded into the grave by the idiotic, thoughtless, incompetence and inconsiderate management of those who were supposed to care for it and the customers who used it.

I shall greatly miss Skype.  It was a reliable, nearly ubiquitous, application that could be used for business or personal needs.  I will especially miss the fun times and conversations, business connections, and family chats I had with Skype.  While my mobile calling plan long ago went to unlimited calling, I still used Skype (and purchased a yearly subscription) as it was a great way to connect from websites and online phone numbers.  What makes me saddest of all is that Skype could have survived and even thrived, with proper care and maintenance, or if the customer's loud protestations had been heeded; but alas, that was not to be.

Skype was survived by company's like Viber, Google hangouts, ooVoo, Line, WhatsApp, Tox, and a host of other video and voip communications products.

R.I.P.
