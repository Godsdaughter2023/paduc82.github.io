dana.d say:
Thanks for that observation.  There is one difference however. In my application, in the very first cell, the Fourier Analysis yields a very large real number that just does not make sense. I did not see that in Mr. Seidenberg's example above. Any idea why?

Hi.  The chart is giving the relative frequency contributions of each frequency.

The very first cell is the zero frequency, and hence is left out of the chart.

The next frequency is the second item in the list, which is the same as the last item in the list, just the complex conjugate... They are the same.

The large number in the first cell is nothing more than the sum of the data.  This is due to the equation that Excel uses.  (There are different equations on can use depending on what one is doing.)

If one wanted to properly plot the first few sin waves, one would offset the plots in the y-direction by the average of the data, or in Excel, by the sum of data (first cell that you are referring too) divided by the number of data points.   So, divide the value in the first cell by N (number of points) to get the offset for graphing.

Hope this helps.  :>)
