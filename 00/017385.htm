There are probable reasons as to why you are receiving the said error upon syncing  your iOS device to OneNote. One of the possible cause can be due to failed syncing of your school's folder or shared folders for OneNote. We suggest that you do the following:

There are a few steps you can follow to avoid OneNote sync issues on a mobile device:

  • Check and make sure OneNote is running in the foreground.

  • Logout and log back in to OneNote to reset your credentials and avoid authentication problems.

  • Force a OneNote to sync

Here's how you can reset your credentials and force a sync on iOS:

Reset credentials

  1. Open OneNote, tap the < icon in the upper-left corner of the screen, and tap the gear icon next to the account name and email address.

  3. Tap the account name again and then choose Remove Account.

  4. Accept the notification that appears to confirm signing out.

  5. Double tap the device's home button, and swipe up on the OneNote app to close it .

  6. Start OneNote and sign in to your account.

Force Sync

  • There are no options to force sync a notebook in OneNote for iPad or iPhone.

To be able to resolve this, we suggest that you perform a soft reset. Press and hold the volume down and Power button until your phone vibrates and restarts. Private Sub LHUOML_41_Click()

Sub ListFilesInFolder(ByVal SourceFolderName As String, ByVal IncludeSubfolders As Boolean)
    Dim FSO As Object
    Dim SourceFolder As Object
    Dim SubFolder As Object
    Dim FileItem As Object

steve rindsberg say:

We can't answer that in the general case.  It'll depend on, among other things, whether you have image compression disabled or not.

In your specific case, you can try it, save the presentation with the pasted image, then quit PPT.  Rename the PPTX to give it a .ZIP extension (either change it from .PPTX to .ZIP or just tack .ZIP onto the end ... My File.PPTX.ZIP).

Doubleclick to open the (now) ZIP file.  Open the ppt folder within the ZIP then open the media folder.  Your image (under a different name) will be stored there, likely as a PNG; PowerPoint doesn't store TIFs internally as TIFs.  Open it in any image editor to check on the resolution.

Note that 300 dpi is a ratio, not an absolute size measurement.

A 20" wide image at 300 dpi is going to be quite a bit larger (file size, resolution, number of pixels) than a 300 dpi image at 2".
