a. user say:

I don't know what you mean by a 'restore,' but when you perform a Microsoft 'reset' - whether it's a 'remove everything' reset or a 'keep my files' reset - your hard drive is effectively erased.

In a 'remove everything' reset, only Windows itself is reinstalled.

In a 'keep my files' reset, the files that you store in your personal user account (documents, pictures, music, videos) are copied to the cloud and then restored after Windows is reinstalled. The only other thing that is restored are the apps you installed from Microsoft Store, and then only if your user account is a Microsoft account.

These are important details that I wish Directly's 'independent advisors' would explain before advising people to reset their computers.

The only way to retrieve programs that you installed from the internet - aside from Microsoft Store - is to reinstall them. That means you have to go to the developers' websites on the internet and find their installers, if they still exist. For programs that you bought and paid for, you'll need to have the license key and registration information, otherwise you'll have to pay for them again.

One way to avoid this is to store the installation files for your software on a USB flash drive, along with their license keys and any information and instructions you'll need to install them.

So you see, resetting your computer is not a simple solution and should not be undertaken lightly or without completely understanding what you are about to do. In addition, it requires a lot of advance preparation that 'independent advisors' don't tell you about.
