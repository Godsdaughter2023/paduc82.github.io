For whatever crazy reason, Microsoft, in their infinite wisdom, decided that Standard would have the ability to *edit* Office docs, but not *create* them. "Professional" phones could do both.

 

Fortunately, there's a pretty simple workaround. The sheet name comparison is case sensitive. here's your code re-written to be a bit more effecient

 

Sub Macro1()

Application.ScreenUpdating = False
' Loop through all sheets in the workbook
For i = 1 To Sheets.Count

D E F
Date Assigned Deadline Date Completed

ashish mathur say:

Hi,

Since the dates your picture are left aligned, I am assuming they are "Dates stored as text entries".  You can check the same by writing an =ISNUMBER(cell_ref) in another set of columns.  If the result is FALSE, then convert the entries into actual date entries.  One way is to select each individual column and go to Data > Text to columns > Finish.  Once entries in all three columns have been converted into Dates, try this

1. Click on cell E4 and go to Home > Conditional formatting > New Rule > Use a formula to determine which cells to format

2. In the Formula bar there, enter this formula

=E4>F4

3. Click on Format > Fill > Green

4. Click on OK/Apply

5. Repeat steps 2:4 for E4<F4

6. Copy cell E4, select the range of data below > right click > Paste Special > Formats > OK

Hope this helps.

bill sanderson say:

I can't answer the questions with regard to Microsoft Security Essentials, except to say that clearly the version of this virus that you encountered was not in their definitions at the time you encountered it.
 
I would strongly recommend running Microsoft's latest Malicious Software Removal tool--this is the one which is updated monthly, and which has targeted apps like the one you saw for several months recently:
 
http://www.microsoft..com/downloads/details.aspx?FamilyID=ad724ae0-e72d-4f54-9ab3-75b8eb1483 56&displaylang=en
 
That may not be enough to get you clean, if this is a recent update to the malware, but you feel you have other resources to get clean--so let us know if those don't do the trick.
 
Here's the other thing that I think is important to learn from your experience:  You're running a supported version of Windows, and keeping it current with security patches.  You've got good anti-malware running, and with current definitions.  You may, however have overlooked some security issues with third party software on your system.  For example, you might have an out of date version of Sun Java, Adobe Reader, Adobe Flash plug-in, or a number of other Internet-facing apps which can have vulnerabilities by which your system can be infected without action on your part.  IE8 can't prevent an infection via a vulnerable Flash plugin, for example.
 
One good tool for home users to assess these vulnerabilities is Secunia's PSI:
 
http://secunia.com/vulnerability_scanning/personal/
 
This can show you what vulnerable or outdated third-party apps you have and help you easily get them patched.  Even the average security professional may be surprised at what they find on their systems--this is worth the modest time involved to check out--and it is free for individual home users.
 
I share your disappointment that Microsoft Security Essentials didn't prevent this infection--but this is always going to be a risk, I'm afraid--so dig a little deeper to see if you can spot what the vulnerability was that allowed this thing to install without action on your part--that will get your system to a more secure place should you ever again encounter something which is not yet in the MSE definitions.
