joeu2004 say: Gary's Student wrote:
http://office.microsoft.com/en-us/templates/results.aspx?ck=1&ex=2&qu=mortgage&av=all#ai:TC001056618|

That template duplicates the mistake that I presume "ExcelRocks" made when he/she complained that "the PMT function [...] does not charge interest correctly".

The template incorrectly computes the biweekly period by the following paradigm:

=PMT(D5/26, D6*26, -D4)

where D5 is the annual interest rate, D6 is the loan term in years, and D4 is the loan amount.

Although there are a variety of ways that biweekly loans are implemented, that is not one of them, AFAIK.  There is little benefit to structuring a biweekly loan that way instead of the more common semimonthly loan.

Instead, the payment for biweekly loans is usually half the monthly payment for the same loan terms.

For example, for a 30-year loan of $100,000 at 6.5%, the biweekly payment is:

=PMT(6.5%/12, 30*12, -100000) / 2

That results in a significant savings in total interest and a shorter loan term because the total of 24 payments is the same as 12 monthly payments, but we are making 2 more payments each 12 months.

That gives rise to the often-repeated, but usually incorrect statement that "the two additional payments per year are applied directly to the principal".

That might be true for some implementations of biweekly loans [3].  But generally, it is an over-simplification.  Nonetheless, it is true that the principal is reduced significantly more quickly with most biweekly loans.  (All but the paradigm used by the template that GS points to.)

I believe the correct amortization of the most favorable implementation of biweekly loans would use the aforementioned payment (half the monthly payment), but charge interest at the rate of 6.5%/26 in my example.

That paradigm matches the amorization schedule produced by one online calculator [1].

However, it differs somewhat from the amortization schedule produced by another onlilne calculator [2].  I am still trying to figure what variant of a biweekly loan this calculator assumes. 
For a comparison of some different implementations of biweekly loans, download "biweekly amort.xls" (click here) [4].


-----
[1] http://mortgage-x.com/calculators/biweekly_payments.asp (click here)


The small difference between this amortization schedule and mine is due to my rounding the biweekly payment.

[2] http://www.bankrate.com/calculators/mortgages/bi-weekly-mortgage-calculator.aspx (click here)


[3] In some cases, biweekly loans involve a thirty-party who holds the biweekly payments in an account, then pays semimonthly or monthly payments to the lender, adding the 2 remaining biweekly payments to the last payment after each 12 months.  This arrangement does indeed apply the last 2 biweekly payments entirely to principal.  But this is not the most favorable implementation of a biweekly loan.

[4] http://www.box.com/s/y4hg724xcjuh6ep3jl2p (click here)

[5] [EDIT] Although a lender is free to calculate actual interest by any "reasonable" method in the US, they must calculate the APR for biweekly payments based on 6.5%/26 in my example, according to Appendix J of the Truth In Lending Act.  I believe most lenders will calculate actual interest the same way.

jay freedman say:
Hold down the Shift key while you open the file. That will prevent automatic macros (Auto_Open or Workbook_Open) from running. You can then go into the macro editor and either fix the problem or comment out the macros until you can examine them.

The most common reason for "User-defined type not defined" errors is that you haven't set references to external code files that contain the definitions of objects used in the macros. For example, if the macros use a FileSystemObject to manage files, but you haven't set a reference to the Microsoft Scripting Runtime, you'll get the undefined type error (VBA assumes that any type it doesn't have a definition for must be user-defined).

To set a reference in the macro editor, click the Tools menu and click References in the dropdown. Put a check mark next to the name of the code file for the object(s) in the macros. If you don't know which ones to check, post the Dim statements from your macros and someone will help you.
