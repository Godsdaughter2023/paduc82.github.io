quietman7 say:

A Potentially Unwanted Program (PUP) is a very broad threat category which can encompass any number of different programs to include those which are benign as well as problematic. Thus, this type of detection does not always necessarily mean the file is malicious or a bad program. PUPs in and of themselves are not always bad...many are generally known, non-malicious but unwanted software usually containing common practice by legitimate vendors to include toolbars, add-ons/plug-ins and browser extensions. PUPs are considered unwanted because they can cause undesirable system performance or other problems and are sometimes installed without the user's consent since they are often included when downloading legitimate programs.

PUPs may also be defined somewhat differently by various security vendors and may or may not be detected/removed based on that definition. That fact adds to confusion and a lot of complaints from end users asking why a detection was not made on a particular file (program) they are having issues with.

Windows Defender detects and blocks PUPs/PUAs if it is enabled.

  • How Microsoft identifies malware and potentially unwanted applications
  • Detect and block potentially unwanted applications in Windows Defender
  • How to Enable or Disable Windows Defender PUA Protection in Windows 10

.

In contrast Malwarebytes has an aggressive PUP Policy.

  • What are the 'PUP' detections, are they threats and should they be deleted?
  • Malwarebytes hardens its stand against Potentially Unwanted Programs
  • Malwarebytes gets tougher on PUPs
  • New Crite (PUPs)
  • Malwarebytes PUP Policy: How do we identify potentially unwanted software?

.

Aggressiveness sometimes can lead to false positives which is why Malwarebytes has a specific policy to remedy the detection.

rob koch say:

Billy,

To summarize and simplify all of the basically correct answers already given, Microsoft doesn't need the hassle of dealing with PUPs for consumers.

The built-in PUPs protection that Windows Defender contains was designed for use only by Enterprise businesses, since they have IT departments and company policies in place that allow them to simply block all such junk from being installed on their computers.  That's why this is the only option available when PUPs protection is enabled manually for Defender.

Consumers, on the other hand, vary as to their tolerance and preferences relating to such questionable junk, since some may wish to install those PUPs which allow them to get special "deals' if they're willing to accept advertising for example, while others want nothing to do with any of them.

Designing and supporting an interface that allows these different types of consumers to each have what they want is difficult and costly, since people vary in their ability to understand and effectively use such tools.  For this reason, Microsoft has decided to stay out of this mess with consumers, but left the workaround to manually enable their PUPs protection for those willing to accept the side effect of no granular control.

Those like Malwarebytes Anti-Malware, on the other hand, can sell their products as a potentially better solution, in exchange for creating and supporting a more granular interface.  That's allowing the marketplace to decide whether someone wishes to pay for these additional abilities or manually run the 3rd-party tool for free every so often in order to clean them out.

Microsoft did not create Windows Defender to replace all 3rd-party security products, only to provide good solid protection from truly malicious software.  PUPs is one area of less true security concern where 3rd-party products can differentiate themselves and still make money.

This isn't written in so many words by Microsoft anywhere that I've seen, but that's been the way they've treated their core security products like MSE and Defender since they first released them around 2006-7.
