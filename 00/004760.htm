Device not recognized by the Windows Phone Recovery Tool - Microsoft Community

jason[msft] say:

Device not recognized by the Windows Phone Recovery Tool -Troubleshooting tips

After upgrading to Windows 10 Technical Preview, if you would like to recover to Windows Phone 8.1, you may use the Windows Phone Recovery Tool (WPRT).  When this tool detects your device correctly, it will display a photo of your device and the device model/name. If your device is not recognized:
 
From the PC’s Start menu, search for Device Manager. Open Device Manager and check to see if the phone is listed. If it is not, try the following steps until the phone is listed.

  1. Disconnect and reconnect the device to the USB cable.
  2. If possible connect the phone to the PC with a different USB cable.
  3. Reboot the PC and check Device Manager once again.
Once the device is displayed in Device Manager, check the WPRT to see if the device is recognized.  If not, continue with the steps below:
  1. Uninstall and then reinstall the WPRT.
  2. Re-launch the WPRT and connect the device.
  3. If the device still isn’t recognized, boot the phone to Flashing Mode:
    - With the phone off, press the Power key to turn it on
    - Immediately press and hold Volume Up
    - Hold “Volume up” until you see the flashing mode icon on the screen (a lightning bolt and gear)
  4. If the device is still not detected, select Phone not detected in the WPRT.
    - To continue, you may need to reboot the phone by holding down the Power + Volume Down keys until the device vibrates.
These steps should resolve the issue of the WPRT not recognizing the phone.

The Beauty of Transitions in PowerPoint - Microsoft Community

glenna shaw say:

The Beauty of Transitions in PowerPoint

Summary
Technical Level: Basic Applies to: PowerPoint 2013, PowerPoint 2010
Details
My MVP brother, Glen Millar, can frequently be heard to say how much he doesn’t like transitions. So I, like any true sibling, love them and always have.  So much so I even wrote articles about how to fake 3D transitions in PowerPoint 2003, create a scrolling effect in PowerPoint 2007 and create pan and zoom effects in PowerPoint.  With the release of PowerPoint 2010, I was thrilled to see I no longer need to fake it.  My very favorite new feature is that transitions play correctly in all directions.  You now have the ability to move forward and backward through your slideshow and your slides will transition appropriately.  No more need for extra slides to compensate.

I’m especially fond of the new Dynamic Content transitions.  When PowerPoint 2010 was first released I found them a little confusing and even complained that they weren’t really transitions but full slide animations.  That was before fellow MVP Troy Chollar showed me a slide he had created using a Dynamic Content transition.  Wow, was I hooked!

 

Dynamic Content transitions are essentially a two for the price of one transition.  The background does a fade transition and the content changes simultaneously using the transition you choose.  This means if your background is the same on your slides it will look as if your content moves independently on and off the screen.

There are seven options for Dynamic Content; Pan, Ferris Wheel, Conveyor, Rotate, Window, Orbit and Fly Through.  With a little experimentation of the options for direction and timing you can create something really special. 

 

I recently had a friend deliver a keynote presentation on Cloud Computing at a major conference.  By using Dynamic Content transitions and alternating two cloud backgrounds I created a presentation that looked as if his content tumbled and flew through the clouds.  The result was light and airy and perfectly suited to his topic.

 

I’ve put together a presentation that demonstrates some of the transitions in PowerPoint 2010.  You can download it from my public SkyDrive.   You’ll notice it’s a zip file.  This is to force you to download it and play it in PowerPoint 2010.  Sadly, transitions do not play in the PowerPoint Web Apps.

All the transitions in PowerPoint 2010 are pretty spectacular and offer amazing potential for the creative slide show designer.

Any SQL queries that are saved in Access lose their formatting (indentation, line breaks, spacing), thus ensuring that complex queries become unreadable.  In order to deal with this, I've kept each saved query in its original format in an external text file.  It's very cumbersome, especially if you frequntly do wholesale modifications across all your SQL.
