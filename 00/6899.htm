brian tillman [outlook mvp 2007-2019] say:

What makes you think your account is sending the messages?  Do you see them in your Sent folder?

The fact that people see spam messages that appear to come from you does not mean that your account was used to send them.  It's child's play to send mail that appears to come from any address one wishes.  I could easily send mail that looks like it comes from you if I knew your address.

I doubt your account was compromised, but clearly your address has been spoofed.  This is an everyday occurrence on the Internet and it happens to most people at some point.  There's nothing you can do to stop it.  Some spammer, who could be on the other side of the world, has obtained your address and is using it on his computer as the sender of the spam messages.  He's not using your account at all.

As to the 30 day wait, you're stuck there.  The wait has no exceptions.  It is unlikely that changing your security information will stop the messages because your account isn't sending them.  Nevertheless, making sure your password is strong and long, that your security questions are up-to-date (and always provide false answers for them - you can write down the answers you shoose), that you have a cell phone connected to your account, that your alternate addresses are always up-to-date and ones you can currently access, and that you've enabled two-step verification.  These steps will keep your account safe from compromise.

brian tillman [outlook mvp 2007-2019] say:

Deleting your account is certainly one way to deal with the issue.  If you don't own the address any more, you won't see any bounces that it receives.  That seems rather draconian to me, however.

My approach to this problem is simply to wait.  Spammers are constantly changing the sender address for their spew.  In a few days or so, the spammer will begin using some other poor victim's address and they'll start getting the non-delivery reports.

You can also send your contacts a message telling them that your address was spoofed so they don't give up on you.

To make sure your account hasn't been compromised, first check https://account.live.com/Activity to make sure all of the successful logins originated with you.  The IP addresses that appear should belong to your ISP's address space.  You can also follow the "Learn how to make your acccount more secure" link on that page for tips on how to armor your account.  One of the best ways is to enable two-step verification, but it does make more work for you when changing passwords or getting the applications you use with Outlook.com configured.  I prefer the safety over the inconvenience.
