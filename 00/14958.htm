Deleting a range of cells that includes merged rows extending outside - Microsoft Community

honest abe say:

Deleting a range of cells that includes merged rows extending outside of range

PROBLEM:  We often have tables (not always defined as a formal table range) that include horizontally merged cells within a row.  On occasion, we need to delete a block of cells from the center of the table where the merged rows span over the cells being deleted.  This operation cannot be performed because we cannot specifically select the block of cells we want to delete.

GLITCH:  When a block selection includes horizontally merged cells, the selection is forced to expand to included the full width of any merged cells included in the range.  This, in turn, forces a widened selection that includes cells in the selection, to the left and right, that we do not want to delete.

RAMIFICATIONS:  We cannot physically select the desired block of cells, therefore, we cannot delete the desired cells.  This is an insane frustration, evidenced by the may questions on the internet regarding this behavior.  Ironically, the user can properly delete complete rows or columns that intersect a range of merged cells.  This anomaly also prevents the deletion of blocks of text with vertically merged cells in a column.

While we would be willing to utilize a simple macro or VBA routine to perform this function, we would certainly prefer a trick that would allow us to select the proper block of cells and delete them as normally done.  Thank you for any assistance.

bernie deitrick say:

Merged cells are really only useful when used for final reports, where you aren't inserting or deleting or making any changes, and you just want to make things pretty.

One trick is to just enter the value (or formula) into a single cell, leaving the others completely blank. The text will spill over the blank cells, and you can adjust the horizontal alignment to get the effect your want.  For example, if you have an odd number of columns, use the center column and center the text. Otherwise, use the left-most cell and left-align the text, and use formatting (fill and outline of the block not the individual cells) to make the value appear to be merged over a number of columns.

honest abe say: Thank you for the reply, Bernie.  As you suggest, we have used this technique many, many times.  However, we use this work-around mainly out of frustration with merged cells.  Ironically, merged cells do have many benefits going beyond just displaying text over a range of cells.  The frustration is that Microsoft so often takes a very intuitive operation and makes it nearly impossible to perform without coding.

bernie deitrick say: Honestly, that's the first time I've heard of "many benefits" to merged cells.
