    There is really nothing useful in that report. Event ID 41 only indicates that you rebooted the machine

    manually. Sometimes it can be a good idea to reboot via Windows when this happens as this allows

    - Have you logged the temps for both the cpu and gpu to verify they are not overheating ?

    - Have you tested the RAM, one stick at a time (You can easily get by on 8GB so to make troubleshooting quicker

    you can test two sticks and run them until you sort out the issue) ?

    - Have you run chkdsk on your HDD to verify there are no errors or bad sectors ?

    - Have you made any changes to the settings in BIOS ? Particularly RAM speed or timings ?

    If so, revert to default (auto) settings.

    - If you are running system monitors, or 'optimising/overclocking' tools be sure to run only one at a time.

    ie- Fraps can conflict with MSI Afterburner.

    System 'optimizing' tools such as Afterburner can also cause problems on their own from time to time.

    ASUS software has a reputation for causing problems.

    Similarly, running two or more Anti-Virus/malware programs in resident (always on) can cause conflicts, and

    use unnecessary system resources.

    There are plenty of other software conflict possibilities. Limiting the number of programs that launch in

    the background when you boot into Windows may help.

    (Type  msconfig  into the Run command. In System Configuration, click on the Startup tab and clear the check box

    next to any auto updating software, or other software you do not need to have running all the time.)

    btw - While it would not be the source of your problem, your power supply is way over the top.

    Power supplies are most efficient when the load is at a minimum of about 25%, and max of around 85%.

    With a psu of that capacity you would be below 25% when not running games.

    What this means is your power supply will use more power, and create more heat than necessary during all

    those idle and 2D hours when you are not playing games.

    When running games you would still not be even close the desirable max 85% load .

    650W would easily be adequate, and 750W would offer heaps of headroom.

    Your whole system would use somewhere around 100W at idle, with small boosts when doing non intensive tasks.

    Playing a video may boost it up to around 200W.

    The peak power consumption of a 290X  when gaming  is about 225W, but 'synthetic' benchmarking tests such as Furmark could push it higher (You can get more accurate details from reviews for the gpu and cpu).

    "CPU gets to about 55-60 C"

    I don't know what the thermal protection cut off is for the newer AMD cpu's, but my other machine

    has an AMD Phenom II 965 (2010) which is supposed to be kept below 62C. The thermal protection temp

    is supposed to throttle the cpu's clocks to protect it from serious overheating, but damage should not happen

    before say 80C.

    You probably should research the safe operating temp for your model though.
    Having said that. 55C should not be a problem. My Phenom II may (haven't checked it is a couple of years)

    run around that temp on hotter summer days (30+C) when under full load. 

    45-55C is a desirable full load temp with pretty much any cpu.

    - Gpu's get a fair bit hotter. My GTX770 will hit 80C under full load on hot days, but generally runs in the high

    60 - mid 70's C . Most gpu's have a thermal cut off close to 100C, but anything over 90C is not a good sign.
