bill manville say:

Correct.

The name of the property is FormulaR1C1 (ie the formula in R1C1 notation rather than in A1 notation.
Change R22C2 back to R1C1

You have R5C9 in one place but R5C5 in the other which looks like a mistake.
But if you want the formula to change as you copy it to other cells you will need to use relative referencing rather than absolute.  R5C9 is equivalent to $I$5, an absolute reference.

Since (presumably) you want R22C2 to reference R5C9 and R23C2 to reference R6C9 you will need to change the R5C9 in the formula to R[-17]C[7] which is equivalent to I5, a relative reference.

Simpler, though , and clearer would be to use the A1 style Formula property:

  .Formula = "=IF('C:\Users\ben biggs\Desktop\BOMServerFolder\" _

            & "[wwtconsumptionentry.xlsm]sheet1'!I5="""",NA(),'C:\Users\ben biggs\Desktop\BOMServerFolder\" _
            & "[wwtconsumptionentry.xlsm]sheet1'!I5)"

Hi,

you can use conditional formatting between 2 worksheets in the same workbook but you must give the cells a name that you reference in the conditional formatting.

Example:

Sheet1!A1: 100 (named sheet1Value )
Sheet2!A1 50 (with conditional formatting): CF formula is:  =sheet1Value > A1

Result: Cell A1 on Sheet2 will be formatted in the chosen format.
