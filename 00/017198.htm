leahfdz say:

How to use a formula in excell that calculates the exact amount of days

Hi, I am trying to calculate the interest that we should pay within a number of days, within specific date range. What formula would recognize that it needs to calculate number of days as they are stated on the spread sheet. for instance, from 01/01/2016 to 01/31/2016 31 days and no 30. in addition if you know a formula where a could use 360/365/366 as basis.

as of now this the formula I'm using:

=(a1-a2)*sum(a3*b3)/365: where A1=01/31/2016 A2=01/01/2016 A3=Principal Balance B3=Interest Rate

Thank you. 

bernard liengme say:

I will assume that the this is a compound interest problem

So the correct formula is =A3*(1+B3/365)^(A1-A2)-A3

The term =A3*(1+B3/365)^(A1-A2) computes the value of A3 after (A1-A2) days when the annualized interest is the value in B3 --- in the finance world this is called the future value.  So we subtract A3 to find the interest.

When A3 is $100 and B3 is 5% pa, I get an interest of $0.41 in 30 days.

If you wish to use Excel's built-in function then the formula is

=FV(B3/365,A1-A2,0,A3)-A3

This results in a negative amount since A3 is positive: the Excel functions consider money passing from you to the bank as negative, and money from the bank to you (the $100 loan in this example) as positive.

Please note that my formulas give an answer very close to your simple interest method when A3 = 100, A1-A2 = 30, and B3 = 5%; you get 0.410959 while I get 0.411776. However, with A3 = 10,000, A1-A2 = 356 and B3 = 5%, I get 512.67 while you get 500

teylyn say:

Oh, I see. That is an issue with cell references in Table objects. Here is a workaround. Add a column with the name "hidden", and give it the formula

=1

Hide that column. Now you can use this formula in your ID column:

=COUNT(Table1[[#Headers],[hidden]]:[@hidden])

That will always be current and won't break or need refreshing when new rows are inserted in the middle of the table.
