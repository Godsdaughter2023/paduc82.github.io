"Sunny/16"... If one is not familiar with this old term - "Sunny/16" means(In film terms), setting the aperture at f16(Or equivalent), and the shutter at the ASA/ISO "film speed"(Or equivalent - assuming "perfect" "normal" film developing.) to get a "perfect" photo, when the sky is clear, and it's "about Noon"(Say, 10AM to 2PM..?). Actually, the same is true taking a photo of the Moon, on an otherwise dark night. "Sunny/16" - as the Moon is "about" the same brightness as anything else "sunlit". More exposure, the Moon is "blown-out"(Too bright white, with less detail). Less exposure, the Moon is "gray", with, again, less detail(Or, less "realistic" detail of either highs or lows in the photo.).

 

That said, All actual, realistic(Closest to the way it actually was.) photos(Film or digital) must use an exposure equivalent to "Sunny/16". Any other exposure is "faking it"(Might be fine but we should know what we're doing.), to try to pull more details out of either highs or lows, or trying to stop or blur action, etc. 

 

To get more or less depth of field, or "action stopping", or other criteria, with film of 125 ISO, one could shoot, for instance, at one 250th. of a second, at f11. Or, 1/60th. at f22...

Unless you've used all the levels of your original MLL, the usual suggestion is to use a lower level for the appendix numbering (see https://shaunakelly.com/word/numbering/numberingappendixes.html). If you're using one of the lower levels of the original MLL, but linked to a non-heading style (that is, a custom style), you can also change the outline level of the style (in the Paragraph dialog). suzanne s. barnhill say:
To get to the heart of the matter, no, you can't reuse heading styles (link them to more than one multilevel list). As long as you don't need the paragraph numbers of the headings for use in page numbers or caption numbers, it should work just as well to use a series of custom styles for the appendices. If your custom styles have outline levels corresponding to those of the corresponding heading styles, your Navigation pane and TOC should come out the way you want.

ken sheridan say: You cannot use aggregation operations in a page footer.  If you want to show an aggregated value in the page footer on the final page of the report only then:

1.  Add the text box control which aggregates the values to the Report Footer and set its Visible property to False (No).  Let's assume the control is named txtGrandTotal.

2.  Add a text box control to the Page Footer and set its ControlSource property to:

=IIf([Page]=[Pages],[txtGrandTotal],Null)

ken sheridan say:

Are you saying the aggregation operations do not work in the page footer?

Exactly; aggegation opearators like SUM, COUNT etc can only be used in a group or report footer.  If you want to show the result of an aggregation operation in a page footer you have to do the aggregation in a hidden control in a group or report footer, and reference the hidden control in the page footer.

Another technique is to cumulatively increment the value of a hidden control in the detail section, and reference it in the page footer.  This gives cumulative totals in each page footer.  If you want a total per page you need to reinitalise the hidden control in the page header's Format event procedure.
