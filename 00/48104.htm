don cuthbert say:

You would have to pay to upgrade to Windows 10 Pro from a Windows 7 Home edition.  Windows 10 Pro does not have a lot different that most users will use, so it may not be worth the cost, compared to the free upgrade to Windows 10 Home.  Pro does have software called Hyper-V that lets you create virtual machines to run on your screen like you are remote controlling a separate computer, but there does exist other free software to do this sort of thing.  You still have to purchase Windows to run on each Hyper-V virtual machine, or it will not activate, but you can run things like Linux in Hyper-V too.

You can always upgrade free now to Windows 10 Home and choose to pay for Pro at any time in the future.  Most of the computers in my house have Windows 10 Home, but I run Windows 10 Pro on mine, because I had upgraded from Windows 7 Ultimate originally.

a. user say:

The standard refresh rate for a digital monitor is 60 Hz, and that's where you should leave it.

In the good old days of hulking CRT monitors, the higher the refresh rate the better because a higher refresh rate meant that the image 'jiggled' less. Most people didn't detect any jiggling once the refresh rate hit 75 Hz, and that's where I set my Sony monitor. Some people who were particularly sensitive would spend a small fortune on a graphics card that could output 90 Hz, even 120 Hz.

But that was then and this is now. Image stability is not an issue in a digital monitor (LCD, LED, OLED) so the standard refresh rate is 60 Hz. There's no point to driving a monitor any higher.

If you want to know why the monitor advertises a 75 Hz refresh, that's for gamers who consistently play at frame rates higher than 75 fps. You're looking at a high-powered (and expensive) system.

Here's my advice. Ignore what Registry Mechanic tells you, and get rid
of that program.

Registry cleaning programs are all snake oil. Cleaning of the
registry isn't needed and is dangerous. Leave the registry alone and
don't use any registry cleaner. Despite what many people think, and
what vendors of registry cleaning software try to convince you of,
having unused registry entries doesn't really hurt you.

removing an entry you need is far greater than any potential benefit
it may have.

the market almost immediately. Many people have used a registry
cleaner and never had a problem with it.

Rather, the problem with a registry cleaner is that it carries with it
the substantial risk of having a problem. And since there is no
benefit to using a registry cleaner, running that risk is a very bad
bargain.
