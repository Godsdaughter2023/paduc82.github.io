I figured it out. So often we try to solve issues by leaping to the "mountain" rather than starting at the "mole hill" - which means look for the obvious solution rather than leaping to some exotic solution.

 

In this case, I was plugging the USB cable into the Matsunichi hard drive and then plugging it into a USB 2.0 port on my XP desktop and the hard rive was recognized and drivers installed.

 

Whenever I tried to plug the Matsunichi hard drive (already connected to the USB cable) into my Vista OS based laptop, nothing worked.

 

HOWEVER, I tried plugging the USB cable into the Vista OS based laptop FIRST and then plug the Matsunichi hard drive into the USB CABLE SECOND, everything worked. Vista recognized the Matsunichi as a USB Mass Storage device and loaded the driver no problem. Geesh.

 

Logically, since the Matsunichi hard drive derives it's power via the USB 2.0 port, it simply "boots" upon power up and Vista sees the device and loads a generic mass storage device driver.

 

Why Vista doesn't load as XP but demands the USB cable plugged into a USB 2.0 port 1st, who knows.

Hi equipment66, 

If you've already allowed a certain application to access your location through Windows Settings > Privacy > Location > Choose apps that can use your precise location, then please be advised that some apps and services may use technologies like Wi-Fi scanning to find out your device’s exact location. If an application requests high accuracy data, then it will choose whichever source is available.

As what I've read somewhere about location privacy: 

Most apps rely on the location platform in Windows to provide the location information. Depending on the hardware available on each device, the availability of the location information and its accuracy will vary. For instance, a device with GPS will provide better accuracy than a device without it and in general Wi-Fi based information is more accurate than the one based on reverse IP.

What this means is that for most desktop users (where GPS presence is very rare) if the computer doesn’t have a Wi-Fi adapter, the location results will be obtained using the IP address. Using this technique, the results can vary widely in accuracy and can sometimes be incorrect. 

Although, GPS also provides information about heading, speed, and altitude. This additional information is optional when the location information comes from other sources, such as IP address, GPS, Wi-Fi, Cell towers.
