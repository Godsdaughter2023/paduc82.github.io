amit tandon say:

Refer below image:

Enter below formula as an array formula (CTRL+SHIFT+ENTER) in cell C4 - this formula will ignore zeros (ie. zeros in cell C5 to C8) and calculate average of the most recent 10 values excluding zeros (note: any inbetween zeros will also be ignored):

=SUM(INDEX($C$5:$C$57,SMALL(INDEX(IF($C$5:$C$57<>"",IF($C$5:$C$57<>0,ROW($C$5:$C$57)-ROW($C$5)+1)),0),10)):$C$5)/10

Alternate - enter below formula as an array formula (CTRL+SHIFT+ENTER) in cell D4 - this formula will include all zeros (ie. zeros in cell C5 to C8) while calculating average - this would give similar results to Ashish's formula:

=AVERAGE(INDEX($C$5:$C$57,SMALL(INDEX(IF($C$5:$C$57<>"",ROW($C$5:$C$57)-ROW($C$5)+1),0),10)):$C$5)

To explain....

Excel and VBA use the native computer's standard 64-bit binary floating-point form (type Double) to encode numbers and perform arithmetic.

Consequently, most non-integers cannot be represented exactly, even though they might appear exact when displayed with up to 15 significant digits, the arbitrary limit for Excel and VBA.  (It actually requires 17 significant digits to convert from and into the exact internal representation.)

Because of these infinitesimal differences, it is prudent to explicitly round any arithmetic (or function) result whenever we expect it to be accurate to a specific precision, for example 2 decimal places in your case.
