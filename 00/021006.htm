1.1.1.1   Watermarks

Most of you know that a watermark, in Word parlance, is a grayed-out picture (such as a company logo) or diagonal text (typically “CONFIDENTIAL” or “DRAFT”) that appears subtlely behind the text on a page. It really isn’t “behind” the text, but it looks that way.I use watermarks all the time, particularly when I’m circulating a draft document that I don’t want to be mistaken for the final.

Until Office XP arrived on the scene, creating a watermark in Word was harder than shoeing a three-legged horse. Microsoft listened to our screams of pain (yes! really! it does happen, sometimes!) and built a very good - but not perfect - automatic watermark generator into Word 2002/Office XP.

If you have Word 2002, it’s very easy to create a picture, horizontal or 45-degree diagonal text watermark that appears on all the pages of a document. That’s the part the Crabby Office Lady talks about in her column (although she got several of the details wrong).

Here’s how you create a picture, horizontal or 45-degree diagonal text watermark on every page in a document, in Word 2002/Office XP or Word 2003:

  • Make sure you’re in Normal, Print Layout or Outline views (e.g., click View | Print Layout). If you’re in Web Layout or Reading Layout view - Reading Layout is new with Word 2003 - Word won’t let you create a watermark. (Watermarks don’t appear when you view a document in Reading Layout - a significant Office 2003 design limitation.)
  • Click Format | Background | Printed Watermark. If you aren’t in Print Layout View, Word flips you over to Print Layout.
  • Pick the picture or the text you want to appear as a watermark, set the obvious choices for formatting, and click OK.

It's really that easy.

But what if you want your text to appear at something other than a 45 degree angle? Or if you want to have the watermark appear on just one page? What if you want to use an AutoShape for the watermark - say, a star burst or a banner or a text box. How about a rotated picture?  Maybe you want to have a different watermark on even and odd pages, or you want the watermark to change at various points in the document.

Worst of all.... what if you're using Office 2000 or 97?

Ah, then you have a problem on your hands.

Ed Bott and I talk about creating watermarks manually on pages 309-311 of “Special Edition Using Microsoft Office 2000”. There are plenty of details, tips and tricks, but the general approach involves attaching a watermark to a header. Since headers can change for each section in a document, and you can have different headers for odd and even-numbered pages, there’s a whole world of options. The basic idea goes like this:

> Open the document and move to the section that will hold the watermark. Choose View | Header and Footer. If the text in the document proves too distracting, click the button marked Show/Hide Document.

> For a text watermark, choose Insert | Picture | WordArt. Choose the WordArt style in the upper left corner. Type and format the text and click OK. Click on the sizing handles and drag the WordArt object to make it larger. Use the Toolbar’s Free Rotate icon (which looks like a circular arrow around a dot) to rotate the text. Click the Format WordArt button on the toolbar and turn the watermark text into some light shade or gray, typically with no line (i.e., no outline on the text).

> For a picture watermark, choose Insert | Picture | From File.

(Warning: if you haven’t yet gotten rid of that %$#@! Drawing Canvas, see WOW 8.31 and zap it first!) Right-click on the picture and pick Format Picture | Layout. Click Behind Text, then click Advanced | Picture Position and remove the check from the box marked Move Object With Text. Format the picture the way you like, and when you’re done, click Close on the Header and Footer toolbar.

If you only want a watermark on a single page, don’t worry about  sticking it in the header. Use WordArt (Insert | Picture | WordArt) for a text watermark or simply insert the picture (Insert | Picture). Right-click on the WordArt or picture, pick Format Picture | Layout. Click Behind Text | Advanced | Picture Position and remove the check from the box marked Move Object With Text. That’ll give you a “watermark” on the current page that you can click and drag to your heart's content.

1.1.1.2   Watermarks part 2

WOWser Graeme Dick from New Zealand wrote and told me that he recorded a macro to insert a watermark, and in the process he discovered that the angle of the watermark can be changed to any arbitrary number of degrees. Somebody inside the Word development team was smart enough to build this feature into Word 2002 and 2003 - but for reasons known only to Redmond, nobody bothered to put it in the user interface!

Here's the easy way to create a text watermark in Word 2002 or 2003, and rotate it any way you like:

Ø      Click Format | Background | Printed Watermark

Ø      Click Text Watermark

Ø      Type the text you want, and choose your formatting. Click OK.

The watermark appears in your document.

Ø      Hit Alt+F11 (that is, hold down the Alt key and hit F11; if you don't have an F11 key, click Tools | Macro | Visual Basic Editor). You're going to bring up the VBA Editor. Don't worry, it isn't as scary as it sounds.

Ø      Hit Ctrl+G (or click View | Immediate Window). You may have to click and drag things around a bit, but at the bottom of the VBA Editor, you should see a window that says Immediate.

Ø      Type this in the Immediate Window (or copy and paste it):

ActiveDocument.Sections(1).Headers(1).Range.ShapeRange.Rotation = 180

Capitalization doesn't matter.

Ø      > Hit Enter.

Your watermark will turn upside down. That's what the "180" means. Change the "180" to whatever you like - play with it a bit. Each time you type over the number, hit Enter, and the watermark will rotate the way you ask. You'll find that a "90" makes your watermark read from top to bottom, and "270" makes it go from bottom to top.

Ø      > When you're done, click the "X" in the upper right corner of the VBA Editor and it will go away. The watermark, however, stays put.

1.1.1.3   Watermark (Word 97)
  1. Choose View + Header and Footer.
  2. On the Header and Footer toolbar, click Show/Hide Document Text to temporarily hide the text and other items in your document.
  3. Choose Insert + Picture; then choose either Clip Art or From File, select your Watermark image, and click OK to insert it.
  4. Right-click the picture and choose Format Picture from the shortcut menu.
  5. Click the Picture tab and set Color to Semi-transparent (this automatically lightens the picture to a suitable watermark shade).
  6. Click the Wrapping tab, set Wrap to None, and click OK.
  7. Size and position the picture as desired. Remember that any part of the picture in your printer's unprintable range will not print (see yesterday's tip).
  8. On the Header and Footer toolbar, click Close.
  9. Your new watermark appears on every page.

dale howard [mvp] say:

CJ --

Good question, my friend.  You cannot use the Organizer tool to copy the local fields to the Enterprise Global file.  Instead, you will need to open the enterprise project in Microsoft Project while connected to Project Online, and then click Project > Custom Fields.  In the Custom Fields dialog, you will need to select each local field individually and then click the Add Field to Enterprise button.  In the Add Field to Enterprise dialog, leave all of the information UNCHANGED and then click the OK button.  Once the field has been imported, you will see a confirmation dialog.

When importing the local fields, you will be able to import both Task and Resource local fields and the system will create an exact match as an enterprise field.  If the field is an Outline Code field, the system will create both an enterprise field AND an enterprise lookup table with the same names.  The process is pretty cool.

After you have imported all of your local custom fields into Project Online, you will then need to log into PWA with administrator permissions.  Navigate to the PWA Settings page and click the Enterprise Custom Fields and Lookup Tables link.  For each of the custom local fields and lookup tables that you imported, you will need to edit the name of the field or lookup table and remove all of the text to the right of the name, including the parentheses characters as well.  For example, to prepare this reply to you, I imported a local task Outline Code field named Task State.  After the import, the enterprise field is named Task State (Outline Code1), so I need to remove all of the text to the right of the word State.

I realize you are probably looking for a faster way to import your local fields into Project Online, but there is no faster way.  Hope this helps.

dale howard [mvp] say:

CJ --

What is the source of the custom fields in the local MPP file?  Are they truly local custom fields you created in the project, or are they residual enterprise custom fields from another Project Online instance?  If they are the former, you should see them in the Custom Fields dialog when connected to Project Online.  If they are the latter, they will disappear as you describe, because they are not local fields (they are enterprise fields from the other instance).  In the latter case, you can either recreate the enterprise fields manually, or you can purchase a tool to migrate the custom fields from one instance to another.  Look at what FluentPro offers in this regard.

Focused Inbox separates your inbox into two tabs—Focused and Other. Your most important emails are on the Focused tab while the rest remain easily accessible—but out of the way—on the Other tab. As of now, the only option on how your messages get organized is by moving a single message from Focused tab to the Other tab or vice versa. You can also choose the option Always move to Focused inbox if you want all future messages from the sender to be delivered to the Focused tab. The instructions on how to do this are slightly different depending on whether you're using the Outlook.com beta.

If your mailbox looks like the image below, here are the steps:

  1. From your inbox, select the Focused or Other tab, and then right-click the message you want to move.
  2. If moving a single message from Focused to Other, choose Move > Move to Other inbox. Choose Always move to Other inbox if you want all future messages from the sender to be delivered to the Other tab.
  • If moving a single message from Other to Focused, choose Move > Move to Focused inbox. Choose Always move to Focused inbox if you want all future messages from the sender to be delivered to the Focused tab.

If your mailbox looks like the image below, here are the steps:

  1. From your inbox, select the Focused or Other tab, and then right-click the message you want to move.
  2. If moving a single message from Focused to Other, choose Move to Other inbox. Choose Always move to Other inbox if you want all future messages from the sender to be delivered to the Other tab.
  • If moving a single message from Other to Focused, choose Move to Focused inbox. Choose Always move to Focused inbox if you want all future messages from the sender to be delivered to the Focused tab.
