If the e-mail message gets autoarchived, then yes it would disappear from your To-Do list.

Solutions:

  • Set a longer auto-archive interval.  3 months may be too short - why not try 6. 
  • Move it to a folder that doesn't get autoarchived.
  • The autoarchive works off the modified date, not the received date.  If you update the due date on the e-mail's flag then the item is modified and you've got another 3 months before it will get archived under your current system.  Change the due date every month and it will never autoarchive.

rob koch say:

It's absolutely normal for most careful PC users, since true malware or badly behaving spyware/adware is rarely experienced by those who don't go looking for trouble.

The reason some 3rd-party security products tend to find more items is they look for lesser items like any adware or even cookies, regardless of whether these are truly dangerous or simply not always desired.  This has always been true, since to prove their value these products need to 'do something', whether there's any real value or security risk involved or not.

The difference with Windows Defender is it is designed specifically to protect a Windows PC from real malware or badly behaving software that could result in financial or personal privacy issues for the PC owner.  It's a free product, so there's no need to provide some supposed results for the money spent on it.  However, this often confuses less technically knowledgeable PC owners, since they're used to the 3rd-party products they've used in the past always finding something, so they assume there's lots of malware in their PC at all times.

In over 10 years of using the Microsoft security products, the only time I've typically found any true malware was when I went looking for it on less reputable or truly dangerous websites.  When I've run a reputable on demand 3rd-party scanner the only things found have been minor adware or other items I'd purposefully downloaded and so knew were present, so the Microsoft security products have never missed anything important on my systems.

If you aren't comfortable running a program that doesn't constantly find minor privacy items like cookies and non-malicious adware then you may want to switch to a 3rd-party product that does.  Personally I don't like all of the "noise" these pointless detections create and would rather only be informed when truly malicious items are detected.  Since most of the free 3rd-party products now advertise and only the paid products provide the most current and best protection abilities, you'd need to decide how important these things were to you vs. using the free Microsoft Defender with no advertising.
