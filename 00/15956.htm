gary's student say:

Many companies use a two level rating/ranking system.  The first level measures performance against expectations.  Say the values are 1 to 1000.  A rating of 1000 means vastly exceeding expectations.  A rating of 1 means no measurable pulse.

The first rating is assigned without regard to the rating of other employees.

The employees are then sorted by the first level rating.  Lines are drawn in the sand and the second level ratings (1 thru 5) arbitrarily based on percent of the population.  Say the top 10% are assigned a "5", for example.  The key issue is that once the two levels have been assigned, it very easy to do "what if" budgetary analyses.  This is because the underlying "bell curve" is unchanged.

You just re-assign the percentages (say from 10,20,40,20,10 to 5,20,50,20,5) and evaluate the impact on cost.

If this is inconsistent with your busines practice, ignore this post.

brian tillman [outlook mvp 2007-2019] say:

You posted your question in the Feedback forum which is, as you can easily tell from the name, is for posting feedback about the Microsoft Answers web site, not for asking Office questions.  I moved your question to the Office 2010 "Installing and Activating Microsoft Office Products" forum.

There is not a lot of difference between Microsoft Office Home and Business 2007 and 2010 in functionality.  The biggest difference you'll see is in Outlook.  Outlook 2007 has the older main window interface, similar to Outlook 2003, while the other applications in the suite have the ribbon much like they have in Office 2010.  There are also differences in Outlook in how some data is stored internally.  For example, Outlook 2007 stores the autocompletion cache in the NK2 file, just like its predecessor, which Outlook 2010 stores the autocompletion cache in a hidden message in the data file.

There are some differences in each of the applications in the suite, but I find them to be fairly insignificant.
