a. user say:

First Storage Spaces like RAID is not a backup.

If the data is important to you then you'll have at least 2 locations for it. One in Storage Spaces, and the other in another location. I use a NAS for my secondary backup.

Next, the different size drives do cause issues with how much you can use. Say you have 2 drives, one is 1 TB and the other is 2 TB. In storage spaces you can only use a maximum of the smallest drive. So in this scenario you'd only have a total of 2 TB of space, 1 TB from each drive with 1 TB not being used on the 2 TB drive. It is best for the Storage Pools to use the same size drives.

You are thinking of JBOD storage, however Storage Spaces doesn't support that method of storage.

jgt say:

It is my understanding that Storage Spaces is very similar to JBOD in that I can use drives of different sizes and the resulting storage pool is the total of all the disk minus space for the overhead. The overhead depends on the type of data resiliency you use, currently following are the different types (NOTE, this is my summary from my readings and I may have some incorrectly stated).

  1. None: Windows will store only a single copy of your data. You’ll lose the data if one of your drives fails, but no space will be used on backups. Requires at least 2 drives, data is lost if one drive fails.
  2. Two-way mirror: Windows will store two copies of your data. If one of your drives fails, you won’t lose your data. This requires at least two drives, one drive can fail and you still have your data
  3. Three-way mirror: Windows will store three copies of your data. If one or two of your drives fails, you won’t lose your data. This requires at least five drives, one drive can fail and you still have your data
  4. Parity: Windows stores parity information with the data, protecting you from a single drive failure. Parity uses drive space more efficiently than mirroring, but file access times are slower.  Required at least three drives, one drive can fail without data loss.
  5. Double parity: In the latest version of Storage Spaces released together with Windows Server 2012 R2 one more layout was added (as I understand this is also in Win10 as well). This layout is similar to a RAID6, which utilizes two different parities meaning that a system can survive a double disk failure. Requires at least seven drives, two drives can fail without data loss.

Another advantage of Storage Pools is that if the system fails, let's say that Windows 10 fails and requires a complete reinstall. You could just remove the disk and put them in a new Windows 10 (or 8) system and immediately whatever is on the disk is available. After you rebuild your system you could then remove the disk from the temp system, install them in the new system and now your data is available without any additional effort on your part other than some very minor task such as assigning the drive letter and such. I don't think this would be true for RAID or a NAS.

a. user say:

If a Storage Pool is upgraded to I believe the Anniversary Update for Windows 10 then it isn't backwards compatible.

It's not like JBOD. For example, your Two-Way Mirror, if you have a 500 GB drive and a 1 TB drive, then the max size for that 2-way mirror is 500 GB.

As for the None, if you have the same drives, the max you be able to store is 1 TB (500 GB on each drive), not 1.5 TB. Storage Spaces writes to each drive equally and when the smallest drive runs out of space it stops.

Doesn't matter about the parity or mirroring, if your computer fails and takes out all your drives and you have no backups, then the data is gone. That is what I'm referring to. You should always have two distinct copies of data to have a backup. If you aren't concerned about the data don't worry about it.

Sorry that you are having this problem hopefully this info will help.

As a security feature in Windows 8 you are unable to add multiple accounts to the Messenger Application on a single Windows Account. The reason for this is because the way that messenger is built it processes the Information from the services you are registered to (Windows Live, Facebook, Twitter etc...) and sort's them into their own group so that it doesn't get confused.

But if you were able to have multiple account's signed in at once it would have to work overtime to sort out the different data from the different accounts and slow down and cause errors. 

The only solution I can give you is to create a second user account on your Computer and use your other account for it. But there is currently no way to have 2 accounts running simultaneously for the same service,
