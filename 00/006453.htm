chris de herrera say: Hi,
You've asked some interesting questions so here's my reply.

Basically the PPP implementation is very different than the RNDIS implementation in the following ways:

1. They use different IPs on the PC and the device.  So if your firewall on your PC does not support the new IPs of RNDIS it will fail.
2. The PPP stack is a private stack that is not the same as the PPP stack on the PC that ships with Windows.  This can allow it to function when RNDIS fails.
3. They use different drivers.

The issue of the IP address of Exchange highlights another issue within Windows Mobile.  Windows Mobile caches the DNS lookup longer than the TTL requirements.  Also, it does NOT flush the cache when a new connection is made.  So I highly recommend to enterprise customers to ensure that the external IP address be accessible from within their network to ensure that users can continue to access their e-mail from their desktop.

BTW, with the latest devices and WMDC or ActiveSync 4.5 you can enable an external internet connection on the device.  This should allow your e-mail to continue to work with Exchange without changing your environment.  However it may expose your PC and network to data coming via the internet connection on your Windows Mobile device.  Some companies do not allow you to connect to the internet while synchronizing due to this risk.

Using spaces to indent paragraphs is bizarrely archaic. However... assuming that he pressed Enter once after each paragraph, you can insert the spaces using Find/Replace. Press Ctrl+H. In Find what:, put ^p. In Replace with:, put ^p{type 8 spaces}, then click Replace All.

You might need to manually add 8 spaces before the 1st paragraph.

If he pressed Enter twice after each paragraph, then use Find what:^p^p, and Replace with ^p^p{type 8 spaces}.

Note: {type 8 spaces} means for you to press the space bar 8 times. Don't type the { } characters.

Desktop Icon Label Shadows Bug in Windows 10

There's a weird long-running bug in Windows 8.1 through Windows 10 where certain actions turn the drop shadow on underneath desktop icon lables no matter what your settings are under Visual Effects under Performance Options. Basically the checkbox labeled "Use drop shadows for icon labels on the desktop" has no effect either way.

There are endless forum threads about this through the internet going back for years.

There are many handy helpfully-intended reg edits available that don't work for many of us, no matter the setting, whether "0" or "1."

Etc.

For those of us with with problem, it manifests in 8.1 like this: you can't have your desktop background picture also be your Start Screen picture. If you do that, you'll be forced to have the drop shadows. There is no fix offered anywhere. No settings, no policy settings, no reg edits (I've tried several dozen, spent dozens of hours scrubbing the reg trying to find the secret), etc.

If you do turn on "same background as desktop," you'll have to turn it off and reboot to make the shadows go away.

In Windows 10, it works like this: if you have full-screen Start Menu with transparency, you are forced to have the drop shadows and no setting or available fix will have any effect.

ALSO, even if you turn off transparency on the Start Menu, but you turn ON window snapping in the Multitasking Settings, you will be forced to have the drop shadows on your desktop icon labels.

It seems silly to me that this bug has persisted so long. YEARS. Through multiple OS versions, thousands of updates and Builds.

I'm not asking for new ideas on things to try. I've been at this for years. I am suggesting that "Use drop shadows for icon labels on the desktop" should do what it's labeled to do without depending on Start transparency and Multitasking snapping options being set just a certain way.

hansv mvp say:

No, all cells in a row have the same height, and all cells in a column have the same width.

You can change the way the text is aligned vertically - by default, Excel uses bottom alignment, which looks odd with wrapped text of different lengths. You might prefer top alignment (Home tab of the ribbon, click the little arrow in the lower right corner of the Alignment group).

You can simulate different cell heights to some extent by merging cells vertically, but I don't know whetger that would suit your purpose.
