Updating old word document with consistent styles

Any help would be greatly appreciated.  I'm using Word 2010.

 

jay freedman say:

First, look through the document and make sure that heading styles have been used consistently -- that is, all paragraphs that are first-level headings have the same style, and all second-level headings have another style, and so on; and that you change the style of any that are out of place.

Using the built-in styles named Heading 1, Heading 2, ... Heading 9 is recommended, because it simplifies creating a table of contents and some other things, but you can use custom styles as long as their outline level numbers (set for each style in Modify Style > Format > Paragraph) are set up in a similar sequence.

Then follow the procedure in http://shaunakelly.com/word/numbering/numbering20072010.html. Follow it exactly and don't skip any steps. (If you've used custom styles, then in the step "Attach the Heading paragraph styles to the 9 levels in the list style" choose those styles instead of Heading 1 etc.) When you complete the procedure, the existing headings will automatically display their numbers.

At this point it would be good to read through the document again to make sure the numbers are as desired. If any paragraphs have the wrong style, you can apply the correct style.

The best ways to manage heading numbering in later editing are (1) applying the proper style to begin with, or changing to a different style, or (2) changing the view to Outline view and using the Promote and Demote arrows in the Outline Tools group.

If you go to File > Options > Proofing > AutoCorrect Options > AutoFormat As You Type and check the box for "Set left- and first-indent with tabs and backspaces", then you can change the style/outline level of a paragraph that's already in a heading style by putting the cursor at the start of the paragraph and pressing Tab or Backspace (or Shift Tab). If the paragraph isn't a heading, though, all that does is change the indent of the paragraph. It can be hard for users to remember rules that aren't consistent, so I don't recommend this method.

suzanne s. barnhill say:

To start, see http://www.shaunakelly.com/word/numbering/numbering20072010.html. You need to set up a multilevel list linked to styles. The styles don't have to be Word's built-in heading styles; you can start with the 1, 1.1, 1.1.1 template offered in the Multilevel List gallery and link it to, say List Number, List Number 2, List Number 3, etc.

You can use Modify Style to fine-tune the paragraph and font formatting of these styles, but the numbering and all indents on the left side (Left, Hanging, First Line) must be set in the Define New Multilevel List dialog.

Once you have the styles, with numbering, set up to your satisfaction, you should be able to work your way through the document tagging each paragraph with the appropriate style. If the styles are at the top of the Quick Styles gallery (which you can ensure with appropriate priority assignment), this will be fairly quick work, and having the styles easily available will make revision easier for those who come after you.

When new content is created, numbering levels can be demoted or promoted using Alt+Shift+Right/Left Arrow, but if you prefer to use Tab and Shift+Tab (or Backspace) then make sure to enable the option "Set left- and first-indent with tabs and backspaces" at File | Options | Proofing | AutoCorrect Options | AutoFormat As You Type.

Exploiting hotmail et al. spam filters with malformed sender - Microsoft Community

jimbo1987 say:

Exploiting hotmail et al. spam filters with malformed sender information

This exploit is sufficiently known as of January 2017 to make Hotmail essentially useless to me, so my hope is that enough scammers/spammers start using it to encourage Microsoft to patch the hole.  It'll be very quick and easy for MS to prevent, but they have lacked the motivation to do so since at least 2014.  Thus, I'm not holding my breath.

Here is how it works:

Spammer/Scammer places one or more specific illegal characters in the "From" field of the email header.  The logic that Microsoft applies to the emails to determine if they are spam, (including comparing the sender information to a backlist, etc.), has a bug in it that causes it to pass along these messages.

Here is a concrete example (where "@" is replaced by "[at]" so it won't be obscured):

Reply-To: <reply[at]severophard.com>

From: "H" <"<info.c9TJcr[at]innerpatent.com-------------->

Sender: "From" <info.jkRLcs[at]innerpatent.com>

To: <jimbo1987[at]hotmail.com>   <--altered to deny harvesting

Subject: 4

Date: Fri, 6 Jan 2017 12:18:17 -0700

Message-ID: <COL004-MC6F1361w4RA001a8a5f[at]COL004-MC6F13[at]innerpatent.com>Fri, 6 Jan 2017 14:18:17 -0500

MIME-Version: 1.0

Content-Type: multipart/alternative;

               boundary="----=_NextPart_000_B342_01D26BED.934C5480"

X-Mailer: Microsoft Outlook 16.0

Thread-Index: AQJO+lU1r//W0X7/bJQvfFJJNoMT6Q==

List-Unsubscribe: <>

Note the above example will display the sender info in Outlook as:

'From <info.jkRLcs[at]innerpatent.com>; on behalf of; H <"<info.c9TJcr[at]innerpatent.com-------------->'

(i.e. 'From' is the alias of the proxy-sender; 'info.jkRLcs[at]innerpatent.com' is the email address of the proxy-sender; 'H' is the alias of the 'real' sender; '"<info.c9TJcr[at]innerpatent.com-------------->' is the (invalid) email address of the 'real' sender)

Note that at least for Hotmail. they don't even need the legal "Sender" email address, and instead just use something like the following in the "From" field:

Military Flashlight > <"<info.23[at]zemal.angewi.com>  

Thus, the alias is 'Military Flashlight >' without the single-quotes.

The sending/from email address is '"<info.23[at]zemal.angewi.com' without the single-quotes.

See the leading double-quote and open-bracket ("<) in the email address, without a closing double-quote and bracket on the other end?  That is the magic that gets it past the filters, no matter how obvious the content is spam.    And it has the bonus that Outlook 2016 email client recognizes that it isn't a valid email address, so it errors when you try to block that sender in your Outlook 2016 app.  Obviously, the way they parse & handle the sender/from info in the fat Outlook client is different from the way they parse the sender/from information in the spam filters prior to delivery. 

This latter version allows unlimited spamming to Hotmail et al. accounts, without even the recipients being able to block the sender.  This of course obviates the need to even cycle through new sending domain names.
