    result:   -1.5134E-09.

    I use Microsoft Office Excel 2007 (12.0.6514.5000) SP2 MSO (12.0.6562.5003)

     

    Thanks,

     

    joeu2004 say:

    I perform the following sum in Excel.  I place the following numbers in say cell A1 to A4:
    27659709.76
    414895.65
    -27659709.76
    -414895.65

    Then perform a sum(A1:A4), or add them up. And surprisingly I got the following result:   -1.5134E-09.
    I expect the number to be exactly zero. Why it's not zero?


    First, let's talk about the solution.

    When you expect an arithmetic result to be accurate to a specific precision, for example 2 decimal places for monetary calculations:  either (a) explicitly round arithmetic and functions that involves non-integers (and division of integers); or (b) set the "Precision as displayed" (PAD) calculation option and choose an appropriate cell format.

    I prefer #a.  For example, your SUM formula should be =ROUND(SUM(A1:A4),2).

    Caveat:  You will not see -1.5134E-09 if you choose a numeric format with 2 decimal places.  But formatting alone usually only affects appearance, not the actual value in the cell.  (Unless you set PAD.)  So you might still encounter surprising results in dependent cells.

    As for why....

    Because of the way that Excel and many applications store numbers, most non-integers are not represented exactly.  You can find some details in http://support.microsoft.com/kb/78113.

    For example, the following is the exact internal numbers that Excel works with for the values you mention.

    A1:    27659709.7600000,016391277313232421875
    A2:         414895.650000000,023283064365386962890625
    A3:    =-A1
    A4:    =-A2

    When Excel sums A1:A4, it calculates the sum with the following intermediate results:

    A1+A2:          28074605.4100000,001490116119384765625
    A1+A2+A3:        414895.649999998,509883880615234375

    As you can see, the magnitude of SUM(A1:A3) is indeed slightly less.  Excel losses some precision because of the finite number of bits used to represent the magnitude.  And that will depend on the order of the calculations -- the relative magnitudes of the pairwise arithmetic operands.

    Consequently, when Excel adds A4, the result is indeed infinitesimally less than zero, namely about -1.5134E-09.

    a. user say:

    Hello Lyle:

    Thank you for posting to the forum regarding your questions about music files managed in Zune and getting these into your iTunes collection.  This forum is specific to the Xbox Music/Zune side of things so information related to working with iTunes will be limited.  I would be happy to clarify how things work with Zune which I hope will help you get you collection managed the way you want.

    It sounds like one of the primary issues is that you have files on your Zune player that are not currently stored on your computer.  If this is the case the first thing to do is reverse sync the player, http://support.xbox.com/en-US/xbox-on-other-devices/windows-phone-7/syncing-media .  This process will copy the contents of the Zune player to your computer via the Zune software.  From there you can look into getting the files moved to where iTunes can see them.

    Zune is a file management software.  This means it simply accesses content saved to your computer storage.  As you mentioned the content most often will be sent to the MY MUSIC library and will be found in a sub folder titled Zune.  In some cases files will be located in other folders or on drives other then your C:/ drive.

    The files that you acquire through Zune, from purchases, from downloads with a Music Pass or from the ripping of your CD's will be saved to the computer in either MP3 or WMA format.  The WMA files are ones downloaded with the Music Pass and have digital rights protections that will prevent them from being copied to devices other then a Zune player or a Windows Phone and they will not be able to be played via iTunes.  The MP3s on the other hand are a file free of digital rights and are files you own and can move as you please.

    When it comes to having these files seen or accessed by another piece of software the key is to learn how this software accesses content.  iTunes is similar to Zune in that is also accesses files saved to your computer storage.  What you will need to do is figure out where iTunes is looking or how to add additional locations to where it looks.  I have never been a user of Apple so have the minimalist experience with iTunes.  The one thing I have picked up is that in most cases those computers using iTunes will have a similar folder to Zune called "iTunes library" or something to that effect.  It may be worth trying to locate this and copying your MP3 content to this folder and moving from there.  If you are unable to make progress, I might suggest a Bing search for "Add MP3's to iTunes" or "add content to iTunes".  If worse comes to worse you might look into contacting Apple support about completing this process.

    If you have any further questions or concerns, please let us know.
