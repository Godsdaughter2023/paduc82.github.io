petrib say:

It is, of course, for a developer, an attractive dream:

 

"If I sell my app to 1M consumers at 0.79EUR (0.99USD), I earn 553 000EUR (70%). But if I sell it to Nokia, who manufactures 500M phones per year, and then I get them to pay 1EUR/phone, then I earn 500 000 000EUR, and I never need to work again!"

 

Sadly, both parts of this are dreams.

 

The first one is a dream for most app developers selling through the various "app stores". The median earning for an Apple App Store app is something like 1600USD (a far bit from the 553 000EUR, and even further from the 500MEUR). Yes, there are some success stories á la Angry Birds, etc., but most develpers/publishers earn absolutely nothing (not even enough to pay for their development PC, not speaking of the months of time they've spent on their app).

 

The second one is even a bigger dream (even if the dream was reduced to only, say 100M phones, and a 100MEUR payback). Device manufacturers do not like to pay anyone any app royalties at all. They absolutely hate the thought. If they do an app licensing deal, they're usually one-off deals + maybe some additional yearly percentage as maintenance fee, and even then it is a very, very hard sell (you have to have something they really, really like, and it is cheaper for them to buy it, than to develop it on their own).

 

Even an harder sell is selling a "runtime" engine like what Alien Dalvik is. Nokia already has a problem of "umpteen" runtimes they have to keep carrying on their phones, regardless of whether it is actuall used or not. In the case of Symbian based phones, for example, there's the original S60/Symbian layer, then there're the old PIPS/OpenC stuff, and Qt, and Java, and Flash Lite, and WRT. Adding another such layer to complicate their testing, compatibility and burden their customer support (a consumer with a problem with a Nokia phone will call Nokia, not Myriad, even if their problem is with an app that's dependent on Alien Dalvik, and the problem is with that, and not Nokia's code).

 

Best of luck to Myriad, but their chances of selling their product to Nokia is, in my estimate, close to zero, which - incidentally - is virtually the same chance that any Nokia phone user has of seeing it included on their Nokia phone when they buy it.

stefan blom say:

Searching for XE only will find that particular text, so your former XE fields are now most likely cross-reference fields instead (as I suggested in my previous reply).

Assuming that you do not have any other REF fields in your document, you can use a macro such as the following:

Sub RemoveREFFields()
'Macro created by Stefan Blom, MVP, January 2018
Dim f As Field
Dim FieldCount As Long
Dim i As Long
FieldCount = ActiveDocument.Fields.Count
For i = FieldCount To 1 Step -1
Set f = ActiveDocument.Fields(i)
If f.Type = wdFieldRef Then
f.Delete
End If
Next i
End Sub

Portable Document Format (.pdf) is not the same as Microsoft Word (.docx) word processing format. If you want to share a file that is editable in Microsoft Word (or any word processor for that matter) choose Microsoft Word format.

The usual purpose of PDF is to provide a document that is fixed in time and is not editable. A PDF file has the same look all the time on any machine.

So please have a look at the attributes. If they are missing, add them for the AD users and run DirSync to see if the primary addresses in the Cloud will be changed. For any problems or further questions, please feel free to post back.
