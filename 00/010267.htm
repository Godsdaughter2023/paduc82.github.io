To anybody out there.  You can create a rule that will work by identifying emails sent to you with the return address set as your own.  SPAM which is specifically designed to defeat your attempts at replying to the sender (which is most of them) will nearly always put your own address in the header's "Return-Path:" field.  Outlook can capture this in a rule if you match the whole field such as "Return-Path: *** Email address is removed for privacy ***" (note the space after the colon) in the text string to look for in the header for your rule.

rob koch say:

Typically most email storage operates like a database today, so ripping random files or portions of them out of these stores tends to corrupt the filing structure, thus in many cases antimalware simply inform rather then attempting to remove the malware they contain.

Since from the file path this infection is clearly contained within an email backup optimization database structure for the archive portion of that particular email program, you'd need to determine which specific email archive message this contains and remove that manually from the archive through the email program itself.

Another option might be to somehow exclude these backup optimization files or folders entirely from scanning by the antimalware program, but you'd need to discuss this at the Symantec forums for your program to determine whether they'd also recommend this and provide instructions for how to do it.

There might also be a way to force a refresh of the data these archive files contain to insure that these aren't remnants of some email message removed long ago which simply haven't been flushed since by whatever process was used to create them.  This would be best investigated in the forums supporting the email program they were created by, since that would be different for each specific program and sometimes even different versions as well.

Rob

Gridlines show the cell (cell: A box formed by the intersection of a row and column in a worksheet or a table, in which you enter information.) boundaries of a table (table: One or more rows of cells commonly used to display numbers and other items for quick reference and analysis.
