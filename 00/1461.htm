Try this for B

=MAX(OFFSET(A1,MATCH(E1,A:A,0)-1,1,4,1))

swischner say:
Apparently, the Onenote developers had mainly tablet (MS Surface) users in mind when they redesigned the user interface of (most) OneNote apps. So tapping the little arrow to open the notebook list as the only option might have seemed natural to them.
Fortunately, on MacOS you can assign your own keyboard shortcuts to menu entries and there is a OneNote menu command to expand the notebook list: “Expand Navigation” in the “View” menu. Here is how you assign a keyboard shortcut to it:

- Open the „System Preferences“ (eg. click on the Apple symbol) and chose “Keyboard”
- Click on the tab “Shortcuts” and select “App Shortcuts” from the left list
In the right list select “OneNote.app” and click on the “+” symbol to add a new shortcut
- In the next dialog, you have to type the name of the menu command in OneNote that you want to add a keyboard shortcut to. Type “Expand Navigation” in the Menu Title field. Use this exact spelling.
- Place the cursor inside the keyboard shortcut field and press the shortcut key (or combination) that you want to use. I tested it with F19, but most keys or combinations should work. Be careful not to overwrite an existing OneNote shortcut that you are using. Unfortunately, there is no overview of the already used keys here.

Back in OneNote, you will see that the command “Expand Navigation” in the “View” menu has got your shortcut assigned.

Unfortunately, this is not a toggle on/off command. So you can open the notebook list with that shortcut but not close it with the same key. There is another menu command for this (“Collapse Navigation”) that you could assign another shortcut to by using the above method.

Viz...

You’ll probably recognise most of the account activities listed on your Recent activity page.

Even if an activity sounds suspicious at first, you can expand it to see more details and decide whether you remember it.

For example, if you accidentally made a typo in your password, you’ll see Incorrect password entered in the list.

Or, if you signed in to your account from a mobile phone, you might find that your service provider routed the signal through a cell tower in another city.

But if an activity looks unfamiliar, or if you see an unusual pattern (like multiple sign-in attempts or changes that you’re sure you didn’t make), let us know.

We’ll help you make your account more secure to minimise any threats.

If you click “This was me”:

When you tell us you were responsible for an activity, we’ll keep track of that info going forward.

For example, if you sign in from a new location that happens to be the hotel you’re staying at on a business trip, we’ll add that location to the list of places we expect you might sign in from in the future.

If you click “This wasn't me”:

When you tell us that you don’t recognise an activity, it’s possible that a hacker or a malicious user has gotten access to your account.

To help protect your account, we’ll walk you through several steps, including changing your password and reviewing and updating your security info.
