Just to give you the 80,000 foot level description in case you weren't familiar, Slipstreaming, is the process someone coined that describes the shoehorning (my terminology) a legal copy of (say Windows 7, 64-bit) on DVD, with a major software release (say SP1), and porting the important aspects of both entities onto a new, separate DVD, that has all of the updates (accumulated to that point) on the new DVD (of your creation) for the purpose of building a system (i.e., you use the newly created disc rather than the original boxed disc).  You still use the original Product Activation key found on the original media.

 

You still have to abide by one disc, one license, one system aspect....

I believe the link I provided does a fair job of detailing the process as well.

 

I first discovered Paul's site back in XP's heyday, and used Paul's instructions to create--first a slipstreamed disc with SP2, and then the same thing again, when SP3 came out.

 

Basically, it affords you the means of installing (from the disc you create) a bona fide, and legal means of building a new system that already has the latest Service Pack integrated on it, without having to go through the Windows Update process (after the build or rebuild) like you would by using just the original disc (and subsequent updates).  Obviously, such an "event" (i.e., disc creation) represents only a snapshot in time and eventually, as more time passes, there will be more and more updates not on the disc, but it's nice to start somewhere closer to the finish-line than the very beginning....

marshall barton say:

Calculated controls are scheduled for recalculation and display when one of the expression's dependent controls' value is changed.  That does not mean the calculation and display are done immediately.  A task to do those things is scheduled, but at a low and lower priority.  Retrieving data from tables, executing VBA code, loading form/report objects, and many other things are tasks that run at a higher priority (not to mention other processes and tasks for other application running on your system).  In other words, you have absolutely no control over when control expressions are evaluated and or when they are displayed.

 

Burried in that long winded explanation is a clue to solving your dilemma.  VBA code runs at a very high priority, so the solution to your problem lies there.  Instead of using control source expressions to calculate values and display them in main form text boxes, use VBA code to calculate and set the main form text box's value.

 

That may or may not be a bit tricky depending on your VBA experience and the calculations you need, so, if you need help doing that, post back with details about the calculations you are using now.

marshall barton say:

I guess those expressions at least prartially explain why Access lost track of one of of the calvulation/display tasks.  The D... agregate functions have to create and execute an aggregate query to, which is yet another task to throw into the mix and with several of those going on nearly simutaneously it's not much of a stretch for something to get lost.

 

Those expressions can be used in VBA code nearly as they are:

 

lngCount = DCount("Trans_Cash","tblBCT","((BatchNum='" & Forms!MyForm!BatchNum & "') AND (Date_Rcvd=" & Format(Forms!MyForm!Date_Rcvd, "\#yyyy-m-d\#")) AND (Description Like 'Cond1*'))")
