XP's recovery console is very restricted in what it's allowed to access -
and user data isn't part of it.
It's there purely to allow a manual repair of the system files.
 
If you can get hold of, or build on another PC, either Bart P|E, or UBCD,
you'll be able to see what's on the drive in a familiar interface (i.e.
Windows), and access a USB to save files.
 
WRT your actual problem.... this appears to be a device driver problem -
bear in mind here that software can also be seen by the OS as a device
(Anti-Viruses are often classed as devices for some of their files!)
 
What hardware/software updates were done in the day or two before the
failure? If it's hardware - then remove the hardware - if it's software, try
booting in Safe Mode - allow at least 15 minutes before killing a Safe Mode
boot!! (I have personally seen the last file name of a boot be on screen for
10 minutes! - followed by a 'normal' boot)

The Command Prompt syntax you are probably used to and the syntax of commands in the Recovery Console are not exactly the same so it is easy to get frustrated with things like - "Why doesn't this work!" when using RC.  The problem of course has nothing to do with RC - it is cockpit error on the part of the user and not understanding that the two interfaces are not the same.

Anywho, like Noel says - RC is not a file copying tool - it is a troubleshooting/diagnostic tool and you are attempting to use it in ways it is not designed to work.

It is very good that you can get into RC though.

I would work on fixing your afflicted system first, but you can use alternatives to try and salvage files.

A genuine bootable XP installation CD

vibroplex say:

You could have an occasional choking of the port 3074 traffic on your internet connection (including the modem and especially the router), as the BlackOps servers will drop you if the Xbox doesn't reply on that port. So, following the general NAT solution track is most likely to help. Some NAT problems are subtle and don't appear in the Test Xbox Live Connection test. Most likely there's a port blocked or misdirected away from your Xbox, in your router, modem, or at your ISP. For more background on Ports, see whatismyipaddress.com/port

 

Since the Xbox 360 uses different ports than PCs & Macs ordinarily use, you can't see the effect of a port blockage except on the Xbox, for the NAT problem is really the blockage or misdirection of ports.  A sequential power cycle is the first step in clearing connection problems, and the order and timing of the steps is important to success:

 

1) turn off the Xbox and unplug the power supply cord from the back of the Xbox then

2) unplug power from the router (Some folks have a combination modem-router AKA 'gateway' or only use a modem with no router. If either, skip steps 3 & 6).

3) unplug power from the modem.

4) Wait at least 5 minutes then replug the modem/gateway power.

5) Wait 'til the light on the front of the modem/gateway which says BROADBAND, ONLINE, READY, CABLE or DSL is on solid (not flashing), then plug in the router. If you have a gateway instead of a modem and router or a modem with no router, then when that light says it's ready, skip to step 7.

6) Wait until the WiFi light on the router is on; you may not use WiFi, but it's still a good indicator your router is finished booting up.

7) Plug in and turn on the Xbox.  

 

If the problem persists, please send us the info we need from http://forums.xbox.com/xbox_forums/xbox_support/f/9/t/155724.aspx so we may work on this further.
