https://blogs.windows.com/msedgedev/2015/12/16/smartscreen-drive-by-improvements/#mZht6XqwH6vrBrft.97

Note that the correct place to perform protection while browsing is within the browser itself, so that's what SmartScreen Filter is designed to do and also why it's integrated into both the Edge and Internet Explorer browsers as well as Windows 10 itself to protect other applications which access the web such as 3rd-party browsers.

Comparative analysis of browser security

If you read many of the linked blog articles you'll eventually find integration between not only SmartScreen Filter, the browser and Windows, but also Defender and the SmartScreen reputation and malicious application detection systems as well.  This is why Microsoft doesn't produce a security "suite" or "Internet security" application like most 3rd-party vendors typically sell, since they've already integrated each of these abilities tightly into Windows 8 or 10 themselves.

That's the key difference between Microsoft's protection of their browsers and operating system and those from 3rd-party vendors, which are typically focused on protecting the less well integrated 3rd-party browsers and other applications from Adobe, Java, and others.

The primary thing that the typical consumer PC user doesn't understand is that each application they add to their PC adds vulnerability and risk, so the best way to retain a high level of security is not to do this.  The most effective security possible today is to use Windows 10 as it is delivered from Microsoft, with possibly a few additional increased security settings in both Windows and the browsers and/or the addition of an anti-exploit tool like Microsoft's EMET if you can tolerate and understand them.

Of course, the typical consumer is again not technically knowledgeable, so more often they opt to use a 3rd-party browser because they prefer something in its interface, ignore security settings completely and at best buy a 3rd-party antivirus based on what some trusted [not necessarily knowledgeable] friend has told them.

Expecting the general public to understand and do these things has clearly not worked, so instead Microsoft has chosen to move in the same direction as the other major software vendors by switching to more limited "apps" and including the Windows Store as the sanctioned place to acquire them for Windows 10.  Most malware today is delivered via social engineering, so that's why the focus on this in Microsoft's browsers and security.

Remember that all of the 3rd-party application vendors and the various "testing" websites exist in order to help support and sell each others products, so their focus is never on the core security of Windows itself.  That's the base fallacy over which all of the online articles are written, so making sense of this is difficult even for a more technically knowledgeable person.

The most basic thing to remember is that when adding applications to your PC, you must determine what additional risks this software might add and how to protect from those vulnerabilities.  For example, when adding a 3rd-party browser you must fully explore and understand its weaknesses and determine what additional security add-ons or application(s) might be required to mitigate them.

Quite technically messy and difficult, huh?  Now do you see why I stated the above and why I not only recommend avoiding the installation of any unnecessary 3rd-party applications, but in fact use very few of these myself, especially no additional browsers?

Rob

How-To: Stop Receiving Insider Builds - Microsoft Community

jason[msft] say:

How-To: Stop Receiving Insider Builds

Over the course of time, some Insiders have wanted to roll back to their original OS and no longer receive Windows Insider builds to a particular PC..  To assist users in this effort, the following guide should be helpful:

Stop receiving Insider builds 

After you’ve been an Insider for a while and tried out the latest builds, you may find that you need to opt your PC or phone out of getting Insider builds. 

There are a couple of ways to opt out your device: 

·  Keep an eye out for the next production, or publicly-released, build to hit your device.

   When it does, you can opt out.

·  Restore your device to its original operating system. 

Opt out on the next production build 

We want to opt you out when your device is on a production build, because they are stable, receiving servicing updates, and you may leave it on this release cadence moving forward.  When opting out on a production build, you won’t lose any of your data on your device.  

1.     Make sure to pay attention to when the next public build is announced – you can use the Feedback Hub, blogs, Twitter, etc.

2.     Make sure the build is installed on your device. On your PC, you can do any of the following:

a.     Go to Settings > System > About, and look for the build edition, version, and build number.

b.     Go to Control Panel > System and Security > System, and look for the build edition.

c.     In a Search box, type winver and look for the build number.

3.     Go to Settings >Update & security > Windows Insider Program, and then click Stop Insider builds.

4.     Follow the instructions to opt out your device.  

Opt out to your device’s original operating system 

If you need to get your device back to a stable build immediately, you can roll back to the previous build, or you may also restore your device back to the operating system you were using prior to receiving Insider builds. Use the appropriate recovery images to restore your device. While you’ll no longer have access to the latest features that you had with the Insider build, you’ll have your device back to the stability you want.  

 

Note: Before you use the recovery images, make sure to back up anything on your device that you don’t want to lose.  

To restore your PC:

·         Use one of the following recovery images: Windows 7, Windows 8.1, and Windows 10. 

If you're simply looking to roll back to an earlier preview build:

  • Settings > Update & Security > Recovery > Go back to the previous version of Windows 10
