How to change the audio mixer settings for individual video clips - Microsoft Community

jamilchoudhury say:

How to change the audio mixer settings for individual video clips

I am using windows live movie maker and I have (very faint) background music on some of my videos. However, some videos do not have any sound at all so i would like the background music to be louder. How do i do this?

considerate_guy say:

The audio mixer is used to set the overall (global) balance between the audio from your video clips and the added audio from your music.  If you want varying levels, it's not the appropriate control.

Instead you want to set the volume of your individual clips.  For video clips, click the Video Tools tab and on the left you'll see controls for video volume.  Similarly for audio clips, click the Audio Tools tab and on the left you'll see controls for audio volume.

Setting volume this way applies to a complete clip, so if you want a different volume within a clip, you would need to split it.  Either use the 'Split' button on the ribbon, or press 'M' on your keyboard.  Do this at the point where you want a clip to change volume.  Then you can set the volume differently for each part.  (This works for video and music).

daavee say: No secret. :-)

SP3 does not require or use more RAM.

However, I have certainly noticed IE8 requiring more than IE7. Then again, using Opera or Chrome as your usual browser would yield better performance than using IE.

How large is your hard drive and how much free space is left on it? How much RAM is installed? Also, keep in mind that malware can certainly slow down a PC. And if you have SP2, you have a MUCH higher chance of being infected.

Here are the usual causes of sluggishness:

 

1. Malicious software (malware). You need to rule this out first! If you need guidance, let us know. Also, make sure you bookmark this page:


http://www.selectrealsecurity.com/malware-removal-guide

 

2. Not enough free space on the hard drive. At the very least, you should have at least 15% free space. A good goal to shoot for is 50%.

 

3. Certain programs that are designed to combat malware (e.g., Norton and McAfee). Ironically, they can slow things down because they simply use way too many resources. Sometime they cause conflicts with other programs. Fortunately, there are other antimalware programs available that use far fewer resources (e.g., NOD32, Avast, and Avira).

 

4. Not enough RAM, which causes the PC to overly rely on the pagefile. A quick way to determine if this is happening is to open Task Manager (Ctrl+Alt+Del) and click the Performance tab. Then note the three values under Commit Charge (K): in the lower left-hand corner: Total, Limit, and Peak.


The Total figure represents the amount of memory you are using at that very moment. The Peak figure represents the highest amount of memory you used since last bootup. If both these figures are below the value of Physical Memory (K) Total, then you probably have plenty of RAM. In case you want to explore this further, you may run Page File Monitor for Windows XP:


http://www.dougknox.com/xp/utils/xp_pagefilemon.htm

 

5. You might also want to check that your hard drive's access mode didn't change from DMA to PIO:


http://www.technize.com/2007/08/02/is-your-hard-disk-cddvd-drives-too-slow-while-copying/

and


http://winhlp.com/node/10
