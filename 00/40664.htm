    a. user say:

    In Windows, the default anything refers to a program that is installed on your computer. So your default email program is the installed email program that is your default for receiving and sending email.

    If Microsoft Office is installed on your computer, then Office Outlook could be your default email program because it's actually installed on your computer. Another well-known example of an installed email program is Mozilla Thunderbird.

    On the other hand, Hotmail or Outlook.com or GMail or Yahoo! Mail are not installed on your computer. Rather, they are websites. Since they are not actually installed on your computer, they cannot be your default email program.

    Makes sense, right? So here is what gets people messed up:

    The Windows Mail app is not an installed email program. That's right ... it looks like a duck ... and it quacks like a duck ... but it ain't a duck. Mail is merely an interface for your webmail providers (GMail, Outlook.com, etc.) which are not installed email programs - they are websites.

    kmccullough(2) say: So what do I do to keep from getting that message?

    kmccullough(2) say: It appears randomly on my desktop. 

    a. user say:

    You'll see that message any time you ask Windows to do something that requires an installed email program.

    Example: When you want to send a document as an attachment, and you right-click on the document and choose Send to -- Mail recipient. You need an email program to do that.

    Sending an attachment from webmail (e.g., GMail) is real easy:

    1. Sign in to your webmail provider and compose the message as you normally would.

    2. Drag and drop your file onto the body of the message.
