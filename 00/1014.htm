macropod say:

You could use a field coded as:

{={MERGEFIELD Field1}+{MERGEFIELD Field2}}

or:

{=«Field1»+«Field2»}

where 'Field1 & 'Field2' are your field names. You can add numeric formatting switches, too, as described at: http://www.msofficeforums.com/mail-merge/21803-mailmerge-tips-tricks.html

Note: The field brace pairs (i.e. '{ }') for the above example are all created in the document itself, via Ctrl-F9 (Cmd-F9 on a Mac); you can't simply type them or copy & paste them from this message. Nor is it practical to add them via any of the standard Word dialogues. Likewise, the chevrons (i.e. '« »') are part of the actual mergefields - which you can insert from the 'Insert Merge Field' dropdown (i.e. you can't type or copy & paste them from this message, either). The spaces represented in the field constructions are all required.

As I see it, you have 4 options to try and resolve this issue:

Option A

As Ariel said, you can contact MS Support and hope they can help. The issue with this approach is that the first person you contact, "front line support" works from a "script" that does NOT include this issue, so they can't directly help you. But they have to work through the "checklist" before they can refer you on to a supervisor / "second level support".   So, the FIRST thing you do when you initially contact MS Support is ask for a "trouble ticket number" (we'll get back to this in a minute). Then ask to be transferred a supervisior. This won't happen, but you can start by giving the important part of your message. Explain your problem. They'll say "duh", and then proceed to ask you a bunch of totally irrelevant questions from their script.  Answer each one and ask to be transferred.  Eventually they will reach the end of the list and or transfer you.   This is where the trouble ticket number comes in.  You can expect to be transferred MANY (many ... many ... many ... ... ... ) times. Eventually someone will "accidentally" hang up on you.  Then when you call back you can quote the trouble ticket number.  Occasionally, it allows you to bypass the repetitive questions from front line support. But not always <sigh again>.  The fix you are looking for is someone who can issue you a new "region correct" key and cancel the old one.  There are one or two (in the world) on a given shift.  You just have to be lucky enough to be accidentally transferred to them.  Good luck with that ...

Actually, for this sort of problem I prefer to use the online chat contact

Online chat support: http://answerdesk.microsoftstore.com/AnswerTech

rather than phone. You are talking to the same group of support people, but online rather than by voice.  Most fundamentally, it avoids the issues of "English as a second language".  And when you are asked to repeat information you can just copy and paste it.  As well, it provides a timestamped transcript of your "adventure" that you can post online as proof of the great customer service you were given (yes, that is sarcasm).

Option B

Find someone in Aus to buy it from you. Maybe a friend or relative can help you with this. Then you can buy a new one locally.  Since the key has not been activated yet, you can resell it without issues (other than getting the person to trust you).

Option C

Contact the store you bought it from in Aus and ask for a refund, or an exchange for a key that will work in your location.  After a year, this option probably will just get you laughed at. But it is worth a try.  After the "customer service" desk stops laughing at you, ask to speak to a manager. They have more discretion. A few people have gotten results that way when they had no other expectation. The manager can just write it off as "customer service/satisfaction", while the staff cannot.

Option D

Use a VPN to connect to a server in Aus to do the activation.  Once it has been activated, you can use it anywhere around the world (until you have to re-install and activate again). This has been reported to work in a few instances.

Good luck, you are going to need it.
