e-mail virus security issues, and 'macro virus' (yes, - Microsoft Community

big bad bombastic bob say:

e-mail virus security issues, and 'macro virus' (yes, MACRO VIRUS) woes

Macro Viruses are back, and apparently Microsoft is well aware of the problem.

"Macro malware is making a comeback with one nineties nasty infecting half a million computers, Microsoft says."

So what is being done about it in windows 10?

One of the BIGGEST sources of virus headache, from what I have observed, is Outlook mail.  The primary reason is HTML mail pre-viewing, and secondarily the pre-view of attachments.  More than once "the accountant" would open some attachment thinking it was something important, like an invoice, or a sales request, or similar, because the e-mail text said so, and VOILA  you're NOW INFECTED with some macro-driven malware.

I personally avoid these problems several ways, one of which is to NEVER open an attachment like that with "the default app", particularly if I don't know who it's from.  I think MOST security-wise people do this.

But in the past, Outlook may have opened it FOR you without you WANTING it to.  I'm pretty sure that this was the mechanism for the infection.  As a result, I *never* install Outlook mail, EVER, since the 90's even.

So, does 'Outlook mail' the built-in "the METRO" app prevent this sort of thing from happening?

CAN YOU DISABLE HTML VIEW/PRE-VIEW in the "the METRO" app the way you can with 3rd party mail readers?  I couldn't find out how.

Will SCRIPT or ActiveX or 3rd party embedded URL graphics in e-mails STILL LOAD with the "the METRO" mail reader?  (I didn't test it that long to make sure, it was causing some trouble with my IMAP setup so I disabled it)

ALL of these things are SERIOUS security risks.  The biggest risk of a 3rd party link opening up when you view an e-mail is that it doesn't take rocket science to carefully craft that in order to IDENTIFY YOU when you just VIEW the thing, before putting it in the spam filter or bit bucket.

And THESE are ALL serious security problems.

Here's what any good e-mail reader should do:

a) NEVER pre-view in HTML unless the user specifically wants it.  Rarely do you need HTML to send an e-mail anyway.  Most of that is the 'forward this to everyone you know' type with ad links at the bottom anyway.  If my mother sends me one of those I'll just quietly say 'thanks' and not view as HTML.

b) Allow an option to NEVER 'open' an attachment, even a preview of it, EVER.  Even image files can have viruses in them with 0-day vulnerabilities.

c) NEVER allow scripting, activex, downloadable fonts, style sheets, HTML 5 media, embedded objects, or any OTHER kind of content to display or pre-view in an e-mail that might even *remotely* have some vulnerability associated with it.  Just "no".

d) verify file types before allowing 'open' or 'preview'.  this means checking for an EXE header on a file with a ZIP or JPEG extension, check valid headers on ALL supported image types, and so forth.  Warnings should *frighten* you if this doesn't match exactly.

I don't know how many of those things are already being done in Outlook mail, but it's basically "a good start".

I hope to NEVER see another case where "the accountant" gets a virus infection or Trojan horse/back-door from an e-mail attachment, because accountants aren't computer security people, they do payables and receivables and tend to open document attachments in e-mails that say "invoice" on them, even when they're virus attachments.

Oh, and "get better virus protection software" is NOT acceptable, in my opinion.  These are OLD problems that could be fixed with a proper e-mail application.

and with the whole 'one experience' thing, do we REALLY need Word doc or Excel spreadsheet macro virus problems on a PHONE ???
