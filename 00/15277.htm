albert d. kallal say:

The code behind a button would look like this:

  Dim strFileName      As String

   Dim strReport        As String

   strReport = "rpt_Meeting Agenda"

   strFileName = Me.LstBox_ChooseDivision & ", " & _

                 Format(Me.ListBox_Venue.Column(1), "YYYY-MM-DD HH-NN") & ", " & _

                 Me.ListBox_Venue.Column(2) & ", " & _

                 Me.ListBox_Venue.Column(3) & ", " & _

                 Format(Now(), "YYYY-MM-DD HH-NN") & ".pdf"

   strFileName = CurrentProject.Path & "\Meeting Agenda Archives\" & strFileName

   DoCmd.OpenReport strReport, acViewPreview

   DoCmd.OutputTo acOutputReport, strReport, acFormatPDF, strFileName, False

   DoCmd.Close acReport, strReport

So the above is "air" code, but should be very close to what you require.

Hi Stefania Castelli

Here's what worked for me...

I found by running ProcMon that something kept looking for a registry value in the TPM key, but the value did not exist.  I decided to enable TPM as a way to create the necessary registry entries.  To do so:

Run TPM.msc

In the Action Pane, click Prepare the TPM...

You are prompted to restart.

When the computer reboots, it will prompt to enable TPM or not.  With my laptop it was F1 to enable or F2 to reject.  It may vary based on hardware.  Choose the option for enabling TPM.  Windows will boot up normally.  When you log in, you will (hopefully) find that your CPU is running normally again.

In my case, I have an HP EliteBook 8440p and an EliteBook 2540p.  Both had the same problems and both were cured the same way.

Good Luck!

UPDATE:  I just disabled TPM using TPM.msc and found that my CPU utilization is at normal levels after restart.  Hopefully the act of enabling, then disabling, TPM creates all of the missing registry entries to prevent further problems.
