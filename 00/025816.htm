Once they arrive in Outlook.com, they can be deleted from the original accounts and they will still be in Outlook.com. There are a couple of things to be aware of. It is possible that sometimes an email may not be able to be received by Outlook.com and be bounced back to the original email account. For that reason I would only delete the emails in the original accounts after a month, rather than a week. This gives you plenty of time to recover an email that you may be expecting but doesn't arrive.

 

Also consider changeing important emails so that they come directly rather than via your original accounts.

Also consider changeing important emails so that they come directly rather than via your original accounts.

woodg say:

Very simple, actually. Let's say you get you bank statements or your utility bills emailed to you. If you use outlook.com as your main email account, it would be better to have your bank statements and utility bills emailed directly to your outlook.com email rather than, say, your gmail account and then forwarded to outlook.com. In theory, it should not make any difference. In practice, if you don't get your statement or bill it becomes harder to find the problem. Did my bank or utility company actually send the email? Was it received by gmail? Did it get forwarded to outlook.com? Was it bounced back as undeliverable? If you close your gmail account (or it is closed for you) will you remember to change the email address for your statements or bills? After all, you are using outlook.com as your main email account.

 

Having your statement or bill sent directly to outlook.com is one less step in the process. One less thing to go wrong.
