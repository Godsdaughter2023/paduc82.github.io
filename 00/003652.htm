joeu2004 say:

[Errata courtesy of Tom Ogilvy]

jcmu99 wrote:

Why doesn't Excel spill the numbers into the next cell (as it does with text)? And it appears that Excel has totaled my thirteen number one's and come up with a funny way to show me the total of my thirteen number 1's. 

1.11111E+12 is not "a funny way" to show the "total" of 13 1s.

Instead, it is called scientific notation, a way to display very large (and very small) numbers.  The General format sometimes resorts to this form, depending on the width of the column and the magnitude of the value.

To understand better, consider the integer 1234567890123.  That might appear as 1.23457E+12.

First, note that the display of 1.234567... is rounded to 1.23457.  Usually, formatting affects only the appearance, not the actual value.  In this case, it is still 1234567890123.

Second, read 1.23457E+12 as 1.23457 "times 10 to the power of 12".  In other words, 1.23457*1000000000000; that's 1 followed by 12 zeros.  "10 to the power of 12" is 10 times itself 12 times.

So, the E+12 part is the number of zeros after the multiplier 1, not a "total" of the 13 digits.

jay freedman say:

Answering the question

But why are they saved in User\AppData\Local\Microsoft\office\unsaved Files instead of User\AppData\Roaming\Microsoft\Word as set in Options

The .asd files created by the AutoSave function do go into (subfolders of) the ...Roaming\Microsoft\Word folder, as long as the document is open and Word is still running. If Word shuts down normally, the .asd file is deleted as part of the shutdown process. If the document hasn't been saved, the .asd file is copied to the Unsaved Files folder just before the one in the Word folder is deleted.
