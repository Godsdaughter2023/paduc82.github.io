wade dorrell say:

I think it'd be helpful to update this answer, since in theory it could've changed.

As of September 2019, no Xbox One model supports automatic routing of receiver/TV inputs to align to its HDMI-Out, or aligning power-on states, using HDMI-CEC.

Xbox One supports sending IR signals (through Kinect, IR extension cable, or via IR emitter on the console itself) to turn on/off receiver/TV, change volume, and control a cable box attached to its HDMI-In, but its IR system cannot be configured to route input automatically. (The IR can be used to manually change inputs, e.g. using the Xbox mobile app emulating a traditional remote control.)

Comparisons are often made to Nintendo Switch, PS3/PS4, Apple TV, Chromecast, Roku, and Fire TV, all of which send commands over HDMI out that some TVs/receivers support for one-touch power-on and coordination of inputs. The standard those products use is HDMI-CEC, which has a number of trade names, e.g. "Anynet+" or a variety of plays on "sync", "link", or "CEC."

Angie,

I have a suggestion.  It is technical but it really is easy to do.  It will gather information that might identify the root cause of the problem.

1  Go to Start menu, Windows administrative tools, Event viewer.

2  On the left hand side of Event viewer, click on the dropdown arrow next to Applications and services logs then in the entries that appear select Windows powershell.

3  Don't bother trying to look through the whole list but starting at the top can you match any of the times shown with the times you have had the Powershell has stopped working warning? 

  • If so, select that entry & look at the bottom half for the entry HostApplication= then copy the whole line here [you can just select it like you were working in a text file then copy it using Ctrl-C]. 
  • Then continue looking further down the list to find further matches.
  • If there is any consistency in the identity of the culprit then this will help in finding a solution.
