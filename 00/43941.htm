john rubdy say:

Hi,

Let me assist with your concern.

It seems you created an "Image" of your system drive, which is not quite the same as backing up your data. The result would be stored in a folder called WindowsImageBackup which you reported as WindowsBackupImage.

This folder is inaccessible to you unless you operate under elevated administrative privileges. This is why you cannot see any files. Here is how you can deal with this folder:

1. Click Start.
2. Type the three letters cmd into the Search box.
3. Press Ctrl+Shift+Enter
4. Click "Run as Administrator".
5. Type the following commands and press enter after each:
    dir /s /a Q:\WindowsImageBackup
    (To see the contents of this folder)
    (Replace Q: with the correct drive letter)
    rd  /s  /q  Q:\WindowsImageBackup
    (To wipe this folder)

Warning: With this method you cannot retrieve the folder from the Recycle Bin.

Hope this helps, Let me know if you need any further assistance.

bigbearjedi say:

Take the drives out of the Dock.  Is that Dock for USB drives?  Remove the drives, plug in one of them using a IDE-TO-SATA Drive adapter.  They are under $20 here: IDE TO SATA ADAPTER

USB drives cannot be seen by your laptop BIOS.  Therefore they don't show up in Windows Disk Management screen until Plug-and-Play makes a connection.  If they are USB 2.0 drives, they shouldn't need any kind of drivers to be recognized by modern Windows versions such as Win7/8.1/10.

Often older drives when they begin to fail won't be recognized by the drive Manufacturer's utilities.  Try to download and use WD DRIVE UTILITIES with your WD drive first, and the IDE-TO-SATA ADAPTER first.  They should be able to identify it and give you a health report.  Run both short and extended tests on the drive.  If any errors are returned by either test, the drive has failed and it must be replaced!  Set that drive aside for data recovery, or take it in to your local repair shop and pay a Tech to get your stuff off of it.

Seagate drives are not as responsive to diagnostics, and even with the adapter they might not work.  In that case, the most likely cause for them not showing up on your laptop is that they have also failed. 

If you have a Desktop PC handy, you can take the bare drives out of the enclosure, open up the PC, and remove the boot drive (C: drive) as well as all other internal and external drives.  Hook up the Seagate drive or other bare drive you want to test, and see if you can see it in the PCs BIOS; if you cannot, then that drive has failed and must be replaced!  Repeat this test with each of your drives.  If more drives failed, then they too are gone and must be replaced!

A couple of people asked how old your drives are and you didn't answer that question.  If those are USB external drives, those tend to fail after 4-5 years of use.  If they are older than that, the electronics in the enclosures if the drives came in those as most do for external plug in USB drives, it's time to replace the enclosure, or the drive, or more likely both.

For $20 you can eliminate 90% of the failed drives you have.  That's a fraction of the cost it will cost you to test 3 or 4 or 5  drives at a repair shop.  That will cost you $100-$200 US.  If this is too difficult for you or you can't wrap your head around it, take all your drives in and have them tested.  Replace the ones that have failed and stop holding on to old hardware and expecting it to work forever!

Hope that helps.
