Hi Rayford,

If your computer is in a restart-loop state after an update failing to install, it could be that the failure of the installation of the update has corrupted or damaged the system files of Windows. We recommend that you use an installation media of Windows 10 to restore or reset your PC. Here's how to download an installation media and use it to restore from a restore point or reset your PC:

  1. On a working PC, go to the Microsoft software download website.
  2. Download the media creation tool, and then run it.
  3. Select Create installation media for another PC.
  4. Choose a language, edition, and architecture (64-bit or 32-bit).
  5. Follow the steps to create an installation media, and then select Finish.
  6. Connect the installation media you created to your nonfunctional PC, and then turn it on.
  7. On the initial setup screen, enter your language and other preferences, and then select Next. If you're not seeing the setup screen, your PC might not be set up to boot from a drive. Check your PC manufacturer's website for info on how to change your PC's boot order, and then try again.
  8. Select Repair your computer.
  9. On the Choose an option screen, select Troubleshoot. From there, you can:
    • Restore from a system restore point by selecting Advanced options > System Restore. This will remove recently installed apps, drivers, and updates that might be causing your PC problems. Restoring from a restore point won't affect your personal files.
    • Select Reset this PC to reinstall Windows 10. This will remove apps and drivers you installed and changes you made to settings, but lets you choose to keep or remove your personal files.

If restoring or resetting your PC using an installation media does not help, do not hesitate to post back to us.

a. user say:

You won't know who - or what - to blame until you find out what is causing the instability of your WiFi signal. 

Your WiFi signal is beamed from your router to the antennas in your WiFi adapters. Windows isn't involved with that. The only software involved with your WiFi connection is the firmware for your router and the driver for your WiFi adapters. The people who deal with that are the manufacturers of your router and your WiFi adapters. Talk to their technical support and/or post a question in their user forums.

Plenty of other things contribute to a weak WiFi signal, including interference from nearby WiFi networks on the same channel, interference from other devices on the same frequency band, third-parties who have hacked into your signal, and reflections from walls and room contents.

So before you go looking to cast blame, find out what's causing the problem in the first place. My favorite resource for solving WiFi problems is the experts in the Small Net Builder Forums. There are some really sharp people there.

a. user say:

I don't understand why a windows 8 machine using the same router has a stable wifi connection, unless the problem relates to the wifi adapter on the windows 10 machine.

The router has its own operating system. It broadcasts signals the same way and just as well (or poorly) regardless of the operating used by the device receiving its signal. If you think your router is the source of your problem, and you are on the most recent firmware, contact your router's technical support.

I had to replace the WiFi adapter on my Windows 8.1 computer after I performed a clean install of Windows 10, because D-Link didn't support my adapter on Windows 10.

Even without that, in the time between you were using Windows 8 and Windows 10 a neighbor might have created a network that interferes with yours, or you and/or someone else might have added a device on the same frequency band that causes interference, or a third-party could be hacking your signal or a physical object could be deflecting your signal. The Windows operating system cannot make the radio waves moving through the air towards the antenna on your WiFi adapter move any slower.
