please help me get rid of stupid spam. - Microsoft Community

grizza77 say:

please help me get rid of stupid spam.

how to remove spam for ever?

rob koch say:

This is the wrong place to ask, which shows exactly why you get so much since you clearly have no idea how spam or even email systems in general work.  This is also exactly why spam still continues to work for the bad actors, since they know that only a small handful of mostly technical users truly understand the complete details.

However, there are a set of common items that anyone can do in order to stop most spam and in the most extreme cases virtually all of it if they'll take the time to do them.  The key here is that the entire point is prevention, not attempting to "block" or detect and remove spam messages received after the fact, since this never works as you've clearly already learned.

The first important move is to choose an email provider with a strong anti-spam profile, which in most cases will not be your Internet Service Provider's free mailbox, since these companies don't typically have the technical experts that companies like Microsoft do.  So instead pick an email service like Outlook.com that is designed specifically to manage email and then consider if you'd also like to pay a small amount (around $20/year) for things like the removal of advertising from the web email client as well.

Once you've chosen your email provider, an important choice is your email address, which most assume should simply be their full name.  However, it's much better to use an uncommon spelling like including your middle initial or adding numbers such as FirstMLast or FirstLast1234 which should be chosen to help others easily remember and recognize this as you.  This is because the most common email names are likely already receiving spam because someone in the past was using this same address, so it will simply never stop.

Next, the key decision will be how to manage your mailbox, since the strongest anti-spam profile requires the most work up front.  For example, choosing the Exclusive junk email filter for Outlook.com to only allow "safe senders" addresses in your inbox requires that every single valid from address must be added to the safe senders list, with all other email sent to the Junk or Spam folder.  Obviously this requires that you examine this Junk folder regularly for potentially valid messages from new addresses you haven't added yet.

I personally prefer to set the Outlook junk email filter to Standard and then only provide my primary email address to a small number of trusted people and major companies like my bank, insurance and utilities companies.  I never give out this email address to people I don't know well and never ever publish it anywhere visible on the Internet, since this guarantees it will receive more spam.

All  less trusted senders (all other companies) receive a secondary email address named similar to RobsUntrustedAddress@... which is a free secondary email account associated with my main Outlook address and delivers directly into a special folder I created of the same name.  I assume that anything in this folder is less trustworthy as a result and will simply delete them if they are at all suspicious.

The mistake most people make is believing that spam is easy for computer systems to detect, which it isn't.  Your own mind is much more capable of discerning which email is invalid, but you actually have to think, so it's not easy to do when you are either hundreds of messages or you're tired, drunk or otherwise impaired, so you'll then make mistakes.  Setting up your address and mailbox on a more secure email system in a way that helps separate the more trusted email from that which is more questionable is the best way to reduce the possibility of a mistake.

If you aren't ready to put this level of effort into protecting your email then spam will be a fact of life.  The best you can do then is to choose the most effective anti-spam email service available and set the spam filter to the highest level you can accept, adding only those email addresses you truly want delivered to the safe senders list if they end up in your Junk/Spam folder instead.

Attempting to block email from unwanted addresses only works if the numbers are very small, which since this clearly isn't true is always destined to fail.  Only if this number is kept small to start with using one of the above techniques can you hope to manage a mailbox effectively over the long term.

Using these methods I've only had at most a few messages in my Junk mail folder per week in 15 years using the same Outlook.com account.  All of these are typically true spam and more recently there have been only 1 or 2 a week at most.

Rob

I have a simple Yes or No Combo Box Control..

If you select Yes, then in another area, Im assuming using a Rich Control Content box?   I want it to read "FILL OUT FOR 2", if it says no, I don't want it to say anything.

Thanks

doug robbins - mvp office apps & services (word) say:

If you put a { DOCVARIABLE varResult } field where you want the text to appear and you give the combo box control the Title of Test and then you have the following code in the ThisDocument object of the document, it will do what you want.

Private Sub Document_ContentControlOnExit(ByVal ContentControl As ContentControl, Cancel As Boolean)
Dim cc As ContentControl
With ActiveDocument
    For Each cc In .ContentControls
        If cc.Title = "Test"

             If cc.Range = "Yes" Then
                  .Variables("varResult").Value = "FILL OUT FOR 2"

             Else
                   .Variables("varResult").Value = " "
             End If        

             .Range.Fields.Update

             Exit For 

      End If        
    Next cc
End With
End Sub

If you insert the Docvariable field from the keyboard, you must use CTRL+F9 to insert the field delimiters { } and you use ALT+F9 to toggle off the display of the field codes.
