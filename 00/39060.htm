blade467 say:

Windows 10 Number lock on by default

There are lots of questions regarding numberlock on switching on when windows 10 boots up. In some cases it comes on after a restart but not after a shut down.

The permanent fix is to edit TWO areas of the registry.

In the registry select:

HKEY_CURRENT_USER/Control Panel/Keyboard. Ensure "InitialKeyboardIndicators" value is set to "2"

NEXT, select:

HKEY_USERS/.DEFAULT/Conrol Panel/Keyboard. Initial KeyboardIndicators value will most likely be set at

"2147483648" Reset this value to "2".

Both values will now be the same.

You do not need to disable "Fastboot" as mentioned in previous discussions.

It is worth mentioning I have upgraded from Win 7 ultimate, through Win 8, 8.1 and number lock was fine until Win 10 re-wrote the registry keys and disabled it.

1. BIOS: Go into your computers' BIOS settings (usually you hit F2 or Delete when booting-up) and turn off any 'power saving' options as CPU throttling seems to be a problem.

2. POWER PLANS: I had a problem on my Samsung NP350V5C-A06, Intel i7 CPU, laptop which would reset the power plan. Turns out the problem wasn't Windows 8 - it was the Samsung settings running in the background. To fix the 'problem' I opened up the Samsung configuration settings and went to: General tab > Silent Mode > and selected 'Off'. The AUTO (default) fan option was controlling the fan speed noise, but, it was also resetting my preferred Windows power plan back to the 'Power saver' plan every time I booted-up! It seems other manufacturers have this annoying hidden 'feature' as well, so check your computer settings.

3. CPU MANAGEMENT: Check the CPU 'Processor power management' under your preferred power plan. Go to Control Panel > Hardware and Sound > Power Options > Edit Plan Settings > Change advanced power settings. Make sure you have these settings: Minimum processor state > Plugged in: = %100 and Maximum processor state > Plugged in: = %100 and System cooling policy > Plugged in: = Active. Check anything else that could throttle back or slow down your computer, such as the hard disk settings or any other power saving options.
