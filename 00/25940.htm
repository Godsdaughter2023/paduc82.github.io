rohn007 say:

OK, then change your point of view.

Nothing is being changed "after" you Copy.  The tracked changes ARE there in the copy.  The change happens when you paste.  If the receiving document is set to show markups that information is included in the paste. If not, then it is stripped out during the pasted.

You see very much the same thing when simply pasting formatted text. In paste special you can paste it with formatting or without. You decide at paste time.  With tracked changes the decision is "automagical", decided in the background without direct input from the user.

paul domaskis say:

As I said in my last post, the problem occurs when pasting into an app other than Word.  The answer link does not address that situation.  I used gvim, but I see the same thing with Notepad.

Try it.

Create a document with tracked changes, both insertions and deletions.  Then show the final version without the markups.  Select, copy (ctrl-C), and paste into the text editor.  Looks fine.

Now RE-select, show final version *with* full/complete markups, then paste into the text editor.  You see the version of the text *with* the markup text (even if the markup represents deleted text).  So what gets pasted is affected by what you do *after* ctrl-C.  That's what is unintuitive.

About changing my point of view, it's not necessary.  I now know the unintuitive behaviour.  However, I would say that one of the the aims of design should be to make functional behaviour as intuitive as possible.  As it currently works, every person running into the need to paste into a text editor will have to go through this discovery of the actual behaviour.  Possibly more than once, depending on how often they do it.

jay freedman say:

The clipboard always contains everything, including markup, after Ctrl+C -- regardless of the display at the time you copy.

Word is programmed to control what gets pasted under different conditions in the destination, as Rohn007 said.

Other programs, such as gvim or Notepad, are not programmed to distinguish markup from non-markup; they just paste everything that's on the clipboard.

Your assertion is that Word should not put markup on the clipboard if it isn't displayed in the source. That's a reasonable assertion, but unfortunately it isn't the way Microsoft did it. It isn't even the most egregious example of counterintuitive design in Word; that award probably goes to multilevel lists.

The lines are drawing objects (shapes).

In Word, on the right end of the Home tab, click Select Objects. When you hold the cursor over one of the lines, it will turn into a four-way arrow. Click the line, so a "handle" appears on each end of the line, and press Delete.
