brian tillman [outlook mvp 2007-2019] say:

If the messages are in the Sent Items folder, then they are being sent, from Outlook's perspective. Outlook does not move the message to that folder until it receives an acknowledgement from the receiving server that the server has accepted the message for delivery.

Now, if you have some scanning add-in configured in Outlook, Outlook may believe that software is the SMTP server and is receiving the acknowledgement from that software. It is then the software's fault that the messages aren't delivered.

Either way, it is a problem in the delivery chain after Outlook is done with the message and Outlook has been told the message was accepted for delivery. Outlook itself is not the problem. If this were to happen to me, I'd enable Outlook's diagnostic logging to look at the conversation between Outlook and the receiving server and then I'd look at the equivalent server-side log to make sure the handshake all went as it should. I'd them trace the message through the server (if I could) to make sure it went to its next destination. If you have a good relationship with Time Warner, they should be able to examine those logs on their end.

The address that shows in the To field has, believe it or not, nothing whatsoever to do with the actual recipient address. It's just a string of characters used for convenience. Just like paper mail, only the address on the envelope matters, not the words on the paper inside the envelope. You cannot see the address that was on the electronic envelope. It is used only by the message transports that pass the email from one system to the next. So, if you received the message, it was definitely intended for your address no matter what shows in the To field.

OK, that is more of a border and I don't see you needing to wrap text around an image. In fact you are just inserting text boxes and other design elements in the 'white space' in the middle of the envelope. With this said then use a master page.

 

It appears that you have a size 10 envelope.

Create a new publication > envelopes > scroll down until you find a blank size 10 (9.5 X 4.125) and create it. Go to View > Master Page. Insert Picture >from file and browse to your image and insert it. In my case it inserts at 90% scale so right click the image > format picture > make sure to lock aspect ratio and then increase the scale to 100% and ok out. Drag or use the arrow keys to center the image on the master page. At the bottom of the left hand pane called Edit Master Pages click 'View publication page'. This puts you back on the main publication page where you can insert text boxes, other images or any other design element you want, with the stationary picture in the background. Just drag those elements around and place them where you want them. Then when you have everything the way you want and are ready to print just go to View > Ignore master page and you can print without the image.

 

Here are better directions for using a master page: http://office.microsoft.com/en-us/publisher-help/create-edit-or-delete-a-master-page-HA001234040.aspx and http://office.microsoft.com/en-us/publisher-help/get-the-most-out-of-multiple-master-pages-HA001080719.aspx

 

[edit] After posting I thought of yet another way to approach this. Insert the picture as a background and then don't print the background. This approach is dependent upon your printer options.

 

Format > Background > More backgrounds > Picture tab > select picture - browse to your picture on your local drive. Design the rest of your publication and then when you print look for the option to 'print page backgrounds' and uncheck. Your printer may not have that option, in which case you can use the Master Page approach.
