Is that saying did not work as well as a keyboard or that you were not able to figure out how to do it?  Also, which "Surface Pro" were you doing that with?  E.g. the Surface 2 Pro has a Logo button with "haptic" buzz which is used for its simulation of a Ctrl-Alt-Delete and in its case I would definitely agree with the first meaning of "as well".  Use a real keyboard to avoid the quirks and unusabilities of such a hodge podge of functionality from trying to overload so few external buttons with so little feedback.

richardeiler say:

This (these) browser hijacker messages are a scam, Microsoft will never contact you in any (much less in this way) way about anything, unless, you have purposely invited them to do so previously (and you would of course know if you have).

This scam has become increasingly more prevalent, even on the most wholesome of websites, in the more recent past. What they are fishing for is the opportunity to convince someone to give them remote control (assistance) of their device under the guise of correcting the perceived as stated "infection" when in fact what they will be doing is, at best, changing your password(s) then instructing you to then shutdown (or restart) the device. You will then be effectively locked out of your PC completely. They will then tell you that you must pay them x amount of dollars to "complete" the "repair" process (all they will do then, again, at best, will be to give you back your password or have you choose a new one, they would also leave the software that they have installed and the settings involved for establishing the remote control of your device intact, again, this is in an at best case scenario, any other malicious software and/or settings to facilitate their nefarious intentions would also be left intact without your knowledge.

What to do instead of calling the number (never call the number) or doing a restart, or a shutdown and start, of the device:

First, disconnect from the internet (either through settings or manually) then wait about 15 seconds or so.

Next, on the tab (or if only one tab is open, then on the browser if need be) click on the X (close) icon repeatedly, it may take a few seconds but it should close after a somewhat brief pause.

In some cases, a popup window may appear with a checkbox present stating "Don't allow more actions from...", if so, try first clicking on the X (close) button, if it then continues to re-popup click on the "Don't allow ..." selection and then continue to close the offending tab (or browser single instance).

After it closes, reconnect to the internet and continue with your work (do not select the same link again, of course).

This works about 99% of the time. In this case, nothing has actually been downloaded on the PC at all. If you are concerned that there might have been (or to just verify there hasn't been), you can then run a scan using your AV product of choice.

If you wish to further verify (or to check for what may be lurking on your PC that has slipped through in the past), then download and run MBAM Free (say no to the full version trial offer if asked) and run its scan, it will pickup things such as PUP's (Potentially Unwanted Programs) as well as browser hijackers, adware, etc.. (Do note that it may pickup something that you have purposely downloaded and installed and therefore may want to keep).

https://www.malwarebytes.com/

These techniques have worked well for me, this particular scam attempt has become even more previlent as of late, do not fall for it as many have before you.
