ashish mathur say:

Hi,

 

When a range is converted to a Table, formulas will update only for additional rows added to the Table (not columns).  The only time columns are considered are when graphs are plotted on the range converted to a Table.

bernie deitrick say: 1) Use a buffer row in each of your formulas, and always insert above the buffer row - in your example above, the buffer row should be row 13, so move your formulas to row 14 and change them to reference D3:D13. When you insert a new row at row 13, then they will update to D3:D14.
2) Change the formulas to reference the entire column D:D instead of specific ranges - even though the formula will be in the same column, as long as you allow iterations in your settings, it will work fine.
3) Change your formula to reference only rows above the first row with a formula using other functions, like OFFSET.  In D13, and cells below, use:
=COUNTIF(OFFSET(D$3,0,0,ROW(D$13)-ROW(D$3),1),"No")
=COUNTIF(OFFSET(D$3,0,0,ROW(D$13)-ROW(D$3),1),"Yes")
=COUNTIF(OFFSET(D$3,0,0,ROW(D$13)-ROW(D$3),1),"NA")

Now let me explain why the behavior happens.

After uploading a new document successfully, we will enter Edit dialog where allows us to edit the existing document and change its content type. They are genuine Microsoft product keys for retail licenses, but not for Retail sale. They are intended for a particular product channel either the Microsoft Software Developer Network (MSDN) or TechNet for IT Professionals who pay a subscription fee. The main purpose is for evaluation purposes. It is for one person only to use and no one else. Unfortunately, regardless of the licensing terms, persons still abuse the program, either giveaway product keys or resell it on auction sites. 

What probably happened is, you bought an MSDN licensed key, which carries up to 10 activations unlike full packaged retail licenses which only carry 1 activation. The person who sold it to you probably sold it to 10 other persons.
