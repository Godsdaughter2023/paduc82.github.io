rohn007 say:
Why do you say all of the bookmarks have to be redone as pages are inserted?

What you want to do is fundamentally massacring the assumptions word operates on.  That is text is a continuous flow. "Pages" are not a physical "constant". Text page position changes as the document is edited, adding and removing text.

If you want separate "status" by page, you have to include a next page section break between each page. Which becomes a problem when the page overflows on to a new page.

You are trying to manage this document in "typewriter mode", which just doesn't work.  I bet you even have page numbers with decimals to handle pages being inserted between existing page numbers, ie 1, 2, 3, 3.1,3.1.1, 3.2, 4, 5 etc

I suggest you are going to have to revisit how change can be NOW managed. For example, rather than managing by individual pages, can you convince your audience to manage by headings.

Assume whole document is in "normal" status. remove the status from page footer. When a specific section is being edited, you could select the title, or whole text and highlight it or apply a different style.  You may even want to make a couple of "editing status" styles (say with special background colors to indicate status). Then you log the current "change" status (editing, approval, approved ...) in the change management table. Insert a cross reference to the heading and heading page number into the change management table. You probably want at very least a status changed date. You now have an automated status table that works with Word rather than living in the past and fighting with it.

rohn007 say:

Did you read my first reply?

What you are trying to do is recreate "typewriter/paper" mode in Word.  This is not easy to do and requires 

of manual effort to maintain changes.   There are very fundamental differences to managing paper and electronic documents.  Actually, when I first got into computing I took over management of an inhouse training manual.  When I started, it consisted of printed copies on about 20+ desks.  As someone made a change they would print and distribute the changes to everyone.  When I got into it, I found a massive mish-mash of fragmented files containing bits and piecies of the various chapters.  I brought them all together into a logical collection of documents.  Over time we eliminated the paper copies and moved everyone to electronic copies.  Much neater, and better for the environment.

Do you deal with many printed copies of the manual?  If not, then change management can be much easier.  If are still working with printed copies, the best thing you can do (for your sanity) is to try to convince as many people as possible to migrate away from paper to e-copies of the document. Perferrably by providing everyone with read access to a single "master" copy, or by maintaining a minimum number of discrete copies of the file.  Don't forget to sell the idea that when your people are using the electronic copy they can use the "Find" feature and hyperlinks to move around in the manual much faster.

Can you convince your auditor or manager (or whoever has signoff authority on the manual) to migrate from change management at the "typed" page level (which no longer is logical) to managing change at the "section" level. Use the built in heading levels, and manage changes to those "sections".  Using the headings you can use bookmarks with logical names.

jan horcicka say:

Hello,

so the problem is solved and I thought you might be interested about the conclusion of the story. :)

When March passed by and we switched to April, I realised that the problem is not that the field is not updating, but it is actually showing the same value all the time. I tried to find in the code how this could be possible and found out the only way is that there are switched months and days. So again, the code wasn't broken, It was just badly written.

See, the fact that my colleagues use Office 2007 is also connected to them using older PC with older OS and these PCs have different regional setting than my PC.
