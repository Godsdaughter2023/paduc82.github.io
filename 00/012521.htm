joedang say:

1. Equation editing and numbering

I'm not familiar with Word's equation editing, but the OpenOffice equation editor is vaguely reminiscent of LaTeX. This is where LaTeX will really shine. Creating the equations is a little cumbersome, but they will look perfect, and it will handle numbering and referencing for you, so you don't end up incorrectly numbering/referencing and confusing your audience.

2. Fonts, symbols, etc: which would render it better latex or word or power point (Is there a difference at all?)

LaTeX will render it better, hands down. The font system it uses takes into account what's happening with the surrounding symbols. It also has access to tremendous libraries of typefaces and symbols. The only bad thing is that it may be obnoxious to find the package and command for very obscure symbols. With Word, you're pretty much "limited" to Unicode, which is still pretty extensive, but sometimes typefaces won't have the symbol you want or they have it but it isn't pretty.

3. Thesis writing: how to split the word document into several chapters ( independent files) and combile them later

LaTeX will do this with the \input{} or \include{} commands, depending on exactly how you want to insert the sub-document. So, you could just edit your chapter with your boilerplate preamble, \begin{document}, and \end{document}, then when you're ready, comment that stuff out of the chapter and compile your Main document with its own preamble and \input{} commands. I don't know how you'd do it in Word.

4. Comparison of Power point and latex for slides : are there any website giving the comparison in a detailed way?

I don't know of a comparison to point you to, but I'll give you my opinion. I found Beamer (the popular LaTeX package for slides) awkward at first, since I didn't know how much stuff would fit on a slide, so I had to tweak/recompile a lot to get it right. However, I think this might ultimately be a good thing, since it gets you to think about how much stuff is on your slides, and hopefully end up with actual slides instead of a transcript of your presentation in landscape format. Of course, Beamer will give you a more consistent and beautiful result, and it will display properly on any system (provided you're not using some out-of-date indie PDF viewer), but it will take a lot more work to create the slides.

5. Block diagrams, Graphs and chart: how to make them look professional in word and power point.

LaTeX has a huge number of different packages for diagrams. Some of them require an unreasonable amount of work to use, but some don't. You usually need to know what the structure of the diagram is before you create it, rather than playing with it like you might in MSOffice. They will pretty much all look more professional than something created in an MSOffice program, but take more effort to produce... You may be seeing a theme here.

6. How to have  a template specification as a separate file which stores the values and setting for formatting in word and power point.

Using LaTeX's \input{} in the preamble does exactly that. You could also make your own documentclass, but unless you intend to make a lot of documents (or want a standard among a group or company), that's overkill.

7. Is it good to combine the latex and word?

I would imagine that there are very few situations where that would be the best option. You would probably end up creating some diagram or equation in LaTeX, and then inserting that into a Word document. I'm not sure if Word likes handling vector graphics. I suppose if you wanted to illustrate a comparison between the two, you might want to insert a Word screenshot into a LaTeX document.

tl;dr:

LaTeX will look much better, but require more work, and [only] other LaTeX users or typography geeks will notice the difference. Since .TEX files are more sane than the underlying code for .DOC or .DOCX (more precisely, .TEX files aren't insane), if you need automated/literate documents, LaTeX is the way to go (if you use R, Sweave or knitr are very nice for this). Obviously, I'm pretty biased towards LaTeX. If you're new to LaTeX, getting it installed and making HelloWorld.pdf may be a large and frustrating barrier of entry. Stuff like writelatex.com might be a good option for getting the hang of it and deciding if you like it, before you facekeyboard trying to make it work.

hansv mvp say:

Here you go:

Sub Delete8()
    Dim n As Long
    Application.ScreenUpdating = False
    Application.DisplayAlerts = False
   
    n = Range("A" & Rows.Count).End(xlUp).Row
    With Range("E2:E" & n)
        .TextToColumns FieldInfo:=Array(1, xlMDYFormat)
        .NumberFormat = "m/d/yyyy"
    End With
   
    With Range("A1").CurrentRegion
        .AutoFilter Field:=5, Criteria1:="<" & CLng(Date - 8), _
            Operator:=xlOr, Criteria2:=">" & CLng(Date + 8)
        If .Columns(5).SpecialCells(xlCellTypeVisible).Count > 1 Then
            .Offset(1).Resize(n - 1).EntireRow.Delete
        End If
        .AutoFilter
    End With
   
    Range("A1:E1").EntireColumn.AutoFit
   
    Application.DisplayAlerts = True
    Application.ScreenUpdating = True
End Sub
