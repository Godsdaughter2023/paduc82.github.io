lemp say:

It's impossible for me or anyone else without access to your computer to know what you may have done with "Tweakbit" (I am not familiar with that program) or what some random "remote tech" may have done to your system.  The "remote tech" may or may not have installed malware.  Either you or the "remote tech" may have uninstalled services or installed unknown services.  Frankly, unless you have a more substantive complaint than "seems far too many services are running," my advice to you would be to do nothing at all.

If you must do "something," I suggest that you reset the "startup type" of the standard Windows services to their defaults ("startup type" determines if a service (a) starts automatically when Windows starts, (b) starts whenever Windows determines that the service is needed, or (c) never starts at all).  This will not re-install services that may have been removed or uninstall services (or anything else) that may have been installed.

Before you do anything, make a backup of your system, or at least create a System Restore point.

Although it is relatively straightforward to reset services to their default "start" value or to a relatively "safe" configuration, I am a bit hesitant to give you instructions if you feel uncomfortable messing around with the guts of your operating system.  If you do things incorrectly, you may make things far worse than they already are.

Go to Black Viper's site, which I linked in my earlier post.  There are several pages at that site that would be useful to you.

First, determine if you have installed Service Pack 1 for Windows 7 (you should have done so long ago).  Click Start, right-click Computer, and select Properties.  If you have Service Pack 1, this is where it will say so.  To use the registry files (discussed below), you also need to know if you have a 32-bit system or a 64-bit system.  This is shown on the same "System Properties" page.

If you have installed Service Pack 1, go here --> http://www.blackviper.com/service-configurations/black-vipers-windows-7-service-pack-1-service-configurations/

If you don't have Service Pack 1, go here --> http://www.blackviper.com/service-configurations/black-vipers-windows-7-service-configurations/

Read through the explanatory material at the top of the page.  After reading through the material, you'll get to the "Service Default Registry Entries" table.  I suggest that you focus on either the "Default" column for Home Premium or the "Safe" column (after, of course, reading what "Safe" means).

You can print the table using one of the icons just above the "show/hide columns" button, but if my experience is any guide, this may not format very well.  It seems to work better, at least for me, if I use the "make pdf" button and then print that.

Start the Services applet by clicking Start, typing "services.msc" in the Search box, and pressing Enter.  You'll get a window like the one below.  If you click the "Extended" tab, you'll see the column with an explanation of what the service does and a "Start" or "Stop" link; if you click the "Standard" tab, that column goes away.

Go through the list and compare the "Startup Type" shown in the Services applet to the Startup Type shown in the "Default" or "Safe" column of the appropriate table from Black Viper's site.  If the Startup Type shown on your computer is different, double-click the line for that service to open its "Properties."  Change the Startup Type using the drop-down list and click OK.  Repeat until you have gone through the entire list.

If that seems too complex, Black Viper provides registry modification files that will reset services to their default startup type.  These files may be downloaded here -->  http://www.blackviper.com/2010/12/16/windows-7-service-pack-1-services-registry-files-2/  You MUST read and understand the text on that page before downloading and using one of the files.  If that material seems too difficult, don't do it and instead take your computer to a local independent computer repair shop -- not a chain store's "tech service" department.  Ask the tech to explain what it is that she intends to do before permitting her to do it -- and make sure that you have adequate backups before anything is done.
