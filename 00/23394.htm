mike middleton say: (1) When you use Add Trendline 3rd order polynomial with an XY plot, Excel computes X^2 and X^3 for fitting the polynomial trendline. When you use the Data Analysis Regression tool, Excel is using your data on the worksheet, which is X, X^2, and X^6 (not X^3). Instead, if you use X, X^2, and X^3 with the Regression tool, you will get similar results for the polynomial coefficients.

(2) Overfitting: If you are not familiar with the concept, search Google and Wikipedia. Since your data are single-bulge instead of S-shaped, you will likely get a more meaningful and more useful fit using a quadratic (X and X^2) instead of a cubic (X, X^2, and X^3). There is a small difference in Adjusted R Square, and more importantly for curve-fitting the standard error is about the same: 0.011225 for quadratic and 0.011092 for the cubic.

(3) If you want to show approximate 95% confidence regions on the XY plot, I recommend using plus-and-minus approximately two standard errors, i.e., the standard error shown at the top of the Summary Output of the Data Analysis Regression tool. That standard error is based on the standard deviation of the residuals, i.e., a measure of the scatter of the actual points around the regression curve. To construct the range, I would not use the standard errors of the individual regression coefficients unless you have some specific reason to do so.

nuculerman say:

Hi,

This is a math problem, more than an excel problem.

You have the following equations:

a1*x + b1*y + c1*z = C1

a2*x + b2*y + c2*z = C2

etc.

Where a, b, and c are the percent weights of your inputs proteins and x, y, z are the fractions of each protein you're using to get C, which is the corresponding percent weight of human muscle tissue.

All you have to do is use some matrix algebra to obtain

{x; y; z} = {a1, b1, c1; a2, b2, c2;....a20, b20, c20}^-1*{C1;C2...C20}

nuculerman say:

I just realized a constraint on x, y, z is probably that they sum to one, so that wouldn't work.

Alternatively, you could do this visually/graphically which is how I prefer to solve similar problems with excel.

Make three new columns with values of protein/x, where x is a cell reference, for each protein and plot the sum of those three new columns vs. human muscle tissue, and vary two of the paraments, with the third being =1-x-y.  If you know how to set up a slider this is even easier.  Then you can just vary the slider until the second graph looks as much like the first graph as you can get it.

As I said, the easiest way to do this, unless you want solve a large computational problem, is to simply graph one curve vs. the other and adjust your three variables (fraction of each protein), either manually or with a slider, until the distributions are as close to on top of each other as you can get them.  Simply put amino acids on the x axis, and the percent compositions on the y axis, with two plots: human tissue, sum of proteins.
