Auto Numbers in sequential order? - Microsoft Community

sross002 say:

Auto Numbers in sequential order?

Hello,

Is there a way to have a field with Auto Number as the Data Type without gaps between the numbers?  I want this field to display in sequential order all the time without gaps or holes between two numbers.

Thank you.

 

marshall barton say:

No.  AutoNumber fields are not guaranteed to be anything.  If you also have a unique index for the field, then thsy will be guaranteed to bo unique.

Besides all that, AutoNumber fields are intended to be used as surrogate keys and thus should never be displayed to users.  If you have a real need for a sequentially numbered field, the best you can do is calculate it with code in your data entry form.  The easiest way to do that is to use the form's BeforeUpdate event:

   Me.seqnum = Nz(DMax("seqnum", "thetable"), 0) + 1

Event then, deleting records will inevitably produce gaps.

sross002 say:

Is there away to automatically assign numbers in a field without producing gaps, even if I delete a record?

 

marshall barton say:

It's conceivable, but it would require a way to find a gap, figure out what number is missing, and then deal with the fact that the sequence number has no relationship to the order the records were created.

All things considered, the only(?) reason for insisting on a minimal number of gaps is to lower some auditor's anxiety level.  In that case, don't delete any records.  Instead, add a field that you can use to mark a tranaction as void (like in a check book).

scottgem say:

Is there away to automatically assign numbers in a field without producing gaps, even if I delete a record?

 

First, yes there are ways to assign sequential numbering to records. I have a blog on sequential numbering that explains one of the main ways (see blog URL in signature). However, the sequence is not maintained if you delete records. Frankly, a system that maintains a sequential numbering while allowing for the deletion of records is meaningless. Any numbering system where the assigned value changes has no value in my opinion.

If you want to explain why you need such a numbering system, we may be able to advise further.

sross002 say:

I have a database where people can add records vis a form and for many reasons they delete some records.  I have an ID field that autopopulates a number for identification purposes.  It just makes sense to have the numbers in a sequential manner.  It is more visualy appealing and easier for the users to understand.

In all, I just feel that it makes more sense to have a sequential numbering system, even when records are deleted.

 

 

marshall barton say:

It's not practical and, in general. not achievable.  Personally I try to hide any numbers that used "for identification reasons" and provide another way to locate a record of interest.  If an ID number must be displayed to users, then making it a random number that is not the slightest bit sequential will remove the feeling that it needs to be "more visualy appealing".

The only way to have sequentially numbered records is to renumber the records whenever a record is deleted and that would make them useless for identifying anything.

john p spencer say:

So if you delete record 252 from the database, do you want to renumber all the records that are higher than that to maintain the sequence or do you want to assign 252 to the next record created?

Renumbering would be expensive and the users would likely get confused.  Oh, I want record 393 (which now is 392 since 252 was deleted and you renumbered the records).  So they go to record 393 and it does not have the data they are looking for.

Re-using numbers can also be confusing since the numbers are in sequence but the record that is now 252 was not the 252nd record created. 

The only way to maintain a real sequence is to mark the record as deleted, but keep it in the table.  You can then filter out the "deleted" records as needed.

Let me add to Marshall and John that it makes LESS sense to have a sequential numbering system when records are deleted. In fact, in any situation where sequential numbering is allowed, deletion is NOT allowed. Instead records are marked as inactive or deleted and filtered out. Nor do I agree that sequentially numbering is more visually appealing. I do agree that it sometimes makes sense to be able to arrange records in terms of when they were entered chronologically. For those situations you use a date/time stamp. I also don't have a problem with using a visible identifier that can indicate the relative order of entry. That does make it easier for users to understand if they need to know the chronologically relationship of entry. 

But having a numbering system where the identifying number can change makes NO sense and makes things more confusing to users. As John pointed out. User A enters a record that was marked as 393. But was changed to 392. User B now enters 393. They may have to discuss the data and don't realize they are talking about two different records.

I'm sorry, but I don't believe you have thought this through enough or you don't have enough experience designing databases to understand the true impacts here. This is no knock on you, but you are here because you don't have that experience and are trying to benefit from those who do. So take our word for it. Renumbering records to maintain a sequence when records are deleted is cumbersome, confusing and meaningless.
