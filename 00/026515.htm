rob koch say:

Quit opening messages without stopping to look carefully at them first and then if they don't look right at all, just delete them.  The "best" phishing messages like those you apparently received are often very simple and use subtle tricks to get you to open them, but if you look at them in the context of what you've seen from that sender in the past, there's always something "fishy" about them, hence the name Phishing.

If a link in the email is asking you to login, that's a huge red flag and should immediately cause you to stop and think, at which point exiting the current screen and opening a fresh one where you manually enter or select an existing favorite for that particular organization is the only safe option.

I have no idea how your particular My Way email works or what specific security features they have in place, that's a question for them.  If your email address is something like my.name @ myway.com then the SPF Record would be for them, if you have a personalized domain like bill.com then My Way would still likely manage this for you, but the SPF Record would need to be specific for that specially chosen domain.  In any case, your email management would typically do this and not you personally, since you have none of the knowledge or access to make this happen in most cases.

A quick look online at a simple "review" of My Way at About.com seems to indicate it's a rather weak service that includes advertising in the outgoing messages, which in the US is typically avoided.  The cons were mostly missing features, so apparently nothing terribly wrong with them, but nothing all that special either, but some of the major names have their own issues so even that's no guarantee.

The most recent type of phishing messages you received are often difficult for even the best Junk mail filters to catch, since I've seen 3 of these myself sent by 2 different people who clicked on these by mistake.  They might seem quite convincing to most people, but to me the relatively terse subject line combined with the senders who I knew obviously made no sense together.  I purposefully opened one of these and clicked the link which took me to a somewhat convincing login page for my own email account, but which had small clues like a copyright years in the future and slight distortions in the images I could personally recognize as invalid.

Your problem is only partially related to My Way, since like many others you are now allowing your worry or haste to override your common sense.  No email system at present is good enough to perfectly block every false message that's sent, so you must take the time to vet them yourself.  This is why I personally keep my sign-ups to any email lists to an absolute minimum and preferably only for notifications that I can personally choose and configure, like alerts from my bank that I then always log into manually (no email links ever!) in order to manage.

I never give out my email address to anyone I don't completely trust and instead have a secondary "throwaway" address which I use for less trustworthy firms where I still may need that ability, since I can always provide my "real" address later if I feel their status changes.  This has kept my own Spam email to a minimum and so gives me more time to examine a questionable message that does make it into my inbox.

You've just learned how much trouble such mistakes can be, so realize that the extra moment spent examining a message or selecting your own favorite or manually entered web address is always worth the extra effort.

Rob
