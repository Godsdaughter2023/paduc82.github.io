You can *not* do this, at least not without third-party software.
 
However, bear the following in mind:
 
Although by default the task bar appears on the bottom of the screen,
it can be on any of the sides you prefer it on. To move it from any
side to another, simply click on an unused part of it and drag it
where you want it. Be sure to click *within* it, not on the edge;
clicking on the edge and dragging will resize it, not move it.
 
If it won't move, it may be locked. In that case, right click on it
and uncheck "Lock the taskbar," then try again.
 
By the way, with today's wide-screen monitors, I prefer the task bar
on the left or right side of the screen. I think that makes a better
use of screen real estate than having it at the bottom.

jason freitas say:

I think I've answered my own question. When an update is installed, a notification does in fact appear in the lower-right corner of your screen, along with the typical chime, that your computer must be restarted. If you miss that notification, it will remain in the Action Center until the computer is restarted. If you take no action, Windows will automatically schedule a restart time "during a time you usually don't use your device". Right now my computer is scheduled to restart at 3:30 a.m., which Windows picked. When I experienced my first auto-restart, it was just after I had upgraded, so it's possible that Windows had no history of my usage history and picked a restart time that was later that afternoon when I happened to be away.

In summary:

  • Windows will notify you that a restart is required, along with an opportunity to choose the time (as the documentation and everyone else has been saying)
  • The notification appears in the Action Center as well as the Windows Update setting screen, which you should check periodically — especially if you're the type of person to leave your computer on and unattended for long periods of time
  • If you miss or ignore the notification, a restart time is chosen automatically, apparently based on typical usage

jason freitas say:

This has happened to me, too. I've given up trying to work with the notification system. Here's what I do now. It's kind of a pain, but I guess you have to pick your poison:

  1. I keep the Windows Update service disabled most of the time. (Open the Services desktop app and find Windows Update. First stop the service, then go to its properties and set the "Startup type" to "Disabled".) This will prevent any automatic updates from being downloaded at all.
  2. Once a week or so, change the Startup type to Manual and start the service. Then go to Windows Update, check for updates, and let it do its thing. Restart if you have to.
  3. Stop and disable the service again (same as step 1).

This way the computer only restarts when I want it to. I try not to let too much time pass before between updates—mainly for the Windows Defender and other security updates, but also because I don't want to end up having to install tons of stuff.

Good luck.
