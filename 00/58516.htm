Can I use different password than my Microsoft Account Password to - Microsoft Community

shiva1401 say:

Can I use different password than my Microsoft Account Password to Sign-in to my desktop?

In my laptop, my Microsoft Account is linked to the Windows 10 installed. I would like to Sign in (either from being Locked, restart or shut down) using a different alpha-numeric password than my Microsoft Password. Can this be done?

a. user say:

It might help to understand that there are two types of user account on a Windows PC: a local account and a cloud account.

A local account exists only on the computer that created it. A cloud account exists only on the internet.

Because a local account exists only on the computer that created it, you can only use it on that computer. Since a cloud account exists on the internet, you can use it from any computer that can connect to the internet.

A Microsoft account is simply Microsoft's branded version of a cloud account, just like iCloud is Apple's branded version of a cloud account.

So far so good, right? Well, here's where it starts to get complicated:

A Microsoft account is not a true cloud account (and neither is iCloud) because you can use a Microsoft account and yet still have programs that are installed on your own computer, and you can still store your personal files on your own computer. With a true cloud account - like a Google account - no programs are installed on your own computer and no personal files are are stored on your own computer. Everything happens on the internet. So a Microsoft account is really a hybrid - part local account and part cloud account.

Now you understand what a local account is and what a Microsoft account is. All is well, right? Now, the Marketing Department will attempt to make a mess of things.

Microsoft wants everyone to use a Microsoft account always and only. Could it have something to do with money? No, let's not be cynical 😊. So it's in Microsoft's interest to trick, confuse and pressure everyone into using a Microsoft account. But now that you understand the difference between a local account and a Microsoft account, the answer to your question will be very clear.

You can choose to sign in to Windows 10 with either a local account or a Microsoft account. If you want, you can have two, separate user accounts on the same computer, with one being a local account and the other being a Microsoft account. And you can sign in to Windows 10 with either one at any time, as you wish. You can even switch back and forth, if you wish. There's nothing wrong with doing this - not technically, not legally and certainly not morally, although the link between software and morality is tenuous.

Every user account needs to have a password. You could use the same password for your local account as you use for your Microsoft account, if you wish. Nothing to stop you from doing that. But from a security point of view, it's better if every user account has its own password, regardless of whether it's a local account or a cloud Microsoft account.

So: If you're signing in to Windows 10 with a local account instead of your Microsoft account, can you use a different password for your local account ... different from the password for your Microsoft Account? Yes - and that's a good idea.

a. user say:

If your family or friends want to use your computer, don't give them the password to your user account - that's crazy. Instead, create a separate user account for your family and friends to use. Mine is called Visitor.

If you want to sign in to your Windows 10 PC with a PIN instead of a password, you can easily do that. It won't change the password to your Microsoft account (or your local account) it only gives you an additional way to sign in to your PC. You'll still need your Microsoft account credentials to sign in to your Microsoft account elsewhere.

When you switch from using a password to using a PIN, your password isn't replaced by the PIN - the PIN gives you an additional way to sign in to your Windows PC. Now, any time you want to sign in to your Windows 10 PC - whether you're signing in with a local account or a Microsoft account - you can enter your PIN or your password. My Android phone works the same way.

Here's more background on using a Microsoft account:

Every Windows user needs to have a Microsoft account, but you don't need to sign in to your PC with a Microsoft account, if you don't want to. You can sign in to your PC with a local account, as I do, exclusively.

You need to have a Microsoft account because that's the only way that Microsoft will let you use their online applications, like Office and OneDrive ... and this forum. To use these you need to be signed in to your Microsoft account.

Okay, here it comes:

Being signed in to your Microsoft account is not the same thing as being signed in to your PC with a Microsoft account.

Right now I am signed in to my Microsoft account - that's the only way I can post to this forum - but I'm signed in to my computer with a local account. Every time you use one of Microsoft's online applications - like Skype or Outlook.com or Office 365 or OneDrive etc. etc. - you have to sign in to your Microsoft account, by supplying your Microsoft account username and password when prompted. Once you do that, you continue to be signed in to your Microsoft account until you explicitly sign out of your Microsoft account.

If I wanted to, after I finish typing this post I could go straight to OneDrive without having to sign in again, because once I've signed in to my Microsoft account in one place I'm signed in every place until I sign out.

The Microsoft account that you use for Office 365, Xbox, Calendar, Outlook.com etc. is the same Microsoft account that you would use to sign in to Windows 10 (if you were so inclined.) So you can't change the Microsoft account password for Office and have it be different from the Microsoft account password you would use to sign in to Windows - it's all the same Microsoft account.

rpwt say:

Battery drain when multiple users logged in?

(Eg if the other person has not logged out -- if they just put the computer to sleep, then I wake it and login to my account.)

jasmine car say:

Hi,

Multiple users or accounts that are logged in on Surface doesn't really affect the performance of the battery. However, there are few steps on how to make your battery charge last longer. Here's how:

1. Check your battery saving settings.

If you've updated on the Anniversary Update, there’s a chance that it somehow changed your battery saving settings. If the battery settings are changed, your device is going to consume more battery. So, before doing anything else, make sure your battery saving settings remained the same, or you can just simply change it if not.

To make sure your battery saving settings are same, do the following:

a. Go to Settings.

b. Go to System > Battery.

Make sure everything is as you left it before the Anniversary Update.

Once everything's okay, we suggest you quickly check the brightness level of your screen. To check your brightness settings, go to Settings > System > Battery, and go to Display.

If you determined that Anniversary Update did nothing to the battery settings, proceed to the next step.

2. Check if your drivers are updated.

If a driver of a certain hardware is outdated, battery drain issues might occur. So, we suggest that you go and check if all your drivers are updated. To find the list of drivers installed on your computer, go to Search, type device manager, and open Device Manager.

Battery drain issue can also be caused if some drivers are not fully compatible with the Anniversary Update. So, it’s important to have all your drivers updated.

4. Find out which programs drain most battery.

To check which program or app drains the most battery on your system, do the following:

a. Go to Settings.

b. Go to System > Battery.

c. Go to Battery usage by app.

From there, you’ll see the list of all the programs installed on your computer, and how much battery it consumes.

Keeps us updated with the results.
