The issue with respect to changing the logon password for a user account is that if the change is done from outside the account, any files that the user has encrypted using Windows EFS will be unable to be decrypted unless the user's logon password is changed back to the original password.  Note that the "logon password" is not the same as the password used to encrypt the files.

However, because you don't have any files encrypted using EFS, this is not a problem for you.  You will not lose data by changing your logon password, however that change may be accomplished. 

I created the new admin account because I was told by a systems engineer of some repute that the keystroke logger might possess my (old) admin account password.

That was good advice.  Given that a keystroke logger is intended to capture every keystroke you type (and some also capture mouse usage and screen shots as well), if you ever entered your password, it's likely that it was captured.

So I disconnected from the Internet, booted to safe mode, and created a new admin account and password, which I now use. 

Disconnecting from the Internet probably did not prevent the keystroke logger -- if one actually is present -- from capturing the information related to your new account, including your new account's password.  Keystroke loggers typically are resident on the computer that is under attack.  They may be in the form of software hidden on your hard drive or they may be embedded in a piece of hardware attached to your system.  In either case, if they use the Internet at all, it is to transmit the file of captured keystrokes back to the person who installed the logger.  The logger will have captured the keystrokes while you were disconnected from the Internet and then transmitted them after you re-connected using your new account. 

**************************************************************

Given the state of affairs you have described, your best course of action is to stop using your computer until your friend (or the "systems engineer of some repute," if that's not the same person) comes back to help you -- or you could take your computer to a local independent (not a chain store) computer repair shop.  If you choose the latter, I suggest that you get recommendations from knowledgeable computer folks who you know.

When attempting to "clean" your computer, keep in mind that the "best" keystroke loggers (from an intruder's point of view) aren't cleanable by the use of any software technique, up to and including a clean reinstall of Windows.  This is because they are hardware devices that operate outside of Windows.  See, e.g., http://www.keyghost.com/
