Okay, so I've played it for most of the evening and I can say that I definitely like it, but for various reasons, others may not.

First of all, if you've played previous Magic games on consoles, this is basically more of the same. What sets it apart however (aside from being free) is that it now uses a "coin" system to unlock booster packs that you can use to create your own custome decks. Furthermore, the chief reason the game is free is because you can use real-world $$$ to buy a certain amount of coins (with the maximum purchase being = to $40). Otherwise, you complete duels either in story-mode, skill-quests that teach you the finer aspects of the game, or in PvE or PvP matches. While this may turn many away from the game, I found that you only need 150 coins to unlock 6 new cards in a single booster (there are more options to get more boosters for more coins, but I did the math and the ratio is ALWAYS the same in that 1 booster = 150 coins with the maximum option giving you up to 32 booster packs all at once). Depending on the difficulty of your opponents in the duels, you can get anywhere between 5 - 20 coins per match. You can further earn more coins via daily quests that changes with each day (I completed 1 just this evening for 40 coins where I had to win 4 duels using a pre-made deck). These quests are pretty cool, because you can have up to 3 active at any time, and they last until you beat them. Furthermore, you can refresh the slots to replace it with a new quest if you find some of them too hard or time consuming.

Customization is similar to previous Magic games when it comes to making new decks, but streamlines it better to make things easier while adding a few cool new features (like being able to not only choose the avatar on the box-art of your decks, but the background as well).

This game, depending on how you look at it, can be a great experience or a grind - it'll all depend on the individual player though and how much they enjoy it and/or are willing to put the time and effort into it (assuming of course they don't throw money into the mix).

The story mode follows the same basic path of previous Magic games on consoles, but this time around focuses on the origins of how many of the Planeswalkers that make up the Magic universe came to be well, Planeswalkers in the 1st place. For players that are new to the series, this is a great history lesson to get their foot in the door and to learn the motivations behind the various characters and the colors of Magic that they wield.

The presentation of the game, while simple all-around, is the best I've seen for ANY Magic game to date with nice rounded corners, neutral colors, and beautiful pieces of art from various cards that permeate through the entire game, including the achievements you unlock, which make for some of the best wallpaper you can get for your home screen anywhere. The music is easy on the ears to boot.

Along with the typical duels and story mode, some staples of the Magic games on consoles make a return - the mandatory training for example is back. It's a minor chore to get through for those of us who are experienced with how to play the card game, but is absolutely invaluable to new players. Another returning staple is the mode called two-headed giant, which allows for games of 2v2 where 2 players on a team share a life-total, but control their own spells. If you have never played this mode in Magic, you owe it to yourself to try it with a friend.

As of now, it doesn't seem like there's as many cards to unlock as in previous Magic games, but there's still enough to make quite a few decks (and you get a starter pack with a ton of random cards to get your feet wet) and the developers have promised new features, cards, modes, etc. down the road, so it doesn't look like this is going to be a simple 1-off like previous Magic games seem to have done in the past. Personally, I'm hoping for an online training mode like they had in the very 1st Magic console game as well as a few other returning modes.

Lastly, and obviously, the game is free! So, stop reading this and play a darn game with me already! ;P

Fixed it!!!
Accessing Mac OS X Windows Sharing from Windows 7
Windows 7 will not work with Mac OS X Windows file sharing support by default. If you attempt to access a folder shared from Mac OS X, Vista will display a logon error repeatedly.
The problem is that Vista, by default, will only use NTLMv2 for authentication, which is not supported by Mac OS X's Windows Sharing service.
The other problem is the Minimum Session Security for NTVLM SSP based Clients.
To get around this:
    1.    In Vista, open the Control Panel
    2.    Switch to "Classic" view
    3.    Double-click Administration Tools
    4.    Double-click Local Security Policy
    5.    Or Secpol.msc
    6.    Expand "Local Policies" and select "Security Options"
    7.    Alternate : Type secpol.msc to get editor up then
    8.    Locate "Network Security: LAN Manager Authentication Level" in the list and double-click it.
    9.    Change the setting from "Send NTMLv2 response only" to "Send LM & NTLM - use NTLMv2 session if negotiated"
    10.    Network Security: Minimum session security for NTLM SSP Based (including secure RPC) Clients
    11.    Change the setting from "require 128 bit" to unchecked (No Minimum)
    12.    Click OK

basically the difference between vista and Windows 7 is lines 10 thru 11
