MS Headset Adapter / No Microphone With Turtle Beach &amp; - Microsoft Community

chillibadger say:

MS Headset Adapter / No Microphone With Turtle Beach &amp; Others - Possible Fix For Many

I had the issue where my microphone wasn't being picked up from my Turtle Beach X1 headset.

This headset is a few years old now, it comes with a green 3.5mm and pink 3.5mm connector, one for headphone and one for microphone.

There is also a control box which takes a cable out into the game controller with separate mute and voice chat volume.  The cable is a funny semi circle fitting at the controller end but in essence a 2.5mm plug.

Plugging the pink 3.5mm connector will not work as the connector isn't TRRS enabled (if you google this you will see a series of revisions on the 3.5mm connector and it can be separated into different zones), looking at several connectors against each other you will notice extra black bands around some of them.  The 2.5mm to 3.5mm conversion cable that comes with the MS adapter demonstrates what the new type plug looks like.

I purchased a 2.5mm to 3.5mm cable in haste to plug into the TB and MS adapter but all this did was cut off Kinect and didn't actually give me any voice still.  Examining the cable it wasn't TRRS enabled with the 4 zones, only had  3 (TRS).

What put me onto looking at connectors was a revelation when my blackberry headset that came with my phone actually worked and this was the new style 3.5mm connector.

I therefore have this solution which may work well for others.  If you have a 3.5mm connector for your microphone the chances it being the new connector is remote (newer headsets possibly combine these).  You can get a cheap extension cable with female/male 3.5mm ends to take it from TRS to TRRS specification and it should work.  I guess those with a 2.5mm connector can use the MS supplied cable the comes with the headset adapter and are currently not having any issues?

I actually purchased the TB 2.5mm to 2.5mm cable as it has inline mute and volume controls (yes I know you can do these on the MS headset adapter too), then I plug the one end into the TB control box and the other into the supplied MS cable (female 2.5mm to male 3.5mm) that in turn goes into the headset adapter.  Important to note my TB cable is not the new standard but plugging into the MS cable converts the microphone into the correct zone that the headset adapter acknowledges/recognises.

If you do have any questions about this happy to try and help out.  I managed to get away from spending on another headset for the XB1 and can now use my TB on both platforms.

EDIT: I misread your post (maybe because I left something out of the quote . )

If you are still able to access the previous Win10 that was upgraded from Win7, you

should be able to reactivate that Win10 using the license key that came  with the new machine.

The main worry here is that is was upgraded from Win7, which may throw a spanner in the works, but

in this case the restore would be blocked, and should not harm the new Win10 installation.

Windows invariably has a option to back out of operations before committing.

Assuming your old Win7 was an OEM version (either pre-installed, or OEM system builder disk version)vs 'retail disk' version, you cannot activate the license for Win7 (or the Win10 upgrade as that used the

license from the old Win7from your old machine to a new machine.

The license is tied to the motherboard on the old machine. Exception is sometimes made if the original motherboard fails and the same make and model motherboard

replaces the original (generally done via telephone activation and speaking to a supervisor).

Regarding files - If the files were 'backed up' to the same drive that failed (which is likely if you selected

Let Windows Choose, but I'm only guessing as I've never used this backup method) then they are most likely gone.

If done to a separate HDD to the one that failed everything should still be intact , and accessible on that drive.

If you don't have an installer for Office, but have a license key, you should still be able to download (if still available)

and reinstall using that license key.

You may even be able to copy/past the Office install to the new machine and find it will run, and reactivate.

If 'Backups' are not done to another internal, or external HDD, USB, etc.  they are just temporary backups/copies

to fall back on should you loose something you are working on if the system crashes while you are in the

middle of something.  Not true backups.

"I also did a system image DVD.  I am assuming that it will have my programs,"

I've never done a system image to a DVD (I do clone/migrate to another HDD), but you should be able to 

find what the DVD or DVD's do have on them by opening the DVD .

Win10 should pop up a message asking what you want to do with the DVD. Select Open folder... .

Or - Should the DVD start to do something without the prompt, eject the DVD and disable 'auto-play'

(Control Panel > Auto Play. Select the option Ask me every time).

Reinsert the DVD and go to  This PC  and R/click on the DVD . Then select Open.

This will give you access to whatever is on the DVD, so you can verify just what exactly is there.

langware say: You need to determine if the modem is at fault, or something else.

Next time the problem happens, instead of power cycling your modem, shut down and power off your PC. Then, restart your PC. See if the problem has gone away or still remains.

If the problem still remains after you restart your PC, then the problem is likely in the modem. Call Verizon and INSIST that they replace it.

If the problem goes away after you reboot your PC, then the problem is likely to be caused by a problem (hardware/software) in your PC. Check/replace the cable between modem and PC (or between modem and your router).

Try shutting down your browser and then restarting your browser ... does the problem go away.

If you have (or can borrow) a second PC, disconnect your PC and hook up the second PC to the modem and see if the problem persists.

If the modem is attached to a router (and your PC is attached to the router), then power cycle the router to see if the problem goes away. If it's a very old router, consider replacing it.

If your PC is wirelessly attached, try attaching your PC with an ethernet cable (and disabling your wireless adapter and enabling your ethernet adapter) to see if the problem goes away.

Good luck!

EDIT:  You may also want to use your anti-virus software to perform a full scan of your hard drive (just to rule out the possibility of the problem being caused by a virus trying to "phone home").

Creating a recovery drive

A recovery drive can help you troubleshoot and fix problems with your PC, even if it won't start. To create one, all you need is a USB drive.

  1. From the taskbar, search for Create a recovery drive then select it. You might be asked to enter an admin password or confirm your choice.
  2. When the tool opens, make sure Back up system files to the recovery drive is selected, then select Next.
  3. Connect a USB drive to your PC, select it, then select Next > Create. A lot of files need to be copied to the recovery drive, so this might take a while.
  4. When it's finished, you might see a Delete the recovery partition from your PC link on the final screen. If you want to free up drive space on your PC, select the link, then select Delete. If not, select Finish.
