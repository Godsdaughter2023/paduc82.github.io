I like to say that Access (actually any relational) databases use the "Grandmother's Pantry Principle": "A place -one place! - for everything, everything in its place". Your characters' biographical information should be stored in the Characters table,and noplace else.

john w. vinson say:

You need to identify the "Entities" - real life (or in this case fictional <g>) people, things or events - of importance to your application. Each type of Entity should have its own table.

Each Entity will have Attributes - discrete chunks of information that you need to know about that Entity. For instance, a Character entity will have one and only one Birthdate. Marriages, though, are a different kind of Entity - each Character may be involved in zero, one, two or more marriages; a person doesn't (necessarily) have a marriage date, he or she might have more than one. So you would have tables like:

Characters
   CharacterID <autonumber Primary Key>
   Shortname <your 'ajs', Text field, with a Unique Index>
   LastName <birth name>
   FirstName <ditto>
   Birthdate <date/time>
   <other unique information about this character>
  
 Marriages
   MarriageID <autonumber primary key>
   HusbandID <Long Integer, link to Characters>
   WifeID <Long Integer, link to Characters>
   WeddingDate <date/time>
   <other info about the marriage as appropriate, dunno if you want to record divorces, etc.>

So you might have records like:

  1. Adam
  2. Eve
  3. Lilith
  4. Cain
  5. Zillah

in the Characters table, and

  1. 1;2;#10/4/1#
  2. 1;3;#4/12/2#
  3. 4;5;#9/4/24#

in the Marriages table (indicating that Adam married Eve, then Adam married Lilith... ooops, that's heretical... <g>)

You'ld have another table of Scenes (I presume); since many characters may participate in a given Scene, and each character may be in many Scenes, you need an Appearance table (I think I called it SceneCast before) with links to the Scenes table and to the Characters table.

The "Apperance" table doesn't "go to" anything though. Again, a Table is  just a data store  - a bucket of records. It just sits there. It doesn't "do" anything, or need to "do" anything! Instead, you would perhaps have a Form based on the Scenes table, with a Subform based on Appearances; on this subform you would have a combo box based on the Characters table, storing the CharacterID in the Appearances table (but displaying the shortname or even the full name of the character to the user).

When you actually open one of the table datasheets, it would be hard to read - a lot of the fields would just be meaningless numbers. Butthat's all right - the table is not human readable, it's computer readable; the humans never need to look at it directly (except for debugging), because that's what the tools (combo boxes, subforms, listboxes etc.) on the Form do.

 

John W. Vinson/MVP

john w. vinson say:

Ok, let's use blue-eyed as an example. Scott's design is advanced, and gives you the option of adding any number of characteristics at will - you could decide that it's important to know big toe length and knee diameter for instance <g>.

But you may be able to decide up front what attributes are important. If you know you'll need hair color and eye color, but can dispense with toe length, you can just put fields in the Characters table for the information you want. Then to find how many blue-eyed people were at the party, you would create a new Query joining the Schenes table, to the Key Scenes table (I'm not sure I understand your name for it), and join the Key Scenes table to the Characters table. You'ld select the event from the Scenes table, and the EyeColor field from the Characters table; on the Criteria line under the event name you'ld put the name of the party; under the "EyeColor" field you'ld put "Blue". Select whatever other fields you want to see, if any; open the query and bang - you'll see a datasheet of all the blue-eyed people who were at the party.

Scott's design, as I say, gives you more flexibility, but correspondingly it gives you more complexity. It's a tradeoff, and you have to decide what features you want and what you can do without.
