To better understand how these confidence schemes (a.k.a. Scams) operate, here's some additional information about this one that was fairly easy to guess or find.

They use the (false) business name Cisco Hub since the Cisco company is a well known US corporation that happens to sell devices that used to be commonly called "hubs", so many people will trust this name even though it's most certainly fake.

The company address you were provided is a shopping center that happen to include a UPS Store.  This is important since UPS Store is known for providing Business Mailboxes, which can include the name of the business (e.g. Cisco Hub) followed by the UPS Store address as the following FAQ entry from the UPS Store web page describes.

What does my mailing address look like?

Your mailing address will be the address of The UPS Store location, with either PMB (private mailbox) or the pound symbol (#) designating your individual box. Instead of “The UPS Store,” your name appears first.

Example:
Joe Smith
PMB XXX or # XXX
12345 Somewhere Street
Some City, Some State Some ZIP 

A search for the phone number 888-512-3119 returns entries including one at 800Notes.com that includes a couple comments as follows:

Just got a call from this number (which I have recorded and saved as I do with all my unusual calls)  11:02pm Man calls asking for Jasmine.  Call the number back and get the run around only trying to get the companies name, the guy is not knowing what to say because I told him I was recording, so he transfers me to his supervisor who tells me my computer is now working fine and reluctantly tells me he is from CISCO.  Not sure what that was all about!"
  • Caller: Cisco
  • Call type: Scam suspicion

"PC issues, want to sell expensive software fixes"

So to sum it up, the Cisco Hub name, though used by a Computer Company in Karachi, Pakistan, doesn't appear to exist in the US and is likely a fake made to sound like the US Cisco corporation in order to build trust.

The company address you were given is a probable mail drop to a UPS Store Mailbox, which is likely picked up by someone else being scammed in a "work from home" confidence scheme.  No such actual business by that name exists at that location, which is why the separate address is used, since there's no real link back to the true scammers who are probably in a different country.

The phone number has been mentioned by others receiving similar calls, while the company name has been mentioned in other threads here in these forums at least twice earlier this year.

https://answers.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/forum/windows_7-security-winsec/cisco-hub-network-tech-scam/9322f61f-e293-42a6-ade2-ad0b8f4b11c5

None of this was really difficult to find and most was found via a simple search for either the name, physical address or phone number you provided.  The problem here is that you even bothered to listen to the scammer's story, since no reputable company of any sort would ever call you out of the blue, without an initial contact from you.

You can't possibly know who's actually calling, since everything you see on your phone can easily be faked including company name and even number, so assume any call you didn't make yourself is always false.  In other words, JUST HANG UP THE PHONE and never do anything a caller asks, no matter how urgent they seem to make it sound.
