    • J38:J999
    • E39:E991
    • J38:J1000

    =SUMIFS(J38:J999,E39:E991,B5,J38:J1000,"celeste")

    Explanation/interpretation of a formula . . . - Microsoft Community

    k-hill say:

    Explanation/interpretation of a formula . . .

    I found this formula online and it works beautifully (pulling the left most numeric values from a string), but I don't understand what it is doing. I'm hoping someone can explain how/why it does what it is doing:  =LOOKUP(9.9E+307,--MID(I18,MIN(FIND({1,2,3,4,5,6,7,8,9,0},I18&1234567890)),ROW(INDIRECT("1:"&LEN(I18)))))

    Specifically - what is 9.9E+307? And what is INDIRECT?  Thank you!  

    hansv mvp say:

    9.9E+307 means 9.9 times 10 to the power 307. This is (almost) the largest number that can be entered in an Excel sheet. (The actual largest number is 9.99999999999999E+307, but that doesn't make a difference here).

    The INDIRECT function converts a text string to a cell reference.

    Let's say the value in cell I18 consists of 5 characters, so LEN(I18) is 5.

    "1:"&LEN(I18) is then equivalent to the string "1:5".

    INDIRECT("1:5") is the range 1:5 consisting of rows 1 to 5.

    ROW(1:5) is an array consisting of the row numbers of this range, i.e. the array {1,2,3,4,5}

    bernie deitrick say:

    9.9E+307 is a very large number, that is used to find the largest number returned by the other parts of the formula. You could use any number in its place, as long as it is larger than what you are looking for. For example, if you are looking for up to 3 digits, the formula could use 1000 as the value.

    INDIRECT allows the range referred to to change dynamically, which is required to build up the array of lengths for the extract.

    An example will help.  Say you have  B12T  and want 12 returned.

    ROW(INDIRECT("1:"&LEN(I18)))  will return the array   {1;2;3;4}

    FIND({1,2,3,4,5,6,7,8,9,0},I18&1234567890)  will return   {2,3,7,8,9,10,11,12,13,14}  and the MIN({2,3,7,8,9,10,11,12,13,14}) will return 2.

    MID(I18,2,{1;2;3;4})  will return {"1";"12";"12T";"12T"}  and the unary operations -- converts those to {1;12;#VALUE!;#VALUE!}

    =LOOKUP(9.9E+307,{1;12;#VALUE!;#VALUE!})  then  gives the largest value, as the final result: 12.

    If your value has two groups of numbers - say, B12T43R, the MID will return {"1";"12";"12T";"12T4";"12T43";"12T43R";"12T43R"} which will convert to {1;12;#VALUE!;#VALUE!;#VALUE!;#VALUE!;#VALUE!})  and still return 12

    daniel pineault say:

    To add to Duane's post, once you split your db, you place the BE on a central server, then you relink your FE to the new BE location.

    I'd urge you to create a persistent Between the FE and BE at the startup of your application.

    Furthermore, you must share the DB over a wired LAN connection.  Trying to share a DB over a WAN or wireless is known to increase the risks of corruption.

    As always, once you deploy your db, be sure to put in place a backup strategy in case you ever experience issues.
