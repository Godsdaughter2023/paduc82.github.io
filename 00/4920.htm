ganesh kumar n say:

Try the following steps:-

Step 1:- By using Macro we can change the language in powerpoint for all slides.

Create a new macro:
1. Go to Tools, Macro, and Visual Basic Editor.
2. Insert a new empty module by selecting Insert, Module.
3. Paste this code on the right panel and save the macro:
Option Explicit  
Public Sub ChangeSpellCheckingLanguage()  
    Dim j As Integer, k As Integer, scount As Integer, fcount As Integer
    scount = ActivePresentation.Slides.Count
    For j = 1 To scount
        fcount = ActivePresentation.Slides(j).Shapes.Count
        For k = 1 To fcount
            If ActivePresentation.Slides(j).Shapes(k).HasTextFrame Then
                ActivePresentation.Slides(j).Shapes(k) _
                .TextFrame.TextRange.LanguageID = msoLanguageIDEnglishAUS
            End If
        Next k
    Next j
End Sub

Step 2:- This "msoLanguageIDEnglishAUS" which is used in the above macro can be replaced by any desired language. The full list of languages can be found in this article http://msdn.microsoft.com/en-us/library/aa432635.aspx

Step 3:- Execute the macro (by pressing F5 within the editor, or by selecting Tools, Macro, Macros, ChangeSpellCheckingLanguage, and clicking Run).
After that all text elements within the presentation will have the new spelling language.

A large part of the reason I got my whole family Windows Phones was the (relatively) greater control over privacy settings than iPhones or Androids offer.  We have been a part of your 3% US market share from the beginning, and I used to enthusiastically endorse the platform to anyone who would listen.  Over the past couple months, however, my enthusiasm has turned to frustration (I'm sure it shows in this ranting post, sorry in advance).

So tomorrow, after a month of using the $600 Lumia 950 flagship ("the Lumia fans have been waiting for"), this one fan is going to return his phone to the Microsoft Store and likely never return to Windows for his mobile solution (certainly not Windows 10 Mobile in its current iteration).  In fairness, let me just put a disclaimer that the Lumia 950's camera is nothing short of remarkable for a smart phone (Juha Alakarhu and his team deserve raises).  Also, the screen is beautiful, the phone charges fast, I love the removable battery and microSD card, and I'm apparently one of the few who actually likes the design language and simple but elegant feel to the body of the phone itself.

But on top of the many glitches (such as auto update time zone not working), bugs (such as stuttering first few seconds of recorded video), crashes (photo app in particular), missing features (such as visual voicemail for dual sim or FM radio icon), and general lack of OS polish of Windows 10 Mobile, the invasion of privacy users must consent to in order to retain basic key smartphone features that have been around since before Windows Phone 7 is beyond absurd and, for me, outweighs any of the positives the phone has.

I refuse to consent to what sounds like essentially a keylogger (or keystroke logging malware) in order to use voice input for text messaging, maps, search, emails etc.  I don't need or want Cortana, but I've been able to use speech recognition since WP7 by only consenting to speech patterns (or maybe additionally contacts... which Microsoft has synced through my account already anyway).  But consenting to sending over every single thing typed on my keyboard to use a VOICE feature seems silly and unnecessary to me.  And yes, I'm one of the few people who complain about a flashlight app needing access to contacts, push notifications, photos, videos, call logs, phone ID, user ID, location services, blood type, full SSN, and full DNA profile to use. 

Most average users don't care or understand the implications of privacy.  Most people say "what, I have nothing to hide, so what do I care?"  But IMO, those people miss the point.  Those customers will gladly click the "recommended settings, express setup" every time without a second thought.  And many of those customers have always been on (and likely will remain on) another platform such as Android.  A few have chosen Windows Phone, and will remain loyal customers regardless of what privacy changes Microsoft makes.  Others like myself, appreciated Microsoft giving users control over what information gets sent over and when.  With Windows 10 (especially Mobile), however, not only is that control diminished, but privacy-conscious customers are now coerced into sending unnecessary information over or losing basic smartphone features that have been around long before MS took over the Nokia cellphone division.

But anyway, I'll get off the soap box now, but privacy aside, Microsoft also needs to do a better job following through with hype they're trying to generate.

Here are some of the promises (or in some cases strongly implied assertions) Microsoft has made and failed to deliver on with the unlocked Lumia 950 dual sim:

-Band 12 coverage (may be physically capable, but impossible in practice since MS has gotten no US carrier involvement to enable it)

-Wifi Calling (turns out it is actually not possible on the unlocked version of the phone even with a compatible sim card and carrier)

-VoLTE (again, may be physically capable, but impossible in practice since MS has gotten no US carrier involvement)

-Speech Recognition without turning on the "Get to know me" keylogger (see two separate FAQ's on this page here that say it should work: http://windows.microsoft.com/en-us/windows-10/speech-inking-typing-privacy-faq )

-Full Microsoft Office over Continuum (they recently announced a change to require Office 365 subscription to use Continuum)

Ultimately, Microsoft may be able to one day steal over enough Android or iPhone users with features like Continuum or Windows 10 everywhere to be considered a player again in mobile devices.  But unless they change their track on privacy settings, they'll be doing that with one fewer customer... because I'm gone.  And since Windows Phone/10Mobile is currently sitting at 1.5% worldwide or 3% US smartphone market share, you would think that every longtime fan lost would count for something.

-RS

petrib say: If you just send MP3 files to your phone using Bluetooth file transfer (OBEX), and retrieve them from the Inbox of the Messaging app, then the Messaging app setting "Memory in use" determines whether they (the messages) end up in phone memory or the memory card.
