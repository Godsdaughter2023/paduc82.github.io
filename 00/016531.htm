rohn007 say:

Office 2016 is a one-time payment of $300, but Office 365 is a you keep paying forever "subscription", $120 per year. So after 2 1/2 years they cost the same. After that Office 2016 becomes cheaper on a pure dollar basis. On this basis, the question is simple, do you plan to "upgrade" to the next version of Office immediately after it is released (normally not a good idea) or will you be more typical and wait "a while" or even skip a version altogether?

Office 365 bundles typically allow each user to install on up to 5 computers that they use at the same time.  The license terms of Office 2016 were "improved" (actually it started with 2013) so that they now only allow install on one machine at a time. So if you are a "hardware happy" company where uses have desktop computers, laptops, tablets and maybe even a smartphone the 365 license quickly becomes a more cost effective deal.

When they were released together in Sept 2015 the programs in both Office 2016 and Office 365/2016 had identical functionality.  But Office 365 includes "free" new features that are released on an irregular basis every 1 to 3 months (whenever MS feels it is ready to).  These new features will not be given to Office 2016 users until a SP1 is released (if there ever is one) or in the next full release of Office, some time in the future.  If you want an idea of the sort of changes that have been released since Sept 2015 this link is one of the places that MS lets people know what new functions have been added to 365: http://blogs.technet.com/b/office_sustained_engineering/

Office 365 bundles tend to have more applications than 2016 bundles.

Office 365 licensing is managed through an "admin" portal web site. The administrator can assign licenses to users, recover them when person leaves the company. Admin can even buy new licenses online, cutting down lead time on purchases.  Admin can also do a lot of monitoring of Office use through the "telemetry" feature.

MS has "improved" <sarcasm> the Office 2016 installation "User Experience".  You now absolutely MUST provide an email address as well as the product key during the INSTALL process.  They did this to "encourage" people to switch to Office 365. Most people providing technical support to businesses absolutely loathe this change. It makes installing very unwieldy. 

For Office 2016, you MUST manually track the product key installed on each specific machine for future re-install activation. 

In another "improvement", MS now only stores the last 5 digits of the Office 2016 product key in a way that users can retrieve (unlike 2010 and earlier where you could extract the full key) <SIGH!>.

Office 365 bundles also have several additional "free features" such as 1TB of "free" OneDrive space, and free Skype time. 

For business users, depending on your local tax laws, you may be able to charge the "rental" / subscription costs of Office 365 as a "current Expense", while the "purchase" price of Office 2016 is typically treated as a Capital cost that has to be depreciated over multiple years.

Both Office 365 and 2016 are now installed through the "Click to Run" (or maybe NOT run) virtual environment.  One of the "features" of CTR is that by default it is an all or nothing installation to the C:\ drive.  You can no longer easily do "custom" installs of just the applications you need. It is also a major PITA for users with small drives (like tablets with TINY 32GB SSD's).  There is a tool you can use to customize the installs, it just takes extra work to learn how to use it.

Being CTR installations, both Office 365 and 2016 are huge bandwidth hogs, downloading much larger chunks of code during the monthly "update" process than 2010 and earlier "local installs" did through Windows Update.

Office 365 REQUIRES connection to the internet no more than every 30 days (or so) to confirm your license is still valid (MS does not trust you). Office 2016 requires much less frequent internet connections.  This is a particular problem for workers in remote locations with intermittent or no internet access for more than a month (a military user noted this as a particular issue for them).  After the 30 days pass, Office 365 goes into read-only mode.

If you wish, I can provide you with links to more information about administering Office 365.  Frankly, for business users who take the time to learn about it and take full advantage of it, the Admin portal can be a major management tool, and provide a significant advantage over simple 2016 installs.

Bottom line, there are a lot more points to consider than simple purchase cost when you are trying to figure out the business costs of Office 365 vs Office 2016 licenses.

I am currently working on an adventure/visual novel type of game within Powerpoint.

I would like to know of a method on how to "Save" & "load" a Game point / Slide

Currently I am contemplating on just adding a "Menu" Button to the game then adding the option to save or load a previous save point but at this part I am pretty much stuck.
Searching the answer in the internet seems to have failed so I am now asking the community.
Answers using Macro are fine.

Moved from: Office / PowerPoint / Windows 10 / Office 2013

steve rindsberg say:

I think you'll want to use a feature called Tags.

Simplest form:

ActivePresentation.Tags.Add "LastStoppingPoint", "42"

This adds a tag named LastStoppingPoint to the presentation; the tag's value is 42.

To retrieve the value:

MsgBox ActivePresentation.Tags("LastStoppingPoint")

How/where you'd use these commands will depend on how you expect the user to quit the game partway through and how you want them to resume play at the last stopping point vs from the beginning.
