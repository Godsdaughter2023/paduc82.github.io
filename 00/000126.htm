julie fka profjulie say:

The only way to transfer or sync media (music, photos, and videos) to a windows phone is via the Zune desktop software.  And the only way to install updates on windows phone is by connecting it to a computer and running the Zune software. You cannot bypass the Zune desktop software. 

 

If you have music and other media files stored on an external drive, you don't have to move the files onto your computer's hard drive.  You'll need to make sure the drive letter for your external drive is static and doesn't change, but then all you need to do is identify the path and folder where these files are stored by opening up the Zune deskto software, selecting Settings > Software > Collections > select the Add Folder button on in the Music or Videos section (under Monitored folders) and then you can identify the path where the files are stored.

 

Documents (that are not media files) are transferred or synced to a windows phone by 1) first uploading the documents to the cloud (SkyDrive, Sugar Sync, Dropbox) and then downloading the file to a windows phone (SkyDrive works very well since syncing is integrated into the windows phone OS); OR 2) by emailing the documents to an email account that you've set up on the phone; OR 3) by using a third party application, like PC File Download, or ShareFolder or Tonido or Tappin (and there are plenty of other similar apps in the Marketplace) - these apps rely on an network connection and a small computer client to transfer files between a windows phone and a computer.

rob koch say:

Le Boule has just provided a commonly recommended workaround using quarantine for those paranoid individuals who don't trust that the Microsoft analysts and definitions developers know what they're doing.

The reason you can't find a simple answer to this question is that there isn't one, because the simple relationship between severity level and action that you want to find doesn't exist.  That's because the severity level is a measure of risk, while the action needs to be based upon the specifics of the particular modifications and damage done by the payload contained in the malware.  Though these are sometimes related, in many cases they are different items entirely and so must adapt based upon the specifics for that particular malware or family of malware.

The reason you don't hear millions of such questions rather than a very few are because knowledgeable people understand these differences and also that there's simply no way they can truly understand these specifics for every malware that exists.  For that reason the user is really always trusting their anti-malware developer, analysts and definitions developers to do this for them.  IF you don't you are using the wrong anti-malware or simply will never be able to use any such program effectively.

In the distant past it was relatively common for malware to attach itself to data or executable files in order to attempt to avoid detection, but these methods have mostly become ineffective today with the addition of digital signatures and other methods of fingerprinting files.  This means that in most cases, files now are either completely malware or utterly destroyed by the payload such as in the crypto ransomware attacks.

So if you don't trust those providing your antimalware application to know better than yourself when the results of a malware attack can be recovered (e.g. "cleaned") or when they should simply be deleted, the quarantine everything method is the only real workaround.  Others like myself though choose to understand that the risk of loss due to our antimalware is millions of times smaller than the risk due to the malware itself and so instead leave that decision up to the antimalware we've chosen to trust.

The other key difference for most knowledgeable people is that they also use an effective backup and re-installation system as part of their recovery plan.  This is because the risk of loss due to malware damage is typically no more common than that of a hard drive or other hardware or software failure.  For this reason, if the backup and recovery strategy is well planned, it will provide a method of recovery that works for any of these types of loss, regardless of how it occurs.

To better understand where I'm coming from, realize that in 35 years in the computer field as an administrator and a security professional, I've personally never needed to try to recover a malware damaged file myself.  In all such cases either the antimalware has performed this act for me or the entire system has required rebuilding and data recovery from backup due to damage done by the malware payload before it could be recognized and stopped (rare).

Only an effective offline backup strategy can truly protect form data loss, especially with modifications such as encryption done by ransomware.  So fretting over the actions that an antimalware might take simply shows a misunderstanding of the true risk and remediation required when such an incident occurs.

Rob
