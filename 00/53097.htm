a. user say:

tl;dr: Your digital license is not linked to an account. It's linked to the computer it activated.

In Windows 10, there is a distinction between a license and an activation.

In order to use Windows 10, a computer must be activated. Activation binds Windows 10 to a specific computer. Once a computer has been activated, it remains activated for the rest of its life, no matter who uses it. To put a finer point on it, you could leave the computer out on the sidewalk and whomever comes by and takes it will receive an activated computer.

With one exception that I'll get to in a moment, in order to activate a computer you have to buy a license for Windows 10. Having a license gives you the right to activate the computer. The license and the activation are a two step process.

To put it another way: A license is the ticket you buy to get into the museum. Activation is the hand stamp you get to show proof that you were admitted.

The one exception occurred during the first year after Windows 10 was released. Until July 29, 2016, if your computer was running Windows 7 SP1 or Windows 8.1, you still needed a license to activate Windows 10, but you didn't need to purchase that license. Instead, your license was the fact that you were running one of those operating systems. Microsoft called this a digital license, which I suppose sounds more high-tech than "free."

Whether you paid for it or you got it for free - oops, I mean 'digitally' - the license isn't associated with a person or a Microsoft account. It's like cash: As long as it's legal, it works, regardless of who owns it. The only detail to remember is that for consumers, a license can only activate one computer at a time.

So to (finally) answer your question, your digital license is not linked to an account. It's linked to the computer it activated.
