amjadtalib say: Thank you for everyone who tried to help. I appreciate the effort. I ended up figuring it out after a little bit of intuitive thinking. Now I want to share how I did it in case someone runs across the same problem and searches the web for it.

I have included a slice of my sheet for visual aid. I will try to be as clear as possible in the explanation. The slice includes rows 10-22 of my data. The range of the data is from September 20th, 1984 to October 8th, 1984. The columns I have included are A (original formatted date) O (new formatted date) P and Q. The columns in between are lengthy and unnecessary so I have cut them out. 

To obtain P was a simple matter and at first only experimental because I did not believe it would help me find the answer I was looking for. the formula for P is as follows:

=MONTH(O13)

That is for row 13. Simply replace the 13 with whichever row you want and it still works.

Moving from P to Q was not as easy and I want to try and explain the logic behind it before I write the formula. I knew that I had separated the data into months which would make it easier for me to manually select what I needed, but it was still a lot of data to deal with and I wanted excel to distinguish the period. I realized that every time the month changed, there was a difference of 1 in the P column (except from December to January but that was not an issue). =IF(ABS((P17-P16)+(P17-P18))>0,1,0)

=IF(ABS((P17-P16)+(P16-P15)+(P15-P14)+(P17-P18)+(P18-P19)+(P19-P20)+(P20-P21))>0,1,0)

The anchor point I started with was row 17 and that is where this formula is located. To extend it, just use the auto fill up and down the column. =MOD(ROW(A1:O1),7)

Gee, but that code looks familiar  l-)

   Me.DrawWidth = N

Where N is the number of pixels.  You'll probably want something between 7 and 20

You should adjust the PAPERHEIGHT constant by the number of inches, not the twips per inch:
    Const PAPERHEIGHT As Long = 7.92 * 1440
