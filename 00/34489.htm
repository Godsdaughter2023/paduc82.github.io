mem-tek say:

You mention that the computer is several years old, so this is why I recommend doing the following in order to assure that a flaky internal connection isn't the issue. Why? Because over time and due to atmospheric humidity, oxidation can build up within connectors. And because over time vibration can slightly unseat other connectors. We will focus on your computer's hard drive and CD/DVD drive connectors and on your computer's RAM sticks.

1. Power off the computer and then unplug its power cord.

2. Press the power button in order to discharge any remaining voltage in the power supply. This usually results in the power light briefly flashing.

3. Open the computer case.

DO THE FOLLOWING WITH ONE HAND ALWAYS IN CONTACT WITH THE METAL FRAME INSIDE THE COMPUTER CASE IN ORDER TO PREVENT ELECTROSTATIC DISCHARGE WHICH CAN DAMAGE WHATEVER COMPONENTS YOU TOUCH WITH YOUR OTHER HAND:

4. Locate the RAM sticks on the motherboard. Put a couple of fingers on top of each RAM stick and slightly wriggle the stick side-to-side several times with only around 1 mm of movement side-to-side. The idea is that the mild wriggling movement will break through any oxidation between the RAM and the RAM socket on the motherboard. Do this for each RAM stick.

5. Locate each CD drive, DVD drive, and hard drive. Unplug and then replug the power and SATA connectors. Note that the SATA connectors may have a small metal tab which you must press in order to unplug the connector.

6. Do the same for the other ends of the SATA cables where they plug into the motherboard.

7. Close up the computer case, plug back in the power cable, and start up the computer.

With luck, this will have fixed the computer. But you still might have to repair errors on the hard drive.

If the above doesn't resolve the issue and since your computer is several years old, the odds are good that the hard drive is failing and doesn't have much time left. The average maximum lifetime of a hard drive is around 5 years. If you can get the computer to boot up, you obviously will want to verify that the hard drive is not failing or that it is failing. To find out, you will need to download, unzip, and run the Crystal Disk Info utility. NOTE TO ALL: THE EXE VERSION OF CRYSTAL DISK INFO PROGRAM CONTAINS A POTENTIALLY UNWANTED PROGRAM (PUP). THE ZIP VERSION OF THE AUTHOR'S PROGRAM DOES NOT. THUS I PROVIDE, BELOW, A DIRECT DOWNLOAD LINK TO THE ZIP VERSION WHICH DOES NOT CONTAIN THE PUP:

http://sourceforge.jp/frs/redir.php?m=jaist&f=%2Fcrystaldiskinfo%2F61888%2FCrystalDiskInfo6_2_1.zip

Simply unzip the above download and run it. CrystalDiskInfo will report if your hard drive or hard drives are either good, or caution because the drive is failing. It is a no brainer to understand the results since the program visually indicates whether the drive is GOOD or CAUTION (failing).

If the drive is failing, then you can use the free version of Macrium Reflect to clone this failing hard drive to another new hard drive. The point of the clone is to try to preserve most or all of your data. No guarantee that the cloned drive will boot, but at least you will have a copy of most (if not all) of your data.
