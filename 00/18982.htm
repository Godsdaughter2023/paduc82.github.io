joeu2004 say: mehmetdogu wrote:
My point is;in PMT calculations all in excel videos, monthly interest rate is calculated by dividing yearly interest rate by 12. I say this is wrong.  APR is yearly rate by law and can not divided by 12 because APR is exponential growth rate over 12 months and this is not linear.

First, I don't know of any "excel videos" that are created by Microsoft [1].  So your ire might be misdirected.

Second, whether APR is a simple rate or compounded rate is indeed a matter of law -- regional law.  It varies from country to country.

Third, many people misuse the term APR when they mean annual interest rate.  The two are not the same.  The annual interest rate is always divided by payment frequency.

(All of the help pages for PMT and other Excel financial functions that I find with a microsoft.com URL refer to annual interest rate, not APR, divided by payment frequency.)

Finally, this is not a problem with PMT per se.  If you calculate the periodic interest rate correctly, PMT does indeed compute the correct periodic payment.

-----

In the US, APR is a simple rate.  See 12 CFR 226 Appendix J(B)(1) (click here), to wit:  "The annual percentage rate shall be the nominal annual percentage rate determined by multiplying the unit-period rate by the number of unit-periods in a year".

I believe that is true of Aus and NZ, as well, based on my experience in working with others in these forums and private conversations.

In the UK and other EU countries, APR is indeed a compounded rate.  So ostensibly, the monthly interest rate to be used with Excel financial functions would be (1+APR)^(1/12)-1 or RATE(12,0,-1,1+APR).

In Canada, APR for mortgages is the periodic rate compounded periodically semiannually.  ("Say what?!")  So ostensibly, the monthly interest rate is (1+APR/2)^(1/6)-1. 

(Canadians tell me that applies only to mortgages.  For other loans, Canadian APR is calculated differently.  But I do not have reliable information about the details of that calculation.)

However, in all formulas above, that presumes the APR is based on only the financed funds.  Often, that is not the case.  The APR might include other finance charges such as initial loan fees and PMI.

So the US -- and I believe the UK, Aus and NZ, for example -- also require lenders to cite an annual interest rate separate from the APR.  The annual interest rate is just applied to borrowed funds.

-----

[EDIT] PS....  I should point out that the annual interest rate, not necessarily the APR, is the true rate that periodic interest charges are based on.  If the APR is based on costs other than the borrowed funds, it is not the rate that periodic interest charges are based on.  So I refer to the APR as a "fictitious rate" -- or "theoretical rate", to be kind.

Ironically, APR is described as the "true cost" of borrowing.  That is misleading, IMHO.  It is the "true cost" in the theoretical sense of "time value of money".  But again, it is not the true rate of interest actually charged.

Moreover, it is debatable whether APR is even the "true cost" of borrowing.  But I digress....
