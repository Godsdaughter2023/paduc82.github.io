a. user say:

If your Stand By and or Hibernate buttons on the Turn Off Computer menu are missing, greyed out or inactive:

Have the options on the Turn Off Computer menu ever worked properly before?

Have you recently installed, reinstalled, updated or repaired your Windows XP?

Has anything on your system been changed that has anything to do with your video display and/or monitor?

What do you think happened since the last time the Turn Off Computer menu options worked properly?

If the buttons used to work and if you just installed Windows, reinstalled Windows, did a Repair Install, used a System Restore point (or other things along those lines), you probably need to update your video drivers as well to restore the functionality to the buttons.

If you accepted any video hardware driver updates from Microsoft (never a good idea), they may have sent you the wrong drivers.

If the video drivers on your system are not correct, those buttons often will not work.

Usually, and easy to check and correct, is the video drivers for your system that should be downloaded from your manufacturers World Wide Web site. Download the correct (current) video drivers for your graphics adapter, install the correct video drivers, reboot your system, then test.

If you can't figure out how to check if your video drivers are correct, can't figure out how to get to the manufacturers World Wide Web site, or can't figure out what do next, perhaps we can look it up for you if you provide more information.

What is your system make and model?

If you are not sure about what you have, do this:

Click Start, Run and in the box enter:

msinfo32

Click OK, and when the System Summary info appears, click Edit, Select All, Copy and then paste back here.

For video driver information, expand the Components, click Display, click Edit, Select All, Copy and then paste the information back here.

There will be some personal information (like System Name and User Name), and whatever appears to be private information to you, just delete it.

This will minimize back and forth Q&A and eliminate guesswork, trying things and assumptions.

I agree with you that the reasons given for disabling POP3/IMAP/SMTP are unconvincing. For a start, these protocols are disabled by default in Exchange Server, so 'intentionally disabled' sounds like an after-the-fact excuse. They would have to be enabled before they could be intentionally disabled. Secondly, email is inherently insecure however it's handled, except perhaps if messages are encrypted using keys only known by the sender and the recipient. Email via HTTP (i.e. OWA) is just as insecure as email via POP3/IMAP/SMTP, and both types of access can be made more secure by using SSL, so the argument just doesn't hold.

It sounds to me as if they just can't be bothered with the marginal extra effort that managing POP3/IMAP would involve and are looking for arguments to avoid it.
