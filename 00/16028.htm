The reason is that these flat file formats typically contain the data and attachments in a simple textual streamed file format, which means that the contents are directly visible to anything that examines the file.  This results in large amounts of overhead as antimalware examine the file(s) as they're written to, including the potential detection of any malware or false positives these might contain, creating a high potential for corruption of the files and thus database integrity.

All of this has been a well known issue for such flat filing systems for over 2 decades now, which is why Microsoft abandoned its Access database engine and many other software vendors did the same over the years.  All of these had exactly these same types of issues and so resulted in both the same symptoms and support issues for vendors of these products.

That's why no one does this anymore.  Except Thunderbird that is.

rob koch say:

Though anything is possible with software, the most likely interaction is with the related real-time components of another antimalware/antivirus application, since these are what most effect the operation of the operating system as a side effect.

The involvement of Thunderbird, however, is as an additional relatively rare type of low-level filing system operation that isn't commonly seen in modern software.  That's because the types of issues this could create with antimalware detection systems often added to the mix creating exactly these types of hanging or freezing symptoms.

In any case, these are typically always found to be the result of 3 different applications, at least one of them a security app, interacting in unexpected ways.  That's why they aren't necessarily predictable and will never be fully resolved, since those responsible for the different products will never be prepared to see these events in combination.

That's also why I personally believe the only way to avoid them entirely is to avoid the most commonly involved application, which unfortunately has been Thunderbird itself.  The anecdotal information contained in all of the reports of these issues as well as that displayed in the Thunderbird support documentation all supports this conclusion.

So there likely is a 3rd-wheel here, but I can't tell you specifically what that might be, since it's always specific to the system where its occurring and its own history of past software installations and removals.  Though past antimalware/antivirus are the most likely cause, anything that operates on a low level such as monitoring, debugging or even development or past damage by malicious software might be involved as well, since all of these tend to look suspicious to antimalware and so can result in undesired interactions on occasion.

The problem for those having these issues is that they always choose to focus on the antimalware application, since that's what's either reporting issues like malware within the TB databases or appears from Task Manager to be causing either large amounts of overhead or hangs.  The mistake though is to blame the real-time monitoring for doing exactly what it's been designed to do, while as your own observation here shows, the same issue doesn't occur on another system with exactly the same combination of TB and Windows Defender.
