Restart of Shut Down after BIOS changes? - Microsoft Community

skuno23 say:

Restart of Shut Down after BIOS changes?

Is it necessary to shut down the computer for all BIOS changes, or does the usual restart work at least some of the time? I saw on MS Virtual PC website to shut down the computer after a BIOS change.
dominicp say: Usually a restart is all that is needed, but you should always check the BIOS settings. BIOS update routines may reset the BIOS to defaults. A major BIOS version update may require clearing the CMOS data: sometimes the data structure is changed.

Many hardware items, including add-in cards, self-check and reload their settings (and BIOSes, if relevant) only on power-up. This does not occur on a restart since power is continuously applied to the motherboard.

If you have problems, it is best to shut off all power to the system for at least 10 seconds (usual recommendations are from 30 seconds to 2 minutes) so that the PSU and any other stored power sources are discharged.

So ideally, shut down all power for a while. Usually, this is not needed.

a. user say:

If you had a power failure while XP was running, aborted restart or ungraceful shutdown, that can cause corruption in the NT File System (NTFS).  Most of the time, running chkdsk  /r  from the XP Recovery Console can straighten that out and remedy the STOP: 0x000000ED errors.

You may need to run chkdsk  /r  several times to get it to fix the problem.  If you only ran it once, run it again and again until you might be convinced it is just not helping, but give it a chance(s).

If your HDD diagnostics reported a problem, chkdsk /r  may not be able to fix it.

Western Digital also has some HDD diagnostics you can download from here:

http://support.wdc.com/product/download.asp?lang=en

You can also read about RC commands here:

http://www.microsoft.com/resources/documentation/windows/xp/all/proddocs/en-us/recovery_console_cmds.mspx?mfr=true

If none of those things will straighten things out, you should consider your HDD might just be kaput, kick yourself in the fanny for not having an uninterruptible power supply on your system and figure out how you are going to salvage off your personal data (documents, photos, music, etc.) to some external device like a separate USB hard drive you may have to purchase and then shop for a new HDD or take your system to a shop where they can transfer as much as possible to a new HDD.

If you go into salvage mode, since you generally have limited access to folders and files from the XP Recovery Console, you need to create another kind of bootable CD like a Hiren's Boot CD.
