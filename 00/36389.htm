how do u update from usb 2 to usb 3

jongrimes say:

I have a usb 3 stick and my computer is usb 2 can i get a program or update to see the new usb 3 stick 

Hi,

USB 3.0 devices are backward compatible with USB 2.0. Therefore your USB 3.0 flash drive should work connected

to your USB 2.0 PC. (It will only work at USB 2.0 specs, but it should work.)

The only way to get USB 3.0 ports on a notebook, laptop, tablet PC is to buy a system that already has USB 3.0 ports.

Desktop PC motherboards that only come with USB 2.0 ports can usually be upgraded to USB 3.0 by simply installing 

a USB 3.0 PCI add in card to one of the motherboards available PCI slots (or buying a new Desktop motherboard

with USB 3.0 ports.)

However, if you don't mind the much slower USB 2.0 performance your USB 3.0 Flash drive will have it should still

be detected & work connected to your USB 2.0 PC. This is a hardware issue, no update or software alone can

change a USB 2.0 port into a USB 3.0 port.

Hi s73,

There are several ways to share your files and folders over a network. I agree that sharing it with "Everyone" doesn't feel right as everyone connected to the same network will be able to access. You've mentioned that you've set a Read/Write access to your Microsoft account and you can view the folder from other device but unable to access it. Have you also shared the folder from your other device and set the same permission to your Microsoft account?

Anyhow, aside from the two options you've tried, you can also share your files/folders through the Sharing Properties, the Advanced Sharing, or through the Share tab in File Explorer. Here's how:

Sharing Properties

  1. In your File Explorer, right-click the folder you want to share and click Properties.
  2. Go to the Sharing tab, and click Share... under Network File and Folder Sharing.
  3. Select your user account account and click Share.
  4. Click Done, then Close.

Advanced Sharing

  1. In your File Explorer, right-click the folder you want to share and click Properties.
  2. Go to the Sharing tab and click Advanced Sharing... button.
  3. Tick the box Share this folder. You can also adjust the number of users and edit the Share name of the folder.
  4. Click Permissions. If you can't see your account name there, then click Add, and set the permission.
  5. Click Apply, then OK.

For other options in sharing your files, please check out Share files in File Explorer.

If you have additional questions, or if the issue persists after the troubleshooting steps above, please let me know by replying to this thread. This way, I'll get notified and respond back to you as soon as I can.

cmdrkeene say:

Restore Points won't really help you anyway, they are just backups of a handful of key system and registry files. Great for a quick undo if you make a bad change, but about useless if the system can't boot up. 

The go back to previous version actually rolls back to the previous Windows 10 version (like 1511, 1607, or 1703 "Creator Update").  It's not a bad choice to try if it failed while installing a major update.

I know you don't like it, but a fresh clean install is going to be the fastest path to guaranteed victory.  Roughly 15 minutes to install, then another 30 minutes or so installing your favorite browser and software and such.  An hour, maybe two tops for a perfect install, versus spending hours/days/weeks otherwise (not to mention how much time you've already spent doing all the work you mentioned above without success).

Summary:

Recovery from Microsoft backup miserably failed as, in the end, I discovered that the backup itself was also corrupt. Details:

For the sake of clarity, my Win 10 image/system backup embodies 4 vhdx files (along with several other XML files), which were presented by Microsoft Recovery as {insert-huge-GUID-here}, C:, X:, D:.

After several attempts in using Microsoft Backup & Restore to use this Windows 10 system image which I had saved two months go, I have to draw several pretty severe conclusions: 

1) Windows 7 B&R is not capable to use/show a Windows 10 backup. Win 7 creates a .vhd file per volume, while Win 10 creates .vhdx files. Despite Windows 10 is officially out since more than 2 years, Microsoft did not manage to make Windows 7 capable to restore backups taken by Windows 10. This is sad and inconvenient but was not a blocking factor: indeed even if I couldn't use my alive Win 7 partition to attempt to restore the corrupted Win 10 partition, I could boot to the Windows 10 Recovery Partition or Windows 10 installation media, but that left much less flexibility in examining my options or what I was doing;

2) Beware, instead of using the handy volume label, MS B&R would present you the partitions to restore as drive letters which is imho pretty stupid as these drive letters depend on the partition/OS you are booting on, while the volume name remains unchanged. And yes, this volume name information is available in the backup XML files next to the backup .vdhx files ! So it's just plain laziness or stupidity, as you prefer, especially volumes never mounted as a drives with a letter and are therefore identified with a rather useless GUID. Reading the .vhdx files with a 3rd party tool (7zip) allowed me to identify that ùy GUID volume was actually the Windows Recovery partition (which has a meaningful volume name nevertheless). And that the C: was the Win 10 partition, X: was the Win 7 partition, and D: was my Data disk (another physical disk in the computer).

Of course, examining the .vhd files and comparing with Win 7 B&R would show the same drive letters but their content is not the same (OS partitions are swapped). This confusion would not exist if the volume label were used, and was cleared thanks to the fact that I had another live OS available from where I could examine the actual backup files (vhd, vhdx and associated xml's);

3) When restoring with MS Recovery, it is a kind of all-or-nothing restore. You can not choose to restore only a specific partition. It either has to be: the "minimum number of partitions for Windows to boot" or "all data". The "minimum" option would besides not let you adapt to your reality -- so  no way to restore only the Win 10 partition backup and overwrite the corrupted Win 10, despite these are all separate vhdx files ! And in my case things got even worse: as I had changed the partition size and despite there was room enough available for the restore, *all* partitions were restored from backup, including my Windows 7 one which I absolutely did not want to overwrite !

How did that happen ? Well, as I discovered afterwards, the recovery would show you the drives it is going to recover in a (non-editable) edit field, but if you don't click in it and move the cursor to the right end of its content, you'll not see the various drive letters that are appended behind the big GUID that was displayed and that unwanted partitions will be taken along So that's how I lost my Windows 7 C: partition also. And, yes, worst of all, also the Data partition on my D: drive, overwritten by the 2 months old backup !!

In summary, a pretty crappy piece of software, with no flexibility, extremely poor visibility and huge unreliability in what MS Recovery (you in the end) is actually doing while attempting to restore partitions from its own backup.
