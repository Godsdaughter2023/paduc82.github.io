Bug fix or workaround? Outlook Task status does not update in OneNote if Tasks are moved outside the default "Tasks" folder (this breaks productivity systems)

In addition, the ability to link Outlook Tasks to OneNote (and have them automatically update across both applications) is a tremendous productivity advantage for Office 365.

 

However, there is a bug in this process: when Tasks are moved outside the default "Tasks" folder, the links and automatic updates between Outlook and OneNote stop working. Any Task in Outlook's default "Tasks" folder will link and update as expected (even if it didn't originate in that folder), but any Task moved outside the default "Task" folder stops updating in OneNote.

 

I've tested this multiple times on multiple machines, and the links and updates consistently work both ways -- that is, *until* I move my Task to a different folder in Outlook. Once I do that, completions and deletions no longer sync, and OneNote can no longer find the Task in Outlook.

 

This is a big problem for individuals and enterprises that use one of any number of productivity systems that require them to file Tasks in folders (for example, David Allen's GTD methodology).

 

Just so we're on the same page, here's what I mean by "linking" Tasks between Outlook and OneNote:

- the Task appears in both applications

- I can right-click on the Task in either application and find it in the other one

- completions and deletions sync across applications

 

Here's a series of screenshots illustrating how this is broken: https://www.screencast.com/t/qevQayYnbbg

 

It seems like this could easily be fixed if the Task status code were searching an all-Tasks view rather than the default "Tasks" folder.

I've submitted this bug report on OneNote's UserVoice site. In the meantime, is there some workaround or fix for this?

hiv and how it effects dementia - Microsoft Community

annnewrv say:

hiv and how it effects dementia

I am doing a dementia course for my community care employment and would appreciate information on the above.

mattkip say:

Decline in mental processes is a common complication of HIV infection (and many other conditions).

Although the specific symptoms vary from person to person, they may be part of a single disorder known as AIDS dementia complex, or ADC. Other names for ADC are HIV-associated dementia and HIV/AIDS encephalopathy.

Common symptoms include decline in thinking, or "cognitive," functions such as memory, reasoning, judgment, concentration, and problem solving.

Other common symptoms are changes in personality and behavior, speech problems, and motor (movement) problems such as clumsiness and poor balance.

When these symptoms are severe enough to interfere with everyday activities, a diagnosis of dementia may be warranted.

AIDS dementia complex typically occurs as CD4+ count falls to less than 200 cells/microliter. It may be the first sign of AIDS. With the advent of highly active antiretroviral therapy (HAART), the frequency of ADC has declined from 30%-60% of people infected with HIV to less than 20%. HAART may not only prevent or delay the onset of AIDS dementia complex in people with HIV infection, it can also improve mental function in people who already have ADC.
