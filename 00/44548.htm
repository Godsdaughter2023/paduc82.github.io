Your ISP -- Time Warner -- correctly explained that it assigns you a "dynamic" IP address.  That means that the IP address may change from session to session (the assigned external IP address will always stay the same until you disconnect from your ISP). 

Because you're using a router, however, you will never see the IP address that Time Warner assigns to you unless you (a) look at the "Status" page of your Linksys router's configuration utility or (b) go to a website like http://www.whatismyip.com/ 

I suppose that it's possible that Norton can access this address and report it, but for most people that would be superfluous.  Unless you're running a web server or otherwise need to know how to reach your computer over the Internet (e.g., to access your home computer while traveling) there's no need to know your external IP address.

             ipconfig /all

If you see an IP address that is between 169.254.0.1 through 169.254.255.254, that is called an Automatic Private IP Address (APIPA) and is assigned by Windows to your computer's network interface card when (a) the NIC is configured to get an IP address automatically (the default) and (b) the NIC is unable to reach the DHCP server (typically in your router) that is supposed to automatically assign that address.  This almost always is an indication of a bad wireless encryption password.  This has nothing to do with a conflict with the router's IP address.

If you see anything about "Teredo Tunneling Interface" you are most likely using Vista or Windows 7 rather than XP, but whatever version of Windows you have, the "Teredo Tunneling Interface" relates to IPv6 and you don't need it and almost certainly are not using it.  The following isn't my own language, but it's accurate:

To make a long story short, it means you have IPv6 installed as part of your networking components. Check the following;

Go to Control Panel and double click Network Connections. Right click on the icon for your Local Area Connection and select Properties from the menu.

On the General page of the properties sheet there's a box which should contain an entry for Microsoft TCP/IP version 6.

I won't bore you with the details but the bottom line is that most people have no need at this time for IPv6. That said, it won't lead to problems if you leave it installed on your computer. That said, uninstalling IPv6 won't cause you to lose your internet connection. The entry you see for Internet Protocol (TCP/IP) is the important one.
