Downsides to peer-to-peer networking

Your computer can only connect to one wireless network at a time. When it is connected to your printer, it cannot also connect to a wireless router (for Internet access, for example).

The connection is not very secure. By default there is no wireless security. You can enable WEP (Wireless Encryption Protocol) security on your peer-to-peer network, but WEP is not very secure.

You may need to manually connect to your printer's wireless signal every time you turn on your computer.

 

If your printer uses any Web Services (such as PrintApps or ePrint) these services will not work with a peer-to-peer connection. To enable these services, your printer must be connected to the Internet through a wireless router (called an "infrastructure network").

 

Overall, connecting your printer to an infrastructure network (with a wireless router) is preferred over a peer-to-peer connection. It is probably more reliable, certainly more secure and allows your computer to be connected to the Internet while simultaneously being connected to your printer.

lemp say:
Please tell me what I will need to set up an "infrastructure network”, if you believe this will resolve my issue.
You need to set up the printer to connect to the mifi rather than to your laptop.  Exactly how you do that depends on both the printer and the mifi, but essentially, you configure the printer to use the Network Name (SSID) of the mifi device.

Some wireless printers default to connecting to an "ad hoc" network with a name like "HPSetupxxx" or the like.  You then look in your laptop for that name and connect.  Instead, you have to go into the printer's configuration (either through its front panel controls or by connecting temporarily with a USB cable) and configure it to connect to whatever name your mifi uses (like "mifinnnnxxxx").

Your mifi is probably configured by default to automatically assign IP addresses to devices that connect to it.  Although this will work OK, you run the risk -- especially if you connect other devices to the mifi while the printer is turned off -- of suddenly becoming unable to print (because your computer is looking for the printer at an old IP address but the printer has been assigned a new IP address by the mifi).  The proper way to deal with this is to configure the printer to use a fixed or "static" IP address.

For specific directions, please provide the particular model MiFi device AND the model of your HP printer.  Note that HP has very many similar model designations, so be sure to indicate whether it's an "OfficeJet", "DeskJet", "Photosmart" or other name, in addition to the specific model number.

Port Forwarding

Login Information

  • Gateway - 192.168.2.1
  • Username - N/A (None)
  • Password - N/A (None)

Port Forwarding

  1. Open a web browser.
  2. In the address bar at the top, type in 192.168.2.1
  3. Click Login in the upper right.
  4. Input the password and click Login (by default, the password should be left blank).
  5. Select VIrtual Servers from the menu on the left under the Firewall section.
  6. On the first available row check the box for Enable.
  7. Under Description, enter Xbox1.
  8. Under Inbound Port, enter 3074 for both fields.
  9. Under Type, select BOTH.
  10. Under Private IP Address, enter the IP Address of the Xbox.
  11. Under Private Port, enter in 3074 for both fields.
  12. Select Apply Changes at the top of the page.

Disable Port Forwarding

  1. Open a web browser.
  2. In the address bar at the top, type in 192.168.2.1
  3. Click Login in the upper right.
  4. Input the password and click Login (by default, the password should be left blank).
  5. Select VIrtual Servers from the menu on the left under the Firewall section.
  6. Uncheck Enable on the row labeled Xbox1.
  7. Select Apply Changes at the top of the page.

halo3productions say:

Although the directions are correct, this doesn't work. I even added the additional ports recommended (although redundant).

Your best bet is to follow these instructions, then put your Xbox in a DMZ. Putting anything in a DMZ is risky, but I don't think a gaming console (like the Xbox) will be at risk. However, enabling UPnP is just as risky (putting your entire network at risk, rather than a single device). For any router, ALWAYS try port forwarding before resorting to UPnP (Universal Plug and Play). It is BIG security risk to enable UPnP. There is no reason to forward ALL ports to EVERY device on your network. Only forward the needed ports.

To put your Xbox in a DMZ: Click DMZ on the left side, under Firewall > Put in the address of your Xbox (it only allows to add the last octet) > Apply changes.

Also, these instructions will be a bit different if you have the latest firmware. For step 8, it says "... enter 3074 for both fields". I don't know what "both" means, as there's only one field. (There's just a single Inbound and Private field.) For step 9, it says "TCP&UDP" as opposed to "BOTH". The firmware I'm on is 5.01.14 (Oct 13 2009), which seems newer than the one listed on Belkin's site.
