kosh vorlon say:

Hi DebiMon,

 

While I'm certain Mike's reply will be far more useful, let me address your actual question and please pardon me if it seems a bit too direct - I'm simply trying to make the point.

 

Why does Microsoft keep changing (Windows and virtually every other product)?  Because if they didn't you'd still be using DOS 5.0 (or one of the hideous releases before that) and wouldn't have even seen Windows 3.1 (or 3.0 or other bad initial tries) at all.  But actually, you wouldn't really be doing that because Microsoft would have been out of business for decades and you'd be using Apple or Linus/Unix or something else that stepped up had Microsoft disappeared.

 

Each change is intented to be an improvement over what existed before.  Sometimes that works on the first try, sometimes it takes a few upgrades to get a particular version to work, and some versions were so bad that they lasted for a very short time before being replaced and many don't even recall they existed.  And that's just the Operating System. It also applies to all the other products as well.  No, the bulls-eye isn't hit every time, but they listen and adapt.  That will occur here as well. 

 

Though I often use Vista Business 32-bit SP2, do you recall all the hoopla surrounding the initial release of Vista - basically prior to SP1 but especially before the initial series of updates - and some might even say until the release of SP2?  I do and it seems to me it was far more negative than with W8 (and IMHO for good reason too - but now I love it and it's virtually a totally different OS now than it was at first).

 

But to take it further than Operating Systems and Microsoft, the same occurs all over the world in just about every imaginable industry (if not all of them).  How many people would drive a Model-T Ford today (and would Ford be a name in history for many decades if they had never released new models every year)?  I suppose some new car models are thought to be not as good as older discontinued ones.  I personally dislike that Ford stopped making the LTD Crown Victoria Police Interceptor (the model many/most police forces used for years) and used mine until it died before getting something tolerable but not what I really wanted.  Now I'm OK with what I have, but I miss my Interceptor.  But they had to change (market demand, hideous miles/gallon, the general move from large sedans in the entire car industry as people who once got them instead started getting SUVs for various reasons).  I didn't like it (and still don't), but if they hadn't done it they'd all be facing bankruptcy.  They must adapt, listen to the statistics, incorporate new technologies, stay in compliance with new emission control and other standards, and remain profitable by offering models more/most people will want (or at least what they think will be the case - some models don't last very long while others last decades and I'll bet the people who dreamed up both types are paid the same amount - though those with the winners may now be in offices instead of cubicles).

 

I could go on and on with radios, televisions, telephones (let alone cellular phones), refrigerators, televisions, stereo equipment (how many LP records do you still have but have to search high and low for a replacement cartridge for your turntable - and I've read articles that the old LPs if in pristine shape and using a great turntable actually can produce better quality sound than cassette tapes or 8-tracks {if you can find any} or even CDs).  But for various reasons, things changed and we all adjusted to these and the million other changes. 

 

Do you mow your lawn with a manual push mower or a power mover if not a lawn tractor?  As each was introduced, some were garbage that hit the junkyard and people went back to what they'd been using or something similar.  Each new lawn tractor isn't always better than prior models (or are in some ways but not in all ways).  John Deere once was king of the mountain there - but then they began producing cheaper versions for sale at Home Depot which were NOT the same as those sold at the John Deere dealerships but the name sucked people in only to discover that quality difference a couple of years down the road (if that long) and often when getting it repaired at the John Deere service center where the technicians shook their heads in sympathy that you bought "one of those" instead of a "real" John Deere tractor.  But in then end, John Deere ended up selling many more tractors, so the idea was profitable even if just a tad deceptive to the public who relied on the name rather than reading the 80-page specifications manual and assumed the lower price was due to where it was bought rather than getting something intentionally of lower quality to permit that price differential.

 

I suppose the point is that progress is unavoidable and inevitable everywhere.  Some is an immediate improvement.  Some seem like a step backwards and in those cases, feedback brings them more into line with public expectations, or they "force" the public to adapt to the new situation, or they make such a blunder that either they change or go out of business.

 

There are parts of W8 I like and parts I don't like (as with all changes to everything I've ever bought).  You were given some workarounds to bring back some of the look and feel of W7, but some things cannot be changed and we either keep W7 (or Vista or XP) until it dies on us or deal with it, try to submit suggestions (which sometimes but often don't seem to matter), or learn to live with it and adapt to the change as well as we can.  I suspect part of the problem here is the amount of change all at once along with some significant limitations (in spite of the improvements) and the difficulty turning back the clock.  Soon getting a W7 computer will not be possible and even finding W7 software (unless already owned) will become just as hard as finding Vista or XP is now.  And in time, new software will be released that ONLY works on W8 or newer computers and we'll all be forced into it.

 

I hear you, but try to put it all in perspective and realize that nobody intentionally releases something they think and know is inferior - but do sometimes underestimate worldwide reactions or the value placed on tiny things they considered of no value that are deal-breakers for more people than they imagined.

 

I hope this helps in terms of understanding the philosophy behind this change (and changes in general).

 

Good luck and best wishes!

Shrink C: drive through command prompt

===========================

C:

CD %windir%\system32

diskpart

list volume

 

Note: After running the "list volume" command, it will show all partition.

 

select volume <volume number>

 

Note: Please replace the "<volume number>" to the volume number of drive C. For example, if the volume number of drive C is 2, we need run the command "select volume 2"

 

shrink desired=<N>

 

Note: Please replace the "<N>" to the disk space you would like to shrink. After run the command, it will shrink <N> MB space on drive C. (1GB=1024MB)

 

After the process finish the command, please restart the computer and check whether we have shrunk the drive C successfully.


If problem persist Shrink C: drive in Safe Mode

======================

1. Restart your computer and start pressing the F8 key on your keyboard. On a computer that is configured for booting to multiple operating systems, you can press the F8 key when you see the boot menu.

2. When the Windows Advanced Options menu appears, select Safe Mode, and then press Enter.

3. Log onto Windows by using the Administrator account or any user account with the Administrator privileges.

 After booting the system into Safe Mode, please try to shrink C: again in Safe Mode.

greg shapiro say:
Hi Harshal,
There is a Skype Number and Skype to Go.

A Skype Number is a phone number that you pay monthly for. People can call you from their mobile or landline and you pick the call up in Skype. A Skype Number is great option if you, or your friends and family live in different countries, or plan to travel abroad and want an affordable way to keep in touch.

Skype to Go is a pay-as-you-go option that allows you to call people all over the world for the price of a local call by giving you a local number to call them on. Skype to Go is a great option if you are abroad and want to avoid international calling charges when calling your friends and family.

How is a Skype Number different from Skype to Go?
A Skype Number allows your friends and family to call you using a number that you pay monthly for and you pick the call up in Skype (on Android app or web app).

Skype to Go allows you to make outbound calls to your friends and family abroad by giving you a local number to call them on. You can call them directly from Skype or from any landline or mobile phone.

The call quality is good, but it depends on an internet connection because a poor internet connection can cause dropped calls, delays, and poor quality audio and video.

Hi Harshal,

I will try to explain it here. You will need Skype to Go, for example, when you are abroad. Say you live in Boston, USA and you want to use Skype To Go to call a friend who lives in London. You get an international call to UK for the price of a local call and a little Skype Credit or a subscription.

With a Skype Number people can call you from mobiles or landlines and you can pick up the call in Skype. It’s perfect if you have friends, family or colleagues who don’t use Skype. You should be online to receive a call on Skype (for both Skype to Skype calls, or when your friend calls you using a Skype number).

If you want to make domestic calls, I would advise you to use a subscription with unlimited calls. You can have both a subscription and a Skype Number at the same time.
