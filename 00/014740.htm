My feedback on Outlook.com - Microsoft Community

yellow badger say:

My feedback on Outlook.com

I switched from Outlook to Gmail years ago because Gmail was clearly better. Recently, however, Gmail has started to go downhill, so I'm looking for an alternative. I was excited when I heard about Outlook.com. Today, I created an account and explored a bit. I'm sad to say Outlook.com is not an improvement on Gmail. Here are my observations:

1) It seems a little buggy. For instance, I can't use the cursor to highlight text in the search box or other text boxes. Also, there's an obvious bug in screen composition where a menu bar clearly overlaps with a scroll bar. Furthermore, Outlook.com seems not to understand how tall my screen is; it is composing my inbox for a much taller screen and giving me a vertical scrollbar; I have to scroll down to see "Page 1", "Go to", etc. Along those same lines, sometimes when I'm looking at messages, I get a vertical scroll bar even though there is nothing to scroll down to. (I'm using the latest version of Firefox with no fancy settings. If these features work properly for anyone, they should work for me.)

2) Navigation is clunky. When you're reading a message, you use the X symbol to get back to the message list, but there is no X symbol when you're composing a message; instead you must use menu commands. When you're in Options, you navigate with breadcrumbs. And to get between mail and contacts, you use a fancy drop-down control. This mix of navigation options is eclectic and confusing. As a first time user, it really stood out as a quirk of the program. Also, when you go to Options, the breadcrumbs lead you back to "Inbox". If you're in the "Sent" folder and you click on "More mail settings", the breadcrumbs say "Inbox > Options". That's just sloppy.

3) The search feature doesn't seem to work. I just did a search on "guacamole". The search results show one message which does, in fact, contain the word "guacamole", but it also shows the welcome message which I'm pretty sure does not contain that word anywhere.

4) Outlook.com doesn't use the screen space efficiently. For instance, there is a big silhouette icon taking up space next to every message. Presumably, pictures of people could go there, but there are no pictures now, and all I see is wasted space. When replying to messages, "Attach files" uses a surprising amount of space. And why is the entire left quarter of the screen needed for addressing?

5) My biggest complaint is the design choices that went into the GUI. Outlook.com seems to be following a design trend that has been popular lately. Among the design elements of this trend are symbols instead of words on buttons, buttons that appear only when contextually required, little or no visual distinction between sections of the page, functional controls that remain hidden until you hover the cursor somewhere, and lots of stuff that pops up when you hover over certain places. I've often heard this design style called "clean" and "modern". I have never heard it called "functional" or "efficient". That's because it is not functional or efficient. In fact, it is frustrating, and one of these days designers are going to have an "emperor has no clothes" moment and realize how misguided these design choices are. Here are just two examples from Outlook.com (I could have included many more): A) When the cursor strays into a message header, a flag symbol goes from being very light gray to a darker gray. This has the effect of being a very distracting alert. Outlook.com is capturing my attention again and again, just to remind me I can do whatever that flag does. B) When composing a message, there is no border between the message text and the surrounding area. Therefore, if you've scrolled down so the first line of your message is not visible, it is very easy to make the mistake of thinking that the first visible line is the first line of your message. If I used a system like that, I know I would sooner or later make the mistake of sending a message with a few lines of garbage above my carefully edited text.

On a more subjective note, I get the impression that Outlook.com is not trying to be the best email application out there. It doesn't have any innovative features that would make it clearly better than Gmail. The reason Gmail succeeded at stealing so many users from Outlook is because it was truly innovative in its heyday, adding great new features people wanted and used. Now Gmail has lost its mojo, and there's an innovation vacuum in the email field. From the look of things, I don't think Outlook.com will be the one to fill that vacuum.
