karissaa say:

Excel adding random decimal points?

My husband is having issues.  He is typing the following information into the cells given.

A4     =Sum(A1:A3)

Why is Excel adding these decimal points?  I expanded the typed numbers, and they are nothing but zeros.  He is trying to do a conditional format based on the answer being "0", but it isn't working b/c of the decimal points.

lars-Åke aspelin say:

My husband is having issues.  He is typing the following information into the cells given.

 

A1     176,936.10

A2     -176,364,54

A3     -571.56

A4     =Sum(A1:A3)

 

 

The problem is that the answer in A4 is coming out to  

  $    (0.000000000002273736754432320000000000000)

 

Why is Excel adding these decimal points?  I expanded the typed numbers, and they are nothing but zeros.  He is trying to do a conditional format based on the answer being "0", but it isn't working b/c of the decimal points.

This is because Excel can not store most decimal numbers exactly. They are rounded.

When displayed the numbers are limited to around 15 significant digits. That's why you can't see that they are not stored exactly.

But when you do calculations using decimal numbers, the rounded values are used and if the result, as in your case, is close to zero you can see those decimals.

One way of overcoming this limitation is to use the ROUND() function to round your results to two decimals.

Then you can use the conditional formatting on the result as you have planned.

Here you can read more about this rounding issue:

http://support.microsoft.com/kb/214118

Hope this helps / Lars-Åke

joeu2004 say: Jeeped wrote:
Assuming that the second number is supposed to be 176364<point> 54 and not 176364<comma> 54, there is no reason these should be behaving in this manner if the entries were typed in and not the result of fomulas and functions.

That is incorrect.  Lars-Ake's explanation is "right on".

As Lars-Ake explains, most numbers with decimal fractions cannot be represented exactly due to the way that Excel stores numbers and performs arithmetic, namely 64-bit binary floating-point.  This often results in infinitesimal "errors" (artifacts, not defects) in arithmetic results.

In this case, 176936.10 is represented exactly as 176936.100000000,00582076609134674072265625, and -176364.54 is exactly -176364.540000000,00814907252788543701171875.  Their sum is exactly 571.559999999997,6716935634613037109375.

But the constant -571.56 is exactly -571.559999999999,94543031789362430572509765625.  Note the slight difference.  Consequently, their sum is -0.00000000000227373675443232,059478759765625 instead of zero.

This problem is very common.  For example, IF(10.1-10=0.1,TRUE) returns FALSE!

As both you and Lars-Ake note, because this problem is so common, it is prudent to usually explicitly round arithmetic to the desired precision rather than simply relying on formatting to alter the displayed value.

Alternatively, some people might suggest setting the Precision As Displayed (PAD) calculation option.  But I deprecate that option for several reasons.  For one thing, it does not solve the problem of 10.1-10=0.1 being FALSE.  But more significantly, PAD can change the value of constants irreversibly and undesirably if you are not careful.  If you want to experiment with PAD, be sure to make a back-up copy of the Excel file first.

The ribbon contains a number of basic styles (Normal, Heading 1, Heading 2 etc) plus a bunch of silly things like strong emphasis, intense emphasis, intense quote, subtle reference and so on--so many that the styles I want to use are often out of sight.
Is there any way to remove these unnecessary styles, and have the ribbon display only the ones I am using? I have created a separate  floating set of preferred styles, but like to have two sets up for convenience.
Also, is there any way to re-order the styles in the ribbon? That way at least I could get the wanted styles in the first line!
john mcghie say:
You "can" remove the rubbish styles from the Ribbon: right-click each one in the Quick Style area and choose "Remove".  It's painstakingly slow, but it works.

Similarly, you can re-order them by sending your Normal.dotm template to a PC, where you can set a "Precedence" between 001 and 999 for each style.  Your numbers do not have to be unique.

When you bring that template back to your Mac, the order you set will hold.

Regrettably, the Styles Management mechanism was left out of Word for Mac.

Like Jim, I use a Custom, but I use a toolbar that contains all the styles I actually "use" (there's only about 20 of them).  That also enables me to use a macro to apply each style, which in turn enables me to ensure that the style is applied to the entire paragraph as a Paragraph Style, instead of having to sort out this "Char Char Char" bug partially-applied styles madness.

Cheers
