Tips to avoid installing malware on your PC - Microsoft Community

bb7548 say:

Tips to avoid installing malware on your PC

Note: I am not a Microsoft employee nor affiliated with Microsoft in any way.

Take caution when downloading EXE files

You know the games on your PC? The apps you commonly use like Office for example? The Windows installation itself when you first set up your PC? Yep, as you might have assumed, these all consist of EXE files. EXE files, otherwise known as Windows Executable Files, are commonly used by developers when creating software for Windows. Every version of Windows, even old ones or new ones support EXE files.

Sadly, some EXE files may be designed to perform dangerous actions on your PC without you authorising it, your consent or you even knowing at all! These might not only be designed to harm your PC, they might also be designed to infect other PC's as well such as the new WannaCry virus for example. These can be designed to do lots of things, such as keylogging everything you type so that if you type in your password or anything onto a website, another person is now aware of what you have typed and may attempt to hijack your account or as severe as locking you out of your own PC or your files with no hope of retrieving them without a professional. Today, I'll show you some tips of how to avoid them.

Check if the file has a verified publisher associated with it

         The examples above are what the UAC prompts look like on Windows 10. Windows 8.1, 8, 7 and Vista prompts will look very similar.

When installing an app, if a User Account Control prompt appears, don't instantly dismiss it by clicking Yes. Review the dialog entirely. If the prompt shows that a verified publisher has signed the app, then it will be more trustworthy. If the prompt states that the publisher is unknown, the publisher may be trying to hide their identity to make it more difficult for people or even police to identify the person behind the app or the person who made it didn't sign it. In this case, I will advise you to scan the app for viruses prior to opening it at all.

Install an antivirus

An antivirus is an app you install on your PC that removes malware and potentially unwanted apps from your PC. Windows has Windows Defender built-in as an antivirus, however, it is recommended that you install a third-party one. There are free and paid options out there. A good free example is Avast and a good paid option is Trend Micro or Malware Bytes. These paid options may also provide a free trial. Please note that there may be some viruses that tell you that they are an antivirus to fool you into downloading them and infecting your PC. To prevent this, only download from trustworthy sources such as well-known corporations.

Download from trustworthy sources

Some software downloading websites such as Softonic and download.com aren't trustworthy as they have been excessively abused by malware creators to the point where it is not trust able anymore. 

Don't allow scammers to download apps on your PC

Phone scammers will request remote access to your PC and when they do, they may download some apps onto your PC. These may be malware and if they do so, revoke their access immediately through the remote access client they are using.

Don't click on ads

Some ads may state that you have outdated drivers or there is an issue with your PC or one of your apps. Avoid clicking on these ads as they are usually malware.

Thanks for taking your time to read this.

Several persons have written in about unauthorized "ghost" devices that appear on their networks and the difficulty in removing them. First, I suggest that one disable the UPnP function on the router. Second, disable the WPS button on the router. Third, enable MAC authentication on the router and list the MAC addresses for authorized network devices. And fourth, change the password on the router.

The next step is to prevent malware from being installed on one's computers. Through trial and error, I have compiled a list of websites that contain or which I suspect contain malware, many of which are associated with adware. To do this I visit IP sites wherein malware is often attempted to be introduced. Then, I note what websites are attempting to connect. Then, I search the internet for those sites to see who owns the domains, or if I can even connect to those sites. I also search for feedback on those sites from other internet users. Finally, I enable website blocking on the router for all known and suspicious sites for all LAN devices.

Enable DATA EXECUTION PREVENTION (DEP) on your computer. And install a reputable antivirus program and scan often and at regularly scheduled times.
