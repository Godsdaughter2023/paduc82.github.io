peter janney say:

I found a way to eliminate "ghost email" from the Inbox in Outlook 2016 for Mac.  Here are the steps:

(1) Create a new folder and give it a name.

(2) move the contents of your Inbox into the new folder (select 'Message' then 'Move')

(3) At this point your Inbox will be totally empty and it should not be showing any emails whatsoever, thereby having eliminated the ghost email phenomenon.

(4) your new folder created for this task should be showing all messages as having been 'read.' If not, go to the 'Organize' tab and select 'Mark All as Read.'

(5) You can then move back the contents of the new folder you created for this task into your Inbox.  Your Inbox should then be showing no 'ghost' emails.

    It worked for me!

Several add-ons come pre-installed in Internet Explorer, but many have to be downloaded from the Internet. Add-ons usually require that you give your permission before they're installed on your computer. However, some might be installed without your knowledge. ActiveX controls are small programs, sometimes called add-ons that are used on the Internet. They can enhance your browsing experience by allowing animation or they can help with tasks such as installing security updates at Microsoft Update.

     

    ActiveX controls and web browser add-ons are small programs that are used extensively on the Internet. They can make browsing more enjoyable by providing toolbars, stock tickers, video, animated content, and more. These programs can malfunction, however, or give you content you don't want.     Hi David, The person who originally asked the question was on Word 2011 for Mac, so here are instructions for that version:

    You'll need to use the Developer tab in Word. If it isn't already visible, please follow these steps:

    1. In Word, choose Word>Preferences>Ribbon.
    2. Scroll down the list of tabs until you find Developer, then check it off. OK out.

    The Developer tab is added to the Ribbon. Now you can add Form Fields:

    1. Click on the Developer tab.
    2. In the section called Form Controls, you'll see buttons for Text Box, Combo Box, Insert Frame and Check Box. 
    3. Click on the type on control you would like to insert. After you insert it, the Options button is enabled, allowing you to set the parameters for the selected control.

    Exploring the Options for a control reveals that a Text Box can be configured to be a number, date, time or calculating field, besides just plain text. To add items to a Combo Box list, enter text in the Drop-down item field, then click the Add button. All form fields can have macros attached to them, so that entering and/or leaving the field can run VBA code.

    Insert Frame places a frame in your document. Frames are similar to text boxes, but they can include form fields. You don't need frames often, but they are useful where controls need to float out of the main page layout.

    After all the content controls are replaced in a form, the final step to activate them is to protect the file. Choose Tools>Protect Document, click on the Forms option and OK out. I don't recommend using password for most uses. The text areas become ineditable and you can tab through the fields, entering data.

    To edit the document, choose Tools>Unprotect Document and the form becomes a regular editable Word file again.

    A form can have protected and unprotected sections. Perhaps you want to include a free text area below the form. Use Insert>Break>Section Break (Continuous) to create a break at the boundary between the locked and unlocked areas. Then, when you use Tools>Protect Document, beside the Forms option, click on the Sections button. put a check mark beside the section with the form fields in it and uncheck the other section. OK out and the locked section is ineditable excepts for fields, while the unlocked section allows normal text entry and most formatting commands.
