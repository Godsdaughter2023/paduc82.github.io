marshall barton say:

I understood that.  The difficulty is in using /3600, which makes the result a floating point number with all the associated imprecision issues.  It can be the case where the time diff /3600 could be 8.625000000001 so Round takes it to 8.63  or on the other side it could be 8.6349999999999 and round takes it to 8.63.  Unformatted, the former value will probably display as 8.625 and the latter as 8.635.

I think the core issue is that both floating point numbers and the Round function are not precise, so nit picking about a .01 discrepency in the expected result is a waste of time.

I'm sure that your question arose deep into some bigger picture that may make this problem more important than what you've posted so far.  In most cases, the task is to display the individual calculated values and their total, but when a bored accounting kind of human manually adds up the values and it does not exactly agree with the total, they get all bent out of shape. In this situation, the best I can reccommend is to total the rounded values, eg. Sum(Round(test1, 2))   The result will be a little less accruate than Round(Sum(test1), 2), but who will notice.  After all. measuring something to the second does not take the stop watch operator's reflexes into consideration.

You may (or may not) prefer the result of using that alternate time difference expression I posted earlier.

jay freedman say:

I expect you know that you can click the Linear item on the Equation Tools tab or in the dropdown from the little arrow at the right end of the equation to display the r² as r^2.

There is no tool to convert characters such as π or various operators to their Math AutoCorrect equivalents such as \pi.

If a feature like that were to be created, there are a couple of problems it would have to solve:

  • More than one AutoCorrect item can be defined to insert the same character(s) in the equation. As an example, you can go into the Math AutoCorrect dialog and define an entry to convert \apple into π. (Pun intended.) Now, how would a reverse conversion decide which entry was originally used to create the final equation?
  • It's possible to assign an AutoCorrect item to a large, complex expression. I think the only one that's present by default is \quadratic, but much bigger entries are no problem at all. Now, how would a reverse conversion recognize how much of the final equation to replace?

mac_austin say:

iphone update

I updated Skype on my iphone and now it seems to have created a new identity (using the same email address) and I can't seem to get connect with the contacts and chats that I have on my mac laptop. Can someone tell me what's going on and how to fix it?

danida_u say:

Hi

Skype contacts are saved in Skype's servers and are tied on your account. They will not be removed from your account even if you update your application unless you remove them yourself. In addition to that, the last 30 days of your conversations are saved in the cloud which you can access across any supported platform as long as you sign in to the correct account.

One possible reason why you cannot see your contacts and conversations might be because you are logged into a different Skype account. Although Skype accounts were migrated to basic Microsoft accounts, users with legacy sign-ins (Skype Name-created accounts) still need to fully update their accounts to enjoy the benefits of a full Microsoft account. What might have happened is you signed in to Skype using the associated email address of your Skype account without updating them yet, resulting to a newly-created Skype account.

Please make sure to sign in to your account using the credentials you used to initially create your account. Sign in using your Skype Name if you created your account with a Skype Name, same goes if the account is created using an email address or a phone number.

For additional information, you may refer to this support link. Why are my Skype contacts or credit missing?

Hope this information helps! Let us know if you need additional assistance.

Thank you.
