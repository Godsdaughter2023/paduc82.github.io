Word grammar check suggests wrong word - Microsoft Community

nicoles117 say:

Word grammar check suggests wrong word

I regularly type "please advise" in emails.

Word/Outlook wants to correct it to "please advice."

I know which is correct, so I can ignore the suggestion. But, I'd like to teach Word the difference so it stops suggesting the wrong word.

Is there a way to do this, other than turning off the "frequently confused" grammar check option?

TIA,

Nicole

jay freedman say:

You can't "teach Word the difference" for a specific word or phrase; the grammar checker doesn't allow you to add entries or exceptions the way the spelling checker does. However, there is a workaround, which I'll get to in a minute.

You'll have to do this separately in Word and in Outlook, because the two programs don't share the same default template -- Word uses Normal.dotm, and Outlook uses NormalEmail.dotm, and there's no mechanism for communicating between them.

The following steps are for Word; after that I'll describe the Outlook steps.

  • In any document, such as a new blank document, type the words "please advise" (without the quotes).
  • Select those words.
  • Click Review > Language > Set Proofing Language.
  • Ensure that your preferred language is selected in the top of the dialog. At the bottom, check the box for "Do not check spelling and grammar". (We also recommend that you uncheck the "Detect language automatically" box, because Word is usually not good at guessing language, and causes trouble when it guesses wrong.) Click OK.
  • Leaving the two words still selected, click File > Options > Proofing > AutoCorrect Options. In the center of the dialog, the two words will appear in the With box.
  • Just above that, click the Formatted Text option button. The "formatted" option causes the "do not check" option to be included in the AutoCorrect entry. It will also cause the entry to be saved in the Normal.dotm template rather than making it available to all Office programs, unfortunately.
  • Click in the Replace box and enter an alias or trigger for the AutoCorrect -- something that, when you type it in a document and press the spacebar or some punctuation, will be replaced by the two words. You want something that won't be typed ordinarily; I recommend using a backslash as the first character, as in \pa.
  • Click OK in each dialog.
  • You can close the document without saving it.
  • When you exit Word, if it asks to save changes to Normal.dotm, say Yes.

For Outlook, the AutoCorrect dialog is the same as in Word, but getting to it is different:

  • Click Home > New Email to open the message editor.
  • Type the words "please advise" (without the quotes) in the body of the email.
  • Select those words.
  • Click Review > Language > Set Proofing Language.
  • As in Word, select the correct language and check the "Do not check spelling and grammar" box. Click OK.
  • Leaving the two words still selected, click File > Options > Mail > Spelling and AutoCorrect. The Editor Options dialog opens, with Proofing selected on the left. Click AutoCorrect Options.

The rest of the steps, once you're in the AutoCorrect Options dialog, are the same as for Word. You can close the email message without saving/sending.

After this one-time setup, type the alias to insert the words with the "do not check" option already applied to it. Because you know it's spelled correctly, you don't need to have its spelling checked; and the grammar checker won't bother you about that phrase any longer.

Hey Jmoma,

 

Let’s check the location of your Favorites to see if it may be set incorrectly. You can do this by:

(These steps may not work depending on your Operating System. If you get to the step that you click on the Location tab and you don’t have it just skip the next two listed instructions and continue from there)

·         Click Start

·         Click Computer

·         In the address bar, paste this location: %userprofile%

·         Press Enter on your keyboard

·         Make sure you have a Favorites folder listed in the %userprofile% folder

·         If there isn’t a Favorites folder listed, create a new folder and name it Favorites

·         Right click Favorites

·         Click Properties

·         Click the Location tab

·         Click Restore Default

·         Click OK

 

If that doesn’t help, you can edit the registry to direct the Shell:Favorites location to the location of your Favorites folder. You can do this by:

 

Modifying REGISTRY settings incorrectly can cause serious problems that may prevent your computer from booting properly. Microsoft cannot guarantee that any problems resulting from the configuring of REGISTRY settings can be solved. Modifications of these settings are at your own risk.

 

·         Press Windows key + R on your keyboard to open the run dialog

·         In the Open: field, type regedit

·         Click OK

·         Click Yes on the User Account Control (if prompted)

·         Navigate to HKEY_CURRENT_USER\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Explorer\Shell Folders

·         Right click Favorites

·         Click Modify…

·         In the Value data: field, paste %userprofile%\Favorites

·         Click OK

·         Exit Registry Editor

 

If that doesn’t help, we’ll need these bits of information to help further:

·         Windows version (XP, Vista or 7) (click Start, right click Computer/My Computer, and click Properties)

·         Service packs that have been applied (may not be applicable) (instructions are same as above)

·         Security software (antivirus, firewall, etc.)
