cbarnhorst say:

The 2025 date is not operative.  It was pro forma to set the end of extended support ten years following the date of first public availability.  The exact date was always the second Tuesday of the first month of the quarter following the fortieth full quarter of support.  The quarter of the release (the quarter which includes the first date of public availability) was not counted. 

But in the case of Windows 10, that policy was superseded.  The support policy changed within a few months after the 2015 initial release when MSFT redefined Windows as a service instead of a product. 

Support is now based on Windows 10 versions, not Windows 10 as a product.  Each version is guaranteed 18 months. 

However, there are three support channels.  Enterprises have options ranging up to ten years for embedded versions.  This is to accommodate installation of Windows in devices that do not need to be updated like consumer devices do, such as ATM machines, security cameras, kiosks and other devices running an embedded version of Windows.  Support depends on how Windows was delivered to you (the channel) and how you are contracted to use it.

In any case, the 5+5 plan inaugurated with Windows XP is gone.  There is no longer any five years of mainstream support followed by five years of extended support.  Of course, Windows 7 and Windows 8.1 remain on the 5+5 plan and will be retired on the predictable dates. 

Not so with Windows 10.
