jeeped say:
... all the US phone number to have the same format, US number w/ extensions, and International.

That's going to be virtually impossible as different international phone numbers can contain the same number of digits but are expected to be formatted into different groups. You would need a cross-reference of country codes and their associated phone number formats in order to achieve total success. For example, you might want to look at the different number formats expected with AU land lines and cell numbers.

However, you can go a long way towards a correct formatting set with native functions. It might be best to split the extensions off first. Parsing text can be very subjective according to the nature of the data but this seems to work with your sample.

         

The formula in B1 is,

=IF(ISNUMBER(SEARCH("x",A1)),RIGHT(A1,LEN(A1)-SEARCH("x",A1)),IF(RIGHT(A1,1)=")",TRIM(SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(MID(A1,FIND("×",SUBSTITUTE(A1,"(","×",LEN(A1)-LEN(SUBSTITUTE(A1,"(","")))),9),")",""),"(","")),""))

The formula in C1 is,

=IFERROR(--SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(SUBSTITUTE(A1,B1,""),"(",""),")",""),"x",""),".",""),"-",""),"+","")," ",""),"")

The formula in E1 is,

=TEXT(C1,"[>99999999999]+## #### ### ###;[>9999999999]### ## ### ###;(###) ###-####")&IF(LEN(B1),TEXT(B1," ×0"),"")

Fill down as necessary.

Addendum: It is probably worthwhile to mention that I have formatted the extensions with a times symbol (× or ASCII 0×215) as a prefix and not an x.
