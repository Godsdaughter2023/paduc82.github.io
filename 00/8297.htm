Normally, if you enter time data in the form h:mm:ss.ssssss, Excel will round the seconds to 3 decimal places.  For example, 12:34:56.789543 is entered as 12:34:56.790.  Note:  that is the actual value, not just the displayed value [1].

In order to retain the 6-decimal-place precision, you need to enter the date/time as text, which you can right-align if you want.

f z z say: Date values are stored as numbers, so are subject to Excel's 15 decimal digit precision limit. 6 digits for date portion (the integer portion) and 6 digits for hours/minutes/whole seconds leaves only 3 digits for fractional seconds. If you need those last 3 decimal places, these date-time values would need to be text, and you'd need to use UDFs to do much date arithmetic with them.

That is an interesting thought.  But your explanation is an over-simplification [2].  And it is mostly incorrect.

The date portion can be up to 7 decimal digits (12/31/9999 = 2958465).  And ironically, it is true that with dates after 11/25/4637, 3 fractional seconds digits is the maximum precision that Excel will
But in fact, even with dates in that range, Excel is capable of distinguishing time with 4 fractional seconds digits [1].

However, with current dates through 10/13/2173, the date portion is 5 decimal digits, not 6.  So through 6/4/2079, Excel will display a difference of times with 5 fractional seconds digits, not 3.  And it is capable of distinguishing times with 6 fractional seconds digits [1].

(That should be self-evident from the examples in footnote #1 in my previous posting.)

Moreover, when there is no date component, Excel will display a difference of times with 9 fractional seconds digits.  And it is capable of distinguishing times with 10 fractional seconds digits [1].

Excel does not use such variability per se to limit the precision of display of other numbers.

For example, we can format decimal numbers with up to 30 decimal places even though the actual number of digits that Excel will convert depends on the magnitude of the number.

Ergo, based on the facts above, I would expect Excel to permit us to format seconds to at least 9 fractional digits.

[2] Excel computation is not subject to a "15 decimal digit precision limit", although that is often asserted incorrectly even in MS documentation.

Excel does limit input to 15 significant digits, replacing digits to the right zero.  And Excel will
But those limits apply to decimal numbers, not to the form of date and time per se (yyyymmdd  hhmmss.sss).

And those limits are somewhat arbitrary.  The true limits are determined by the 64-bit binary floating-point representation that Excel uses internally.  For example, Excel can correctly represent 16-digit integers up to 9,007,199,254,740,992.  And Excel can exactly represent decimal fractions as small as 2^-1022, a number with 308 zeros after the decimal point followed by 714 significant decimal digits.

Similarly, even though Excel arbitrarily limits input to 9.99999999999999E+307, it is capable of producing numbers in computations as large as about 1.79769E+308 -- exactly (2^1023-2^970)*2.

ronrosenfeld say:

Your original entries, in the form yyyymmdd cannot be interpreted by Excel as valid dates.  Dates in Excel are stored as the number of days since 1/1/1900.  Changing the format only changes the appearance, it does not change how dates are parsed on entry.

Try this formula in B1, assuming your original entry in mmddyyyy format in A1:

=DATE(MOD(A1,10^4),INT(A1/10^6),MOD(INT(A1/10^4),100))

and fill down as needed.

That will give you "real" excel dates, which can then be formatted as you wish.
