Hi BenjaminCasey,

 

Follow these steps:

 

a)    Turn off the computer and examine the rear of the unit. Find the cable that comes from the computer’s monitor and follow it to the back of the PC. The cable will be plugged into the output of the video card, which will be either a 15-pin VGA port or a 29-pin DVI port. If you are fortunate, there will be a second port directly next to it, of either the DVI or VGA variety. On the newest machines, you may even find a regular HDMI port on the card.

 

b)    Choose the connection for video output. If the computer has an HDMI port, you can simply connect an HDMI cable of the correct length between the computer and the TV. Otherwise, you will need to make a few decisions. If the computer has only a single video output and it will not be used solely with the television, you may want to invest in a new video card. Purchase one with either a DVI or an HDMI output, and install it in an available slot, according to the instructions included with it

 

c)    Connect the HDMI cable from the television to the computer. Install the correct adapter or converter for the computer, if necessary. Unless the computer has HDMI output, cords will also need to be connected from the sound card output to the HDMI video cable or adapter, because HDMI carries both video and audio signals.

 

d)    Boot the computer and configure the new display by selecting “Control Panel” from the Start menu. Double-click the “Display” icon and click the “Settings” tab. The manual for the HDMI TV will list the screen resolutions it supports, along with the refresh rates. Configure the resolution in the “Settings” tab, and then click the “Advanced” button to set the refresh rate. If you are using the HDMI TV as the second monitor, you will also have to configure how it is used by Windows, according to the instructions pertaining to the video card. Most cards offer a “Clone” or “Mirror” mode, where both screens display the same content. Alternately, the “Extended Desktop” mode will allow each monitor to operate independently.

 

e)    Reboot the computer to apply the changes.

john_barnett say:

Hello NeedHelpGuy,

The fan has one main purpose, to keep the CPU and other internal parts cool. The longer the laptop is on the more heat is produced and, consequently, the more the fan will usually run. The same applies to cpu intensive programs, i.e., AutoCAD. Because AutoCAD is both CPU and Memory intensive the system has to work harder to process the data and, thus, generates more heat.

If your laptop was older (a few years for example) I would have suggested that the problem was a build up of dust. As your laptop is new then dust can be ruled out.  One thing that can cause the fan to work harder is if the air exit grill is partially blocked, for example, if you have the laptop on your knees or you have books etc., close to the air exit grill. This then would prevent hot air from venting from the laptop and would, inevitably, cause the fan to rotate at a faster speed in an attempt to dissipate the heat. High ambient temperatures within a room can also  be a cause of overheating.

If the air exit grill is free from obstruction then it could simply be a case of the laptop having a noisy fan - they do exist. As your laptop is new and, presumably, still under warranty it may be worth taking it back to the retailer you purchased it from and getting them to take a look at it. It isn't wise for you to start tinkering with it as you would invalidate any warranty.
