Avast! is only using their software to detect that your router has this known vulnerability, neither they nor we have anything to do with supporting your router, so as Le Boule mentioned, you'd need to go to the router manufacturers website to learn whether a firmware update is available and exactly how to perform this.

However, here's some general information that should give you a starting point, with the first article explaining the vulnerability that was discovered late in 2014, though it had been around for over a decade before then.

"Misfortune Cookie" Vulnerability Exposes Millions of Routers

This second article discusses the general process for managing and performing router firmware updates, though the manufacturer's webpage will explain this better for your specific router.

wikiHow to Update Router Firmware

Generally, if your router is a few years old it may be simpler to just go buy a new router, since at this point these shouldn't contain this old problem and the security of these devices is generally better than it used to be.  That doesn't mean you'll never need to upgrade the router firmware, but typically those built more recently contain software that makes this process easier as well.

There are routers available that cost hundreds of dollars, but the typical home user doesn't need one costing more than $50 and they are often even less online.  The concern is whether your router is owned by your ISP, which is unlikely or they'd have already solved this problem or you should be contacting them.  If you own it, then the next question is exactly what interface(s) you need on a new one, so get the router's brand name and model number off the (typically bottom) of the old device and provide that to the store to help decide which new router to buy.

wikiHow to Choose a Wireless Router

jacques raubenheimer say:

Thank you for that additional explanation. That does clarify things a bit.

Remember, though, that just because the program is not doing what you think it should be doing, does not qualify what is happening as a bug.

From what you have described, I would expect Excel to do what it is doing.

So if you expect it to do one thing, and I expect it to do something else, surely that does not make what is happening a bug.

The issue you have is that Excel is not recognising 16th May 2016 as a date, but rather as text. Excel has certain rules about how dates are recognised, and you would do well to familiarise yourself with them--it will make life a lot easier. If you use the Show formulas option as I suggested, you will see that 16th May 2016 is not recognised as a date (it stays 16th May 2016 even when Show formulas is toggled, instead of showing the dateserial value).

Generally, the best way to enter dates is in the International (ISO) date format: yyyy/mm/dd (e.g., 2016/05/23), which may be shortened depending on what you are entering (e.g., 16/5/23). This will ensure that you get exactly what you want.

Try this. Enter 16/5/16 into a cell. Excel should convert that to 2016/05/16, and change the cell number formatting to Date when you press Enter. Now, while positioned on that cell, open the Format Cells dialog (Ctrl+1) is a shortcut, and choose Custom format in the Category list of the Number tab. Then, in the Type box, enter this: dd mmm yyyy.

That will show the contents of the cell as 16 May 2016. And if you autofill that down, then it will switch to the next month when it hits the end of May. The trick is that you have to be sure that what you have entered in Excel is recognised as a dateserial, and that is why I recommended you to use the Show Formulas toggle.
