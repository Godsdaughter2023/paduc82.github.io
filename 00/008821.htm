I'm not going to get into the level of detail regarding issues with your individual PCs that PA Bear will, so continue with him if that's what you wish to do.

I'll just make a few comments about your misunderstandings regarding those 3rd-party supposed "security" programs and the issues with MIDI controllers with Windows 10.

First you state that both Iolo System Mechanic Professional (version?) and Fix-It [Utilities Pro] 15 are "better than Defender yet Win 10 Defender does not seem to know they exist".

Personally, I had great difficulty finding any real information about the supposed security components for these, especially the Fix-It product which doesn't appear to have any real certification whatsoever, so where you get the idea they are "better" than Defender is beyond me.  Based on what I found, it appears these are both simply attempts to stay relevant and able to sell their utility products in a time where really none of this is necessary any longer, by adding some sort of **** virus scanner as an [optional] component.

The Avanquest Fix-It Utilities 15 review I found at TechAdvisor, the most reputable such site I could find, indicates that if this application "spots existing antivirus software while it's [being] installed, it leaves it alone and doesn't install it's own.  That same review indicates in the 3rd paragraph that they repeatedly saw a CRC error on installing Fix-It with MSE removed, and couldn't get the software to run with its AV component intact, even after repeated attempts.  So I don't see this description as "better" than anything, let alone Defender.

Iolo System Mechanic Pro's Enhanced System Shield, on the other hand, at least appears to have passed the Virus Bulletin:  VB100 certification testing with Windows 10 Pro once in December 2015, though it had failed in the only other such test ever performed a few years earlier.  It's apparently based upon the CYREN engine, but in the testing it both performed much more slowly and received a much lower RAP (Reactive and Proactive) score at 67.6% than any other consumer product including the CYREN Command product which got a 85.6% score.

Functionally, it appears that the Iolo System Mechanic Pro might contain a functional enough antivirus that it may be able to disable Windows 10 Defender as required, but I see nothing anywhere stating that it is certified as Windows 10 compliant to do this.  In any case, I would never trust the protection of my own systems to such a basic and simplistic security application.

Personally, I'd dump those trashy 3rd-party utility products and switch completely to Defender, since with Windows 10 that's all that most will need.

As for Windows 10 and MIDI controllers, that's exactly the type of problems I'd expect to see with the software-as-a-service model of Windows 10.  The reason is simply that each release of a major new build of the operating system is at least as complex as that of the typical service pack that used to exist with earlier versions of Windows.

The problem is that as Windows evolves the 3rd-party software vendors supporting Windows 10 really need to perform a complete set of testing just as they used to for a major new release of Windows or service packs, but few actually do so.  In fact, for niche products such as MIDI controllers, the more typical method has always been to wait for customers to report problems and then react, which as you can easily see is nearly non-functional with something as dynamically changing as Windows 10.

So the issue here isn't really the new Windows 10 model itself, it's the historically shoddy software maintenance practices of the software and in the case of MIDI, hardware/software vendors.  For this reason, the most sensible thing for most requiring such hardware to do is either to confirm that the MIDI vendor they choose is going to guarantee this maintenance of their products or barring that, remain on an older less changing platform such as Windows 7 or 8 as long as possible.

Eventually these software maintenance issues will likely force a shakeout in the MIDI industry to a small number of players who actually maintain their products, so at that point you'll be able to make a more informed decision as to which vendors are going to remain for the long term.  They'll likely be the higher priced products, but since apparently all of them are, the real question is who creates better, more secure and stable software to go with them.
