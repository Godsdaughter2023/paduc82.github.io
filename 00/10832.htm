You are correct, these types of script launched popups are often injected by the advertising networks on the web pages you are browsing for other purposes.

They also aren't really malware, just simple scripts designed to launch web pages containing fake messages like this example you're all seeing.  For this reason, there's typically nothing for an antivirus to detect, though some of the more aggressive products like Malwarebytes Anti-Malware (MBAM) contain malicious script monitoring abilities within their real-time protection modules that can sometimes detect and block the initial script from operating.

Typically the AV products like Windows Defender will only detect these after they've been around and repeated for a while, so they can be linked to some other well known similar pages via text or other signature information within their scripts or web pages.  More often, they'd be blocked by the SmartScreen protection instead, since this allows for more quickly adding such fast changing web pages to its blocking lists.

For that reason the two best methods for handling these are either to simply learn how to properly close them, which can typically be done using combinations of clicking the close buttons and/or pressing the ALT-F4 key combination or if the desire is to try and block them, either try MBAM Premium or add an ad-blocking extension to whatever browser you're using.  The latter option is sometimes free and has the added benefit of quieting the overall noise from dynamic advertising as well.

I use the latter technique by enabling the free Tracking Protection - EasyPrivacy and Stop Google Tracking lists for Internet Explorer (Win10) as well as having learned how to relatively easily close those I've encountered when performing purposely risky browsing to test my security profile.  I've never needed to use the alternate option of ending the browser task with Task Manager for either Edge or IE that's often recommended by others, since it's just a matter of learning when you need to combine closing one window like a fake login box by clicking it's close button while preparing to quickly press Alt-F4 to get the standard browser offer to close all or only the current tab.

People just make what's really nothing more than a typically obviously fake web page out to be so much more than it truly is.  IOW, people in general don't pay attention and the malicious actors know this and take advantage of it.

The first thing I see wrong with that web page example above is of course the non-Microsoft address in the browser address bar, but it's also a glaring omission not to include the copyright, trademark or an official logo for the Microsoft Windows displayed in the first line as well as the missing copyright symbol in the Microsoft 2018 line at the bottom.  <EDIT> Forgot, what does Intel have to do with Windows Update and Microsoft's not supporting AMD any longer?

In short, there's so much wrong with that page that only a child should ever be fooled by it.  I've built better pages in 10 minutes that fooled the executives of companies and banks when used in phishing test campaigns sanctioned by the security operations within those organizations.

In fact, it's been confirmed that these pages are built with such mistakes on purpose, since those who don't recognize such obvious errors will also typically be easy to fool into clicking to run the HTML Application file on that page after downloading and saving it.  Or calling the phone number displayed on other pages and allowing the person who answers access to their PC and paying large amounts to "fix" it.

So these web pages are actually intended to filter out the intelligent and not waste the fake support call center operator's time on people less likely to give them access to their PC and money.
