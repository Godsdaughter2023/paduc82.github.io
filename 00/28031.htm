Converting a newsletter in Publisher to a Template - Microsoft Community

phyllistubbs-gingerich say:

Converting a newsletter in Publisher to a Template

We have a quarterly newsletter ranging from 15-25 pages in color with text and images. We want to convert this from Publisher file of our own design to a template of the same design. We will email this to our email database so that when the recipient opens the email the first page of the newsletter is seen and all others can be opened in sequence.
davidf2 say:

Your question seems to be two parts.

If you want to save your Publisher newsletter as a template you can File > Save As > Save as type: Publisher template. Then you can open that template in the future by going to New > My templates.

You could also just go 'old school' and open last month's newsletter and do a Save As a new file name for the new month's newsletter. Perhaps just add a date to the file name.

As per delivering that newsletter to your email recipient there is no practical way of sending a multi-page newsletter as the body of an email message. You would have to convert the publication to one page and you would have to have the same version of Outlook as Publisher as your default email client. And even if you do try this approach then chances are the HTML formatted page would not look like or maintain the formatting and layout of your original Publisher file. Publisher cannot convert every print format to a comparable HTML format. In some cases there simply is nothing equivalent in HTML format.

IMHO the only way to get consistent results is to convert your newsletter to a PDF file and attach it to your email.  If you write a simple but effective introduction in the body of your email message and your message is of interest and wanted by the recipient they will open the PDF. If it is spam, then it won't matter whether it is in the message body or a PDF...it will be deleted. If you have Publisher 2007 or newer then you can Save As > PDF file type. If not www.primopdf.com is my favorite freebie. Just watch carefully as you do a custom install and opt out of any bundled toolbars and other junkware.

One alternative to this is to upload your PDF file to the 'cloud' somewhere such as DropBox, OneDrive or perhaps a company website, and provide a link to that file within your email message. Most browsers have a PDF 'plugin' installed and can open that PDF file within the browser. Alternatively the recipient will also have the option of downloading and opening the file in a PDF reader such as the Adobe or FoxIt readers.

You also have the alternative of converting the newsletter to a multi-page website but again you would likely find some parts of your newsletter would not easily convert to HTML format. Print medium and Web medium are just too different. It is possible but you would have to learn all the methods of tweaking the formatting and layouts in order to get the Publisher web pages to render correctly and enjoy good cross browser compatibility. Again, going PDF is the easiest solution in my opinion.

If you must send HTML formatted messages then instead of Publisher consider using specialty software such as:www.constantcontact.com or www.group-mail.com and a free version: http://www.group-mail.com/asp/common/downloads.asp or another popular one is http://mailchimp.com/ - some free services - Store up to 2,000 subscribers & send up to 12,000 emails per month. Again, you would need to convert your newsletter to a single page.

graham mayor say:

Widow and orphan control causes stray short lines at the end of the last paragraph on a page to move with the previous line to the next page.

Keep with next is usually reserved for headings or sub headings and keeps that heading paragraph with the paragraph that follows. If you have lots of paragraphs set with keep with next set, the process gets hopelessly confused.

suzanne s. barnhill say:

That is your problem. When you have "Keep with next" enabled, Word tries to keep all the paragraphs together. If there are more than will fit on a page, it will keep them together as long as it can and insert a break whenever it encounters a paragraph that doesn't have KWN set. This results in unexpected breaks and sometimes large blank spaces. Having "Keep lines together" enabled just aggravates the problem, as it does not even allow Word to break to a new page in the middle of a paragraph. So the whole document becomes one large stuck-together clump. Not surprisingly, this can make it unmanageable, and Word can become confused.

Unless you're so determined to have uniform bottom margins that you're willing to have single lines of multi-line paragraphs at the bottom or top of a page, I recommend leaving "Widow/orphan control" enabled. For more on all of this, see http://wordfaqs.ssbarnhill.com/BottomLine.htm

Hello Dennis,

If you would like to import your photos from your phone to your Windows 10 computer, please do the following:

  1. Connect your phone to the PC with a USB cable. Make sure the phone is on and unlocked. If it's an Android phone, you might also need to change the USB setting to allow you to transfer media or photos and not just charge the phone.
  2. Open the Photos app on your PC (select the Start button and then Photos), then select Import and select From a USB device, then follow the instructions. You can pick the items you want to import and select where to save them.
