al jarvi say: If your calling the Remote Desktop [RDC] host PC from another PC on your local network [LAN] use the private LAN IP of the RDC host. If you use the public IP of your router then that may fail because many consumer grade routers do not support the so called loopback feature.

http://theillustratednetwork.mvps.org/RemoteDesktop/TroubleshootingDiagrams/Basic.html

If your calling from a remote location you need to...

* Open/forward TCP Port 3389 through any firewall/router the RDC host PC is behind.
* Call using either the public IP of the router or use a fully qualified domain name [FQDN].

http://windows.microsoft.com/en-US/windows7/allow-remote-desktop-connections-from-outside-your-home-network

Most ISPs provide a dynamic IP address to residential accounts. You can use a free FQDN service like No-IP.com or DynDNS or similar service to get around that issue. The way it works if you run a small program on a PC on your network, ie. the RDC host PC for example, that contacts the No-IP.com servers on a time scheduled basis. The No-IP.com servers then map your current public IP to the FQDN and propagate that information across the public internet. You then call the FQDN.

http://www.dyndns.com/
http://www.no-ip.com/

Note that many consumer grade routers support No-IP.com or DynDNS or similar services natively. Check the users guide for your router for help with that.
