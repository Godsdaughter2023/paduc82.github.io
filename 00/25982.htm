sodium say:

I would really like the scans to take a lot less time than they do, since I can tell that my disk drive is not the bottleneck and there is CPU that is not being utilized, why not improve the code that does the scans to support multithreading better?  I guarantee there will be lots of happy users out there.

 

Edit:  Ok I found the feedback link to submit this suggestion.  I don't really need an answer to this post; I just wanted to throw this idea out there.

rob koch say:

As I mentioned, the discussion I saw about this with written by the developer of another antimalware product, not me, and it was his indication that this was far less trivial than it might appear.  Just a few moments of thought about the issues he raised made it clear that he was correct and that for most systems the minor gain in scan speed that might result wouldn't be worth the effort and added risk and complication that would be required.

Much of this comes from the fact that the real bottleneck in most consumer and small business client systems today isn't the processor, it's the disk subsystem, usually a single cheap disk drive of medium performance.  So increasing the processing power would in most circumstances simply result in multiple cores waiting for the disk to respond.

In any case, this operation isn't a function of MSE anyway, it's actually controlled by those producing the core antimalware engines and definitions.  So for this to change it would require a re-write of these core engines, affecting all Microsoft amntimalware products.

Though something like this sounds simple, the reality is that even the Windwos operating system itself deals with some of these issues in a relatively simple manner, by limiting certain functions to the first core to avoid contention and other complications.  Since these design decisions create their own limitations, it's unlikely that multiple-core scanning could ever effectively increase the overall speed.  In general the best methods to increase scanning speed are to focus on the engine and core definitions design optimization which are modified with every new engine release, typically on a monthly basis.

In computing as in most complex systems design, the simplest solution is often the most effective in terms of performance, since complexity actually adds cycles and thus decreases performance.  The MSE engines already do take advantage of available memory during a scan, by loading things like compressed files into RAM and scanning them there to increase performance, which reduces the affects of disk latency for files that require multiple passes with complex decryption or decompression algoritims.

Rob

rob koch say:

Sodium,

Though I understand your wish to see the the scanning able to use more of the available resources, I've read in another security product forum elsewhere why this isn't commonly done.  The problem is that operating a scan in a multi-processor fashion leads to technical issues with controlling the interaction of the separate processes, since they could find different portions of the same malware threat resulting in contention.  Unlike some other applications, dividing the tasks between multiple processors isn't trivial and would basically require more effort than it's really worth in most cases.

The other issue with this idea is that the biggest value provided by multi-core processors is their ability to avoid saturating the entire processing resource with one application, something that anti-malware scanning applications are infamous for.  So by only using one processor core, this problem is easily avoided with the only other major bottleneck being the storage subsystem (hard disk), which often becomes saturated even when only one processor is performing the scan.  So even if the anti-malware application could use more than one processor, this would typically quickly overwhelm the storage subsystem still resulting in less than full utilization of all processors and sluggish response for any other applications, including the operating system background processes.

So though on the surface this sounds like a good idea, in reality it wouldn't be as effective or easy as it sounds and would actually simply complicate an otherwise relatively simple linear scanning process.  The discussion I saw elsewhere related to a third-party anti-spyware application and the developer indticated that though he might attempt to offload certain ancilliary tasks to another processor, he was unlikely to ever attempt a truly 'parallel processing' approach to the scanning itself for these reasons.

< EDIT > Noting that you mentioned having an SSD drive, of course this reduces the storage subsystem saturation issue to some extent, though not completely since these drives still have certian sequential file read limitations.  Though I'd guess that it might be possible to scan different logical drives at the same time, the potential conflict situation would still exist across multiple drives in the same system and so the core issue of process contention still exists as a result.

Rob

Alas, you can't, directly. Access does NOT "play nice" over the Internet; it requires a fast, stable, preferably wired local area network for its intensive back and forth communication.
