john korchok say:

There are 3 different checkboxes in Word 2010:

Assuming your audience is using Word 2010 or later, the Content Controls will be easiest to use. To protect a form using these, follow these steps:

  1. In Word, insert and select a checkbox Content Control.
  2. On the Developer or Review tabs, choose Restrict Editing.
  3. Under Editing Restrictions, check Allow only this type of editing in the document.
  4. Leave the dropdown below at No changes (Read only).
  5. Under Exceptions, check Everyone.
  6. Click on Yes, Start Enforcing Protection. Do not enter a password.
  7. Click on Stop Protection.
  8. Select the area where you want comments to be entered. This could be an open text area of the document, or it could be a Rich Text or Plain Text content control.  Repeat the above steps.

Word remembers which areas were selected for editing and adds the new area to the editable parts of the document.

If you want this form to be filled by Mac users and those who use earlier version of Word, then you use Legacy Form Fields. Follow these steps:

  1. In Word, insert a legacy Checkbox Form Field.
  2. For editable comments, you can insert a Text Form Field into which people can type text. As an alternative, you can also insert a Continuous Section Break before and after the editable area (Page Layout>Breaks>Sections Breaks>Continuous).
  3. Under Editing Restrictions, check Allow only this type of editing in the document.
  4. Change the dropdown to Filling in forms.
  5. If you are creating an unprotected area for comments, click on Select sections. Uncheck the section for comments. OK out.
  6. Click on Yes, Start Enforcing Protection. Do not enter a password.

Test your form to ensure you can check the box and enter text in the comments area.

rob koch say:

You'd need to acquire a new model of a vendor's device in order for this vulnerability to have been fixed, since all of the known affected models listed by Bastille use an older USB dongle design that was susceptible to HID Packet Injection or other vulnerabilities.

MouseJack Affected Devices

Though this type of attack can be perpetrated by an attacker as much as 100 meters distant from the target PC, it's not a very likely attack scenario in most cases. This is due to the fact that it's easily fixed by simply replacing the affected devices with a newer model, which most truly important targets such as business or government would have done already due to age of the device if nothing else.

In truth it's not worth spending any time trying to "fix' this problem, either throw the problem device away and buy another or if your PC is located in a relatively remote area and you have no reason to believe anyone would want to attack you, then just ignore it.

The only people likely to be targeted are those with either significant amounts of money or important information that could be acquired by malicious actors, so the individual consumer, especially those not living in high density locations such as an apartment or a school dormitory, are highly unlikely to be attacked.

If you truly feel you might be attacked or are simpy the paranoid type, then the obvious solution is to buy a new device that doesn't contain the vulnerability.  You're wasting everyone's time, especially your own asking such intricate questions about a situation with such a simple, low cost solution.

rob koch say:

This is a community forum, so you are most often receiving responses from other consumer users like yourself, whether owning a small business as well or not.  Even if the tags indicate the responder is from "Microsoft", these are in most cases just contract employees.

I provided the link to the best information I've seen relating to this relatively minor technical issue in my earlier post.  My response is based on my own background in computer security, radio frequency electronics and just plain common sense, which isn't often seen on the Internet from my own experience.

The point most miss with the MouseJack Attack is that it isn't Internet based, so it can only occur if the attacker is within roughly 100 meters of the target PC and even then only if the attacker can detect the frequency being used by the dongle in the first place, which depends upon a number of factors relating to how radio frequency signals travel through walls made of various materials.

If you wish to simply insure you can't be attacked on a single machine, then as the various documentation referenced in that Bastille Threat Research document indicates, only USB dongle devices are affected, while Bluethooth and possibly other newer technology devices aren't susceptible, which is what I was referring to.  The older USB dongle technology was cheap, lower range typically (a few feet vs. ~25 feet for Bluetooth) and often unencrypted, which is where this security issue comes from.

All of this information is clearly stated in documents linked from that one Bastille reference, but only someone who understands all of the technologies involved as well as the potential attack scenarios these allow will truly understand the risks that might exist and how to mitigate them.  That's why I was short and abrupt in my answer, since the response will always be, buy a new technology keyboard and get rid of the technically outdated, cheap device that is what causes this problem.

I personally have one of these older technology designs myself, though I don't see mine listed under the Microsoft vendor heading as susceptible.  However, even if I did I wouldn't replace the device for this reason, since the typical suburban neighborhood I live in is highly unlikely to have anyone (other than myself) knowledgeable enough to understand this attack, let alone interested in pursuing it. Realize that an attacker first needs to know that you have one of these devices operating, which means they already have intimate knowledge of the devices in use in your organization or home, so they might have simpler forms of attack available.

All of the above is why I stated that this is a pure waste of time, since the complexities of the attack scenario itself means that only a highly motivated attacker might care to attempt it.  If your business or personal profile (e.g. wealth or importance) makes you a potential target for such an attacker, then buy a Bluetooth or other newer device that doesn't contain this issue.
