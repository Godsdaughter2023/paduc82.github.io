a. user say:

I think you mean 'ergonomics.'

Most monitors are set at the factory with the brightness jacked up all the way. You can get a tan from all that light. One of the first things you want to do when you plug in a monitor is to adjust the settings, for brightness at the least.

Monitors have built-in controls to adjust things like brightness and contrast, and so do most graphics drivers. The advantage of using the monitor's adjustments is that they will apply to all images, all the time. The Windows accessibility settings apply only to the Windows interface. 

On the lower left corner of the bezel (the plastic edge around your monitor) you should see buttons or dials. Those buttons and dials can significantly alter the way your monitor's image looks. Instructions for operating those controls should have shipped with the monitor, but no worries if you can't find them. Just go to the website of the monitor manufacturer and you can find them there.

Some monitors even let you save your settings, so you can try something else and then easily switch back to your settings.

j w stuart say:

The password to logon to the router's configuration software is separate from the Wireless WPA2 password. So you are dealing with 2 passwords to recover or reset/change your WPA2 password.

Temporarily connect your laptop using Cat5/ Cat5 cable and then logon to the router using the password you choose, them go to the wireless configuration option to reset / change your WPA2 password.

If you forgot the password to log into the router via a Cat5 / Cat6 cable then contact your ISP (Verizon, Comcast, Etc.,) or the router manufactures Tech support for the instructions on resetting the router, configuring a new "Router" password and then configuring WPA2 and the WPA2 password.
