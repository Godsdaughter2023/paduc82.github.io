rob koch say:

The client for Microsoft security products has always been simply the GUI, with the true anti-malware engines and definitions contained in the separate modules by those names contained in the same listing you provided above.

The primary difference though is that unlike the 3rd-party security programs, which have a long history of marketing their new versions to you every year by a new name in order to coerce you into paying for an upgrade, the Microsoft products for consumers are free.  This means there's simply no reason to upgrade the program on an annual cycle and thus Microsoft's products tend to be aligned to changes within the operating system itself, exactly as with the other less visible, but truly far more important security changes built into Windows.

The key difference though, is that unlike the 3rd-party security developers, Microsoft is aware of these relatively obscure operating system security changes long before they are released to the public or even the security developers, since they are part of the design of a new upgrade revision from the beginning of its development inside Microsoft.  So unlike the 3rd-parties who must wait until the early developer versions are released and often wait far later, Microsoft is instead developing any necessary changes to Windows Defender in parallel to those of the upgraded Windows release itself.

So if you still don't understand what I just said, this means that the copyright date for Defender will typically be aligned with the release of the last significant Windows upgrade, in this case the Anniversary Edition that released in August 2016.

tom rune berg say:

Hi,

I just want to make sure that we talk about the same things here...

There are three major editions of Office 365:

For non-business:

- Office 365 Personal, a subscription based offering to get continuously updated Office for PC/Mac for one person on 1 PC or Mac + 1 tablet + 1 phone, also extra OneDrive storage capacity (1Tb) and Skype credits (60 minutes) and included support. Basically, get improvements in the products as they are released and the newest version of Office when its done.

- Office 365 Home, like Personal, but for families. A cheap way for a family to have Office on the home computer and the childrens computers. Personals perks but for 5 family members and 5 computers.

The non-business versions is a way to get Office Professional, a rather expensive Office product, at a lower cost and with more frequent product improvements than waiting for major releases.

For Mac, we have not seen as many benefits of this, but as of 2015, Microsoft are synchronising their product schedules for PC and Mac and the level of improvement in the new Mac products are very promising. In the end its up to you to decide whether you feel that paying a subscription fee instead of the full flat fee is worth the OneDrive storage capacity, included support, Skype minutes and a likely promise of frequent product improvements going forward is worth it. It is my opinion that PC users have seen more benefits up to this date than Mac users admitablly, but I am optimistic that this will change and it seems likely to change from this autumn.

For small business

For smaller businesses, the additional services like shared company calendar, SharePoint for document collaboration, OneDrive for business and 5 installations per employee in plans that include Office begins to make sense quickly even for Mac users.

Subscription offers here is Bring Your Own Office or Browser, and subscribe to server services, or just subscribe to Office (similar to the Home subscription above) or both (services + Office programs).

Benefits of Office 365 for SMB:

  • Outlook webmail and the new coming Outlook client works great.
  • Document collaboriation with SharePoint in the middle works with Office 2011, and significantly better with the new Office 2016 Preview (due for release in autumn) has been stable for me.
  • The browser features of SharePoint works beautifully on Mac, whether in Safari, Chrome or Firefox and you can collaborate with colleagues using the PC or iPad/iPhone and Android versions of Word, Excel, PowerPoint and OneNote.

This is where Office 365 immediately makes sense even for Mac customers today. No need for a server on premises, but all the advantages of the standard edition of the products.

For enterprise

Everything I mentioned for small business apply here too, and there the additional enterprise features works on Mac even in the rare cases where you need a PC to configure things at first. This is for larger business with access to a PC at least in a virtual machine to get things going or a Microsoft partner to help them get started.

I hope I have clarified Office 365 a bit with this post. I don't work for Microsoft but I often work WITH Microsoft in Norway to teach Office 365 to customers and partners.
