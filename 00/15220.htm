Hello,

Antimalware Service Executable is a background process of Windows Defender, and is part of the Windows operating system. It is responsible for checking files for malware when you access them, performing background system scans to check for potentially harmful software, and installing antivirus definition updates. If it is taking up a large amount of CPU or disk resources, it is likely that it is scanning your computer.

However, if it renders your PC unusable due to low resources, you may disable it by turning off Real-time protection option in Virus & threat protection in the Windows Defender Security Center.

tangerinedreams say:

Many people think of the Deleted Items folder as a temporary/long-term "holding pen" for items that may or may not be needed at some undetermined point in the future.  In my humble opinion, it is unwise to view the Deleted Items folder in this way (this opinion is based off of years of being an Outlook/Exchange support technician for my company).  My understanding of Microsoft's intention of the Deleted Items folder is that it's to be used as a "trash can" or a "recycle bin" of sorts (also implied by the recycle bin icon for that folder).

 

In comparison, if you have a stack of papers/documents on your desk, and you've decided some of them are possibly trash, but you're not ready to throw them away yet...would you put them in a your trash can, or would you put them in a separate stack off to the side of your work space, to be reviewed at a later time?  Personally, I would put them in a separate stack.  I would NOT put them in my trash can, because I want to be able to empty my trash can on a regular basis...knowing that any true trash is not being kept around and causing undue issues for me (i.e. taking up more space than necessary, creating or inviting nuisances such as ants - or returning to the Outlook scenario, server connectivity issues, increased OST file size, keeping spam/virus laden emails in your mailbox, etc.).

 

The fact that Microsoft allows you to use the Deleted Items folder as a long-term "holding pen" is a flaw, because it allows you to develop this bad habit, and avoid learning the true concept of "Deleted Items" vs. a "long-term or mid-term holding pen".  I think Microsoft has identified these concerns, and are attempting to mitigate them...partially by removing some features that in previous versions allowed people to develop bad habits.
