bossdweeb say:

"the AMD Radeon HD 7350. It has a fan but it doesn't spin for whatever reason."

Ah! Right (I wasn't thinking ;) ). There are a couple of possibilities.

In and attempt to keep the machine quiet,  the mfg may use a fan profile that does not ramp

up the speed until the gpu hits a high temp , or the fan is borked.

"So your saying that both 12V rails work together to power specific components correct?"

- It's a complex topic, and this is my simplified take on gaming machine psu's.

I work off the 'better safe than sorry' , or Murphy's Law scenario, and this is why I mentioned that

your current psu should be OK, in theory.

But I'm not a electronics engineer,  and like most things  PC there is some debate on the issue ,

so I'll link a detailed article below.

The +12V rails do work together to a degree, and the +12V circuit does supply specific components.

But you also need to be careful not to connect everything to only one rail,  and while the rails do have

overcurrent protection you should avoid  overloading them.

- Some of the better quality psu's apparently will shift the full load to one rail if you do connect

everything to it (I believe that with these models, if you add the two rails Amps together it would

result in the same total as that which is listed for the +12V circuit) , but when in doubt,  it's safer

to try and balance the load between the rails.

- I did er in mentioning USB with +12V earlier.  USB  runs off the 5V rail.

There is some variation with what uses 3.3 & 5V with some components using both/either.

Roughly - RAM, the  PCIe slot (including graphics card fans) and the motherboard chipset run

off  the +3.3V rail.

HDD's , I believe use both +5V and +12V

cpu, gpu,  and case fans run off +12V.

- The gpu and cpu are the big current users, so having them on separate rails should be the safest option, but I tossed in the option of having both on the more powerful rail because the rail

labelled as 10Amps, in practical terms, is probably best run with only about 5-6Amps load to

allow for what is running on the second +12V rail. Mostly it was just to illustrate how it can be

tricky getting a good balance when using a minimum spec psu.

Keep in mind that you cannot run the maximum for each rail on both rails at the same time, because

in your case that would require the psu to be able to provide 312W total on +12V,

not the 240W it is capable of on the +12V.

(My understanding is that to multiply, or divide by 12 to ascertain the Amps or Watts is not exact,

but close enough.)

- Games tend to put full load on a machine for extended periods, which stresses the psu more than when running office type programs, so even though an office machine w/o a dedicated gpu,

or with a low end gpu, may run a lower wattage psu safely, it still does not stress that psu as much

as would a gaming machine running high end games under full load.

- Ideally a machines psu should run at no more than 80% of it's actual capacity if you expect

a long life from the machine.

- Note that you can go too high. If a psu does not run somewhere around 30% of it's capacity,

it does not run as efficiently, and will use more power, and as with running over 80%, hotter.

So you need to strike a balance between non-gaming activities and gaming under full load.

- The other important issue beyond how much load is being drawn from the +12V circuit is

that psu's lose  power with age, and cheap psu's age faster, due to cheap component parts.

The capacitors in particular are susceptible to ageing, which can result less steady supply of current.

This can lead to system instability.
