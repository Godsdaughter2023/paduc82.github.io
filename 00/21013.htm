How to get a Unicode Black Up-Pointing Triangle (Unicode 25B2) into MS Word equation

 

(1) Create equation: [Alt]+[=]

 

(2) Create equation field inside equation: put cursor inside equation, type [Ctrl]+[F9]  A pair of empty curly brackets { } will appear.

 

(3) There will be spaces in these curly brackets. Delete them.

 

(4) Inside the equation field type: EQ[space bar]25B2

 

(5) Toggle the characters 25B2 into their symbol equivalent by putting the cursor right after the 25B2 and typing [Alt]+[x]. This will appear to be unsuccessful, because a box with a question mark in it will appear where the 25B2 characters had been. So it will look like {EQ [?]}.

 

(6) Toggle the field code into its display setting by putting the cursor somewhere in the {EQ [?]} region and typing [Shift] + [F9]

 

The result will be a Black Up-Pointing Triangle

 

----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

How to get a Unicode Black Up-Pointing Small Triangle (Unicode 25B4) into MS Word equation

 

Follow steps (1) through (6) above, but in step (4), type 25B4 instead of 25B2.

 

The result will be a Black Up-Pointing Small Triangle IN ITALICS.

 

(7) Highlight the small italics triangle and type [Ctrl] + [i] to toggle out of italics.

 

The result will be a Black Up-Pointing Small Triangle

Create two lists and save them in the UPROOF folder (locate the folder on your system--probably in a Microsoft directory)

2. American spelling--save as ExcludeDictionaryEN1009.lex

Then you just have to make sure that your proofing language on your document is set to English (Canada) and it will use the appropriate dictionary and exclusion list.

Hope this helps (the reason I'm on here is to find out why they say it is not case sensitive).

how do I set crew sizes in MS Project 2010? - Microsoft Community

scott davis1853 say:

how do I set crew sizes in MS Project 2010?

Different tasks have different HR requirements, whether it be the installation of a Door frame or the installation of the wall framing. It may require one person or twenty. Can  I set this in the Resource Sheet? Or should I make a resource for each crew member or group of crew members. I want to get rid of the over allocating 

john - project say:

Scott,

In Project you can either create individual resources (i.e. Joe, Fred, Tom, etc.) or groups (i.e. carpenters, bricklayers, electricians, etc.). For the former you could also declare a generic resource so instead of Joe, Fred and Tom, you might designate each as carpenter, bricklayer and electrician, meaning that the carpenter might be Joe or it could be Ben or any other carpenter in the crew.

For individual resources the Max Units indicates the availability of the individual. So if Joe is a full-time carpenter, his max units would be 1 (100%). If he only works half-time, his max units would be 0.5 (50%). If Joe is assigned to work more than his max unit level (i.e. two full time tasks simultaneously), then Project will flag that as an overallocation. However the threshold for overallocaiton can be set from anywhere from minute by minute to month by month.

Let's say you set the overallocation basis to week by week and your calendar is a normal 40 hour week. Then you assign Joe to work two tasks on Monday such that he puts in 10 hours instead of the normal 8. Same thing Tuesday and Wednesday, such that by Wednesday quitting time, he has 30 of his 40 hours. On Thursday and Friday, Joe is assigned 5 hours each day. Even though Joe worked overtime on Monday, Tuesday and Wednesday, he will not be overallocated because he is not assigned to work more than the max of 40 hours in the week.

For a group resource, you might set up a crew of carpenters, bricklayers and electricians, each a 5 man crew. Since there are 5 individuals in each, the max units for each group is 5 (500%). You can have all 5 working on simultaneous tasks without overallocation, assuming the overallocation basis is set to day by day for the group.

Hope this helps.

John

Scott,

Since you are doing construction projects and I am very familiar with them, allow me to say something about the necessary level of detail that is typically required for the tasks and the resources if you are to achieve the degree of control that is needed.

Say you have $1M worth of construction. You do an update of progress every week, say on Thursday afternoon. You want to break it down into tasks that have a duration of no more than 5 days, and the resources will be, say. two people plus a sack of materials. Typically, a task will be about 50/50 labour/materials, so each task will have a cost of, say $2000. You need $1M worth of tasks, so that's 500 of them, minimum. If you take into account, and you should, that each task will have an order for materials plus a wait time plus a delivery, that's another 1500 tasks or "events" that have to be scheduled and tracked, so 2000 total so far. Add to that the summaries and sub-summaries that go into building the WBS, plus other events etc, then 3000 lines would be about right.

Any fewer than enough and you may as well not bother to start.

It is better to overdo it than under do it.

And the work type resources should be a list of individual people, not teams.

Every time you aggregate either tasks or resources you are moving away from the original objective which was to break it down into component parts.

Hope this helps.

john - project say:

Scott,

The number of tasks depends on how large the project and what level of detail you need to track/report. Unless you have very small projects, and with a potential crew of 20 (per your initial post), the director who told you 60 tasks was adequate was obviously only referring to a set of reporting tasks, not the full working plan.

I do not agree with Trevor with respect to identifying each individual resource. For a large project, that will give you no end of tracking minutia that you really don't need. For example, let's say you have a concrete finishing crew of 10 guys and you need 4 to do the finishing on a house slab. Do you really care if its Joe, Bob, Tim, and Wally or do you simply care that you have 4 of the 10 available guys?

Creating a plan is always a balance between to little detail and too much. To find that balance, you must determine what information you need in the way of reporting metrics and then set up your plan to provide those.

John

trevor rabey say:

John, we hardly ever disagree, and I take your point about the progress updating presenting the extra difficulty of needing to know, ie have records kept, which guy did which hours on which tasks.

On a tight run project, those necessary records should be kept, but that problem doesn't surface until the update.

But I was looking at his original question about getting rid of the over-allocation.

Unless you can assign joe and bob to this task, and tim and wally to that task, and joe, bob and tim to another task, etc, you can't plan the project for minimum duration, or allow leveling to find a solution "within the available slack". If a decision about who should do what isn't made at planning stage, then it has to be made on the job.

If it is just a few carpenters on a few tasks, and the project is building a house, and it doesn't matter if the finish date ends up a bit lter than it could have been, then making those decisions on the job, and allowing the carpenters to make them, may not matter much.

But if you have 50 boilermakers to do 300 tasks in two 10 hour shifts per day, during a 45 day, $7M, ship refit, and the ship is definitely coming out of the dry dock at 12:00 on the last day, the individual assignments have to be done in advance. And you don't want to be assigning bob to do task A, B, C and find out later that he ignored the plan and decided to do D, E, and F instead. The ship would leave the dry dock and 30 or so tasks would not have been done, even though with good scheduling, and working to the plan, meant they could have been done.

This why some shipyards go broke and others do not. Building companies too.

As you so rightly say, it's always a balance. Too much aggregation diminishes the opportunity or the possibility of more control. Extra control means more work in planning and record keeping and updating, and that has a cost.
