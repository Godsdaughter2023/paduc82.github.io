The program and/or process can be from malware or can be legitimate (such as an invasive antivirus like Norton or McAfee). If you are using a Norton or McAfee product,  uninstall it and replace with a better program such as NOD32, Kasperksy, or Avast (free). The Windows Firewall is adequate for most people. Shutdown issues can also be caused by old/poorly written drivers so make sure all drivers are updated, particularly on Windows Vista and Windows 7.

A.The first step is always to make sure your computer is virus/malware free.

http://www.elephantboycomputers.com/page2.html#Removing_Malware

B. If the computer is virus/malware-free, drivers are current, and no Norton or McAfee programs are installed, then do clean-boot troubleshooting to see which program/process is the culprit:

How to perform a clean boot in Vista and XP - http://support.microsoft.com/kb/331796
How to perform a clean boot in Windows Vista or Windows 7 - http://support.microsoft.com/kb/929135

john john __ say: Most routers use IP address 192.168.1.1 or 192.168.2.1  you simply type in the address in your web browser address bar and press <Enter> and you will get to the router log on page.  You need to supply a username and password to access the router, look on the back or bottom of the router to see if there is a sticker with the default username/password information or ask your ISP to supply you the information and user manual.  These ISP supplied routers are usually just re-branded routers supplied by major manufacturers and are packaged in a case with the ISP's logo on it, inside the box the router is usually just a version of one of their other routers available to consumers, if you know the manufacturer name and model number you can get most of the information online.  Once logged on to the router you can verify if the wireless segment of the network is isolated from the wired LAN.

When the Sky technician installed the new setup he simply made sure that the modem was connecting properly to the internet and that your main machine could connect to the internet, the LAN portion of the setup is not their responsibility so they don't usually bother with this part of the setup.   However, if you bought or if you are renting the router from your ISP then they should at the very least be able to definitely tell you if the router is IPv6 capable, if it isn't there may be a firmware update for it that would enable IPv6, they should know about this.  If the router is in fact not IPv6 compatible then you will have to abandon the Windows Homegroup configuration and opt for the old type Workgroup configuration, this isn't that big a deal, you just have to configure it manually rather than having Windows do everything for you, in the end once properly setup you may find the older Workgroup setup easier to maintain and administer than Homegroups.  Without IPv6 on the present router if you insist on using Homegroups then you will have to disable the routing functions on the Sky router and just use the modem functions and use a second router with IPv6 capabilities for your LAN. 

Your ISP should be able to troubleshoot router connectivity  issues or at the very least give you the username and password and a user manual for the router.  It isn't unusual to have routers fail right out of the box, yours may be defective and your ISP should be able to help you rule this out.  As I said earlier, if the wireless portion of the network is isolated from the LAN it isn't a problem, you just need to log on to the router to change the setting, your ISP should be able to guide you through this or supply you with the information necessary to do the changes.  As for using a second router for your small LAN that isn't all that complicated, to keep things simple you just need to be able to log on to the Sky router and disable all the routing functions before you can add another router to your topology.
