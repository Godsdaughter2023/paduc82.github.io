arminatorx say:

[...]Is there something I need to do in network settings or on my router settings to facilitate a lower ping?[...]

First of all, you'll have to understand how Ping is measured, and you'll see that Ping is not a setting that you can easily tweak to get a desired Ping measurement. 

Since I don't know how knowledgeable you are with networking terms, I usually use a car analogy to better explain how ping works. 

Imagine the Internet is the public road system. When you buy a certain internet speed from your internet provider, you can imagine that as being your private driveway from your garage to the curb where your house is at the next public road. 

Your WiFi connection is comparable to an invisible tightrope that leads from your front door to the garage, whereas the wired connection is your standard path on the ground from your front door to the garage. 

Whenever you issue a Ping command, it's like taking a stop watch, and telling a race car driver to get to the destination you're pinging and then driving back. 

Let's assume you want to Ping the supermarket down the road. You'll hand your driver a test packet, and he'll either wobble through thin air or via the garden path to your garage while you start your stop watch. He'll get into the car, and speed down the driveway you got from your Internet provider. Then he'll join the public traffic on the road, and has to weave through lorries, taking detours because of road construction or accidents, and eventually arrive at the supermarket. There he'll exchange the test packet for a response test packet, return to the car, and again follows the road towards your house, stopping at red traffic lights, slowing down for trucks etc.

Eventually he'll turn onto your driveway, goes full throttle until he reaches the garage again, and then wobbles/walks back to your front door either via invisible tightrope or your garden path. 

As soon as he hands you the response packet, you'll stop the watch, and read out the time. 

That time is the Ping you just measured. 

So unless you are in control of the actual internet itself (not just your driveway to join the public road), you have no influence over the ping you get. 

Also, if you join a multiplayer match, you might be connected to a person that is on the other end of the world, living in a rural area with bad internet coverage. If you ping that person, your imaginary race driver would speed down your 16 or 100 lane driveway, join the public road system, drive to the nearest airport, fly across the globe, land, enter a new race car, speed along the highways towards the person you're playing with, then having to slow down, because the only way to reach the house of your play partner is via a rocky dirt road. 

After the race driver crawled along that dirt road, he'll hand your play partner the ping packet, receive a response packet, crawl back along that rocky dirt road, return to the airport, fly back, speed as fast as other traffic permits towards your home, and finally races up your driveway, resulting in a horrible ping time. 

You see: Ping is not a setting that you can simply modify to improve your ping. Ping depends a lot on how your whole road or town is connected by your Internet provider, and how clogged up the streets are that you have to go to reach your Ping destination. 

So you'll see why the small distance the "driver" has to traverse via invisible "WiFi" tightrope or standard ethernet garden path to the garage doesn't have much influence for your ping, when the actual traffic on the road, and the distance to that destination are much more affecting your ping that the method you're connecting to your router.
