Hi Paul,

In Office 365, by design, the default permission level of users’ calendars for internal users is set to Availability Only, which means the free/busy information is accessible by other users.

If you want to disable the sharing of the free/busy information, I suggest you use PowerShell to set the default permission level of all existing users’ calendar to None. Follow the steps below:
1. Connect to Exchange Online using remote PowerShell.
2. Run the cmdlet:
foreach($user in Get-Mailbox -RecipientTypeDetails UserMailbox) {$cal = $user.alias+":\Calendar" Set-MailboxFolderPermission -Identity $cal -User Default -AccessRights None}

For any newly created user, you can run the cmdlet below to set the default permission level to None:
Set-MailboxFolderPermission -Identity *** Email address is removed for privacy *** -User Default -AccessRights None

ellen finkelstein say:

Use a texture as a frame for your slides

Technical Level : Intermediate

Summary
This article will show you how to create an interesting background by using texture as a frame for your slides. 
Details

Do you want an interesting background but fret that it is distracting from your message? One way to resolve this conflict is to use a textured background around the edges of your slides. Here's an example.

The slide provides the steps, but I'll explain them in more detail.

  1. Take a photograph of something that has a nice texture. Another source is Stock Xchng's abstract textures.
  2. Open the Slide Master (View tab, Slide Master).
  3. Click the top thumbnail in the left pane (the Master Layout). I sometimes call this the Master's Master, because it controls formatting for all of the layouts in the Slide Master.
  4. Insert the photo (Insert tab, Pictures). Now, it covers all of the placeholders.
  5. Right-click the selected photo and choose Send to Back.
  6. Still in Slide Master view, click the Home tab. Click the Rectangle shape in the Drawing group. Drag a rectangle across the slide s text placeholder and the bottom-right corner of the body text placeholder as your corner points. Use the above image as an example to resize and move the rectangle into place.
  7. You need to move the rectangle behind all the placeholders but in front of the texture photo. The quickest way is to right-click it, choose Send to Back and then right-click again and choose Bring to Front, Bring Forward.
  8. Readjust the size of the placeholders to make them slightly smaller. If you don't do this, your text will seem to crowd the edges of the white rectangle. In other words, your text needs more of a margin around the edges. For the title text placeholder, adjust the top edge downward slightly and do the same for the left and right sides. Adjust the body text placeholder accordingly.

Here you can see some other photos I've taken that could be used as textured background frames.

ronaldoonenil say: Assuming your range of cells A1:E225 is on Sheet1, create a named range using the following in the refers to box. (Change the sheet name for your actual sheet name)

=OFFSET(Sheet1!$A$1,0,0,COUNTA(Sheet1!$A:$A),5)

In my example I have called this range MyRange

You can then change all your 20 COUNTIF cells similar to the following

=COUNTIF(MyRange,5)

As your range grows, the named range will automatically include the new rows so your COUNTIF formulae will not need to be changed to include those new rows.

amit tandon say:

Use below formula:

=COUNTIF(OFFSET(A1,,,F1,5),5)

Enter the number of rows in cell F1. If you enter 225 in F1 the formula will be equivalent to "=COUNTIF(A1:E225,5)". If you enter 450 in F1 the formula will be equivalent to "=COUNTIF(A1:E450,5)".

You may update the range per your requirement. However, in this case you will need to update formulas in all cells once initially and thereafter changing value of 1 cell will update the range in all formulas in one go (assuming the range in all formulas is the same).
