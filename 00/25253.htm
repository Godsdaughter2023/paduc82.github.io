Excluding words from the word count - Microsoft Community

ginger_claire say:

Excluding words from the word count

Is there a way to exclude words from the word count? For example I'm writing my dissertation and the words in tables do not count towards my 5,000 word limit and it is difficult to keep track. 

I believe I'm using the newest version of word on Office365 for mac if that helps.

macropod say:

Ignore the advice from Zixuan Yuan; it has nothing to do with what you're trying to achieve.

Word cannot exclude a table's contents from the word count. You could, however, use a macro to tally the table word counts and deduct those from the overall count, leaving you with the difference. For example:

Sub TableWordCount()
Dim Tbl As Table, d As Long, t As Long
With ActiveDocument
  For Each Tbl In .Tables
    With Tbl
      t = t + .Range.ComputeStatistics(wdStatisticWords)
    End With
  Next
  d = .Range.ComputeStatistics(wdStatisticWords)
End With
MsgBox "There are:" & vbCr & _
  d & " words in the document body, including" & vbCr & _
  t & " words in tables." & vbCr & vbCr & "There are" & vbCr & _
  d - t & " words in the document body, excluding tables."
End Sub

For PC macro installation & usage instructions, see: http://www.gmayor.com/installing_macro.htm
For Mac macro installation & usage instructions, see: https://wordmvp.com/Mac/InstallMacro.html

rob koch say:

ol' Codger,

I'm afraid you're kind of barking up the wrong tree here, so you're unlikely to receive any better response, since there simply isn't one.

The only people who could provide a precise answer to your questions are those developers whom originally created the core scanning engines for the Microsoft security programs over a decade ago in 2005/6, since that's where the source information comes from that's displayed in the several different graphical user interfaces (GUIs) that have been created since that time.

Unfortunately, those original developers have either moved on to different groups or left Microsoft altogether since that time.  I'm aware of this because I personally met with both the lead development project manager (PM) for both the original Windows Live OneCare, as well as Microsoft Security Essentials (MSE), along with several of the individual lead developers of the groups responsible for the initial OneCare product.  All of these had left their respective roles either when the OneCare project ended in late 2008 or at least by the time significant MSE development ended in 2011, as Windows Defender took its place.

There were only 2 Microsoft MVP (Most Valuable Professional award) recipients whom had regularly met with these developers at the time and I was one of them, while the other only rarely visits these forums any longer.

What I'm also more aware of than most is that over time, Microsoft developers have generally attempted to move the security programs further into the background, with less visual and manual controls and more automated functions.  This has meant that whatever is being displayed is often not as technically accurate as it might appear, meaning that trying to validate the specific information displayed is nearly impossible.

As I tried to convey in my last response, the only true reason these displays exist at all anymore is to appease those who tend to monitor them, since most consumers have learned to ignore them and let them operate on their own.

The problem in your case is clearly a large hard drive containing a significant number of compressed files, so a manual scan takes a long time to complete.  Since the Microsoft file scanner has always done what they at one time called a "deep" scan (e.g. exhaustive), this means these take longer than the equivalent scans from some 3rd-parties, which have often used several well-known programming "tricks" or assumptions in order to shorten the time required.

As I learned from that Microsoft PM lead in roughly 2010, they'd never intended that a full scan be used regularly.  Instead it was only meant to be performed after the initial installation or when a malware infection was either known to have occurred or suspected.  The need to do these manual scans regularly had ended long before when both real-time scanning and various monitoring functions were added to the operating system and critical applications such as the web browser.
