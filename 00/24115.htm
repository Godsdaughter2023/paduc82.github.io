suzanne s. barnhill say:

The person who created the document created a manual TOC because he/she was unaware that this is something Word can do automatically and/or was unable to do so because the text had not been formatted properly for the purpose.

A TOC can be based on styles, outline levels, or TC fields or some combination of the three. By default, it is based on Word's built-in heading styles, which have associated outline levels (Level 1 for Heading 1, Level 2 for Heading 2, etc.), along with the Title (Level 1) and Subtitle (Level 2) styles. Outline levels can be assigned to other styles either through the Table of Contents Options dialog or as part of the style definition (Paragraph dialog), and the TOC level used for the built-in heading styles can be changed using the TOC Options (or they can be omitted altogether by deleting the level number).

What can't be done through any TOC formatting is to omit a portion of a paragraph that is included. That is, if an entire paragraph is in Heading 1 style (or any other included style) or any other style with an outline level other than Body Text, then the entire paragraph will be included in the TOC.

That being the case, there are two ways to include just a portion of a paragraph in the TOC:

  1. Insert a style separator or a Hidden paragraph break between the "heading" portion and the rest of the paragraph and apply a different style to the remainder of the paragraph. See http://sbarnhill.mvps.org/WordFAQs/RunInSidehead.htm.
  2. Alternatively, create TC fields with the desired TOC entry content and base the TOC only on "Table entry fields," deselecting "Styles" and "Outline levels" in the TOC Options. More on TC fields can be found at http://office.microsoft.com/en-us/word-help/field-codes-tc-table-of-contents-entry-field-HP005186197.aspx. You can quickly create a TC field based on selected text by pressing ALT+SHIFT+O.

For more tips on TOC formatting, see http://sbarnhill.mvps.org/WordFAQs/TOCTips.htm.

Excel Sumif (adding and subtracting)

a b c d e
1 When? Who? Where? + (Credit) - (Expenses)
2 1/1/2018 Spouse 1 Company 1 100.00
3 1/2/2018 Spouse 1 Company 1 50.00
4 1/3/2018 Spouse 2 Company 2 200.00
5 1/4/2018 Spouse 2 Company 2 100.00
6 Total 150 300

Total   (Expenses)       = 150            (Expenses (300) - Credit (150))

Spouse 1   =   50  (100   -   50)

Spouse 2   =  100 (200   -  100)  

^this is what I had in mind

The formula that I entered: 

Spouse 1: sumif(a2:a5, "Spouse 1",e2:e5) - sumif(a2:a5, "Spouse 1", d2:d5)

Spouse 2: sumif(a2:a5, "Spouse 2",e2:e5) - sumif(a2:a5, "Spouse 2", d2:d5)

Total: sum(e2:e5)-sum(d2:d5)

What I should expect is for the sum and the total of both Spouse 1 and 2 to be the same, but I'm getting different numbers for both. The total of both Spouse 1 and 2 still comes shorter than the total sum of both, and I'm thinking that it's because the formula is mistakenly calculating all the credit expenses for both, except when I add the credit to both Spouse 1 and 2 to the calculations that I received for both Spouse 1 and 2, it's still not the same number as the sum of the total of both spouses. hansv mvp say:

Congratulations on getting married!

The names are in column B, not in column A, so you should use

=SUMIF(B1:B5,"Spouse 1",E1:E5)-SUMIF(B1:B5,"Spouse 1",D1:D5)

and

=SUMIF(B1:B5,"Spouse 2",E1:E5)-SUMIF(B1:B5,"Spouse 2",D1:D5)

With the sample data in your post, these formulas return 50 and 100, respectively. The sum is 150, which equals E6-D6 = 300-150.
