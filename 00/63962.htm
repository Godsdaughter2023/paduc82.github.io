Everything you've described is how a scam or con (Confidence scheme) actually works.  The scammer uses various items and names a person might already trust, such as Microsoft in this case, to convince the mark (you) to trust the con artist as well.

Once they've gained your trust via this (typically untrue) association, they move on to tricking you into doing something you might not otherwise do, such as giving them access to your computer, believing whatever they say about the computer's security or even paying them money for mostly worthless or free items.

Microsoft Safety and Security Center - Avoiding technical support scams

I don't know how you'd have found a certificate indicating that Microsoft was the verified publisher for an app clearly produced by someone else, but my guess would be that either the certificate was somehow falsified (unlikely) or possibly you were simply confused by something else displayed during the session with the fake tech support personnel?  It's also possible that the certificate was provided by Microsoft for the purposes of signing apps, but that itself doesn't mean that the app signed with it was produced my Microsoft, since other vendors like Symantec provide signing certificates as well.
