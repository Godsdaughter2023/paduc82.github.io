bob jones aka: cybertaz say:

By using the Tab key at the beginning of a paragraph you didn't [technically] indent :-), you tabbed. If you now attempt to adjust the First Line Indent to something less than where the Tab Stop is positioned it will have no effect. That sounds like what you're describing.

If you turn on the nonprinting characters [¶] you will most likely see an arrow [→] at the beginning of the paragraphs that are giving you the problem. Clear those tabbings & you should be able to set the Left Indent.

I'm sure there are ways to expedite the process, but knowing nothing about the structural details of the document it's difficult to offer a suggestion. One that may help without causing any problems elsewhere is to use Advanced Find & Replace to locate ^p^t & replace with ^p.

You can then set an actual First Line Indent at any point you wish.

Before you write your next novel ;-) you might do well to learn more about the use of Styles. By setting the First Line Indent (along with any other formatting) as a part of a Style you can avoid such issues altogether. Also, if you later decide to adjust you need only modify the Style. Any paragraphs having that Style applied will be reformatted to the new specification instantly.

I have done his many times with Word docs, but I'm faced with an Excel at the moment that has a box I'd like to check, but I can't figure out how to check the box. Can you help? Thanks.
jim gordon mvp say:

A single click checks a box.

A double-click checks the box and then unchecks it again.


The control must be linked to an unlocked cell. If the linked cell is locked, then the form can't change the linked cell's value to change the state of the check box.
