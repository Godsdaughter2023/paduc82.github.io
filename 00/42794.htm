Can I switch my gamertag to a different Microsoft account?

You can change the Microsoft account that is associated with your Xbox LIVE gamertag. Here’s how:

  1. Sign in using your Xbox LIVE gamertag.
  2. Go to Settings and select Account.
  1. Scroll right to Your Information and select Account Security.
  2. Select Change Windows Live ID. (Your Windows Live ID is your Microsoft account.)

    Do one of the following:

    • If you have another Microsoft account, select Yes.
    • If you do not have another Microsoft account, go to accounts.live.com and click Sign up. After you have another Microsoft account set up, select Yes and continue with the next step.
  3. Enter the password for the Microsoft account that is currently associated with your gamertag, and then select Sign in.
  4. Enter the email address and the password for the new Microsoft account that you want to associate with your gamertag, and then select Sign in.
  5. Select Yes, change to change the Microsoft account associated with your Xbox LIVE account.
  6. Select Update Contact Information to update your contact information or Done to finish.

Notes

  • When you change your Microsoft account, your Microsoft Points are automatically transferred to the new Microsoft account.
  • You can make only one Microsoft account change for a given gamertag every 30 days.
  • You must also wait 30 days after your gamertag is downloaded from Xbox LIVE before you can switch that gamertag to a different Microsoft account. If 30 days have not passed since the gamertag was downloaded, you’ll get an error message.
  • Before you can change the Microsoft account associated with a child account, you must provide the Microsoft account information for the adult account that has parental control.

tricky300 say:

Hmmm.

 

Whist I suspect that Richard is correct that you do not have any network drives (based on your own description of yourself), it does not necessarily follow that a standalone PC does not or cannot have any network drives. There is nothing to say that someone couldn't create a network drive on their own standalone PC, for example to a deep subfolder of "My Documents". This might be to facilitate browsing to that subfolder without having to navigate the folder structure. As an actual example...

 

You might have a folder structure in My Documents as follows...

 

My Documents\A Folder\Another Folder\Yet Another Folder

 

You could create a network drive directly to "Yet Another Folder", which might be given the drive letter Z (just as drives/partitions on the PC itself are given a letter, so are all network drives). If you then open My Computer and then open the Z drive you get straight to the contents of "Yet Another Folder" without having to navigate through all the intervening folders.

 

Normally this would be done to folders that actually reside on other PCs in a network of PCs, but it isn't forced to be that way.

 

In the simplest XP environment you will have a C drive and almost certainly a D drive. The C drive will be your main bootable drive that contains Windows, installed programs and all your data. The D drive will be your optical drive. A and B are typically reserved for floppy disk drives and not used if the PC doesn't have any floppy disk drives. If you created a network drive on your PC, or any other PC, the drive letter given to it could be any of the letters E through to Z.

 

If for example you had two optical drives, these would probably be lettered D and E leaving F through to Z free for future devices added or network drives. If you have a card reader, this will also be given a drive letter (multi format card readers will be given more than one driver letter). External disk drives and USB memory sticks will also be given a drive letter (or letters) as will USB/card reader slots on attached printers.

 

Open My Computer and examine the drive letters listed. If you have any network drives they will have been given a letter and probably described as being "on PC" where PC is the name/description of the PC where the data resides, which might also be your PC.

 

Like Richard, my bet is that you won't have any.
