It won't do at present because you are not using the SUM operator.  The following would sum the hours worked per employee per shift date.  Is that what you want?

 

PARAMETERS [Forms]![frmSwitchboard].[txtStartDate] DATETIME,

SELECT tbluEmployees.EmployeeID, tblShiftDay.ShiftDate

SUM(tblShiftMachinesRan.HrsWorked) AS TotalHours

FROM tbluProducts INNER JOIN (tbluEmployees INNER JOIN ((tblShiftDay INNER JOIN tblShiftMachinesRan ON tblShiftDay.ShiftDayID = tblShiftMachinesRan.ShiftDayID) INNER JOIN tblMachineOutput ON tblShiftMachinesRan.ShiftMachineID = tblMachineOutput.ShiftMachineID) ON tbluEmployees.EmployeeID = tblShiftMachinesRan.EmployeeID) ON tbluProducts.ProductID = tblMachineOutput.ProductID

WHERE tblShiftDay.ShiftDate BETWEEN [Forms]![frmSwitchboard].[txtStartDate]

AND [Forms]![frmSwitchboard].[txtEndDate] AND tbluEmployees.EmployeeID

= [Forms].[frmSwitchboard].[SubformTreeMenu].[Form].[cboEmployees]

GROUP BY tbluEmployees.EmployeeID, tblShiftDay.ShiftDate;

 

Note:

 

1. The parameters are declared, which is always wise for parameters of date/time data type to avoid their being inadvertently misinterpreted as arithmetical expressions.

 

2.  The use of the WHERE clause rather than a HAVING clause to restrict the result on EmployeeID.  The latter operates after grouping and us used to restrict a query's results on the basis of an aggregated value, the former operates before grouping, which is what you want here.

 

3.  Is the reason you used the LIKE operator and asterisk wildcard character to allow you to leave the [cboEmployees] empty?  If so the way to do this would be:

 

WHERE tblShiftDay.ShiftDate BETWEEN [Forms]![frmSwitchboard].[txtStartDate]

AND [Forms]![frmSwitchboard].[txtEndDate] AND (tbluEmployees.EmployeeID

= [Forms].[frmSwitchboard].[SubformTreeMenu].[Form].[cboEmployees]

OR [Forms].[frmSwitchboard].[SubformTreeMenu].[Form].[cboEmployees] IS NULL)

GROUP BY tbluEmployees.EmployeeID, tblShiftDay.ShiftDate;

 

Note the inclusion of the OR operation within parentheses to force it to evaluate independently of the AND operations.

ken sheridan say:
...............where would I even use the /COUNT(*)?

You'd add it to whatever part of the expression is incorrectly multiplying by the number of rows per grouping.  So if you start with:

SUM(abc)/SUM(xyz)

it could be:

(SUM(abc)/SUM(xyz))/COUNT(*)

to reduce the overall result by a factor of n, where n is the number of rows per grouping.

or;

(SUM(abc)/COUNT(*))/SUM(xyz)

to reduce only the result of the summation of the abc values by a factor of n.

or similarly:

SUM(abc)/(SUM(xyz)/COUNT(*))

to reduce only the result of the summation of the xyz values by a factor of n.
