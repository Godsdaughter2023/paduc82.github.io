Full version (Retail):

- Includes transfer rights to another computer.

- Doesn't require a previous qualifying version of Windows.

- Expensive

Upgrade version (Retail):

- Includes transfer rights to another computer.

- require a previous qualifying version of Windows.

- Expensive, but cheaper than full version

OEM System Builder version:

OEM versions of Windows 7 are identical to Full License Retail versions except for the following:

- OEM versions do not offer any free Microsoft direct support from Microsoft support personnel

- OEM licenses are tied to the very first computer you install and activate it on

There is nothing wrong with using it too, especially since all future releases of Windows will be full versions anyway.

    1.  Pull the disk out of the computer and attach it to a working system.  The easiest way to do this is by using an external disk enclosure, but you can also put the disk in an available bay in a desktop/tower or get an adapter kit or cable.  Be sure to get a device that's compatible with the type of disk you have.

    2.  Use a boot disk.  This can be a live Linux distro like Knoppix or some sort of "rescue" disk like Hiren's or UBCD.  For example only: https://www.makeuseof.com/tag/5-best-rescue-disks-windows-system-restore/

    bossdweeb say:

    You don't say which monitors you find inaccurate, how you determine they are inaccurate,

    or by what range they are inaccurate.

    Some monitors measure each core while others average the whole cpu. The different cores do

    not run at the same temp. Partly due to load on that core, but also due to it's position on in the cpu.

     All you really need is a close figure to know if there is a heat issue.

    I just ran the tool that comes with my AsRock motherboard software (averages whole cpu), Hardware Monitor

    and Real Temp and the three concurred with each other. Not exactly, but within a couple of degrees +/- 

    ( about 33 C for the warmest cores with four browser tabs open and a total of 55 processes, atm - Intel i5 3570 ).
    - The motherboard also factors into accuracy, as this is where the sensors are located.

    'Modern' 4/6/8 core AMD cpu's have a lower thermal protection throttling temp than Intel,

    but both generally run at similar temps.

    It is recommended to keep these AMD cpu's below 62 C for stable operation though.

    Depending on ambient air temps, my  PhenomII 965 runs around 50C average under load,

    but will push  60 C on hot days.

    I use the small 90cm fan version of Noctua cpu cooler with this cpu.

    (btw - The old AMD Athlon single core were room heaters that ran in the 80's C under load )

    - The motherboard temp should generally be around 40-45 C. Over 50 C may be a worry.

    The most popular coolers are the ones that don't blow toward the motherboard. Probably because

    they  exhaust better. Cooling is as much about exhausting hot air and good air-flow,  as it is aiming air

    toward the components.

    I have the one on my AMD cpu pointing toward the top exhaust on the case (only one fan on my Noctua though).

    The motherboard for this machine uses an SB770 chipset.

    I don't overclock the cpu, but the stock coolers get loud when under load, so the more quiet running is my

    main attraction to the aftermarket coolers.

    The one on my Intel machine (Coolermaster Hyper 212 EVO) points toward the rear exhaust, as do most.

    If your motherboard chipset is getting hot it may be due to poor case cooling or bad cable management

    blocking the air flow.  If the motherboard temp is high you can verify if the case flow is good by removing the

    side panel and aiming a small desk fan into the case.

    If the temps drop the case air flow could do with improvement.

    Yes, and I don't think you have to worry, but the better test of cooling efficiency is under full load,

    On those occasions when the temps go to 35+ C  I tend to keep gaming sessions short, not radically short.

    About an hour.

    The theory being that avoiding sustained periods of high temps should promote longer hardware life.

    Theory being the operative word. I don't know if it actually matters if the cpu is hitting 60C for four hrs straight

    ( I believe AMD thermal protection clock speed throttling is supposed to kick in around 70+ C ),

    is kept out of corners or confined spaces.

    - Temps rise quickly under load,  but should remain fairly stable once they reach
