ken blake - microsoft mvp say:
On Thu, 23 Sep 2010 14:00:32 +0000, muambs wrote:
 
> how long the startup should be take?
 
 
 
There is no answer to this question that's right for everyone. It
depends on the specifics are your hardware, as well as what you have
starting automatically when you boot.
 
My personal view is that the attention many people pay to how long it
takes to boot is unwarranted. Assuming that the computer's speed is
otherwise satisfactory, it is not generally worth worrying about. Most
people start their computers once a day or even less frequently. In
the overall scheme of things, even a few minutes to start up isn't
very important. Personally I power on my computer when I get up in the
morning, then go get my coffee. When I come back, it's done booting. I
don't know how long it took to boot and I don't care.
 
However if you do want to address it, and assuming that you are not
infected with malware (a common problem these days) it may be because
of what programs start automatically, and you may want to stop some of
them from starting that way. On each program you don't want to start
automatically, check its Options to see if it has the choice not to
start (make sure you actually choose the option not to run it, not
just a "don't show icon" option). Many can easily and best be stopped
that way. If that doesn't work, run MSCONFIG from the Start | Run
line, and on the Startup tab, uncheck the programs you don't want to
start automatically.
 
However, if I were you, I wouldn't do this just for the purpose of
running the minimum number of programs. Despite what many people tell
you, you should be concerned, not with how *many* of these programs
you run, but *which*. Some of them can hurt performance severely, but
others have no effect on performance.
 
Don't just stop programs from running willy-nilly. What you should do
is determine what each program is, what its value is to you, and what
the cost in performance is of its running all the time. You can get
more information about these with internet searches and asking about
specifics here.
 
Once you have that information, you can make an intelligent informed
decision about what you want to keep and what you want to get rid of.

cmdrkeene say:

Windows does not itself provide any edge swipe options or functionality, it's something that gets added later by the PC maker.  Some PC makers put the options into the Mouse Control Panel, others supply their own settings panel.  Some, like Toshiba, make you go download the Touchpad software yourself from the Toshiba website (they don't include it with the PC).

The best thing you can do is contact the manufacturer, because all of them put the options somewhere else.

djkirkus say:

Lenovo Yoga Pro (may help on other devices too)

1. Open the Control Panel 
  (RIGHT click the windows icon in the lower left corner of the screen and select "Control Panel" from the menu that appears)

   The "Control Panel" will appear

2. In the upper right of the Control Panel, select either View by: "Small icons" or "Large icons"
   (If you stay in the default View by: Categories, you'll go crazy trying to move forward!)
3. Click on the mouse icon "Mouse"
   The "Mouse Properties" window will appear
4. Select the "Device Settings" tab
5. Click the "Settings..." button in the middle on the right side of the window
WAIT - the next window my not appear quickly
   The "Synaptics Control Panel" will appear (it may take a moment)
6. On the "One Finger" tab, deselect/uncheck "Enable Edge Swipes"

   (Its at the top of the lower section)

7. Click "OK" at the bottom of the window.

8. Rejoice! Your pain has ended.
