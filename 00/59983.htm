DX12 is more complicated that previous DX versions.

- Without going into all of the gpu models and which level they support you can rest assured that

the Display tab in dxdiag  is showing what your card is capable of running, and that your Win10 does have DX12.

- Some gpu's support some of what MS calls  DirectX "Feature Levels", but only the most recent gpu's support

all  DX12 Feature Levels. It is only with these graphics cards that you will see DX12 reported

( eg- my GTX980 shows DX 12.1 & 12.0 and so forth, down to 9 ) .

- Graphics cards that are capable of some DX12 Feature Levels will be reported as DX 11.2 , 11.3 or 11.4

More details here


btw -  Currently there are only two graphics related DX12 files - D3D12.dll . One in System32 and sysWOW64.

MTU (or maximum transmittable unit) is the UPPER limit of a packet size in the network.

Let's compare it to the postal service. They can tell you the maximum size of a packet to be 30x40x60 cm and not exceed 10kg.

Therefore you know you will be able to send a tiny pea in a 30x40x60cm packet, and it will be delivered as quickly as a 30x40x60 cm parcel full of stones that weigh exactly 10 kg.

Xbox Live has as a requirement a minimum of 1364 bytes as a MTU.

So this means, that usually game data won't usually exceed that limit, which means your game data packets won't be fragmented because your opponent has a different MTU.

So as long as you can connect to Xbox Live, you don't have to worry about MTU. It does NOT have to be altered or tweaked. And you won't get performance benefits or penalties for a different sized MTU, as long as its larger than 1364.

lemp say:

Whenever you have problems printing to a networked printer, the first thing to check is whether the port assigned to the printer on the computer uses the same IP address as the one assigned to the printer by your router (assumes the "networked printer" is actually connected to a router and not just a shared resource connected to a computer on the network).

To check:

  1. Right-click the icon for the printer in Devices and Printers and select "Printer properties."
  2. Click the "Ports" tab
  3. If the printer is connected to the router, the checked port will look something like the following (that is, it should be a "Standard TCP/IP Port" or possibly an HP TCP/IP Port with an IP address).  You may have to expand the "Port" column as shown below in order to see the complete IP address:


  4. Now that you know what IP address the computer "thinks" the printer is located at, use the printer's front panel controls to display the IP address that the printer is using.  See your printer's User Guide for instructions.

If the printer has been assigned a different IP address (by your router's DHCP server) than the one configured on the computer, the print job will go nowhere.  Usually, the job just disappears from the queue without a complaint, but perhaps the newer printer drivers have become cleverer about noticing this problem.

If that is the problem, the solution is to configure the printer with a static IP address and then uninstall/reinstall the printer so that the computer uses that new static IP address.  If this is, in fact, your problem and you need help in configuring a static IP address, post back.
