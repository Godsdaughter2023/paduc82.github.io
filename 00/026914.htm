suzanne s. barnhill say:

An automatically generated table of contents in Word is a TOC field. If you have field shading set to "When selected" or "Always" (File | Options | Advanced | Show document content), you will see this shading. More likely, though, you've inserted one of the "automatic" selections from the Table of Contents gallery, which is inserted as a sort of content control field, and it does show a blue shading when you mouse over it and some buttons at the top to update the TOC or access the TOC dialog. This is by design.

If your first heading is being included in this shading, then you have somehow gotten it inside the TOC field or the content control. You should see the content control surrounded by a blue box. If your heading is inside that, then you have created it in the empty paragraph that Word automatically includes after the TOC, and you will need to delete the text from there and re-enter it below the box. If the heading is actually inside the TOC field (which you can display by pressing Alt+F9), then you may be able to Cut/Paste it from there to a location outside the field.

The important thing is that you must leave an (apparently) empty paragraph between the TOC field and any following text.

suzanne s. barnhill say:

In Office 2010,Table of Contents is on the References tab, and if you click that button, you get a gallery that includes two automatic TOCs (field-generated) and one manual one, plus the Insert Table of Contents command, which opens the familiar dialog. That last is what I recommend because it is the only way in Word 2010 to get just a TOC field without the extra baggage of the building block thingie in the gallery (which includes a heading that says "Table of Contents" or "Contents").

Between TOC and following text is one of the very few locations where an empty paragraph is recommended. Because of the way Word displays the TOC, it is all too easy to get following text inside the TOC field or at least in the paragraph in which it sits. I find this so confusing that I have never taken the time to entirely sort out how it works; I just do whatever I have to do to make sure following content doesn't get slurped in. I suspect that anomaly is part of the reason Word 2007 and 2010 have these built-in TOCs surrounded by a blue box, to make it clear that you should not type anything else there.

As Alan has said, you can set field shading to "Never," but if you're seeing the shading when you just mouse over the TOC, I doubt that what you're seeing is field shading, which would be grey and would appear either all the time ("Always") or only when you clicked in the TOC ("When selected"), whereas you seem to be seeing the light-blue shading when you mouse over the TOC, which is what happens when you insert one of the built-in ones from the gallery.

With regard to field shading, I prefer to keep it set to "When selected" so that I can easily see the extent of fields I have inserted.

brian tillman [outlook mvp 2007-2019] say:

The symptoms you describe indicate that your Outlook window is positioned off of the screen. You should be able to get it back on the screen by one of several ways.

One way uses the steps you describe: hold Shift as you right-click the Task Bar handle and choose Move (or if you have Aero Peeks enabled, hover over the handle to display the peek, right-click the peek, and choose Move). This will cause the mouse pointer to jump to the hidden window. Press one of the stand-alone arrow keys to lock the pointer to the title bar of that window. You can then use the mouse to position the hidden window back on the screen. By dragging the mouse around some, a portion of the window should move back onto the screen so you can tell where it is and can drag it where you want. Click the mouse button and it will drop the window at the new location.

Another way is to right-click the Task Bar and choose Cascade windows, Show windows stacked, or Show windows side by side. This will reconfigure your desktop so all windows are on the desktop. You can then rearrange them as you see fit.
