tom van stiphout say:

The reason this is difficult to you is because the database design is incorrect. I'm guessing CL, CPR, FA are different kinds of certifications that expire on that date.

Your current design is called a "repeating group" and it violates proper database design rules.

Rather you would design like this, realizing there is a many-to-many relationship between employees and certifications: each employee has 0 or more certs, and each cert is held by 0 or more employees.

tblEmployees

EmployeeID PK Autonumber
EmployeeFirstName text(20) required
EmployeeLastName (text(30) required
'etc.

tblCertifications

CertificationID PK AutoNumber

CertificationAbbrev text(3) required uniqueindex

CertificationName (text(80) required uniqueindex

And now the junction table to express the M:M relationship:

tblCertsPerEmployee
CertificationID long int PK

EmployeeID long int PK

ExpirationDate datetime required

Then open the Relations window, put the tables on the design surface, and drag-n-drop between the corresponding fields. Then importantly in the dialog that pops up check the box to Enforce Referential Integrity. Congrats, you just created a Relational database.

Now, as I'm sure you will realize if you play with it a bit, your query becomes trivial. Enjoy the first benefit of working within the Relational framework.

marshall barton say:

One minor point in addition to what Tom said about designing tables.  A UNION query only returns unique records, i.e. no duplicates.  Since all your records are unique, because of the ID field, it is at least a waste of time to ask the query to check for duplicate records.  That means you should use UNION ALL instead of just UNION.

In other situations where the records might not be unique, give some thought to whether you want all the records or only the non duplicate records.
