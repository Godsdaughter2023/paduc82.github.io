Hi there,

I'm sorry to hear about the trouble playing games online due to a moderate NAT type. I'd be happy to provide some information.

NAT stands for Network Address Translation. NAT is typically done by your router/internet device and is a security measure, what it basically does is mask your IP address either your source or destination IP address. Most systems that use NAT (like Xbox) do so for multiple hosts or computers that are using your internet connection and that's what happens when you play multiplayer. For that reason NAT is important to stay OPEN to make sure your connection is safe.

Mobile hotspots are difficult to fix because there aren't many configuration options available since it comes from your mobile device. The moderate NAT type may be due to some difficulties with your service provider and the hotspot can't keep a secure connection for multiplayer gaming. It's not impossible to game with that NAT type but it will be difficult to play with other's.

ken blake say:

Three points:

1. If I acquired a used computer, no matter who previously owned it, the
first thing I would do with it would be to reinstall the operating
system cleanly. You have no idea how the computer has been maintained,
what has been installed incorrectly, what is missing, what viruses and
spyware there may be, etc. I wouldn't want to live with somebody
else's mistakes and problems, possibility of kiddy p0rn, etc., and I
wouldn't recommend that anyone else do so either.

Your choice of course, but in my view, you're playing with fire if you
don't reinstall Windows cleanly.

2. If you don't have installation disks (or a recovery partition that the manufacturer provided; (if you haven't already, you should check into that with the manufacturer--the partition may be hidden), to do a reinstallation you will need to buy a copy.

3. You say "Also, when I do, I want to repartition so there is a recovery drive." Exactly what do you mean by that? Do you mean the same sort of thing that manufacturers provide to let you reinstall from it, or are you talking about  a backup drive?

If you mean the former, you don't need it if you have an installation disk. In fact an installation disk is better, so you won't lose it if the drive dies. One of my standard recommendations to anyone with a new computer is to follow the manufacturer's instructions to copy the recovery drive to a DVD.

If you mean the latter, I don't recommend backup to a second non-removable hard drive (or worse a second partition on your only drive) because it leaves you susceptible to simultaneous loss of the original and backup to many of the most common dangers: severe power glitches, nearby lightning strikes, virus attacks, even theft of the computer (and for a second partition, death of the drive).

In my view, secure backup needs to be on removable media, and not kept in the computer. For really secure backup (needed, for example, if the life of your business depends on your data) you should have multiple generations of backup, and at least one of those generations should be stored off-site.
