Background information and understanding about fonts and Office 2011:

In regard to user downloaded fonts and MS Office 2011, John McGhie writes in a post thread with subject:  "Office 2011 for MAC - Crashes in Normal Mode."  [Thread link].

"Fonts to be suspicious of are non-TrueType fonts.  Microsoft Office will crash if offered non-standard TrueType fonts.  It cannot use Type 1 fonts at all.  And it can use Open Type Fonts ONLY if they contain TrueType characters (which most of them do, get back to us if you do not know how to check for this). PostScript fonts, and raster fonts (Type 1) cannot be used by Office, and won't be displayed.  But they shouldn't crash it. You should be extremely suspicious of freeware TrueType fonts: those that don't follow all the rules can crash Word."

To install or not install Office standard fonts only?

This background should help you understand the issues around fonts, and help you decide what the best choice is for you.

There is another reason apart from the risk of Office crashes for not using fonts other than the Office standard set.  Its the visual accuracy of how other people see your documents when you share them.  

If you use some downloaded fonts of your own in an Excel spread sheet or Word document, and send that spreadsheet or document to another person, if their Mac or Window machine does not have a copy of that special downloaded font, how can their computer display correctly your file in Excel or Word?  

The fact is it can not. 

What typically happens is the application at the recipients end will replace the special font you have used in your sent document with a standard font like Arial, just so that the file can be opened and displayed on the other persons computer. 

The recipient will still have the words you have typed, but with the font changed, the appearance of the document may be significantly different.  For common types of files it probably does not make much difference, but imagine a carefully sculptured Wedding Invitation design in MS Word, with elaborate special flowing font.  It wont look the same with the text rendered in Times or Arial font.

This problem is not just with MS Office.  If you are using uncommon fonts, and sending files to others from any application, then this issue can happen.  This includes emails by the way.  The problem arises irrespective of the OS you or the recipient is using.

A different solution some applications use with non standard fonts is to load a copy of the fonts used in the document you have created, and send a copy of your special font with the document. Their same program at the recipients end will then render the document as you intended, as it also has a copy of the special fonts you used.   The downside to this solution is that it makes for much bigger files to transfer.  Font files can be multi-megabytes in size.

From what I can see, MS Office solves the problem slightly differently, if you opt for their solution. 

It simply supplies a standard set of fonts. When you initially instal Office 2011, you can set Office to only use those standard fonts in all Office applications.  This works as a solution, as your documents will only contain the same standard fonts as held by all other Office users, Mac and Windows. If they have Office, they have the same fonts installed on their computer.  Problem solved!

However, this means you can not make documents in Office using non standard (but still compatible with Office) fonts that you may have downloaded onto your computer.  You may not like this.  You may want a lot more variety and choice in the fonts you can use than the standard Office font set.  

If you are only printing your work with special fonts yourself, then, provided the fonts do not crash Office, there is no problem with using these fonts.  This leaves you with lots of creative freedom in what you can design and create.  As you are not sending your work with unusual fonts to others to edit or appreciate just as you create it, its not an issue.

Consequently, with installation, they give you a choice of what you want to do. Have Office use only a limited set of standard MS Office fonts, or access all your fonts in your Font Library.  (At least, that is my memory of the installation process a year or more ago. )

I chose for Office to access all my fonts.  A choice I regret just for the moment given I need to have MS Office  access only standard fonts to test if the crash problems I am having are being caused by non standard fonts.   (Before this I did not regret my choice.  I don't send documents with fancy fonts to others, but I can do some home printing with them if I want. Cool!)

Making your decision. A few more tips and work arounds with fonts to consider.

If you work with unusual creative fonts, and you send those docs generally to the same people, collaborators, to view and/or edit your work, you can ask your collaborators to download and install the same set of special fonts you use in your documents onto their computers. They can then see your docs with the correct font rendered, just as you want them to. 

If you use creative unusual fonts, and you just want others to be able to view what your work looks like, but not edit and change and evolve your work on their computer to send back, then the simple solutions is to send images of what your final work looks like rather than the file itself.  For example, take screen pics of your work. No exchange of fonts needed.  Good if you just want to check they are happy with the look of what you are doing.  You will still need the fonts you have used to be available to where ever you print this work. If you are printing it yourself, no problem.

How can you tell what you chose when you installed Office? 

Open a Word or Excel document and look at the drop down font list in the Menu Bar at the top. If you have a long list of fonts available to you, then you are using more than just the Office standard fonts.

Here are the MS Office standard fonts in the OS X Font Book. This is all of them. Hence why if you selected Office fonts only the font list in the drop down menu should be quite a short list.

SOLUTION:

Opening Office 2011 applications without User Installed Fonts being loaded.

(And without a full removal and re-install of Office.)

1. Quit All Office applications.

2.  Open the Font Book application. (Installed with the OS in your applications folder.)

3. Under Menu >File, choose >Restore Standard Fonts...

This will first explain what it will do before doing it, so relax.  It removes, but does not delete, your user added fonts. It puts them in a different folder, where in later you can put them back into your Font Book.

4. In the Finder Menu, Select >Go and while the drop down menu is visible press the option key so that >Library appears in the drop down list.  Select Library.  A window showing your user account's Library Files should open on your Desktop. 

5.   In that window open the following folders, one inside the other:  >Application Support, then >Microsoft, then >Office, then >Preferences, then >Office 2011. In this last folder, select the file "Office Font Cache" and drag it to the desktop or trash.  Leave this window open so you can see what happens.

6. Open Office Word or Excel.  When Office finds this file missing, it rebuilds it.  Since you have removed all the user fonts from the Font Book, it can only build the list from what is available in the Font Book.  So the new rebuilt font list telling Excel and Word what fonts are available has been reconstructed using only OS X and Office standard fonts.  Done!

Because this new list will include all the Mac Standard OS fonts (as well as the MS Office standard fonts) it will still be a long font list once you check the available fonts in the drop down menu in Word or Excel.  Go on  .. go have a look!   What should be missing is all the User installed fonts.  These are the ones that are likely to be causing problems and crashes. (If any of them are, that is!)

So there you have a solution. 

If you are doing all this to problem solve a crash or other issue in Office, and the crash or other problem does not go away, then the problem was likely not in your user added fonts.  I suggest load your fonts back in and enjoy them.

On the other hand if you do this, and your problems in Office 2011 apps go away, you have a bad font or perhaps several bad fonts in your user fonts causing the problem. You need to identify which ones are the problem ones.

Also solution to identifying problem fonts if you have some:

There are a few ways to go about this, each with different levels of robustness and time involved.

The standard and most robust way is unfortunately slow.  You need to quit your Office apps, then load your removed fonts back into Font Book in batches of 5 or 10. Opening your Office app after you add each batch, and seeing if the crash or problem returns.

If after adding a batch brings the problem back, then remove that batch, then load the fonts in that batch one at a time, until by a process of elimination, you identify the culprit font or fonts. If you have a lot of user fonts, this could take a long time.

An alternative way involves remembering the description of types of fonts that can crash Office quoted earlier in this post.

In Font Book menu, choose >Preview and select >Show Font Info. Instead of seeing an example of the fonts, you see technical summary information of each font.  Now you can go through your font list and check for font types that, in the above description, are likely a problem for Office. Remove those and see if the crash or issue is resolved.  This may be a quicker route than adding all back in batches and testing.

A third option.  If you look back in the OS window where you removed the "Office Font Cache" you should see that it has been replaced (updated) and the modified date of the file now the current date and time. 

You can go back and look at the modified date of the file you removed, either on your desktop or in the trash, to see the difference.  It probably has the modified date of when you installed Office, or the last time you installed new fonts on your computer, and hence the cache file was updated.   If you have had crashes due to bad fonts you have added, this modification date is probably also the date you added the problem fonts.  Could help you narrow down your search for the culprits.

These are the options I have found.  The alternatives beyond this are, leave user fonts removed from your available fonts to all applications. Essentially, abandon those lovely fonts you spent hours downloading.  Or finally, just putting up with the problem that brings you here (if it is not to serious).

Some further thoughts:

Some may argue the steps 4 and 5 in my solution (to find and remove the "Office Font Cache" file) is not necessary. 

I think this step is important to ensure there is no legacy issues from troublesome fonts.  Even if they are no longer available to load, this ensures Office apps don't even look for them and cause a problem in the process of not finding them.

When I first looked at the "Office Font Cache" file, the modification date was back to when I installed Office. I have probably not added any new fonts in that time.  I suspect this file gets checked every time an Office app opens, but is only updated if there is a change in available fonts. In which case, having removed it once, it probably does not need to be removed each time you add a batch of fonts to seek a problem font.  I am not certain of this. Perhaps someone else can check this and add a post.

If this solution takes you to places on your Mac you have not been to before, I strongly recommend not interfering with any other files in the Library files unless you know quite specifically what you are doing.  Having Office not work is one thing, having your entire computer not work within the next 10 minutes from a little "harmless fiddle" in here is quite another.  Don't be tempted.  Look yes, change no.  Finish what you are doing, close it all up, and leave it all alone.
