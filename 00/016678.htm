    plasticbrain---01 say: Hi Yage,
    The specification on the Nokia N95 (and N90... and others presumably) is not strictly accurate. In fact these chargers will supply something in the order of 6 to 7 Volts when open circuit and will be pulled down to around 5 Volts when charging the phone. In the case of the Nokia N95 I assume the rating of 5V 800mA means that when a load of 800mA is drawn from the charger the voltage will be 5V. So your 6.2 V charger should be perfectly OK. Also note that the phone will only charge the battery when necessary - so you can leave the charger plugged in all the time.

    I know all this because I am just in the process of making my own Li-Po powered battery charger for my N958G... I originally made it supply exactly 5 Volts - which does not work! (the phone reports "Not Charging!" I'm not sure what exactly the minimum voltage required is, but 6 to 7 volts works (with some current limiting). I have 2 genuine Nokia chargers - one (N90) reads 6.5V the other (N95) reads 7.2 V
    Cheers,

    plasticbrain---01 say: OK just a quick clarification to my previous post just in case there is anyone interested in making their own battery powered charger... The N95 will charge with a 5V current limited supply. The current limited part is important. Initially I used a high current 5V supply which the phone didn't like. Simply adding a 1.1 ohm resistor in series appears to have fixed the problem. I'm guessing that the phone is intermittently switching the external supply to the battery, so requires external current limiting in order to protect the battery. What I said before still stands though regarding the higher voltage - 7Volts open circuit is OK as long as the current is limited.

    So I have ended up with a 2200mAh 11.1V nominal Li-Po battery pack, with a 5Volt switch mode regulator and a 1.1 ohm resistor on the output for current limiting. I would expect to get at least 4 full N95 charges out of this setup... We'll see!

    Well, with my first example and the details of the assignment, I think you should be able to do this on your own.  I usually do not like to spoon-feed an answer to someone, especially when I don't know the context.  Is this a graded assignment or take-home exam?

    Nevertheless, you seem to want to get a handle on this, and you seem frustrated.  Under the circumstances, it might be helpful to give you one answer with an explanation.

    Put the following formula into J5:

    =PMT(B$23/B$24, F5*B$24, -I5)

    The presumption is:
    1. B23 contains the annual interest rate, as you say.  It should be written 5.75% or 0.0575, which are equivalent.
    2. B24 contains the number of payments per year; 12 for monthly.
    3. F5 contains the loan term in years (1).
    4. I5 contains the loan amount as a positive number (400).

    The mixed cell reference (B$23) is useful when you might have several different pairs of interest rates and payment frequencies going across columns in rows 23 and 24.  For example, C23 and C24, and D23 and D24, etc.

    I suspect that is not the case in your situation.  The teacher just wants you to use mixed references to demonstrate the form.  Too bad if he/she failed to provide a useful context so that you could understand the significance.

    We negate I5 because with most (but not all!) Excel financial functions, it is important to use opposite signs for inflows and outflows.  Typically, we use positive values for inflows (money that we receive) and negative values for outflows (money that we pay out).  But the choice is really arbitrary:  inflows and outflows to a borrower are outflows and inflows to the lender.

    In this case, we are taking the point of view of the lender.  The loan is an outflow, and the payment is an inflow.  More to the point:  if we want PMT to return a positive number, the "present value" parameter (loan amount) must be a negative number.  Opposite signs.
