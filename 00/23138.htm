Customize a bullet list

john mcghie say:

Hi Wendy:

The short answer is "With a List Style".

The style of type "List" is a structure in Word that connects a List Template (Which contains the indents and numbering of a list) and up to 9 paragraph styles (which contain t he spacing and fonts and line spacing).

Paragraph styles can also contain indents, but when the List Template is applied, the List Template wins, and when a List Style is applied, it automatically applies the whole combination, including the list template.

  1. So first off, create your paragraph styles.  I recommend customising the built-in styles List Number through List Number 9, or List Bullet through List Bullet 9 -- that's what they exist for and I am all for avoiding unnecessary work.
  2. Then Format>Style>New and assign a name.  I normally make the name descriptive of what I will USE the style for, not what it contains.  So "BodyTextNumbering" rather than "My multilevel list".
  3. Change the Type to "List"
  4. As soon as you change the type, the dialog changes.  To the OPPOSITE of what you need! Whoever designed this mechanism really has no clue how users use numbering, and it's obvious that they never do!  All the controls are there somewhere, but you're in for an irritating game of hide-and-seek.
  5. First you want to drill down one more level, to the Professional controls. Leave the amateur playground to the people who don't know Word. So at the bottom left corner of the dialog, click "Format" and choose "Numbering".
  6. Now we're in the professional control set and you can see what you are doing. You have seen this dialog before in the Bullets and Numbering command, but this time, only the Outline Numbered tab is offered because while it is the same dialog, it is configuring a different structure.
  7. Choose the sample that looks most like the numbering scheme you are going to create.  If there's none that look right, pick any of them: we can completely reconfigure if needed, but try to avoid that much work.
  8. Now click Customise.  What we're going to do now is run through and configure the styles you created in Step 1 into the List Style.  Once they are configured, they will hold and most of the possibilities for problems are removed.
  9. At the bottom of that Customise Outline Numbered List dialog, click the blue disclosure triangle at the extreme bottom left to reveal the Link Level to Style field. 
  10. In the Link Level to Style field, choose "List Number" as the style.  Ignore the other settings for a moment. 
  11. In the top left of the Customise Outline Numbered List dialog, set the Level to "2" 
  12. In the Link Level to Style field, choose "List Number 2" as the style.
  13. Repeat these last two steps 11 and until you have used the higher levels. I recommend configuring one more level than you need, so you can use Outline View properly.
  14. When you have set up levels 1 to 5, OK back to the New Style dialog and enable the "Add to template" checkbox At the bottom left of the dialog.  This stores your creation in the template so you can use it for all your documents.
  15. OK your way all the way out and save the document (so if your changes blow up later, you have a foundation to go back to).
  16. Now go back to Format>Style... and choose the name of the style you just created.  Hit Modify>Format>Numbering>Customise...  Notice that several options are greyed out, because you are working on a List Style, which does not have the other options.
  17. NOW you can go from Level 1 down to Level 5 and set your indents and tab positions.  All of the other settings will probably be correct, so leave them alone.
  18. When you have set the tabs and indents for all levels, OK you changes will be stored only in the document you are working on.
  19. OK your way out now, hold down the Shift key, and choose "Save All", which will appear on the File Menu when you hold down Shift.
  20. Notice that we do not change the indents in the paragraph styles?  We do not need to, the List Style will reflect our indent and tab changes into them automatically.
  21. Notice that we have not yet "applied" the list style to anything?  We never need to: it will be linked in automatically when we apply the paragraph styles to text.
  22. You can now close that document and create a new one.  If everything worked, our new List Style will be in it, copied in from the template to which you added it in step but not applied to any text. 

Now put some text in there. 

Select the paragraphs you want numbered, and apply the paragraph style List Number.  Behind the scenes, Word will automatically attach the List Style we just made. 

To adjust the level of numbering in each paragraph, place your cursor at the beginning of each paragraph and hit Tab or Shift Tab.  When you do that, the List Style automatically switches in the correct paragraph style for you, which automatically calls in the correct level of numbering as defined by the List Style. 

The list style acts as a "holder".  It is actually a tiny list structure like an Excel table that contains a line for each level of the list, and a row of data for each level that indicates which paragraph style, character style, or List Template contains the indents, fonts, or numbering level, respectively. 

Yes, you are right: it's quite complex.  There are other ways of setting it up (suitable for lawyers...).  I strongly recommend that you do not use those ways. 

Done this way, the list style and all of its associations will be stored in your template, will copy into each new document automatically available for us, and will even paste into another document if you copy a paragraph formatted with it. 

It works very well, and its stable.

Oh:  Word will never show you which List Style is applied.  It does not need to, because when the list's paragraph styles are applied, the list will automatically be applied.


The paragraph styles defined into the list style must not be used (CAN not be used...) for anything else in the document.

PC Word has a "Style Inspector" which will show you the formatting of selected text, and enable you to see which formatting properties came from which of the styles applied.  They removed this from Mac Word to make way for the much-reviled Ribbon.


We need to be very careful to keep the various entities separate in our minds.

  • List Style: A named list template containing the numbering format.
  • Paragraph Style: A named collection of formatting containing all paragraph
    and font formatting, but not the number formatting.
  • List:  A collection of one or more paragraphs to which a List Style has been
    applied.

Now:  A List Style can exist independently.  It CAN have no paragraph styles
associated with it.  A Lawyer might use this to number their text
independently of the formatting. Nice people would not do such things in public!

Normal users should always associate paragraph styles with a list style.  In which case, their List Style should have a minimum of one, and a maximum of nine, paragraph styles defined into it.

As a normal user, you would apply the Paragraph Style to some text.  That

would then bring in the List Style and apply the correct level of the List
Style to the text.

Alternatively (if you wanted to make things hard for yourself) you could
apply the List Style to the text.  Word would then apply the Level 1
paragraph style to the paragraphs and wait for you to correct that.

Hope this helps

john mcghie say:

Hi Wendy:

In Step 1, it does not matter which styles you use, provided that they are, and remain, PARAGRAPH styles.  You need up to 9 of them, they can be any styles you like, but they can't be used for any other purpose in that document.  It's much easier if you use the built-in styles (less work).  Certainly when you come to using list styles for headings, it is essential that you use the built-in styles.  But for this purpose it doesn't matter.

In Steps 2 through 9, you are creating the List Style.  You create only ONE of these, which links all of the paragraph styles together into the list (up to 9 of them). It doesn't matter what you have selected when you do this: you are working on a background structure that is not applied to text.

So when you say "Clicked on random List Bullet 2 text. Then Format>Style>New, named it, changed Type to List, closed without Applying and so on." I think you have stepped out of the creation of the first list style and created a second list style.  That will not work: you need ONE single list style to connect all your list paragraph styles.  Maybe name it "OutlineBullets" or some name suitable to yourself.

Then in steps 8 through 15, you define into OutlineBullets list style the paragraph styles you will use to format the text: maybe List Bullet through List Bullet 9, but they can be any styles you like, provided that each style is used only as part of THIS list (OutlineBullets).

Think of the List Style as an egg carton: it contains as many as 9 eggs, and keeps them together as a single entity.  This is what enables you to hit a Tab at the front of the paragraph and get the next level: it's the list style that controls the levels and bullet or number formatting.  The paragraph styles control the text formatting.

In Steps 10 through 13 use "The paragraph styles that you prepared earlier".  If those are the "List Bullet" series, then use List Bullet.  In these steps, you are setting the paragraph styles that will format each level of your list.  You cannot use any styles twice: for each level, you need one paragraph style; and you cannot use the paragraph styles defined into the list for any other purpose in the document.

Step 13, you are correct, you do not need to OK anything until you have done all of your levels.

In Step 15, "Close" is correct. You never need to "Apply" a List Style, it remains in the background, linking the paragraph styles together.  When you apply any of the paragraph styles to text, the List Style is called in to play.

Step 17 is a very badly designed dialog box.  The tiles in this box are showing you the List Templates in the document.  I was trying frantically to avoid discussing List Templates, because they add another layer of multi-level confusion to the process.

A List Style has nine levels.  It connects a List Template, which has nine levels, with up to nine paragraph styles.  The paragraph styles format the text.  The List Template formats the bullets or numbering.  The List Style connects the two so they work together and can be copied from document to document.

The tiles are showing you a list template for each of your previous efforts to customise the list style.  Because you were creating a new list style each time, you will see multiple entries here. If you had been working to modify a single list style named OutlineBullets, there would only be one customised tile showing here.

Decide which one has all the levels you are using in it, and click "Reset" to get the rest out of sight. Yes, if you have more than one customised tile in this operation it is creating a problem: there must be only one customised tile, setting the bullet formatting for all the levels of this list style. If you later create several list styles, then you will have a tile for each.

Step 18:  You do need to click "Add to Template" every time you make a change to a style.  However, you can take advantage of an undocumented feature: once you have enabled "add to template", ALL the changes you make to the styles will be saved at the next Save.  So you need to check it only once for each time you save.

You will notice that "Start at" is greyed out if you are defining a non-numbered list. So the "1" doesn't matter.

If you are seeing "Apply formatting to 1st level" you are two levels too high in the dialog.  In Step 4 and 5 I warned you about this.  It may be possible to define a list style using that dialog, but I would not try it.  Go down two levels further to the professional controls.

Step 19: This suggests to me that there is something very wrong with your Normal template, and this process is never going to work until you fix that.  It sounds as though your Normal template has gone "read-only".  Templates sometimes do that if you make too many changes that overlap and conflict.  It's very possible to get them into that condition by making mistakes in this procedure.  It may be that you have more than one copy of Word running.  No big problem: here is how to get them back:

  1. Stop the other copy of Word if there's more than one (Quit, not minimise...)
  2. Save the new template to the desktop or wherever you can.
  3. Rename the old template
  4. Move the new template to the "/Users/<user name>/Library/Application Support/Microsoft/Office/User Templates" folder where it is supposed to be.
  5. The new template will contain all your customised styles, plus the changes you are now making, and should be read-write, which will enable you to maintain it.

Step 22: The name that will appear in the style windows when you use the List Style is the name of whichever paragraph style you assigned to that level.  So if you have a bulleted list style, and you are at the third level, you might see "List Bullet 3".  You should never see the name of the List Style: it is simply a linking structure used behind the scenes.

Sorry, you got yourself into a muddle: this process is unconscionably complex (badly designed!) and you need to follow the steps very carefully, changing the steps as appropriate to your circumstances. You will find it easier to start again than to fix the one you have.

Yes, you can fix them when they go wrong, but you need to know what you are doing, and you need to successfully create a few of these before all the pieces drop into place.  Don't beat yourself up, it took me a long time to understand this stuff because it is really complex (and shouldn't be, but we have to work with what exists, not what we would like...)

Deep breath, and recite after me: "A List Style is a structure that connects nine paragraph styles to the nine levels of a List Template."  The paragraph styles format the text at each level: font, line-spacing etcetera. The List Template formats the behaviour of the list: the indents, the bullet characters, the way the numbers behave. The List Style ties the two sets of objects together.

It has taken me a long time to find out how this stuff actually works, and I am still not clear why they chose to design it this way. They were looking for a way to make list formatting copy from one document to another.  Two problems they faced are: you can't just copy a bunch of styles into a destination document: either you will overwrite the destination formatting or you must re-name the styles, which breaks the lists.  And you can't simply copy a list template into another document -- you corrupt the document if you try.  So the List Style is not actually the formatting of a list, it is a list of instructions by which you can MAKE a list that formats the way you want it to.

The distinction is getting a bit esoteric.  But it means you can copy this thing from document to document, and every time it gets there it will create the list formatting you want.

john mcghie say:

Short answer:  I don't know.

As you will see in my later answer to Wendy, there are three objects involved: paragraph styles, list templates, and list styles.

As far as I know, List Styles are not supported in the obsolete .doc format.

I don't know much about OpenOffice, so I do not know if it supports List Templates.  I suspect it will have a similar structure: but remember that .doc is a binary format and there is no guarantee that they have implemented the hierarchical XML ordered list structure as used in .docx.

That said, the way we used to solve this problem in the .doc format was with the use of Named List Templates.  Otherwise known as "The Margaret Aldis Method".  Margaret's code is here: http://word.mvps.org/faqs/numbering/ListRestartFromStyle.htm

Some of us believe this is where Microsoft got the idea for List Styles from.  What Margaret's code is doing is looking through the list templates in a document for one that has the "name" we are expecting.

The problem we face is not in defining the list formatting, but in finding the definition again later. Margaret's code slaps a label on the thing so we can find it again.

Now:  Don't get led astray here: List Templates do not HAVE a "Name".  That's the problem Microsoft solved with List Styles.  But List Templates DO have a "name" field that was intended to store the name of the SEQ or ListNum field list that the list template would format. No problem: it is almost unknown for that field to be used for its intended purpose, so there's no real downside to overloading it as the name of our list template.  Just be careful what you write in there: various list names are reserved: LegalDefault, NumberDefault, OutlineDefault are reserved.

Margaret's code is complex, but it will certainly run in Word 2011 (and, I guess, in OpenOffice).

Note that this system is not foolproof.  You have to train users NOT to touch the Bullets and Numbering button at any time: because as soon as they do, they will break the list template, potentially even removing the name from it.

You can check for these conditions and repair the damage on document open.  It's a lot more code, and you need to understand the whole thing really well. A commercial coder would run this up for you for around $40,000 if you could find one who understands the mechanism well enough. But it's still not 100% bullet-proof: there are ways users can cause such damage that you can't fix it.

For this kind of money, it really becomes a lot more cost-effective to buy a Mac or PC and a real copy of Microsoft Word that will provide List Styles.  Then you can get rid of the obsolete .doc format and your life will become a lot easier.

Hope this helps

Oriel, Apex and Other Themes from PowerPoint 2007

Technical Level : Basic

Summary
What do you do when you need Themes that were included in older versions of PowerPoint, such as PowerPoint 2007.
Details

PowerPoint 2007 included several Themes, most of which are not available in newer versions of PowerPoint -- so what do you do if you need a particular Theme from PowerPoint 2007 for a project or a study exercise?

You can ask anyone who has PowerPoint 2007 to create a single slide presentation based on that Theme and send it to you. Alternatively, if you have PowerPoint 2007 installed on an older computer, you will find all Themes within this folder:

C:\Program Files\Microsoft Office\Document Themes 12 


Your location may differ if you installed Office 2007 on a drive other than your C drive. Your folder path may also differ if you did not choose to install Office 2007 to the default location.

Either way, the Themes included within Office 2007 were:

Apex

Aspect

Civic

Concourse

Equity

Flow

Foundry

Median

Metro

Module

Office Theme

Opulent

Oriel

Origin

Paper

Solistice

Technic

Trek

Urban

Verve

You can find out how these Themes look at this resource page: Which Themes are Installed within Office Versions?
