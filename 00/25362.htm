john w. vinson say:

Well... generally, you don't.

"Too big" is big enough to have a demonstrated - not assumed - impact on performance. I've worked with 3,000,000 row tables in native Access with acceptable speed. Will you have over a million clients? If so, congratulations... and you can certainly afford an enterprise installation of SQL/Server!

If not, I'd just base your active clients forms and reports on a Query selecting on the basis of the completion date. If that field is indexed, you should have no speed problems.

However... you clearly need to reconsider your table structure for another reason! If you have "one big table" with "six fields that may contain ref data to documents" then you're "Committing Spreadsheet". What if someday you need SEVEN kinds of documents? or eight? Redesign your table, rewrite all your queries, all your forms, all your reports!? OUCH!

Much better would be to have (at least) three tables: Clients, with ClientID as the primary key, and biographical and contact information; DocTypes (DocTypeID primary key, six rows at present, probably include the cost of this kind of document); and DocsUsed (with fields for ClientID, DocTypeID, date used, and other info about THIS document for THIS client. You would not store any counts or totals anywhere, in any table; just calculate them on demand with a Totals query.

You don't have a database, you have a spreadsheet. Access is a relational database platform, not a spreadsheet. If your data was setup properly with normalized tables, then your data would be stored more efficiently and space would not be a concern.

We need to understand your table structure as well as what the Recordsource of your forms.

The List4_AfterUpdate code will position the form's record pointer to the record that matches the ClientID selected in the listbox. This assumes that ClientID represents a unique value within the form's Recordsource.
