Use of RAM as Hard Disk cache to improve performance - Microsoft Community

nemij say:

Use of RAM as Hard Disk cache to improve performance


Seagate does Hard disks(HD) that have a built in SSD cache. They advertise these as drives with HD capacity and cost but with performance of a SSD. They basically cache frequently used data  to the SSD. The original version had a 4GB SSD. These days they are using 8GB. Independent tests show this approach actually works. The performance of the HD improves after it "learns" the usage pattern of the user and caches relevant data.

My suggestion is instead of using flash memory, why not use system RAM and actually have this feature built into the operating system. 8GB "extra" RAM doesn't cost a lot these days. And this 8GB is the worst case scenario...for example Windows already caches some data, but even after that I have about 3GB free right now in my Task Manager. This is on a 6 GB RAM laptop.

Algorithms can be devised to cache frequently accessed data in whatever free space is available. The free space can be dynamic in size depending on how one is using the computer. We effectively create a RAM cache. This will be faster than HD or even SSD(25GB/s vs 50MB/s vs 500MB/s). Some HD space will be used to keep persistent track of usage statistics, but in TB storage era, I don't think anyone will complain.

Incidentally, the battery life on laptops will also increase as all the frequently used data can be cached at one go on system startup. The HD can then be put on standby and used less intensively.

This also becomes interesting from sales perspective because about 600 million PCS/laptops are more than 6 years old, so will typically  have limited RAM. We could roll out this new "feature" and tell people that we will do the best with their limited memory, but if they replace their PC with a new one containing more memory, we can give them better performance. The customers, OEMs ,hardware manufacturers will all love this "feature" for their own different reasons. So in summary, the business case is strong.

I am running on a older version of Windows and am not on 10 yet. I have been sent links that suggest that Win 10 is more aggressive in keeping things cached. However, my point stand. Why waste even a MB of RAM. The cost of overwriting caches if more RAM is needed is trivial.

big bad bombastic bob say:

I just got done mentioning this, "smarter caching" for NTFS.

Anecdotally, there's what I call "paranoid caching" going on, particularly with the registry.  The evidence of its existence is the RADICAL speed improvement you get with a non-spinny drive like an SSD.

There appear to be 2 problems:

a) apparently, no 'lazy write' caching of relatively unimportant things [a built-in in Linux],

b) frequently re-reading physical media for commonly used things (like the registry) that don't change much,

c) NOT doing 'write through' disk caching for those 'commonly used things' at the very least.

a proper journaling file system can deal with changes NOT being physically written to the disk "right blanking now" (and stall EVERYTHING waiting for it to happen, dang-it!).

If the power goes out NOW, or 2 seconds from now, the luck of when it happens shouldn't matter much, and laptops typically have a built-in UPS so there you go.  It should be OPTIONAL to allow 'lazy write' caching and speed up the OS, maybe even on a directory-by-directory (or file-by-file) basis.

Anyway, it's a feature I'd like to see.  FreeBSD and Linux have this.  Why not Windows, too?

rick rothstein, mvp - excel say:

This formula will return the contents of the last occupied cell in Column A (no matter what is in the cells within that column... numbers, dates, text, etc.)...

=LOOKUP(2,1/(A:A<>""),A:A)

although it would be more efficient if you could limit the range to as small an interval as will handle your current and future needs; for example...

=LOOKUP(2,1/(A1:A100<>""),A1:A100)

assuming your data will never be in a row past 100. Note that for XL2003 and earlier, you must use this formula...

=LOOKUP(2,1/(A1:A65535<>""),A:A)

or limit it as indicated above for later versions.

Inconsistent ability to modify Outline Level of custom Headings - Microsoft Community

theorist9 say:

Inconsistent ability to modify Outline Level of custom Headings

I'm using Word for Mac 15.31 in MacOS Sierra 10.12.3, but this issue appears to be cross-platform, since a Word-for-PC user has experienced it as well (see end of this post):

Here's what should be the procedure for modifying the Outline Level of a custom Heading:

1) In the Styles Pane, click on the down-triangle next to the Heading whose Outline Level you want to adjust.

2) Then select:  Modify Style –>  Format –> Paragraph –> Indents and Spacing –> Outline Level.

However, usually the Outline Levels are grayed-out and thus not changeable (for both built-in Headings and custom Headings).  The only procedure I've uncovered that consistently allows you to change the Outline Level for a custom Heading (this doesn't work for built-in Headings) is the following workaround.  Suppose you want to change the Outline Level of "Custom Heading 1":

1) Highlight an existing Heading, whose style is "Custom Heading 1",  in the document.

2) Go into the document's Paragraph dialog (as opposed to the Heading's Paragaph dialog), and change the Heading's Outline Level there.  

3) Go back to the Styles Pane, click on the reverse triangle for Custom Heading 1, and then select  "Update to Match Selection".  

This roundabout method is obviously unnecessarily complicated and laborious — there's no good reason you shouldn't be able to do this directly, in the Styles Pane, with Modify Style –>  Format –> Paragraph –> Indents and Spacing –> Outline Level.  After all, why does Word offer "Outline Level" as an option under Modify Style, only to leave that option (usually) disabled?  

[Note that all my custom Headings were created based on styles of built-in Headings.]

Further, as I mentioned above, when it comes to built-in Headings, even this procedure doesn't work.

But here's the additional puzzle:  One's inability to directly modify the Outline Level of custom Headings (using Modify Style) is not consistent.  For instance, suppose you click some body text, and then change that text to a custom Heading style.  Then, usually, if you try  Modify Style –>  Format –> Paragraph –> Indents and Spacing –> Outline Level, you'll find that this custom Heading's Outline Level is no longer grayed out!  [I.e., you just have to first change some text to that Heading, and then you can internally modify the Heading's Outline Level; the problem is if the text already is in that Heading style, in which case Outline Level will typically be grayed-out.] As a further oddity, if that custom Heading was based on a built-in Heading, if you go to the corresponding built-in Heading, you'll usually see its Outline Level is no longer grayed out either!  But if you do some other stuff in the document, and then return to check either the custom Heading or the built-in Heading, you will see that their Outline Level pull-downs have reverted to a grayed-out status.

An inconsistent ability to modify the Outline Levels of Headings has also been observed by Word MVP Doug Robbins, who is using Word for PC (indicating this issue is cross-platform).  He found that, for built-in Headings, sometimes Outline Level is grayed out, and sometimes it's not. Here's an excerpt from one of Doug Robbin's posts at https://answers.microsoft.com/en-us/msoffice/forum/msoffice_word-mso_mac/three-issues-with-navigation-pane/9933f8eb-d25b-4c42-a743-623815949d60 :


For the Built-in Heading # styles, it is not possible to modify the level. (checking again, I find that it is possible.  I do not know why the Outline level: drop down was disabled when I took the screen shot.)

neo yu msft say:

Hi JK812,

After a migration batch has finished running and you’ve verified that all mailboxes in the batch are successfully migrated and the initial synchronization of mailbox items to Exchange Online is complete, it’s recommended that you convert the on-premises mailboxes in the migration batch to mail-enabled users. Why? After a staged Exchange migration, a user has an on-premises mailbox and an Exchange Online mailbox. Because mail sent to the user’s on-premises mailbox is forwarded to their Exchange Online mailbox after migration, users need to connect to their Exchange Online mailboxes to access their email. But if a person uses Outlook to open their mailbox, the Autodiscover service still tries to connect to the on-premises mailbox.

After you convert on-premises mailboxes to mail-enabled users, the Autodiscover service uses a mail-enabled user to connect Outlook to the Exchange Online mailbox after the user creates a new Outlook profile.

For more details, please refer to the step 6 as described in: Migrate mailboxes to Exchange Online with a staged migration.
