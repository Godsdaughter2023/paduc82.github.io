jllatham say:

At this point it becomes a two-level security problem.

First how secure is where ever you have it from outside invaders?  In other words if you store it on 'cloud' storage somewhere such as OneDrive or those storage spaces provided by services such as iTunes, Facebook, etc. the question is HOW GOOD IS THEIR SECURITY?  Other questions that fall into that same category is 'where is the storage physically?' since this probably has a lot to do with who can legally look at your files in that country/location.  Now, as we've seen from many recent news stories there doesn't really seem to be any such thing as total security on any system that has an internet connection!  Geeze, banks, governments, credit card companies, and even makers of hardware that has built in firmware like smart phones, hard drives and SIM cards for phones have been victimized.

OK, assuming that you acknowledge that if it is in any file on a computer connected to the internet that it is vulnerable, then you have to consider how tough is it for someone to get inside of that file and peek at what is in it.  As Norman David Jones said - with Excel it is almost a trivial matter to remove workbook, worksheet and even VBA Project passwords.

I think your best hope is keeping the file in your physical possession: on your computer or perhaps on a removable storage device like a USB drive.  Be sure to also keep a backup copy available.  Now you don't have to be so concerned with how well some storage service keeps up their anti-intrusion protections.

Next, it would be helpful if you had strong encryption that is relatively easy to actually use to keep the file encrypted except when you are actually viewing it.  Something that is a little more difficult to 'crack' than Excel's passwords.

For that, I would look at this free offering from the U.S. Department of Defense, made free to the public!!  It will encrypt/decrypt your file rapidly using FIPS 140-2 compliant processes.  This is deemed 'adequate' for data at rest (on your device) and

http://www.spi.dod.mil/ewizard.htm

Basically it is a Java applet that you can just put on your desktop and drag files into it to either encrypt or decrypt.  But be sure to:

#1 - don't forget the password you assign a file, because cracking this one won't be easy.

#2 - use a password that follows established guidelines for "strong" passwords:

        At LEAST 8 characters long (longer is better)

        A mixture of lower and UPPER case characters

        Contains some numeric characters, and

        Contains some special characters like _$!# etc.

Now, someone is going to ask "since the DoD developed this, don't they have a backdoor into it?"  I don't know.  Since seeing what the NSA seems to have been up to over the past decade or so, while my gut feeling says "no", a reality check has to admit to the possibility.  But how much time do you think the NSA would actually spend looking at stuff on your computer as long as you haven't attracted their attention through other activities such as emails/social networking/site visits to rather questionable people/organizations/locations?

bob jones aka: cybertaz say:
The straight explanation to your 1st issue is that Word documents typically do not have toolbars. If you send a Word document as an attachment it stands to reason that the recipient will open the attachment in Word [assuming s/he has the program]. The toolbars they see are a part of their Word installation & will appear any time they use Word. The only way to prevent that is to complicate the document with VBA that manipulates their copy of the program to hide the toolbars -- Something which most users will not tolerate & may not open because of the macro warning. If they open the Word document using a different program (Open Office, Apple's Pages, etc.), that program's interface is what will be presented.

It sounds like what you would prefer to do is create a PDF & send a copy of the PDF as an attachment. Word does not open PDF files, so the 'toolbars' the recipient sees when they open the PDF are those that are a part of the User Interface of the PDF reader they're using (Apple's Preview, Adobe Reader, etc.). Consequently, neither can Word send a PDF as an attachment. You need to use the features of your email software to attach a copy of the PDF after generating the PDF from the Word document.

To do that you can either use:
  • File> Save As then select PDF from the list of Formats in the Save As dialog, or
  • File> Print then click the PDF button in the Print dialog & select Save as PDF. If you have Adobe Acrobat, Save as Adobe PDF also will be available when using this option.
  • If you do have Adobe Acrobat you may also be able to use it to generate a PDF from the Word document. It depends on which release of Acrobat you have. Note that Adobe Reader cannot create a PDF other than by way of the [fee-based] online service offered by Adobe.
BTW: Adobe is the name of a software development company which offers a variety of programs. There's no indication of which of those programs you've installed, but none of them are available as tools in Word, itself. It's a service added by OS X in the Print dialog.

The real problem, then, seems to be in creating a PDF. Unfortunately, you give no description of how you're attempting to do that or exactly what happens when you try. I'm afraid "not able" doesn't give anything to go on. Without detailed information it's impossible to determine what might be the cause of the problem. In general:
  1. Make sure you have OS X [10.9.1] & Office 2011 [14.3.9] fully updated
  2. Run Disk Utility to repair disk permissions on your HD
  3. Restart your Mac
If you still have a problem generating a PDF using one of the methods described above, please reply here with a full description of what that problem is. Indicate which method you're using, the exact steps you're taking as well as the specific results.
