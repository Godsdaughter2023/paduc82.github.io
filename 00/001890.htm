rob koch say:

Microsoft has never built a 'self-defense' module into the program because they understand that once malware has successfully bypassed your anti-malware it owns the PC and can do whatever it pleases.  The fact that these 'tests' were passed by some doesn't mean that someone with better malware can't disable any of them, since they often do.

A side effect of adding self-protection is 'bloat', since this necessarily increases the size and complexity of the anti-malware program as a whole.  This is a trade-off in the attempt to create the 'do-it-all' anti-malware, which has always eventually resulted in overly large and complex programs.

The best method to protect the anti-malware and thus the PC as a whole is to normally operate as a 'Limited' or 'Restricted' user, since these don't have the privilege to remove or disable programs that only an Administrator can install.  Operating as an Administrator increases the risk of a successful infection, since many of the vulnerabilities discovered only allow the malware to attack with the privileges of the user logged in at the time.
