rich michaels - mvp (greatcirclelearning.com) say:

I can confirm now that this is a more recent setback in Mac Office 2016. MS has changed something in the startup and you now have to put in a time delay and move any calls to a designated subroutine that you've assigned the delay to.

Here's what I've done and it now works again.

Sub AutoExec()
    OfficeVersion = Val(Left(Application.Version, 2))
    #If Mac Then
        #If OfficeVersion = "14" Then
            'This delay is required because Office 2011 for the Mac is pig slow
            'and the toolbars can't be loaded until the Office app fully loads
            Application.OnTime Now + TimeValue("00:00:02"), "SetWritingEnvironment", False
        #Else
            'This prevents an Add-in tab from displaying if run on Office 2016 for the Mac
            'which is more annoying slow than Office 2011
            Application.OnTime Now + TimeValue("00:00:04"), "SetVersion15Environ", False
        #End If
    #Else
        'this prevents an Add-in tab from displaying if the template is run on a PC
        Application.CommandBars("Zapps Pro Scripter").Visible = False
    #End If
End Sub

Sub SetWritingEnvironment()
    Application.CommandBars("Zapps Pro Scripter").Visible = True
    If LoadVariables = True Then MstrFilesOK = True
    RegOk
End Sub

Sub SetVersion15Environ()
    Application.CommandBars("Zapps Pro Scripter").Visible = False
    If LoadVariables = True Then MstrFilesOK = True
    RegOk
End Sub

The routines "LoadVariables" and "RegOk" that formerly could be called directly from the AutoExec routine if the code was used in an Office 2016 environment now have to be run from a time delayed subroutine. I tried delaying it by 2 seconds, just as we've always had to do with Office 2011, but that gave me trouble. I moved the delay to 4 seconds and it worked.

Mail enabled object - Microsoft Community

sahilsharma5307 say:

Mail enabled object

What are mail enabled objects and why do we create those after staged migration?

brookzhang_msft8047 say:

Hi SahilSharma5307,

After you have successfully migrated a batch of mailboxes, you need some way to let users get to their mail. A user whose mailbox has been migrated now has both a mailbox on-premises and one in Office 365. Users who have a mailbox in Office 365 will stop receiving new mail in their on-premises mailbox.

Because you are not done with your migrations, you are not yet ready to direct all users to Office 365 for their email. So what do you do for those people who have both? What you can do is change the on-premises mailboxes that you've already migrated to mail-enabled users. When you change from a mailbox to a mail-enabled user, you can direct the user to Office 365 for their email instead of going to their on-premises mailbox.

Another important reason to convert on-premises mailboxes to mail-enabled users is to retain proxy addresses from the Exchange Online mailboxes by copying proxy addresses to the mail-enabled users. This lets you manage cloud-based users from your on-premises organization by using Active Directory. Also, if you decide to decommission your on-premises Exchange organization after all mailboxes are migrated to Exchange Online, the proxy addresses you’ve copied to the mail-enabled users will remain in your on-premises Active Directory.
