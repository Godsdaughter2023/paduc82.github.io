Try the following steps below:

 

 

Step 1: Uninstall all previous Office Programs you have on your computer. This includes the trial version that normally gets installed when you buy a new computer. Go to Control Panel and select Programs and Features and select the Office product then Uninstall it.

 

Step 2: Press "Window key" plus R on your keyboard and type %temp%. Delete all the temporary files on that folder.

 

Note: Some files might not be deleted because some programs are associated with it. Just "Skip" if ever you get that message.

 

Step 3: Press "Window key" plus R on your keyboard and type regedit. Once your registry is open, click on "File" then select "Export". Save a backup on your Desktop.

 

-> Go to HKEY_CURRENT_USER/Software/Microsoft and delete the "Office" folder.

-> Go to HKEY_LOCAL_MACHINE/Software/Microsoft/Office and delete all folders except for "Outlook".

-> Go to HKEY_USERS/S-1-5-18/SOFTWARE/MICROSOFT and delete the "Office" folder.

 

Step 4: Go to your C: Drive and open your Programs Files Folder. Delete the "Microsoft Office" Folder.

 

Note: You might have 2 "Program Files" Folder since you are using a 64 bit computer. Check both folders and delete all Office folder.

 

Step 5: Press "Window key" plus R on your keyboard and type "services.msc". Right click on  "Print Spooler" and choose "Stop".

 

Step 6: Make sure you deactivate your Security Software prior to installing Office again. (eg. Norton, McAfee, AVG, etc.)

 

Step 7: Reinstall your Office program.

 

 

I hope this helps.

vincentchoy say:

For you to appreciate Yammer, and when to use what kind of question, you first need to understand the Microsoft Office365 stack, and what Microsoft has been doing with the stack.

A lot of IT people have a view that the end-user is generally quite backward, easily confused on which app to use, and generally have a low understanding of tech and automation. This leads to IT generally looking for a tool that meets all requirements (one size fits all), and deciding on behalf of the company(or with the company) what tool to use.  I tend to agree to that view. ...until the smart phone came about.

Today, imagine a end user manager who may work in a 300 staff SMB. In his personal life,  he has a great grasp of technology, he knows exactly what apps to use (on his phone), and doesnt even mind if the app only does one thing (eg book a starwood hotel, not all hotels, just one chain). The end user, with his mobile phone, chats with her sister in Asia, gets updated about her brother's new born in Europe, replies his messages in the subway, and books his fav restaurant via Opentable. His church group updates their meeting schedules on google drive, and he reads the minutes of his country club committee meetings on dropbox.

Yet back in the office, he gets email to do his work, using Office 2007, probably lagging 5-10 years behind the tech he uses in his social life. So to simplify his work, what he does  is he brings what he is familiar with in his personal life to work. This results in the problem of Shadow IT in the workplace, where users use lots of other tools, under his personal account, with no IT department oversight and ownership of the infomation.

What Office365 does, is, it gives the IT users similar choice of tools, yet they are corporate owned, sits on the same authentication platform, and provides a robust (and improving) set of security technologies to protect the company data and the end user. If the end user needs file sharing, there's OD4B instead of Google Drive. If they need Kanban, there's Planner instead of Trello. If they like WhatsApp, Wechat etc there is Teams, If they like Google Forms, there's O365 Forms, if they like Slideshare, there's Sway, Facebook there's Yammer .etc.

All these apps then sit on top of the Office365 Substrate, where data is easily passed from one app to another, and each user can belong to specific groups with access to appropriate apps.

In the case where non-Microsoft apps are also used in an environment that has adopted Office365, Microsoft provides connectors to these apps. For example when someone posts a survey on survey monkey, results can be posted on Teams for discussion, or Microsoft Office can open a file stored on Dropbox..etc

In the end, the user basically decides, assuming he has a reasonable understanding of Office365, for a particular use case, which productivity app best suits his purpose, and executes his use case using that app.

In the case of Yammer vs Teams, Microsoft now talks about inner loop vs outer loop. In use cases where it requires regular discussions with a smaller group of people, Teams would be the better fit. In use cases where you need to address a larger community, then Yammer has a better fit.

OK. I've solved this problem.
