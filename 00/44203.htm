cmdrkeene say:

To access Safe Mode in Windows 8 or higher:  from your login password screen, click the Power icon at the bottom then hold

Most machines don't ship with recovery discs anymore, they just put it in a separate partition on the hard drive.  Of course it's harder to access that because most modern machines don't use the old bootloaders that allowed you to get into safe mode with F8 during startup.  If you can reach the Windows Login screen, you can get to safe mode by restarting while clicking the restart option, but that doesn't help if you can't get that far.

Given that, most manufacturers have come up with their own solutions. My Sony Vaio computer has a special pin-hole button I had to press with a paperclip to enter recovery mode.  Other manufacturers have other options, some require a USB thumbdrive to enter recovery, or a CD.

At this point, you probably want to contact Asus to find out what recovery method they use.  I hate to bring bad news, but they are probably going to say "why didn't you make a USB recovery drive?" (as if anyone creates their own set of repair discs when they receive a new computer).

Helping a friend with a Dell laptop (didn't have a problem, but wanted a clean install anyway), Dell sent us a free overnight FedEx ship of a CD to restore. Asus might be so kind. I know Sony wasn't. If my Vaio recovery button didn't work, they wanted $60 for a CD!

So anyway, manufacturer is your best bet now if you don't have a self-made USB stick for recovery.

As for your other question. My subjective opinion is that it's probably not worth the cost and effort of buying Windows 7. Most of your drivers and functions probably wouldn't work with Windows 7 anyway.  But if you already own an unused copy of Windows 7, there's no reason not to try I suppose.

lemp say:

Microsoft uses the following descriptions for files that are appropriate for different versions:

x86 -- Intel and AMD 32-bit systems

AMD64 -- Intel and AMD 64-bit systems

IA-64 - Intel Itanium 64-bit systems

The 64-bit architecture that is common today was first developed by AMD.  It's basically a 64-bit extension of the 32-bit (x86) instruction set.  Soon thereafter, Intel adopted AMD's architecture for consumer level CPUs.  The designation remains "AMD64" regardless of whose chip it is.

As far as I know, the only non-server version of Windows to use IA-64 was "Windows XP 64 bit edition" (not the same as Windows XP Professional x64, which was for AMD64 architecture).

64-bit and even some 32 bit installations might have AMD in their folder/file names because it was AMD who in 2003 invented/produced the first 64-bit consumer based CPU - then Intel copied it later in 2004 (Intel called it cloning):

2003
AMD introduces its Opteron and Athlon 64 processor lines, based on its AMD64 architecture which is the first x86-based 64-bit processor architecture. Apple also ships the 64-bit "G5" PowerPC 970 CPU produced by IBM. Intel maintains that its Itanium chips would remain its only 64-bit processors.
2004
Intel, reacting to the market success of AMD, admits it has been developing a clone of the AMD64 extensions named IA-32e (later renamed EM64T, then yet again renamed to Intel 64). Intel ships updated versions of its Xeon and Pentium 4 processor families supporting the new 64-bit instruction set.

barb bowman say:
does a recovery image boot following the beginning of these instructions?
 
Go to https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/surfacerecoveryimage  
(with a different Windows computer if yours is not working properly) 
and download a recovery image for your Surface. You will need a 16GB USB stick
formatted FAT32 (use a Windows computer). Use FULL format and not quick format.
Give the volume a name like surfboot.
Unzip/extract the recovery zip to the formatted USB stick and then-
 
To reset your Surface from a USB recovery drive:
Step 1:    Make sure your Surface is turned off and plugged in (hold down the power
button for 30 seconds).
Step 2:     Insert the USB recovery drive into the USB port on your Surface.
Step 3:    Press and hold the volume-down button while you press and release the
power button on your Surface.
Step 4:     When the Surface logo appears, release the volume button.
Step 5:    When prompted, select the language and keyboard layout you want.
Step 6:     Select Troubleshoot > Reset your PC.
If prompted for a recovery key, select Skip this drive at the bottom of the
screen.
Step 7:     Select the target operating system you wish to reset. This
refers to the current operating system installed on your Surface.
Step 8:    Select Yes, repartition the drives, and on the next page, select Next.
Step 9:     Choose Just remove my files.
Step 10:    Select Reset.
Surface restarts and the Surface logo displays while the reset process continues
(this can take a while).
