yu silly goose say:

@HyruleBaveline is right, the system was originally designed to be discless, and there was a backlash against DRM policies except that's not the whole story. The games would not only install to the Xbox One (under the original design), but games would also registered the rights to the game to the user account, much like modern disc PC games register to somebody's Steam account and all the rights to those games are backed up on the Steam account. Much like on Xbox 360 for digital rights, online isn't required unless someone uses the content on a remote console, or signs on at a friends place to download a game to play there temporarily, you need to be signed into XBL to play those games.

Where the restrictive DRM issue became a problem was reversing policy on used games. See, if a game registers to your account its there for good, you can't go trade it in to GameStop or give it to a friend. You don't even need the disc anymore you can throw it away as all rights would be tied to your account. However, getting rid of used games was bound to upset a lot of gamers, so they reversed policy too late in development. Problem is, it was designed to be a discless system, so only way to allow for used games and a discless system was to have all rights being tracked with that always online DRM. It also would have required a convoluted trading system that retailers would have to be willing to going to great lengths to participate in. In the end it was better just to scrap it, require disc presence as a form of rights verification.

If you want a no disc experience this gen, just buy digital, I've been going that route and I mus say I like it so much more than managing discs. So easy to install, installs in background so I can play other games while they install. Just make sure you have a decent internet connection (and well maintained home network) as game file sizes this generation are huge. Would also recommend getting a larger external HDD, nothing smaller than 2TB (make sure a USB 3.0, I'd go Western Digital).

Downside of going all digital is Microsoft is horrible about keeping the digital market flexible with pricing, unlike the brick and mortar retail market with constantly fluctuating digital pricing. It would have been nice to get those digital rights through such a market but instead we're stuck with this dinosaur of a digital pricing system of the Xbox One's digital store. Digital and the elimination of the used game market is also a way to keep revenues going toward publishers. Only problem is, the industry has yet to make this leap. Given how competitive things are between Microsoft, Sony, and Nintendo, if one makes that leap its way too advantageous for the others not to. Nevermind though Sony too tried to do away with used game media on plans for PS3 back in 2005, but reversed decision too in face of backlash. Everybody wants to do it but is too afraid to do it if their competitor's don't.

cylon0 say:
