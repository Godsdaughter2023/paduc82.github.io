canadian tech say:

Sjagers, yes I have some pretty strong views on the issues you raise.

SSD's are way over hyped.  It is definitely true that a computer with an SSD will boot significantly faster.  A reasonably modern PC with an i5 processor that is not too loaded down with a barn yard full of apps that insist on loading at startup, will boot to the music in about a minute (mine) up to 1.5 minutes.  The same PC with an SSD will boot in 30 seconds or less.

However, except for unusual applications, SSD makes little discernible difference in normal operations after start.

SSD's have a limited life span, much more limited than rotating drives.  The fundamental physics of SSD are that each write actually wears the device.  At some point, cells will "die" and no longer be usable.  Most SSD's come equipped with spare cells to make up for this.  Defragging an SSD is a sure way to shorten its life.  SSD for backup drives is a serious no-brainer.

A failing rotating hard drive almost always gives lots of warning signs, which means you have time to save your data.  A failing SSD dies abruptly.

Because the primary advantage of an SSD is in startup, they make no sense to me in a situation where the PC is started once a day.  This is the case in most desktop PCs, and most laptops as well.  If you carry your laptop around with you all day and use it sporadically many times a day, an SSD will be a significant time-saver.

I am well aware that my view on SSDs will be debated by other professionals.

When I replace a hard drive, I only replace with a 7200 rpm one.  Most PCs come with 5400 rpm and the 7200 makes a significant difference.

I would NEVER divide a drive into more than one partition.  Again, many will debate this position.  However, I see people all the time running into all sorts of problems trying to manage issues about not enough space in one partition and too much in the other.  There are some reasons for partitioning data from programs but in practice they don't really hold up.

In most cases, I replace hard drives with WD black  of 500G to 2T.  They come with 5 yr warranties and are designed for 24x7 running.

azam k say:

Hi,
 
The Code 10 error code is generated when Device Manager has insufficient information or the error that is bubbled up by the device drivers is not recognized by Device Manager. Additionally, this error may occur when one of the drivers that the device needs does not start. If many drivers are being used by a particular device, it can be difficult to determine the one that failed.

The most common resolution is to update the drivers that are associated with the failing device. To do this, follow these steps:

1.In the device's Properties dialog box, click the Driver tab.
2.Click Update Driver to start the Hardware Update Wizard.
3.Follow the instructions to update the driver.

Note You may be prompted to provide the path of the driver. Windows may have the driver built-in, or the driver files may still be installed from the last time that you set up the device. If you are asked for the driver and you do not have it, you can try to download the latest driver from the hardware vendor’s Web site.

If the above step does not help, I would suggest you to update drivers for your Network Adapter as to support Teredo tunneling protocol in Windows 7.

Also make sure that IPv6 is enabled on your Windows 7 machine.

1.Click on Start and type ncpa.cpl and hit enter, this will open your local area connection.
2.Right click on it and choose Properties and under properties enable IPv6 if it is not.
