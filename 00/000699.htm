Now is it possible to calculate an IRR in excel using these as cashflows?

joeu2004 say: TBaz wrote:
> when i put in a guess of say 0.15 im getting an IRR of 3,900.76%

I wrote:
> Okay, I get a #NUM error from IRR(A1:A48) when I use -45000 in A3.
> And 12*IRR(A1:A48,15%) returns about 3,900.7625%.

Now that I understand the correct net cash flows to be 2000 in A1:A2, -45000 in A3, and 1179.74 in A4:A48, you can get the correct answer by using:

12*IRR(A1:A48,15%/12)

Note that the "guess" is 15%/12, not 15%.  Recall that IRR computes a periodic rate, not an annual rate.  Ergo, the "guess" should be close to the expected
But IMHO, it is "bad" that you have to put in a guess in the first place.  Unless you have good numerical sense for time-value problems like loan amortization (I don't), I don't see how you might guess 15%/12 for the periodic rate.

Often, I find that IRR works more reliably with a "guess" of -1% or -10%.  The choice of a negative rate is arbitrary.  It makes no sense to me that IRR should work better with that than with no guess at all.  And I should note:  the negative "guess" does not always work.  But sometimes, a negative rate works better than even a guess that is about equal to the expected periodic rate.  Go figure!

When in doubt, I use Solver.

***EDIT***    My latter comments are based on XL2003.  I believe that IRR is among the functions that were "improved" in XL2010.  So YMMV.
