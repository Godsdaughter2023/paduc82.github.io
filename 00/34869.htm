About MSDN or TechNet Product Keys

They are genuine Microsoft product keys, they are actually retail licenses, but it is intended for a particular product channel either the Microsoft Software Developer Network (MSDN) or TechNet for IT Professionals who pay a subscription fee. The main purpose is for evaluation purposes. The great thing about them, unlike trial Microsoft software, MSDN or TechNet keys don't expire. Because the agreement under which the subscription is provided is a single license, none of the software should be distributed outside of it. Even though its $50,000 worth of licenses, it is for one person only to use and no one else. Unfortunately, regardless of the licensing terms, persons still abuse the program, either giveaway product keys or resell it on auction sites. Microsoft licenses it in good faith that customers won't do so, but I guess human nature wins out.

In your case, what probably happened is, you bought an MSDN licensed key, which carries up to 10 activations unlike full packaged retail licenses which only carry 1 activation. The person who sold it to you probably sold it to 10 other persons. Somewhere along the way, one of those persons might have installed it on a second system, activated it, because it went past 10 activation threshold, Microsoft detected it that it was being abused and blocked the key from further use.

Unless a drive is not being used, a magnetic disk drive will fail from mechanical or electrical causes long before the magnetic media itself deteriorates.  The sort of activity you describe will cause no problems.

Wiping a hard drive periodically will neither "enhance nor compromise the disk performance or mortality" if you are talking about a conventional magnetic drive - except to the extent that any disk activity contributes to normal wear of the mechanical parts.  The write head magnetizes / demagnetizes (more precisely changes the magnetic field strength) of individual bits positions.  This cannot "saturate" the surface.

I don't see any benefit to your annual reformatting in terms of drive life.  Some believe that there is a benefit to rewriting an entire drive with a tool like DiskFresh (http://www.puransoftware.com/DiskFresh.html) to address "bit rot" which is a deterioration of the magnetic field in areas that aren't rewritten for years.  I'm a bit skeptical of it's value with modern drives but it's makes some theoretical sense.

As you likely know, but for those who may not, the life of solid state drives could be adversely impacted since flash memory has a total number write cycles it can support and does wear out.

From day one, the countdown to failure starts.

The more pressure you put on the hard drive, the sooner it will fail.

If it runs too hot because there is insufficient cooling, the failure date will be moved forwards.

If you drop the drive, the failure date will be moved forwards.

Nothing you or any utility can do will stop the process.

Formatting and re-installing Windows will speed up Windows until you put all of the rest of your stuff back but no, formatting will NOT enhance the hard drive. However, the stress of a format and complete reload will bring the failure date forwards.

Welcome to Microsoft Community.

 

Loud fans generally mean the CPU is running too hot.

 

Fans inside computers are intelligent and speed up when the temperature of the components rise. Typically this is the CPU but can also be the graphics chips and possibly other devices as well. When these processors are working hard, they generate a lot of heat, and the fans speed up creating noise.

 

If it's really loud, you may have a faulty fan that needs replacing. 

If you don't know enough about hardware to troubleshoot/fix the problem, You'll have to take it into a computer shop or contact the computer manufacturer, they'll check it out and let you know what they suspect is the most likely cause.
