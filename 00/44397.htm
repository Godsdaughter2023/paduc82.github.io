andre da costa say:
You can download a recovery image for your Surface Book 2 and use that create a recovery drive:

How to download Surface Book 2 recovery image

Head over to the Microsoft’s Surface support page (https://www.microsoft.com/surface/en-us/support...) and sign-in with your Microsoft Account.

Register your Surface Book 2 if you haven’t done it already. If it’s already registered your Surface Book 2 will appear in the list. Simply select the device and click Continue.

The link to download the recovery image for your Surface Book 2 will appear, click the Download recovery image link.

How to create recovery drive for Surface Book 2

The process involves connecting a USB flash drive, formatting it using FAT32, and copy all the unzip files into the drive.

Connect your USB drive to your Surface. (The USB drive should be at least 16GB.)

Open Start.

Search for recovery, and then select Create a recovery drive.

Back up system files to the recovery drive isn’t selected, and then select Next.

Select your USB drive, and then select Next.

Click Create.

Click Finish.

Go back to the recovery image .zip file that you downloaded and open it.

Drag the files from the recovery image folder to the USB recovery drive you created. Then choose to replace the files in the destination.

Check your regional settings in control panel. Make sure your time zone is set to the one that your PC is actually in.

 

If your time changes after switching off the PC, it may be that the battery in your PC that keeps your clock "ticking" is dead and needs replacement. considerate_guy say:

The ability to burn to the legacy video DVD format was only available on Windows Vista and Windows 7. Assuming you are on a later version of Windows, the option is no longer there.

The video DVD format requires a specific format with video encoded in MPEG-2. But that requires a licensing fee be paid. Given that the DVD format only handles up to 480p vertical resolution, it isn't even a good choice for a photo slideshow anyway.

If you still absolutely must output in this older format, you can find software that will do it (say DVD Flick or DVD Styler). But you might want to explore more modern options such as just playing the video (.mp4) from a USB stick to a Smart TV. Or streaming over a network or via something like Chromecast.

Another option, if you have a Blu-ray player (also included in gaming consoles like XBox 360, PS3, PS4, etc.) is to burn the .mp4 file that Movie Maker creates onto a DVD (manually as a data DVD). That is in H.264 format which is understood by Blu-ray players. It won't have any fancy menus, and you are limited to the length of the movie (maybe 30-40 minutes, perhaps more for a slideshow), but it would be in HD quality instead of the older SD format of a DVD.

Windows ReadyBoost can use storage space on some removable media devices, such as USB flash drives, to speed up your computer. When you insert a device with this capability, the AutoPlay dialog will offer you the option to speed up your system using Windows ReadyBoost. If you select this option, you can then choose how much memory to use for this purpose. However, there are some situations where you may not be able to use all of the memory on your storage device to speed up your computer.

Some universal serial bus (USB) storage devices contain both slow and fast flash memory, and Windows can only use fast flash memory to speed up your computer. So if your device contains both slow and fast memory, keep in mind that you can only use the fast memory portion for this purpose.

The recommended amount of memory to use for ReadyBoost acceleration is one to three times the amount of random access memory (RAM) installed in your computer. For instance, if your computer has 512 megabytes (MB) of RAM and you plug in a 4 gigabyte (GB) USB flash drive, setting aside from 512MB to 1.5GB of that drive will offer the best performance boost.
