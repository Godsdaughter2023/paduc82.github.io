and for the rest of the battery's life Here's what they have to say:

 

 

  • Avoid frequent full discharges because this puts additional strain on the battery. Several partial discharges with frequent recharges are better for lithium-ion than one deep one. Recharging a partially charged lithium-ion does not cause harm because there is no memory. (In this respect, lithium-ion differs from nickel-based batteries.) Short battery life in a laptop is mainly cause by heat rather than charge / discharge patterns.

  • Batteries with fuel gauge (laptops) should be calibrated by applying a deliberate full discharge once every 30 charges. Running the pack down in the equipment does this. If ignored, the fuel gauge will become increasingly less accurate and in some cases cut off the device prematurely. 

  • Keep the lithium-ion battery cool. Avoid a hot car. For prolonged storage, keep the battery at a 40% charge level. 

  • Consider removing the battery from a laptop when running on fixed power. (Some laptop manufacturers are concerned about dust and moisture accumulating inside the battery casing.)
  • Avoid purchasing spare lithium-ion batteries for later use. Observe manufacturing dates. Do not buy old stock, even if sold at clearance prices. 
  • If you have a spare lithium-ion battery, use one to the fullest and keep the other cool by placing it in the refrigerator. Do not freeze the battery. For best results, store the battery at 40% state-of-charge.

hello cthulhu say:

RocketRob,

Two solutions present themselves.

First, try to activate your Zune device's wifi. Access the Marketplace through the device. You just need to do that for a moment or two. You won't be able to download any more content from the device's Marketplace, sadly. But then, try playing some of the DRM-Zune Pass content on your device, while you're still logged in. See if it plays. I suspect it will, as long as you still have the Zune/Groove account. This will "reset the clock" on your Zune device's Zune Pass Content, and give it all at least 30 days worth of continued playing. (Also, check the thread here called "Zune Marketplace, RIP." A user name Paul identified a workaround that will still give you some Marketplace access, and also extend the playability of all Zune Pass content on your PC. The details are all there.)

Second, if you just want to get rid of all your Zune Pass content in one fell swoop, try this. Fire up your PC's Zune software. Sync your device. On your Zune library, above the list of artists, you should see something that says, "Show." Normally most users leave that on "All Content." But tweak that, to "Zune Music Pass Content." Now, there are two things you can do here. The simplest would be to just delete everything you see. Your device, when it syncs, should automatically remove everything. The second would be to highlight everything. Right click, and on that menu, select "Never Sync With [Zune Device's Name]." This will also remove everything from the Zune Pass from your Zune device.

I hope this works! Post here if you have any difficulties with either of these solutions.

julie fka profjulie say:

I've just spent the weekend cleaning up my 2 Zune HDs, removing all the no longer playing Zune Pass music from them.  This task took a long time but my Zune HDs were practically useless since probably more than half the music I had stored on them was old, outdated zune pass subscription music.

Zune Music Pass music that was downloaded as part of a subscription (not purchased), is stored on the computer (and a Zune player) as wma formatted files.  Those are the files that won't play any more. 

Music that is purchased is stored on the computer as mp3 formatted files.  This is the music that you own and will continue to play.

If you downloaded some Zune Pass subscribed music and then later decided to purchase it, either by paying for it or by redeeming Zune music credits, the music is downloaded again as mp3 files and the originally downloaded wma formatted files are deleted (this happens in the background and so you may not even be aware of this).  But I have noticed sometimes that the wma formatted songs/albums are not always deleted properly.  I've also noticed that in some instances when the wma formatted music is still on the computer, that music is the music that the Zune desktop software recognizes and displays in your collection and that's the music that the Zune desktop software will attempt to play - it's like it doesn't even recognize that the mp3 files for the same album are also stored on the computer.

Music that is classified as "protected" or "zune music pass" music in the Zune desktop software are wma formatted files.

If when you log into the Groove website, you see that some of your purchased music is also stored on there as purchased music, you can safely delete the wma formatted album/music from your computer and download the purchased album/music from the Groove website - it will be downloaded as mp3 files.  When you download Groove purchased music to a computer you can find it in an xbox music folder on the computer that Groove creates when it's downloading the music (and once created that's where Groove continues to download purchased albums/music).  I had to search around a bit to find the exact location of the xbox music folder (that contained my Groove downloaded music) and you'll probably need to do that too since your computer's file system is probably set up different than mine.
