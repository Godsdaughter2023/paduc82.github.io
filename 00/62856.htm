cmdrkeene say:

Let me try to answer your questions in order.

I did set up Outlook.com, and I think it's connected to the Mail app because when I go to 'add a new account' in the Mail app, choose outlook.com, then type in my Microsoft userid and password, it says I have already added this account. How am I supposed to know that?

It sounds like you probably login to your PC using that account.  The account you login to your computer with is automatically synced to the mail app with no additional actions necessary on your part.

You've said Outlook.com is like a full rich email client, but in another thread, you said once you have outlook.com set up, you can just use the Mail app. But the whole purpose (for me) in setting up outlook.com is to get away from the deficiencies of the Mail app.

Outlook.com is like a full rich client in that it supports collecting email from an unlimited number of other accounts (even POP, which the mail app can't do). There are also some enhancements to Outlook.com this month that allow incredibly advanced rules too (think the level of Office Outlook).

Your original question (having all accounts flow into the same 'inbox' folder):

Once you've added those accounts to Outlook.com, they will all flow into the same inbox folder as you wish (it's optional of course, if you desired you could have them flow into other folders).

Once setup, you can visit Outlook.com to read that mail, or open the Mail app which will reflect the same display locally as you see on Outlook.com (without adding any accounts to the Mail app itself).

How the Mail app and outlook.com work together remains a big mystery to me, even after reading the link you provided (in the other thread).

Basically the Mail app works like a mail app on cell phones/tablets would work: It doesn't store any mail locally, but rather caches it temporarily and stays synchronized. The difference may sound subtle, but the effect is that if you delete or move a message at Outlook.com, the Mail app will also show that change nearly instantly. And vice-versa.  Mail app is basically an app front-end for the Outlook.com service, with the added ability to add accounts from other services that support synchronization (IMAP or ActiveSync).  That's also why the Mail app doesn't do POP.

As for the printer I honestly cannot think of any possible way that would be related.

andre da costa say:
  • Disable (preferrably uninstall) your Antivirus utility before attempting the upgrade.
  • Restart a few times and try again.
  • Disable General USB Devices (example - Smart Card Reader). 
  • If you are using a SCSI hard disk, make sure you have drivers available for your storage device on a thumdrive and it is connected. During Windows 10 setup, click the Custom Advanced Option and use the Load Driver command to load the appropriate driver for the SCSI drive. If this does not work and setup still fails, consider switching to a IDE based hard disk.
  • Do a clean boot and try again.
  • If you are upgrading using the .ISO file, disconnect from the Internet during setup, if you are connected by LAN (Ethernet) or Wi-Fi, disable both and attempt setup again. 
  • If you are updating through Windows Update, when the download reaches 100% disconnect from the Internet LAN (Ethernet) or Wi-Fi and proceed with the installation.
  • If that does not work, try using the .ISO file to upgrade if possible.
  • If you are connected to a domain, switch to a local account
  • If you have any external devices attached to the machine, disconnect them (example, gaming controllers, USB keys, external hard disc, printers, non-essential devices).
