MS does not use a 14-digit "registration number". It does use a 25 character "Product Key".

After you do your activation you don't need the key again to download and install any "Windows Update" security patches or enhancements.

Reading between the lines, and letters, I am guessing that you want to "upgrade" to 2010 or 2013. The upgrade process does not work that way.  You have to buy a whole new license. 2007 was the last version that MS sold price discounted "upgrade" licenses for that required an earlier version to "upgrade from".

However, you do need the Product Key to re-install Office. So if you don't have a record of it outside of your computer you MUST do that now!  The product key is your best, almost only, proof of purchase that MS accepts. If you lose it, ie a computer crash, you lose your right to use that copy of Office.

Reset your password for global administrator account, in O365 - Microsoft Community

brunorrfernandes say:

Reset your password for global administrator account, in O365

Technical Level : Intermediate

Summary

If you are a user, and you can’t login to your O365 account then you should address this matter to your company/global administrator, he can reset the password for you.


Details

If you are a global administrator and for some reason you can’t access your account, then – either you ask another global admin to reset your account password, either – you can reset the password yourself by clicking Can’t access my account link, choose work or school account,

 

this will take you to https://passwordreset.microsoftonline.com/ where you can reset your password.

Before going forward, it’s important to know, that you will need the access to the alternate email and the phone number you put when you created this account.

Now, you are on the password reset page, just put your account name in account field, type the captcha code and hit NEXT.

At this point, you must choose a contact method that will be used for verification. Can be your alternate mail or phone.

 a security code will be sent to your alternate email address, or to your phone, depending on the option you selected. Paste the code in the field hit next and you can reset your password.

Some of the accounts can have additional security options, case in which, you will get addition security verification. Meaning that, after first step verification, let’s say you received a security code by email, you pasted it and hit next, another security code will be sent to your phone (or you can choose to be called) and after confirming that also, you will be redirected to the reset password page.

The steps are easy and clear, but the most important part is to have your Admin details up to date, otherwise none of the above will work and you should call Microsoft support for help.

My strong recommendations are:

  1. Verify periodically that your global admin account details are up to date (both – phone and alternate email)
  2. Have an extra admin account. You can create a global admin account without assigning any license. You can still access the tenant with this account and perform all the admin tasks with it.

It is perhaps worth discussing the expectations we should have for the reports we make to Microsoft.

Everything that goes in through the Smile Icon ends up in the Triage Team, a group of computer engineers whose job it is to ensure they can replicate the problem.

Obviously if they can't reproduce the issue, it is treated as a "one-off" and no further action is taken.  The data is not discarded: if they get further reports of the same issue, they will connect to your report.

If they do get a repro, they then send a formal bug report off to the product team (the coders) who follow the normal methodology common in large software companies using the Agile methodology:

  1. The issue is confirmed.
  2. The cause is identified.
  3. An estimate of the effort and resources to fix is made.
  4. The defect report then ends up in the Defect Prioritisation Meeting.
  5. The defect meeting then considers the number of users affected, the impact on each, and the cost and time to fix.
  6. As a result of this, each defect report gets a points score.
  7. The defects that score enough points get scheduled for fixing.

The Smiley Icon does note that Microsoft will never respond to the reports they receive.  They have been known to break this rule, if you give them a working email address and they need more information.

Either way: memory leaks, especially those that lead to crashes, get lots of points and thus have a high priority to fix.

A realistic response time for a defect is about two to three months.  Yes, I have seen them fix defects in three days, but that was a data-loss bug in a "Released" product.  The Preview products are still in beta-testing: even if they don't work at all, we should expect 2 to 3 months response on defects in the Preview.

So Microsoft is not "totally ignoring" your report.  It is following a process that actually takes a lot more time than many people imagine.

So I encourage everyone to report every problem you find at least once.  The only bugs we can be sure will not be fixed are the ones that don't get reported!
