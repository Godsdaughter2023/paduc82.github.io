¡firedog say:

This is solely the result of settings in Outlook 2016. You are sending to some or all of the recipients using Outlook's proprietary RTF format. This uses an encapsulation method known as TNEF which only Outlook and some webmail clients understand. Other mail clients can't cope with TNEF-encapsulated attachments. You'll find a complete explanation of the phenomenon along with detailed instructions how to deal with it in this article: 

How email message formats affect Internet email messages in Outlook

RTF should only be used within the corporate environment, or when corresponding with users you know to be using Outlook clients (Outlook 20nn) or Outlook.com or Gmail webmail. I'm afraid I don't know whether Outlook apps on iOS and Android are capable of unencapsulating winmail.dat attachments.

It's unfortunate that 'rich text' is in every other situation synonymous with HTML. Only Outlook uses it to describe a proprietary format which also covers functions like meeting invitations and voting buttons. That's why it's immaterial whether you choose plain text or HTML as the sending format: if RTF is invoked, many recipients won't be able to use the attachments you send.

It has nothing at all to do with your web host or the type of account involved at your end.

rohn007 say:
Expanding on Doug's point. MS and computer sellers do not usually give away full copies of Office for free.  If you read the fine print in the store ad for your computer you will see that there is a 99.9% probablity that the "came with" you think you got was actually "Office Starter 2010" (a crippleware version of Office) or the 60 day time limited "free" trial.  That is probably what is asking for the product key now that the time limit has expired.

Buying Office 2010: Summary

This page has similar information: https://rstlaw.wordpress.com/2010/11/04/may-i-see-your-license/

When home users and small businesses buy office, it is my personal preference to insist on the Boxed CD "FPP" or Retail license of your choice of the 3 Office bundles. In the Retail version you get the Installation disk built into exactly the same price you would pay for software only in the online download. And, it gives you the best set of user rights of the license. It allows you to install on 2 or 3 machines at the same time and to transfer the installation when you want to. The "PKC" or Product Key Card is slightly cheaper, but allows installation on only 1 machine and no transfer rights. If your brand new computer "melts down" in the hardware warrantee period, it will be replaced, but you will have to BUY another PKC license!
