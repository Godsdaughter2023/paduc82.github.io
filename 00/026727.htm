    sd1233 say:

    PowerPoint will always display your presentation at a size that's proportional to the slide size you've chosen in the Design | Page Setup dialog box.

    Your slide size is probably proportional to your monitor, which is just what you want for normal purposes (ie, showing the presentation full screen), but by electing to show the title bar and task bar, you're lopping off a bit of the vertical space available to PPT.  

    So it reduces the height and width to fit the new, slightly less-tall space, which is no longer proportional to the slide size; hence the black bars at each side.

    So what you do is this:

    Put the presentation into slideshow view, press Alt + PrtScrn to take a screenshot of the entire screen.

    Start an image editing program (www.irfanview.com Irfanview is a good one and free if you don't already have one you like).  Paste the screenshot into the image editing program.

    Crop out the title bar and task bar, leaving just the PPT slide and any black surround.

    Note the height/width of the new image in pixels.

    Now go back into PPT and (probably on a COPY of your original presentation) change the slide size to something proportional to the dimensions of the cropped screenshot.

    For instance, suppose the cropped image is 1920 x 1000 ... make the slide width 19.2 inches and the height 10 inches.

    Now start the show again.  It should pretty much fill the available area, sans black bars.

    Hi Steve,

    4.  Changed Dimensions in PowerPoint to Match cropped image in Photoshop

    5.  Pressed F5 for final result (note: although you can't see the taskbar completely, this does work for my purposes as I will use this slide as a background for a training video I am doing with Camtasia Recorder and studio).

    The processing of images will have some responsibility for the delay you are experiencing, even on a Mac.  I experienced the same type of delay with a 100+ page doc that had two or three screen shots per page. If you are moving text or images around, injecting more text or adding additional graphics, the analysing will take place as Word recalculates where elements appear in the document. 

    Some solutions

    You can speed up the processing by swithcing off the display of images.

    You can create a master document and therefore have less content in RAM at any one time.

    You can port the document to a DTP application that would deal with the document in a very different manner.
