jim gordon mvp say:

Hi

The fact that booting in Safe Mode temporarily fixed things points to specific things you can test for.

Your initial report specifically found that the cause of your crash was due to faulty memory:

CrashedApp_Virtual_Memory_Size_MB: 15298466
CrashedApp_Resident_Memory_Size_MB: 8993.429

Are the current crash logs still indicating they are caused by bad memory?

Booting to safe mode does several things, any of which can lead to the source of your crashing problems.

1. It clears the font cache. If there was corruption in the font cache, it could indicate you have a problem font on your Mac. Things went well until you used that one font. Is there a font you used in PowerPoint (but not in other apps) that might be bad? Before you try radical things, use Font Book(it’s in Applications > Utilities folder) or other font utility to validate ALL your fonts. Delete old versions of duplicate fonts. Delete fonts that don't validate completely. Restart your Mac and empty the trash to get rid of the unneeded fonts and free up disc space.

2. Safe Boot fixes many file issues. Disk Utility runs in the background and fixes directory and B-tree problems. Third party disc maintenance utilities do a better job. Consider letting Drive Genius, TechTools Pro, or other disc maintenance utility check your drive for errors.

3. Safe Boot prevents 3rd party apps from running at startup. Often a third party app (for example TypeIt4Me, WebEx, etc) that is out of date will cause problems with Office apps.

Having your physical RAM checked by Apple is a good idea. You can run the antagonistic tests yourself, though. The thorough test takes many hours.

https://support.apple.com/en-us/HT201255

Reverting

You have several options:

  1. Use Time Machine to restore the previous version of just PowerPoint. There is a possibility that interaction with other office apps could cause unexpected behaviors (crashes, strange results). People have done this without trouble. All I can say is try it and see what happens. AutoUpdate may try to update the old version and you may have to change AutoUpdate to Manual in Help > Check for Updates.
  2. Go forward instead of backwards. This is the option I suggest rather than go backwards. Join Insider fast. Yes, the builds are pre-beta, have undocumented new features and new bugs. But this is where you’re going to get the first fixed build. Plus, you won’t have mixed versions of apps trying to work together. You will get to interact directly with the Office team in Insider Forums. What is Office Insider? - Office Support
  3. Completely remove and then reinstall an older version of Microsoft Office. It’s a lot of work. Be careful not to delete your Outlook profiles. Your Outlook profiles may not work in an older build of Office
