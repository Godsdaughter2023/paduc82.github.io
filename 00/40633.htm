juliuspiv say:

Not sure I'll be able to solve everything but I want to share something with you.  If you, engrattny, already understand what Symlinks and Junction/Junction Points are skip ahead; this is mainly for those who may not be familiar and thus may not understand how we arrive at our conclusion.

Since Vista, various folders no longer exist.  For instance 'C:\Documents and Settings' is not a real folder; its called a Junction or Junction Point.

If you open up a command prompt and type `dir %SystemDrive%` you won't see a  'Documents and Settings' folder.

  • `%SystemDrive%` is an Environment Variable that stores what drive Windows Vista/7 is installed.  I could have just as easily said type `dir c:` but if Windows Vista/7 is installed on your 'D:' drive then you will not be able to reproduce the intended and desired results of this test.  So if Windows Vista/7 is on C type C:, if its on D type D: (etc).

If you type `dir /a %SystemDrive% (or `dir /a c:`) you will see a lot more files and an entry that reads:

  • <JUNCTION>     Documents and Settings [C:\Users]

Just so it's said: Its not a shortcut!  It's nothing like the icons you might have on your Desktop or Start Menu.  Its more of a pointer that says "If an application tries to access this location, redirect/remap to C:\Users."

Having said all this, when you `cd C:\Users` from a command prompt and perform a `dir /a`you'll see a <SYMLINKD> and a <JUNCTION> entry.
Jump (or `cd`) into your profile (which would be `cd c:\Users\Name` following the example above) and do another `dir /a`.  You'll find plenty of junctions.  This continues even if you go into C:\Users\Name\AppData and in other places as well.  Junctions and Symlinks are' everywhere'.

-----------------------------------------

To answer some of your questions engrattny:

  • Where you see two folders: My Documents and what looks like a shortcut to My Documents, AppData and what looks like a shortcut to Application Data, they're there for backwards compatibility.  This allows applications not 'coded' for Vista/7 paths, or are hard coded to look for specific locations that no longer exist, to ultimately place files in the appropriate location.  (Its been my experience however, that in certain circumstances, files get placed in a path different from what you would have expected/anticipated but everything works fine.)
  • Junctions and Symlinks are not 'browse-able' like a normal folder.  You cannot double click on the 'Application Data' 'folder' (remember, its not a real folder; I use that term loosely here just because it looks like a folder. Again do not be misled: its not a true folder) for instance and access its contents.
  • Having said that Junctions & Symlinks are not browse-able, you cannot 'open' them to see the contents.  So for instance the 'Application Data' junction points 'C:\Users\Name\AppData\Roaming'.  So if you want to see what's in 'Application Data' browse to 'C:\Users\Name\AppData\Roaming'.  (Experiment: From a command prompt do a `dir "c:\documents and settings"`and see what you get.  Then try to do a `dir "c:\Users\All Users"` and see what happens.  Try to understand why it works that way.)
  • There's no need to rename or otherwise modify the Junctions.  They're there by design and its best to leave them alone.  If you want to rename the real My Documents folder to just plain Documents, have at it, but I don't know how other things in the system will handle that.
  • If my understanding is correct: When you open an Explorer Window you've got the Folder listing on the left pane.  When you browse to C:\Users\Name from the left pane you see both the real folder and the Junction, correct?  You can click on the real folder in the left pane (to reveal the contents of the folder in the right pane of the Explorer window) but when you click a Junction in the left pane, it fails and shows an access denied error right?  If what I just said is true, I can't explain why the Junctions show up, regardless of whether or not 'show hidden files' is enabled, when one cannot access them.  (Good point and nice catch.)   I've tested this on my current Win 7 Ultimate machine (Version 6.1.7600 Build 7600) and two Windows 7 Enterprise machines with mixed results so its hard to 

Pulling from elsewhere, here's a brief explanation of what's what in the %userprofile%\AppData folder

  • Roaming is the folder that would be synchronized with a server if you logged into a domain with a roaming profile (enabling you to log into any computer in a domain and access your favorites, documents, etc. Firefox stores its information here, so you could even have the same bookmarks between computers with a roaming profile.
  • Local is the folder that is specific to that computer - any information here would not be synchronized with a server. This folder is equivalent in XP to C:\Documents and Settings\User\Local Settings\Application Data.
  • LocalLow is the same folder as local, but has a lower integrity level. IE8, for example, can only write to the locallow folder (when protected mode is on).

This document from Microsoft (download is a .doc file) has a long explanation for what these three three folder areas are and how they are used, as well as the changes implemented between XP and Vista and of course Windows 7 since it retains the Vista structure.  This document is one of a series of documents in the Windows Vista Step-By-Step Guides for IT Professionals.
