You've asked a lot of questions. I'll try to answer in sequence.

  • The current Word interface is intended to make more operation "discoverable". Earlier versions of Word had mostly the same commands grouped under menus, but research revealed that most users couldn't (or didn't bother) to find them. The interface in the last 10 years has concentrated on giving the commands more screen space so it is easier to find them. The downside has been that every operation takes 8 clicks where it previously might have taken 2.
  • If you move an object a little and it jumps a lot, it's because of how you have specced the positioning. Position objects relative to the page rather than to the paragraph or margin and incremental changes will be more reliable. I can give you the steps if you're unsure how to do this.
  • Delicate layouts? Again, it's in the technique of positioning. It's possible (I do it every day) to make rock-solid layouts that are very reliable. One example: for very complex layouts, consider placing the elements in a table. 
  • For shapes "buried" under others, learn about the Selection Pane.
  • Things that should be simple: as with PhotoShop or Illustrator, the more time you spend learning the software, the faster you get. But not every person needs to learn every program. Consider specializing in what you do well and send the Word files to an expert. After 30 years working with Word, it's pretty easy and transparent, but there is a learning curve.

Others have addressed your questions in more detail but I'd like to interject one simple point based on your reference to "other companies" :-) That point is that Adobe doesn't supply a word processing program. No offense, but that comparison, alone, indicates that you are expecting Word to behave in a manner comparable to software which is based on a totally dissimilar design for a completely different - albeit tangentially related - purpose.

Unlike others, I make no claim that Word is the "end-all & be-all" for the creation of every type of document. It's true that numerous convenience features for dealing with graphic objects have been added over the years, primarily due to popular demand. However, it is not, nor is it intended to be, a page layout/desktop publishing program. It may well be that the nature of your work would better be served by a program of that genre than it is by any word processor.

As for the UI, that's a quite subjective matter. I'm not enamored by the Ribbon concept, either, but having been exposed to it since the introduction Office 2007 (PC) I've come to find it far more functional & useful than I originally had, but anything significantly different takes some getting used to. It's far better now that some degree of customization has been added & should get better over time. IMO, it's far more manageable than the plethora of palettes splayed all over the screen in Adobe products & significantly more robust & usable than what is provided in the Apple software.

A couple of suggestions that may be useful if you haven't yet 'discovered' them... For dealing with multiple objects &/or stacked objects in a document;

  • Turn on the Selection Pane available by clicking the Arrange button on the Shape Format contextual tab,
  • Assign keyboard shortcuts to the DrawSelectNext & DrawSelectPrevious commands,
  • Assign a keyboard shortcut to the DrawToggleSelectObjectMode command &/or add the Select Objects tool to the QAT.

Understandably frustrating, but you do have to understand, each individual cellular carrier has to develope the ROM flash for their devices, so that it still connects, and complies with their network once it's flashed.  NORMALLY, the hardware comes to them from the manufacturer with their network specs pre installed, and it's part of their "purchase agreement" with the hardware vendors (HTC, Motorola ect.)  When an update is released from Microsoft, even if, say HTC is ready for implementing it onto their hardware, the cellular carrier, has already placed an agreement to purchase the hardware, with OTHER software installed.
