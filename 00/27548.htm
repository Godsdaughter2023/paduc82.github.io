Windows Core OS The Complete Guide | Windows Central

Another major step on the way to the Core OS was something called "Windows 10 S", which though it still contains a few legacy components, removed many of the least secure and most attacked, resulting in a version that's not only more secure, but also much leaner and faster, making it ideal for less powerful hardware like tablets.

Introducing Windows 10 in S mode – Performance that lasts

What Is Windows 10 S, and How Is It Different | How-To Geek

Though as the first Microsoft link above describes this as intended for Students, they also mention that it's useful for anyone wanting better security and performance for the long-haul, especially for those consumer users who really don't understand the technical issues or how to properly protect the more complex older Windows based PCs that contain all of the vulnerabilities of that legacy OS.

For example, Windows 10 S only allows installing apps from the Windows 10 store, which along with being a more secure place to get them as these are tested by Microsoft, also means they operate without most of the less secure legacy components that were most easily attacked by malware.  In fact, most of the popup and other malicious advertisements that display messages to call Microsoft won't even work on Windows 10 S, along with most of the malware that depended on those same old portions of legacy Windows to operate.

So as is often the case, Microsoft itself is far ahead of the computing public, but few are even aware, so they keep buying the big old PCs and laptops and/or "upgrading" their new PC or tablet to an inferior version of Windows 10 Pro, that though a few business users have some old applications that will only work with this outdated version, most consumers have no need to install.

Think about it.  If the malware can't run, then why do you need the additional software to protect from it, so the problem's already solved.  Most of the extra software the geeks and experts installed on Windows were to protect, maintain or monitor portions of the legacy operating system that with Windows 10 S, simply no longer exist.

This is one of the commonly misunderstood parts of where Microsoft is heading with Windows 10, to a leaner, more secure and faster version that doesn't have most of the old problems, while it still runs nearly all of the major software that has bothered to keep up with these changes.  So if you have a favorite software that doesn't, either tell them to get it up to current standards or simply move on to something that does, voting with your money and your feet.

The basic truth here is that the PC is actually a general purpose computer, which though what most of us have grown up with since the early 80's, was never the proper tool for the general consumer population.  Instead, what was supposed to happen was that these PC devices would be used and reworked by engineers and technicians into simpler, more limited computing tools that employees or consumers would use to perform daily tasks.

Unfortunately, the fact that the PC was so capable and available, especially as early competition brought their cost down to reasonable levels, meant that most software developed quickly to use the PC itself as the platform for many applications, often simply hiding it in cupboards or back rooms where only technicians or knowledgeable employees required access.  Over time though, the PC became more common in visible locations, as well as in the homes of those needing to perform certain management or other tasks from there as well.

What's changed in the last dozen years or so is that people like Steve Jobs finally began to create tools like the first smartphone and others that were more suited not only for consumers, but anyone needing the ability to be more mobile to perform certain limited computing tasks in multiple locations at effective cost.  So what the mobility trend finally did was to begin the conversion of the computer itself into more of a dedicated purpose tool that though more limited, was typically both easier to use and mobile as well.

The remaining set of general purpose tasks including banking, online shopping and general browsing or reading have often stayed with the PC, but even those are often more effectively done on other devices that are either more mobile like tablets or even less so like TVs with Internet capabilities.  SO as people learn these options are available, the trend away from the PC is only increasing.

That's why Microsoft along with others is concentrating their efforts in these areas, especially where consumers are concerned, since that's where they know they'll need to resolve the issues of security and education we're discussing here.

That's also why Microsoft is creating these lean versions based on Windows Core, starting with the Windows 10 S Mode and parallel products for business like Windows IoT, since these applications require smaller, simpler to use interfaces and devices that can either be very mobile or included as part of some other larger device.

Its here that Microsoft is working to simplify and harden the operating system, since along with often inherently simpler tasks, the consumer or lightly trained employees will often be involved with using them.  So designing out not only the obvious risks of technological complexity and vulnerability inherent to the outdated legacy Windows general purpose computer design, but also the overhead this creates is obviously the best way to resolve most of these problems in the future.
