ronbarker say: You need to look at the license. A clue about usage is in the description, for example: Office for Home and Business. If the Office suite has the word 'business' description then you can use it for business purposes. You cannot use Office for Home and Student for business purposes. But remember the license in most cases is for use on a single computer. Again see the license>File>Account>About PowerPoint>Click on the blue hyperlink 'View the Microsoft sofware License Terms'

steve rindsberg say:

Some versions of Office include language like this in the EULA (End User License Agreement):

"The service/software may not be used for commercial, non-profit, or revenue-generating activities."

If your colleague is paid to create and/or deliver talks using PowerPoint, that'd seem to fall afoul of the "revenue-generating" prohibition IF he or she happens to have one of the versions of Office that prohibits this.  The EULA isn't very clear about which versions this set of terms applies to.

>> ... why would anyone want PowerPoint at home when they can't show their creations to anyone outside their house?

That's not entirely prohibited. They can show the presentations at school, to friends/neighbors/relatives and so on. I expect that by "non-profit" they mean in support of the activities of a 501c3 or similar organization, not more generally any activity that happens not to turn a profit.

They can also use their copy of PPT to view presentations that others have created (though I suppose viewing company-provided presentations is off-limits).

What it boils down to in the end is not that jack-booted software-STASI will break down our doors and invade our houses in search of illicit PPT usage, but that your colleague's employer is covering its sensitive bits (legally speaking) by insisting on a professional licensed version of PPT.
