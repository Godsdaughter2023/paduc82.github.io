Instead of "inserting" the PDF file into a separate Word document, use the Open dialog to open the PDF file. Word 2013 will attempt to create an editable Word file from the PDF original (which won't be changed).

This should work if the PDF was create from text (such as a Word or Notepad file). To create editable text in that case, you'll have to use optical character recognition software to "read" the pages and output text. If you have OneNote, you can use that -- see http://office.microsoft.com/en-us/onenote-help/extract-text-from-pictures-and-file-printouts-HA102749810.aspx.

After you have the PDF contents in a Word document, you can add the other pages and save the result as a new PDF file.

Let me start by saying my hardware experience is very close to yours. I still have and use my Zune HD, however. Had a Trophy, still have an 822 (my tester for Win 10) and also a Lumia Icon.  Second, not all the changes are because they benefit Microsoft. The record labels have much say in DRM and if you want your service to provide a lot of variety, you have to satisfy the labels.

Starting with Xbox Music, the Microsoft music service became a subscription service and did not really include a media management component. You still had it with Zune and/or WMP11 until a few weeks ago, but now that is retired (along with the older DRM it used).

The major problem with Microsoft music, as I see it, is it continues to evolve a lot faster than its loyal users can keep up with. Combine this with the legendary (cough) Microsoft documentation and you have confusion, frustration, etc..

To eliminate some pain, try to forget about a USB cable and syncing. It is only an afterthought since Windows 8 and Xbox Music (as you may know). Today, your "collection" is in the cloud and you draw from that for portable devices. Xbox Cloud Match died quietly, too (never really acknowledged by MS). I read it caused many sync issues and generally MS will can something that screws up that much. Your local collection has to be either manually tagged again from the catalog (add your own mp3 tracks to your "collection" if they are in the Groove catalog) OR now you can upload stuff to OneDrive and make your own cloud collection (I have the entire Beatles catalog there, for one, as everyone knows there is zero Beatles stuff online except for iTunes).

Modern (Groove, Spotify, Apple Music) music services work mostly the same way---they need your device to acquire DRM after downloading from them, at least initially and then periodically to maintain it and make sure you're still paying. No more plug in, sync and go. Only Rhapsody still has this and those days are probably numbered.

Zune software features have been largely re-created in a different form in Groove. Smart DJ is now "Radio", but "Radio" is only catalog content (not the mix of catalog and "owned" like Smart DJ could do). You can fix this, though. Create a "radio" station. Save as a playlist. Sprinkle in some of your own stuff. Pretty similar function, but a different way to accomplish. The CD burning, tagging, etc. functions are not in Groove. Since Groove is the front-end of a music subscription service, it is irrelevant for burning or tagging---since you can't do either one with subscription tracks anyway. If you want to burn, tag, do advanced auto playlists, etc., you are going to use your own music and Windows Media Play, MediaMonkey or any number of other programs that still function well in Windows 10. Groove is a player---period. If you have tons of music not in the Groove catalog, upload it to OneDrive. Groove plays it fine, just like stuff in the subscription catalog.

Not to nit-pick, but that latest Groove update did add many features. Re-organizing playlists is a big one. Several other things.

This is the new Microsoft system, apparently. They release something that is at least basically functional, then constantly release improvements. Is this better than waiting for one huge update every year? In my opinion, it is FAR better this way.

I can't comment on how Groove works with an Android phone, since I only use Lumias. It's basically fine with Lumias. Offline music works well, downloads are fairly fast, I rarely have a problem I can attribute to Groove (I am running pre-release Windows Phone 10, and updates to that have screwed things up now and then which is not the fault of Groove).

I have also though Groove works much better on my Xbox 360 than Xbox Music did. Playlists sync and play faster, for example.

This is really just different than the old way of doing things. You can still get it all done, but it happens in a different way now.

It can probably never be truly simple to sync music anymore, because it would then be a lot easier to steal music and the labels are not going to allow this.

Ask on this forum---there are enough of us now who can probably address specific issues.
