There are many editions of Windows 10 to choose from, each of which is intended for a different purpose or set of purposes.

The Home edition is intended for just that, "home" users or general consumer use. The Pro edition is for computer enthusiasts or possibly home office users. Enterprise is for business use and the Education edition is for educational institutions.

The Enterprise edition allows greater control of the system that could be helpful in meeting your needs. Hi ,

 

The delay that is occurring is a result of the computer processing the guitar signal before playing it though the computer speakers.

 

If you’re using additional effects through the software you’re using to record the instrument, it may add to the delay.

Sometimes a long cable can create a slight delay as well.

 

You might try using a shorter cable from the guitar to the computer sound card.

 

You may also be able to find a hardware solution, in the form of a “break-out” box sound card that may reduce that delay.

You might try closing any unnecessary software that may be running on the computer, as it could potentially add to the delay.

 

You can check your recording software to see if it has a way to offset the delay, though it is usually only a degree of milliseconds that can be correct.

 

You may be able to quantize the tracks in post-production to correct the delay, but you would have to consult your recording software vendor for information regarding that option. http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantization_(music)

 

I’m not aware of a way to correct the delay using any native Windows software or settings.

Hi,

I also had this problem after upgrading from Win 7 to Win 10 - I had 2 tasks returning a similar error message. I had not created those tasks - they seemed to be remnants from previous applications - one I think was related to Internet Explorer  RSS Feed Synchronisation - the other I have no clue.

Methods 1,2 & 3 proposed above did not fix the issue.

The tasks were nowhere to be found within the Task Scheduler, but did exist in the C:\Windows\System32\Tasks folder.  I deleted the tasks from that folder.  Deleting those tasks did not solve the problem.

I then went to the registry (regedit.exe) and did a search for all registry entries for the deleted tasks (eg in your case i would have searched for "Auto Shutdown".  From memory I found 2 or 3 entries related to each task.   On close inspection it was fairly clear in my case that the registry entries I found were related to the problematic tasks that i was dealing with.   I deleted those registry entries.  

I restarted, crossed my fingers and  held my breath.   All was good, and the task scheduler errors were gone.

I should add that I first took a full image backup (Easeus Todo Backup is great for that - the free version is fine), so I could do a full restore in the case of failure.

Not for the faint hearted, but might be worth a try if you are keen.
