Here is a step-by-step technique to do put text within a picture, using Word 2016 (I will assume you know how to get the picture into your document):

[1]  Open a blank Word document.

[2]  Insert your picture into Word (lots of ways to do this, I will not specify a particular way, as you apparently already have the picture in your Word document, for this particular question).

[3]  On the Insert menu, select the "Text Box" button, and then select "Simple Text Box".

[4]  Select the picture, and then select "Format -> Position -> More Layout Options".

[5]  Select the "Text Wrapping" tab, and click the "Behind Text" button, and click OK.

[6]  Your text box is now on top of the picture, but is has a white background, which you might not want.  If you want to remove the white background, select the text box, and then select "Format -> Shape Fill -> No Fill".

[7]  If you do not want a border on the text box, then select "Format -> Shape Outline-> No Outline".

[8]  You might want to change the color of the text, within the text box, to make it more readable.

[9]  You might want to change the font size of the text, within the text box, to make it more readable.

[10]  You might want to make the text bold, within the text box, to make it more readable.

[11]  The following screen-print shows an example of the above steps, shown within Word 2016.  I used a picture that I took of a tree, and a text box that I formatted to 14-point bold yellow:

david marcovitz say: I'm not sure if there is something specifically for PowerPoint, but I would start with the principles laid out in the Cambridge Handbook of Multimedia, edited by Richard Mayer. One thing that you will learn is that "PowerPoint overload" is a specific case of the theories of "cognitive load." What you are likely to find is that most things that are specific to PowerPoint won't talk about them in terms of psychological theories, but if you have a good understanding of cognitive load theory, you will be able to see how the tricks and tips relate (or fail to relate) to good solid psychological research.

crs kumar say:

I have reviewed the online document "Five Ways to reduce the power point overload".

During a recent presentation, I observed that the presenter had lot more information in his each of his slides such as several images, details etc. For a layman, the presenter looked to have depth and well researched. But from the perspective of "Power point overload", we can observe that he was violating all the principles listed there.

 

The principles listed in the "Five ways to reduce power point overload" document by Atkinson and Mayer  are( with my comments:  

1. The Signaling Principle

Doc: people learn better when the material is organized with clear outlines and headings (the Signaling Effect).

> Headings cannot be more than 5-6 words. The heading need to relate to the content of the slide.

2. The Segmenting Principle

Doc: People learn better when information is presented in bite-size segments (the Segmentation Principle)

>There needs to balance in segmenting. We need to be judicious in breaking the content into right sized segments to suit the slides.

3. The Modality Principle

Doc: People understand a multimedia explanation better when the words are presented as narration rather than on-screen text (the Modality Effect).

> A picture is worth thousand words. Clear pictures are more suited than colorful catchy ones.

4. The Multimedia Principle

Doc: people learn better from words and pictures than from words alone (the Multimedia Principle)

> The narration that goes along with slides are going to very important. Also, animations, video clips have movements in them which have a different impact.

5. The Coherence Principle

Doc: people learn better when extraneous material is excluded rather than included (the Coherence Principle).

> Distractions need to be avoided. But a very long presentation without breaks is going to tax the audience interms of their ability to focus on the presentation.

 

Most important principle which is very good takeaway from this document is the clear purpose of power point presentation in comparison with a word document.  The way content is organized in the power point presentation is quite different from word file.

 

Warm regards

CRSK

steve rindsberg say:
I'd never show the audience the slide sorter or even PowerPoint itself, not so much because I'd be worried about overload, but simply because a jumble of little images isn't part of the presentation itself, contributes nothing to it, and looks unprofessional when it pops up on the screen.

I'm not clever enough to remember the slide numbers of all the slides in my presentation and if slides are moved/deleted, that could change at the last minute, so I'd prefer to use hyperlinks to other slides, either visible or hidden, depending on the situation.

As to the right length of the presentation ...

Someone once asked (the very tall, very long-legged) Abraham Lincoln how long a man's legs should be.  Lincoln replied "Long enough to reach the ground."

Likewise, the length of the presentation should be sufficient to carry the amount of information to be presented within the time available.  That might mean adjusting the presentation or the amount of content you're going to present to fit the time-slot.  

By the way, look up Pecha Kucha.  It's a fun way to learn more about presenting and a very effective discipline to follow (or adapt) when planning/writing presentations.  It helps drive home the lesson that you can't always tell the audience all you know or even all you'd like to tell them.  Sometimes trying to do that will overwhelm them, to the point that instead of coming away with half of what you've presented, they leave with nothing (except perhaps the will never to sit through another presentation like THAT again).  ;-)
