ken sheridan say: You apparently have a many-to-many relationship type between customers and services.  This is modelled by three tables, in broad outline like this:

Customers
….CustomerID  (PK)
….Customer

Services
….ServiceID  (PK)
….Service

and to model the relationship type:

CustomerServices
….CustomerID  (FK)
….ServiceID  FK)
….DateProvided

The primary key of the last table is a composite one of the three columns.  The table can also include further columns representing other attributes of the relationship type, e.g. the charge made for the provision of the service.

The usual interface for this sort of thing would be a customer's form, in single form view, within which would be a customer services subform in continuous forms view.

To return the number of instances of each service provided within a date range a query along the following lines would be used:

PARAMETERS [Enter start date:] DATETIME,
[Enter end date:] DATETIME;
SELECT Service, COUNT(*) AS NumberProvided
FROM Services INNER JOIN CustomerServices
ON Services.ServiceID = CustomerServices.ServiceID
WHERE DateProvided BETWEEN
[Enter start date:] AND [Enter end date:]
GROUP BY Service;

The user would be prompted for the dates at runtime.  In developed application, however, the parameters would be unbound controls in a dialogue from which a form or report bound to the query would be opened by means of a command button.

ken sheridan say: I'm afraid that you have confirmed my suspicions as regards the bad design of your database.  You are really using a spreadsheet design not a relational database design.  Relational databases are very different from spreadsheets in both concept and implementation.  By having 'various services in numerous columns' what you are doing is 'encoding data as column headings'.  A fundamental principle of the database relational model is the Information Principle (Codd's Rule #1). This requires that all data be stored as values at column positions in rows in tables, and in no other way.

The outline model which I described in my earlier reply would form the basis for a correct model in which all data is represented as values at column positions.  Aggregation over sets of rows is then possible using the built in aggregation operators such as COUNT, SUM etc, making it very simple for you to design a report based on an aggregating query, with parameters entered by the user at runtime to restrict the results.  If you wish to present the results under service column headings rather than as rows in a result table, you can do so by means of a crosstab query.

tom van stiphout say:

> Other table consists of various services in numerous columns with combo box lists to check more than one

That is a REALLY BAD IDEA because it is a flagrant foul against proper relational database design. You are opening yourself up to a world of misery if you continue this way. Rather scrap it, and redesign it using the rules of normalization. That's what Ken is trying to explain to you. Follow his guidance. This is really not up for debate.

scottgem say:

Let me add to Ken and Tom's advice. Relational databases should be tall and thin, not wide. Each service that is performed for a customer should be a record in a table, not a field in a record.

a. user say:

Hello there, laurie prevost! Thanks for posting your query on Microsoft Community.

About your concern, if your phone stops working or responding like it used to, restart it to get back on track.

To restart, press and hold the Power button until a Slide down to power off message appears on-screen, then swipe down. (It usually takes about three seconds for the message to appear.) To turn your phone back on, press the Power button.

If that doesn't work, press and hold the Volume down and Power buttons at the same time until you feel a vibration (about 10 to 15 seconds), then release the buttons. Your phone will restart automatically.


Please be reminded that:

Your phone may vibrate as soon as you press the Volume down and Power buttons because it's changing your ringtone to vibrate. Wait to release the buttons until you feel the second vibration 10 to 15 seconds later.

If you don't let go of the buttons immediately after the vibration at 10 to 15 seconds, your phone may show a black startup screen instead of restarting normally. If that happens, try pressing the button combo again.

Not all phones restart this way, so check with your phone's manufacturer to find out how to force a restart.


If your phone has a replaceable battery (it's often under your phone's back panel), you can also restart your phone by removing and reinserting the battery, and then pressing the Power button to turn on your phone. The date and time may be off after you restart this way, but you can fix that by swiping over to the All apps list from Start, then selecting Settings > Time & language > Date & time.
