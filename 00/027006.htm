steve rindsberg say:
No can do.  Not in PowerPoint, at any rate.

PowerPoint is pretty good at editing "vector" graphics (pictures constructed of mathematical descriptions of lines, curves and so on).  It's not much at editing "raster" graphics (pictures constructed of lots of dots of different colors that, like a pointillist painting, coalesce into a picture if you stand far enough back).

You'll need to do the fills in an image editing program or, possibly a better idea, use a tracing program like the one included with CorelDraw to convert your raster image to vector graphics that can then be filled however you like.

Another thought: where do the technical drawings come from?  Probably not an image editing program in the first place.  If you have access to the originator of the drawings, see if they can export them in WMF or EMF formats.  You could bring these into PPT then ungroup to get at the individual shapes.  And fill those.

There is a big difference between three separate lines (which are not fillable) and a freeform shape (which is fillable)

 

Like I said, draw your triangle with a Freeform shape (usually on the right-edge, middle row of the Insert Shapes gallery in the ribbon).  Close the shape by double-clicking on the starting node.

It may help to have the Selection Pane showing when you ungroup the re-imported picture, as there may be more than one freeform shapes that result when you ungroup the imported picture.  Home tab > Select (right-hand end of Home tab)> Selection Pane

 

Finally, it may be that ppt2010 or 2013 may have improved functionality in this area due to the Combine Shapes utility.  Steve may be able to comment more about that.

 

As for pattern fills, they got hidden in the User Interface in 2007, but you can still apply them with VBA code, or an add-in (http://www.pptalchemy.co.uk/patterns.html or http://www.andypope.info/charts/pptpatternfills.htm).  Not sure if they made a come-back in 2010 or 2013....

Rich

jay freedman say:

The text of a document has been saved into your Normal.dotm template, which serves as the basis for every "new blank document". To fix it, you can either open the Normal.dotm template itself and delete the text, or (if you don't have a lot of customizations saved in the template) rename or delete the existing Normal.dotm and let Word create a new one.

Instructions for doing these procedures are in http://word.mvps.org/FAQs/AppErrors/BlankDocNotBlank.htm. However, as the article is rather old, you can ignore its instructions for finding and opening the template, and use this instead:

  • Press Alt+F11 to open the macro editor.
  • Press Ctrl+G to open the Immediate window in the editor.
  • Type or copy/paste the expression NormalTemplate.OpenAsDocument into the Immediate window and press Enter at the end of the line.
  • Close the macro editor. The Normal.dotm template will be visible in the main Word window.

hansv mvp say:

With the year (2008 for example) in A1, and the birth date in A4, the birthday in the specified year is on

=DATE(A4,MONTH(A1),DAY(A1))

With the date of employment in A2, the number of days of employment on that birthday is

=DATEDIF(A2,DATE(A4,MONTH(A1),DAY(A1)),"d")
