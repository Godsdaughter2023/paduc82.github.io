Unrequested phone calls of this nature are almost always a common scam. Do not give them any info, do  not give them access to your PC, do not give them any money and do  not go to any websites they suggest. Hang up.

 

Please see:

http://www.microsoft.com/en-gb/security/online-privacy/avoid-phone-scams.aspx

 

Microsoft never makes unasked for support or security phone calls.

 

These types of callers may use completely fictitious names or bogusly claim to be from genuine companies other than Microsoft as well.

 

If you gave them access to your PC then what you do will depend on the sort of information you store on it and how you use it. Personally, I would, as a minimum:

·        Change all passwords that may have been compromised. Do this on a different PC as yours may

         have been infected and send the new passwords to the scammers.

·        Do a clean install (including a format) of Windows or restore to factory condition. You will lose all

         data, so back up to a separate drive. If you do not feel confident doing all this get

         professional/expert help.

·        Check the above back up for viruses using at least two independent up to date anti-virus scanners

         (only install one real-time scanner though).

·        Tell your bank, credit card companies, etc. what has happened.

·        Watch for suspicious charges/payments on your credit cards and bank account(s).

·        Watch your email accounts for suspicious activity.

·        Consider telling the police/local authorities.

·        With the main exception of cleaning up your PC, much of what you need to do is common sense

         stuff. Think how the bad guy could misuse anything that was on your PC and take steps to

         counteract it.

 

If you did not allow them remote access then "Software Bundler" may still be associated with malware - see one example here:

http://www.microsoft.com/security/portal/threat/encyclopedia/entry.aspx?Name=SoftwareBundler:Win32/Cakeport  .

rdrewd2 say: SA:
I'll try to answer all 5 of your questions here,
Should you get rid of it?  If it houses your recovery files, probably not.  But if you create a recovery usb drive, then yes, you can delete it if you really need that space.  To create a recovery drive, get an 8GB USB flash drive handy and then type "create recovery" into your search box, click on "create recovery drive" and it will guide you through how to do it.  If you format D:, yes, that erases everything on that partition.  How to get rid of it?  If you've created an external recovery USB drive, here's how:
Right click your Windows start button and click "Disk Management".  On the bottom half of that screen, right-click the D: partition and click "Delete Volume".  That will turn it green and now it will say "free space" on it.  Right-click it again, and click "Delete Partition", and it will go from green to black and say "unallocated".  Now right-click on your C: drive and select "Extend Volume", and in the wizard just type in the maximum space/total volume left, and your C: drive will extend to fill that space.  I'd still recommend keeping D:, but if you really need the space and have created a recovery USB drive, go for it.  Until then, deleting D: would be a very bad idea. What else is it helpful for?  If you don't use it for recovery, you can store backups on it or files you want to keep separate from your C: drive.  Why would you ever need to repair Windows?  Because stuff just breaks sometimes. Go to the search bar on this site and type in "lost files" or "BSOD" (blue screen of death) and see how many pages of results you get.  Congrats on the new computer; protect it by getting an external hard drive or large volume USB drive and make running backups your new habit.  Hope that helps.  Cheers.

Your ability to work on documents doesn't have to do with what version of Windows you are running. It has to do with what kind of documents they are and whether you have an appropriate program for them installed.

ken blake say:

OK, then these are Microsoft Word files. You say you want to work on them, and I assume that that means that you want to edit them.

Neither Windows 10, nor any other version of Windows, comes with Microsoft Word, nor with Microsoft Office, of which Word is a part. But presumably when you bought your Windows 7 computer,  the vendor you bought it from bundled Microsoft Office with what he sold you.

So now, for you to open and edit these .docx files, you either need to get Word, or some compatible program. You have several choices:

Buy a copy of Microsoft Word

Buy a copy of Microsoft Office

Buy a copy of WordPerfect

Download and install a free copy of LibreOffice

Download and install a free copy of Open Office

The last three choices are compatible choices, but you should always bear in mind that they are *largely* compatible, not perfectly compatible.

So what I recommend is to download and install LibreOffice. Give it a try and see if it meets your needs.

Also bear in mind that .docx is a relatively new extension for Word files; the used to end in .doc. So whatever program you get, make sure it's a recent version.
