jay freedman say:
The blue-and-underline formatting of hyperlinks in Word is supplied by applying the character style named Hyperlink to that piece of text. Ordinarily that style will be applied to hyperlinks in text that you paste from other documents or other programs (including browsers) -- assuming that in the Options > Advanced > Cut, copy, and paste section, the pasting options are set to the default, "Keep source formatting".

To find out what's going on, position the cursor within one of the hyperlinks that doesn't look like a hyperlink. Then:
  • Press Ctrl+Alt+Shift+S to open the Styles pane.
  • At the bottom of the pane, click the middle of the three buttons to open the Style Inspector.
  • Look at the "Text Level Formatting" section. Does it say that the Hyperlink style is applied there? If so, hover over the word Hyperlink in the box to see its definition. It should say "Font: Underline, Font color: Hyperlink".
My guess is that the Hyperlink style actually is applied, but its definition has changed to just "Default Paragraph Font" -- that is, no underline and Automatic color. If that's true, then you need to modify the style back to its original definition. Unfortunately, Microsoft neglected to supply any way to say that you want the Hyperlink color (a hidden member of the set of 12 theme colors) as part of a style definition. The easiest way to do it is to use the Organizer (click the Import/Export button in the Manage Styles dialog) to copy the Hyperlink style from an old document where it appears correctly, to your Normal.dotm template and to each existing document where the style is incorrect. An alternative would be to pick one of the blue colors (either a theme Accent color or the standard blue) in the Modify Style dialog's color picker -- this won't be exactly the same as Hyperlink blue, but it's easier to accomplish.


As additional information about why a couple of your attempts failed:

The AutoFormat As You Type setting governs only what happens when you type a hyperlink followed by a space or punctuation. It completely ignores pasted text.

The AutoFormat setting governs only what happens when you manually invoke the AutoFormat command (Ctrl+Alt+K, or the AutoFormat Now button that can be added to the Quick Access Toolbar). It also ignores pasted text until the command is invoked.

I have an elegantly simple "black box" solution.

Visit

FindAll

http://www.tushar-mehta.com/excel/tips/findall.html

and copy the FindAll function into a standard module.

Then, use the code below.

Sub findAllAndHide()
    Dim StartTime As Double: StartTime = Timer()
    FindAll(What:="0", _
         SearchWhat:=Application.Intersect(ActiveSheet.UsedRange, ActiveSheet.Columns("AJ")), LookIn:=xlValues, LookAt:= _
         xlWhole, SearchOrder:=xlByRows, SearchDirection:=xlNext, MatchCase:=False _
         , SearchFormat:=False).EntireRow.Hidden = True
    Debug.Print Timer() - StartTime
    End Sub

I tested the above with 100,000 rows of data, 1,000 of which met the criterion, with no contiguous matches.

Mike's code took between 0.79 and 0.88 seconds.

My first solution took between 0.42 and 0.45 seconds.

The above took between 1.67 and 1.71 seconds.

Unless my customer complained of the performance, I would use the above solution.  The simplicity and ease of maintenance may be well worth the additional 1.2 - 1.3 seconds.

My tests were with Excel 2010 32-bit on a Win 7 64bit OS.
