a. user say:

Re: hiding FormControls group boxes on WS

I don't believe there is an 'manual' way of hiding FormControls group boxes on a worksheet.
If you right-click the sheet tab and choose "View Code" then...
In the big white window on the right, paste in the following code...

'---
  Sub HideInPlainSight()
  Dim shp As Excel.Shape
    For Each shp In Me.Shapes
      If shp.FormControlType = xlGroupBox Then shp.Visible = msoFalse
    Next
 End Sub
'---

Then place your cursor anywhere within the pasted code and tap the F5 key once.
(that runs the code).  All group boxes should be invisible.

To reverse the process change "msoFalse" to msoTrue and tap the F5 key.

brian borg say:

This can also be caused by a problem with your router, if you have one.

A router is commonly part of a wireless access device, or a broadband gateway.  The router usually provides NAT and a DHCP server, and configures each attached computer to refer to it for DNS.  It then refers DNS requests to the ISP.

Sometimes, you simply need to reset the router by shutting it off and on again, or unplugging it and plugging it back in.

Sometimes, your ISP has changed its DNS servers, or the primary one is temporarily down.  You can find that out by talking to them.

You can often tell if you are on a router by determining your IP address.  Do "ipconfig /all" in a command prompt.  If your IP address starts with 192.168, it is a "private" address and you are on a router.

Instead of pinging, you can use nslookup (in a command prompt) to see if how your DNS server is responding.  Not all routers respond to that, but it would work like this:

C:\>nslookup www.livejournal.com
Server:  abitw2k3r2.borg.local
Address:  192.168.2.253

Non-authoritative answer:
Name:    www.livejournal.com
Address:  208.93.0.128

Hi David,

There are three times to consider - Transition time, Transition Effect time,  and Total Transition time.

Transition time + Transition Effect time = Total Transition time.

ex.  1

Your Transition Time =20.00

+

Your Transition Effect Time = 1.50 (split effect)

Total Transition Time =21.5

ex 2. 

Your Transition Time =20.00

+

Your Transition Effect Time= 2.00 (wind effect)

Total Transition Time=22.0

ex 3. 

Your Transition Time =20.00

+

Your Transition Effect Time = 0.70 (fade effect)

Total Transition Time=20.7

The answer is in the type of 'transition effect' you have chosen for each slide

 because the inherent nature of each effect needs a basic minimum time. Ex.'Wind Effect' cannot be completed in less than 2.0 secs..

Ex. You can,  set 'wind effect'  duration to 00.01 as you have done.

The program now has a dilemma, 'wind effect' requires a minimum 2.0 secs or you get no wind effect.

Powerpoint chooses (defaults to)  the correct minimum time (2.0) for a wind effect - keeping wind effect at the expense of your own 00.01 'duration' setting. 

To see this clearly go to the 'Transition' settings and set your duration to 00.01 as you have mentioned.

Now watch the 'duration' time change as you select different transition types.

For example, the 'morph effect' requires 2.0 sec, fade effect 0.70 sec, split effet 1.50 sec. etc.

If each slide has a different 'transition effect' each slide's 'total transition time' will differ.

To solve the problem:

Ex Wind Effect =2.0 sec set Transition to 18.0 sec so that Total Transition Time = 20.0 secs
