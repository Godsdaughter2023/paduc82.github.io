single letters not picked up in spell check - Microsoft Community

cayce1 say:

single letters not picked up in spell check

I have noticed that single letters added accidentally when typing into the body of a paragragh are not picked up by spell check. Why and how can they be made detectable?  
yves dhondt say:

I doubt they can as single letters can be used in a sentence. How else would you write "The word hotel starts with the letter h and ends with the letter l."?

What you could do is create AutoCorrect entries which transform single letters into something else. For example, you could map b => bWRONG, c => cWRONG, d => dWRONG, and so on. As the words bWRONG, cWRONG, dWRONG, ... don't exist, your spellchecker would mark those as spelling mistakes.

Note that AutoCorrect entries will not work when followed by a period as Word thinks you are typing an abbreviation. One way around that would be to create an extra set of AutoCorrect entries which map b. => bWRONG., c. => cWRONG, and so on.

To add AutoCorrect entries in Word 2007, press the "Office" button => "Word Options" button => "Proofing" tab => "AutoCorrect Options..." button => "AutoCorrect" tab.

quietman7 say:
Actual ransomware usually will have obvious indications (signs of infection)...it typically targets and encrypts data files so you cannot open them on your computer (and all connected drives at the time of infection), in most cases it appends an obvious extension to the end or beginning of encrypted filenames (although some variants do not), demands a ransom payment by dropping ransom notes in every directory or affected folder where data has been encrypted and sometimes changes Windows wallpaper. Somes types of ransomware will completely rename, encrypt or even scramble file names. Less obvious symptoms include adding or modifying registry entries and deletion of when dealing with real ransomware, the cyber-criminals generally instruct their victims to contact them by email or website for decryption...they do not provide a phone number to call for assistance.

If there are no obvious extensions appended to your file names, no ransom notes, no demands of payment, your data is not actually encrypted, then you most likely are dealing with "your computer is infected with ransomware", “all your files are encrypted", "suspicious ransomware activity" or some other version of a Tech Support Scam as noted by Le Boule. Typically the scammers provide a phone number for their victims to call for assistance.
