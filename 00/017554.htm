.Hello PPT

First, going to go through some basics given that you have covered a real wide range of topics

a) Outlook.com (formerly Hotmail) is a provider of <email accounts> which you can access either via your browser or by configuring it in any <email client> of which Outlook desktop (part of the Office suite) or the Windows 10 Mail app are just two.

b) Without knowing anything else - am assuming that when you refer to Windows 10 Outlook that you are referring to the free Windows 10 Mail app (an email client) which in now way is intended as any kind of replacement for Outlook desktop (part of Office).

So with the above out of the way

Re: #1 - As stated in (b) above - Windows 10 Mail (free) is completely separate and distinct from Outlook desktop (part of Office) - the latter is by far a more complete and superior of the two. Neither of which limit you to the number of email accounts you can configure.

Re: #2 - The fact that you've been using Outlook desktop for so many years AND you have historical data that you want to retain access to would almost answer the question about the ultimate choice to be made going forward. If it were me - would be a non-issue - stay with Outlook desktop (part of Office).

Re: #3 - As already stated, your Outlook.com email address is an "email account" - everything else are "email clients".. I have an article on that topic at

Understanding Email Accounts and Email Clients
https://www.contactgenie.info/understanding-ema...

Summary answer: changes to <email clients> will have no impact on your <email accounts - otherwise referred to as your <email address>

Re: #4 - Your various email account/addresses.

Every email address is always specific to a particular email service provider (ESP) regardless of what email client(s) you use. Change your ESP for whatever reason and your email address will change.

Personal opinion based on what you've described/asked.....................

Save yourself a whole lot of aggravation and just continue on with using Office 365. If you get a new computer (and everyone does at some point or other) - then just install Office on it and copy the relevant files over as required. There's no "one size fits all" description of what does or doesn't need to be copied - all comes down to how the email accounts are configured which is why am not addressing that particular portion of your post.

bob rettie say:

I bought a new windows 7 computer.  After importing Inbox into Outlook, I now have 2 inboxes.  the first Inbox has my new incomming messages since installing Outlook on the new computer the second Inbox is a folder under the first Inbox with the messages  that were imported from my old computer. 

How do get everything I imported moved into the first Inbox? 

russ valentine say:

Importing has never been the correct way to transfer Outlook data, as you are demonstrating. Nor is there any such thing as a separate "Inbox" to import. Suggest using one of the supported methods for data transfer that we post here daily. The file you need is your Personal Folders file (*.pst) It's where all the mail, calendar, contacts etc are stored.

Take a look at these pages for info on Outlook data backup or transfer:
http://www.slipstick.com/config/move_outlook.asp
http://www.howto-outlook.com/Howto/backupandrestore.htm
http://office.microsoft.com/en-us/assistance/HA010771141033.aspx

Note that some of the information on the Microsoft page is outdated and contains errors, particularly when applied to Outlook 2003, 2007 or 2010. Specifically:
1. Ignore any advice to copy your PST file to Outlook's default location to connect it to your profile. Doing so will often corrupt your Outlook profile. Copy it anywhere BUT the default location and then open it within your Outlook profile.
2. Ignore any advice that tells you to use export or import to transfer an entire Outlook data file. That has never been sound advice, but the process has become too deeply flawed to be trusted.
