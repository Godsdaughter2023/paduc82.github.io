cmdrkeene say:

Let me answer the last question first.  IMAP doesn't provide any way for actions on one end not to be replicated elsewhere.  When you delete a message from a mail client program (like the Mail app or Outlook or whatever email program you use), the IMAP protocol tells the server to perform that action and the program just re-syncs to show whatever the server did.  In most email programs you can configure where these special IMAP folders map to.

The purpose of IMAP is for total synchronization.  If you move an email to a folder (or delete it, which is the same thing because you're moving to the deleted folder), that action is performed on the IMAP server and any device that gets that email (your phone, PC, tablet, etc) will reflect the move.  Using IMAP, you can never have a device "out of sync".

--

Although most people consider Outlook.com to just be a simple competitor to Gmail, it actually functions as if it was a full email program. By this I mean, it can send and receive emails through any other server, just like a full email program might do. You can leverage this power to use Outlook.com to allow you to use POP email accounts through the Windows 8 mail app.  And because it functions like a rich full email program, you don't have to change your email address and no one will ever know you're using it.  I describe this, and the difference between POP and IMAP implications, to much greater extent in this post:

http://cmdrkeene.com/use-pop-email-windows-8
