After 2 1/2 weeks of fruitless troubleshooting, I was not able to find the update causing this problem.  I was not able to make this problem reappear except by performing a fresh install of Windows 8.1 (nuke and pave), installing updates, and then adding my Microsoft account and trying to create a picture password. The solution I found is as follows:

  1. Perform a System Refresh.
    1. Go to PC Settings.
    2. Tap or click "Update and recovery" on the left.
    3. Tap or click "Recovery" on the left.
    4. Tap or click the "Restart now" button.
    5. Tap or click on the "Troubleshoot" button.
    6. Tap or click on the "Refresh your PC" button.
      • It's the icon with diamonds above the hard drive.  Make sure you click the right one, or you'll be doing a next-to-nuke and pave instead.
    7. If the administrator account has a password, you will have to log in.
      • Beware if you use a keyboard layout other than Sholes's Workaround (aka US aka Qwerty). Before entering the password, tap or click on the "Change keyboard layout" text, then tap or click on "See more keyboard layouts" until the keyboard layout you use appears. Except for "US," they are listed in alphabetical order by language.
    8. Insert your Windows 8.1 media if prompted, read what will happen, tap or click the "Next" button, and when ready, tap or click the "Refresh" button.
      • When Windows says a list of apps removed will be saved to your desktop, it means to the desktop of the administrator account you use for System Refresh.
    9. Wait while Windows performs the System Refresh.  The PC will reboot a few times.  You will know it's done when the lock screen reappears.
  2. Log in to the account(s) affected by this problem, and create a picture password.
    • When you log in after a System Refresh, Windows will treat it as it did when you logged in for the first time ever. Double check and readjust every security and privacy setting that you customized before the System Refresh.
  3. Reinstall your removed apps and reapply updates from Windows Update as normal.

Note that System Refresh may not work if you upgraded from Windows 8 to Windows 8.1 via the Microsoft Store.  It will only work if you created Windows 8.1 installation media or if Windows 8.1 was preinstalled.  See the thread "How to refresh Windows 8.1 if upgraded from Windows 8 and files are missing" for more information.

ken blake say:

Two points:

1. Separating data from Windows is a false comfort if it’s done with the thought that the data will be safe if Windows ever has to be reinstalled. The reason I call it a false comfort is because I fear that many people will rely on that separation, think that their data is safe there, and therefore do not take appropriate steps to back it up. In truth the data is not safe there. Having to reinstall Windows is only one of the dangers to someone’s hard drive, and not even the most likely one. This kind of “safeguard” falls into the same category as a partition for backup of other partitions; it leaves you susceptible to simultaneous loss of the original and backup to many of the most common dangers that affect the entire physical drive, not just the particular partition. Safety comes from a strong backup regimen, not from how you partition.

However for some people it can be a good idea to separate Windows and programs on the one hand from data on the other, putting each of the two types into separate partitions. I think that most people’s partitioning scheme should be based on their backup scheme, and backup schemes generally fall into two types: imaging the entire hard drive or backup of data only. If you backup data only, that backup is usually facilitated by having a separate partition with data only; that permits backing up just that partition easily, without having to collect bits and pieces from here and there. On the other hand, for those who backup by creating an image of the entire drive, there is usually little, if any, benefit to separating data in a partition of its own.

By the way, in all fairness, I should point out that there are many well-respected people who recommend a separate partition for Windows, regardless of your backup scheme.  Their arguments haven’t convinced me, but there are clearly two different views here.

2. Some people make a separate partition to store backups of their other partition(s). People who rely on such a "backup" are just kidding themselves. It's only very slightly better than no backup at all, because it leaves you susceptible to simultaneous loss of the original and backup to many of the most common dangers: head crashes and other kinds of drive failure, severe power glitches, nearby lightning strikes, virus attacks, even theft of the computer.  In my view, secure backup needs to be on removable media, and not kept in the computer.

Here are comprehensive instructions that will keep you safe and resolve your issue the "right" way.

A "Cannot find...", "Cannot start..., "Cannot load...". "Could not run..." Cannot run" "Error loading..." or "specific module could not be found" message at startup is usually related to malware that was set to run at startup but the referenced file(s) has been deleted after a malware scan leaving behind a registry entry or startup item pointing to a file that does not exist.

HI Donna. I'm Greg, an installation specialist and 10 year Windows MVP here to help you.

That message normally means that someone has activated Windows with a Volume License Key meant only for business or organizations that activate a large number of PC's, which need to check in with the organization's server regularly to reactivate.

Sometimes this is done by shops reinstalling Windows, unnecessarily because the PC either came with a Windows Product Key embedded in the BIOS/UEFI firmware, or it was already activated by free Upgrade or a retail Product Key, and would have reactivated after a reinstall had they given it the chance and not interrupted this by installing the illegal key.

In your case if it's certified Refurbished then that's licensed by Micrrosoft and they must meet standards including the licenses they sell or they will have their license pulled and can even be prosecuted.

If you know Windows was originally activated legitimately by one of these methods then to fix the problem what I would do is go offline to remove the Volume License key and then restore the Generic Product Key that is used to activate with the Digital License issued by Upgrade or Factory install, the factory key embedded in the UEFI/BIOS firmware chip, a refurbisher key you were provided on a sticker or insert, or a retail key you used. Then we'll see if it activates:

1) Go offline by opening Action center at the right end of Task Bar, then clicking on Airplane mode to turn off internet.

2) Next type CMD in Start Search, right click to Run as Administrator, then right click to copy and paste this command in and press Enter: slmgr -upk

3) Restart PC and then insert Generic Product Key for your licensed version from here: http://winaero.com/blog/generic-key-to-install-...

This sets everything back to default so that if there is a Digital License issued during a proper Upgrade it should reactivate within a few days. If not we will need to try something else:

If it doesn't activate after going online and you originally had factory installed Windows, read the Product Key embedded in the UEFI firmware chip at the factory by running ProducKey: http://www.nirsoft.net/utils/produkey-x64.zip, copy and paste the OEM-BIOS key (only) into Settings> Update & Security > Activation.

If you were sold a Volume license product key or the PC came from seller activated with one, then It is piracy when someone resells Windows with no way to legally activate it. You can report this to Microsoft here: https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/howtotell/cfr/r.... In the past when victims cooperated with an investigation they would sometimes give them a free copy as thanks.

You can also contact the seller and let him/her know you are taking these actions and that they need to make the activation good. Many will comply rather than risk investigation or prosecution.
