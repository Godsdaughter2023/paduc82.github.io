john korchok say:

I can't think of a simple way to print your document, but here are some ideas:

You actually want to be printing 2 copies of page 1 on the first side of sheet 1, then 2 copies of page 2 on the second side of sheet 1. Although plenty of printers can print 2 pages on each side of a sheet, they're always a sequence of pages rather than duplicate pairs. I can't remember ever coming across a printer that could do that.

You could duplicate each page in Word, so that your document had 2 copies of page 1, then 2 of 2, etc. This would create a problem with automatic page numbering, if you're using it.

Or you could print a PDF of your document. If you have access to a full copy of Acrobat, you can duplicate the pages in it. If you don't take a look at the free CutePDF online editor: CutePDF Online PDF Editor. Using the Advanced tool, you can hold down the Control key and drag pages to duplicate them. Then when you have your PDF with pages 1,1,2,2,3,3, etc. Download it and print it using the 2 pages per sheet setting on your printer.

If, for some reason, CutePDF doesn't work for you, you can save each page of your document as a separate PDF, then use Word's Insert>Object>From File command to create a new Word document with each PDF page placed twice on a landscape A4 sheet. Then just print normally.

It appears that you have two extra paragraph marks included as part of the footnote separator (the horizontal line). To remove them:

  • Change your document view to Draft.
  • Click Show Notes on the References tab to show the Footnotes pane.
  • From the Footnotes drop-down list, select Footnote Separator.

djsteele, mvp say:

Many of us would advise you not to put combo boxes into tables.

Users should never be interacting directly with tables (or queries): there should always be a form provided as an interface. The combo box belongs on the form, not the table.

 

daniel pineault say:
To further add to Doug's post (with which I strongly agree), this would be when you'd create a lookup table, a table to hold you options and use this table as the Row Source for the combo box on your form.  The added benefit here is that you can then create a minimalistic form to allow the admin user to add/delete/modify the options themselves!  You can also, using the not in list event of the combo box, enable automated additions to your existing list of option.  All sorts of possibilities becomes possible with this type of setup.
