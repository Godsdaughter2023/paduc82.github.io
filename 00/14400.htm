The link you included refers to Office 2016 for Windows. There's a frequent recommendation to turn off Detect language automatically in Windows, as it causes many problems. It's not even an option in OS X. Your keyboard has nothing to do with language switching. Nothing is "deducing" that you have a Spanish-made keyboard. 

My advice for people who use multiple languages is to create separate templates for each language, or at least separate styles in their Normal template for different languages. Word has only one Normal template and each style in that template has a language attribute. If the language attached to your styles is Spanish, then that language will always resurface.

But if you only need to use mainly one language, here's a basic outline of how to set it up in OS X and Word (you may have already completed these steps, I'm including them for other readers):

  1. In the Finder, open System Preferences>Language & Region.
  2. In the left pane are listed your preferred languages. The language at the top is your preferred language. If Spanish is primary, and you don't want it to be, drag it down to a lower position, so English is at the top. When you're finished, close the Preferences.
  3. Look at the OS X menu bar. At the right end, you should see a flag, indicating the current language that OS X is set to. My installation shows a U.S. flag, for U.S. English.
  4. Now open Word. Choose Tools>Language. Select the language you want to be the default while you are in Word, then click on the Default button. Word will ask whether you want to set the default language, click on Yes.

With the settings above, you should be set to type in U.S. English all day long. If you need to switch among languages, setup gets a little more complicated, but this should avoid your switches back to Spanish. Let me know how it works for you.

swischner say:

I am sorry, but I have to tell you that when using the app there is no offline backup you could use to restore your lost notes.

You can stop looking for any usable local OneNote files; there aren't any. 

You are right, there is a sort of offline version, enabling you to edit your notes while not connected to the internet. This is the OneNote cache, a copy of all content of currently opened notebooks, but in a binary, not reusable format.

All editing of your notes is done to that cached copy first. The cache is then sync'ed to OneDrive when you are online (again).

This cache is kept as long as you don't close your notebook, reset or reinstall the app. Usually a crash of the app **should** not affect the cache, but it seems in your case it has.

Generally it has to be said, that as long as your notes are not synchronised to OneDrive, they are somewhat at risk; the cache as only storage is rather "fragile".

The desktop version of OneNote (OneNote 2016) does have a backup mechanism that saves your notebook locally in a format that allows a restore, but for it to work you have to be in that desktop version and open your notebooks from there. This is no backup mechanism which would work indepentently from the OneNote client and the app does not have a backup mechanism at all. Sorry to be the messenger.

a. user say:

Hi ChintanSheth,

 

Thank you for replying! If you have already your contacts on CSV file, please have a look at the steps here below.

Import your contacts into Windows Live

  1. Go to http://contacts.live.com, and sign in to your Windows Live account.

  2. Click Manage and then select Import.

  3. On the Add people page, click Outlook.

  4. Select the Microsoft Outlook (using CSV) option.

  5. Click Browse and find the CSV file that you exported from Outlook.

  6. Click Import contacts.

  7. On your phone, swipe left and tap Settings, tap Email & Accounts, tap and hold Windows Live, and then tap sync. This may take a few moments.

Hope this helps you.

Carlos
