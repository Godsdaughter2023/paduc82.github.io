When the Zune stuck in charge mode, you have differents cause for this :

 

Battery Issues

Instead of a serious issue with your Zune, the problem may just be a completely dead battery. Even after you plug the device in, the Zune requires that there be a little bit of power in the battery before it will come on. Make sure that the charger is secure and let it sit for about 30 minutes, then try to turn the Zune on and see what happens. Also, if your Zune has a lock switch at the top, ensure that the switch is in the "unlocked" position.

Minor Software Glitch

A minor glitch may cause the Zune to freeze at the charging screen and not allow you to turn on the device. You can fix this glitch by simply forcing the Zune to restart its system software. Press and hold the "Back" button while holding "Up" on the direction pad for about five or six seconds and your Zune should restart normally. If you have a Zune HD, instead of the button combination, just press and hold the "Power" button for about 10 seconds.

 

Leap-Year Problems

There was an issue in older Zunes that caused the devices to stop working on the last day of a leap year. The only way to get the device working again is to remove the charger and allow the Zune's battery to die, then plug it back into the charger after 12 noon GMT on the first day of the next year. This bug only affects the Zunes on the last day of leap years, but if you don't let the battery drain it will stay frozen forever

Major Software Issues

Perform a standard reset on your Zune, then press and hold the "Back" and "Play" buttons, along with the center of the directional pad until you see "Connect Zune to Your PC" appear on the screen. ken sheridan say:

I'm not sure the Access Query Designer lets you work with subqueries in the designer.

To a degree.  The outer query can be built in query design view, but the SQL for the subquery has to be entered in the criteria row of the column by which the subquery is restricting the outer query.

Going off on a slight tangent, care needs to be exercised with the NOT IN predicate as it will fail if the subquery returns at least one NULL.  You can easily see why if you expand it algebraically, e.g.

x NOT IN(a,b,c,NULL,d)

expands to (x<>a) AND (x<>b) AND (x<>c) AND (x<>NULL) AND (x<>d)

which, by virtue of De Morgan's rules, will always evaluate to NULL, neither TRUE nor FALSE.

I don't think that can apply in the OP's case, but on the whole the NOT EXISTS predicate is a safer bet, and often more efficient.

We have recently implemented a security update called Familiar location protection to keep our customers’ accounts secure.

The message "You haven't signed in from this location recently." will occur if you are signing in from a location more than 300 miles from your normal location.

The Microsoft account service will prompt you for an additional challenge to complete the log in.

Codes can be the date of birth, or receiving a code sent to your SMS number or alternate email if configured.

 

To prevent this you will need to configure your computer as your trusted PC and other security information for added protection to your account.

 

With the new system update, you can remove old inaccessible security information on your account automatically after 30 days.

1.       Log in to this link.  

2.       Click Delete on the security information you do not have access to. (Alternate email, Trusted PC, Mobile number)

3.       Select  "No, I Don't have access" button. (The date on when the security information will be removed will appear )

4.       Click Delete Security Info.

5.       Update the account with new Security Info.

6.       Click Save.

 

You just need to wait for 30 days for all the information to be removed and the new security information to be automatically confirmed.
