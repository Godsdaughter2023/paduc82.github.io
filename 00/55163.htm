Hi JoelBenitez2,

I think that the cause of audio shuttering could be a bad old audio driver or that your BIOS need an update.
However it isn't bad to perform a complete maintenance of your device.
First of all I suggest you to perform a scan and repair of filesystem:

1. Type "cmd" in windows search bar
2. Right click on "Command Prompt"
3. Select "Run as Administrator"
4. Type "sfc /scannow" without quote and press ENTER

Then you can perform a clean boot:
https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/help/929135...

At this point proceed with the driver update (I suggest you a remove and install again procedure) like this:

1. Press WINDOWS + X
2. Click on "Device Manager"
3. Click on "View"
4. Click on "Show hidden device"
5. Click on each element on list and if you see "transparent" device then:
a. Right click on the transparent device
b. Click on "Uninstall device"
c. If asked, click on "Remove software driver" option and the click on
"Ok"
6. Once deleted every "transparent" device, then click on "Audio video and game controller"
7. Right click on your audio device
8. Click on "Uninstall device"
9. Click on "Detects Hardware change" (monitor icon)
10. Right click on your audio device
11. Click on "Update driver"
12. Click on the online option

Once finished reboot your system and see if the problem still exists.

bossdweeb say:

The  Intel(R) HD graphics on a G6950 processor is limited to Open GL 2.1.

It is compatible with DX10 but not DX11.

This is a hardware limitation. There is nothing that can be done to fix that.


You may well have owned systems that were older than 5 yrs previously but were never

confronted by hardware limitations such as this, but 5 yrs is, unfortunately,  a long time in computer/dog years. Particularly with graphics.

Apparently your cpu was released in 2010, and I believe it does have an early version of the newer

Intel HD graphics that are integrated into the cpu vs a chip on the motherboard, but is a bit too

early for Open GL 3.0.


"...if I get a third-party graphics card, on what basis should I expect them to support their product BETTER THAN Intel does?"

Intel don't have a long graphics support period. This is at least in part due to the fact that drivers get modified to suit motherboards, which requires that you download the drivers from the

motherboard mfg if you want a guarantee of stability, and the motherboard mfg's and laptop mfg's

don't often offer a driver update after one or maybe two years.

- However, Intel does often update drivers for their graphics for a longer period than the motherboard mfg's, so you can sometimes download more recent drivers for intel graphics directly from

Intel or even via Windows Updates (the 'optional' section) and have no problem.

Mfg's of dedicated graphics cards do tend to support their cards with the new drivers for longer

periods  ie - Nvidia's latest drivers still support graphics cards released in June 2010.

They were still supporting gpu's in their drivers in July 2014 that were released in '05

Note that while the drivers may still support these 'older' cards, there is little or no benefit

in updating drivers for graphics cards more than about three years old, and the 'optimisations'

are directed more at the newer series cards, particularly the latest series (largely because

the 'older' series have been optimised as much as possible).

Which brings us back to the motherboard. You may need a BIOS update for your motherboard

to be able to recognise a current, or previous series dedicated graphics card (with Nvidia this is

the 700 and 900 series cards).

This is assuming your motherboard has a recent enough BIOS update available.

Then we come to the part about the power supply. You also have to verify the power supply

has adequate Watts/amps to run the dedicated graphics card, and power supplies loose

capability to produce the full current and current stability, with age.

This would be less of an issue if you were to add a low end graphics card, as they can often run

off the PCIe slot, with no additional power connection required.

- Short answer....

If you need this software for professional purposes (or want to use the 'newer' version at all),

you may want to get a newer machine if  you want to avoid complications.

And with a new machine you may not need a dedicated graphics card. The newer Intel HD graphics

are far more capable than what you have currently, particularly on the i5 cpu's.

- My  'older' i5 3570 cpu with HD400 from 2012 can use up to Open GL 4.0.
I believe i3's with HD3000 are also capable of up to Open GL 4.0, but haven't verified this.

You do still have to be wary of which newer cpu you use as not all of them have integrated graphics.

EDIT: Did some double checking and found this on the Open GL site (This would be for on going support, and would not affect the Intel HD graphics that is capable of using up to OpenGL  4.0)  -

"Intel does not provide a proper, up-to-date OpenGL implementation for their integrated GPUs. There is nothing that can be done about this. NVIDIA and ATI provide good support for their integrated GPUs."

davem121 say:
Hi MSaied10 it is possible you are logged into a temporary user profile . ..

Before you do anything or move any files around:

Restart (not shut down) your PC 4 times in a row, this will fix this issue a lot of the time . ..

Otherwise:

Please open Windows File Explorer
Navigate to C:\Users
Do you see a user folder that look like yours?
Open that folder and check inside Pictures, Documents . . . etc. and see if your files are there

If your files are there:

Create a new user profile with Admin privileges
Log into the new user profile
Copy all your data from the old profile to the new profile (Documents Library, Pictures Library . . . etc.)

If you still have not found your files, look for a Windows.old folder on your C Drive, expand that to open the users folder contained inside - check there for your files, icons . . .etc.

If you do find your files . . . etc. there, copy them out as soon as possible as Windows will remove the Windows.old folder in time

NOTE VERY IMPORTANT: Do Not copy your files into the Temporary profile you are currently in, when the situation is corrected, your files will be deleted with the Temporary Profile and will not be recoverable !!

trendingnow say:

Cannot open safe mode. Blocked by "Logon use interface CSGINA.DLL failed to load", message. Only option is to restart.

Removed Cisco VPN software only to create this problem.  Cannot get to windows login in either regular boot or safe mode boot. The regedit fix for this issue is straight forward but first I have to get through windows boot.

a. user say:

GINA = Graphical identification and Authentication

It sounds like you know what the problem is...  the Cisco VPN software added a reference in you registry to their GINA DLL (CSGINA.dll)  then you uninstalled their software and the uninstall did not remove the entry in the registry telling XP to load the now nonexistent CSGINA.dll.

In XP, the default GINA DLL is called msgina.dll and assuming XP is installed on your C drive, you will find the msgina.dll file here:

c:\windows\system32

By default, there is no registry entry to load a specific GINA DLL and in that case, XP just loads the msgina.dll by default.  If your system has been afflicted with malicious software, or sometimes installing and uninstalling some third party applications, they may have adjusted your registry forcing it to try to load a different GINA.dll file. 

If for some reason that particular GINA.dll file no longer exists on your system (maybe you uninstalled that software) but the registry is still telling XP to load that GINA.dll file, your system will not be able to load any GINA file, then it complain about it and not boot.  It will of course not even try to load the default msgina.dll file since the registry is telling it to load something else.

Your registry is still telling XP to load the nonexistent CSGINA.dll, but you can fool it temporarily long enough to get your system to boot and then fix it.

You need to boot into the XP Recovery Console or some other bootable media, and copy the c:\windows\system32\msgina.dll to the same name as the GINA file that XP is trying to load.  Something like:

copy c:\windows\system32\msgina.dll   c:\windows\system32\csgina.dll

Then when XP tries to load the csgina.dll (since that is what the registry is telling it to do), it will really be loading the Microsoft MSGINA.dll instead.  Ha!  Foiled again.

Then you system will boot and you can edit the registry here:

HKLM\Software\Microsoft\WindowsNT\CurrentVersion\Winlogon\GinaDLL

...and remove the GinaDLL entry from the Winlogon section since it probably doesn't belong there anymore.  If there is no GinaDLL value in the Winlogon section, XP will just load the MSGINA.dll (this is what you want to happen for this problem).

So, you are going to have to boot on something like a genuine bootable XP installation CD to get into the XP Recovery Console or something else...

Do you have a genuine bootable XP installation CD?  This is not the same as any Recovery CDs that may have come with your system.

If you don't, you can make one (no XP media required). 

You could also make a Hiren's Boot CD (or Hiren's Boot USB) and do the same thing and that CD also has a remote registry editor you could use, but the above is "easier".

It would be a good idea to test your bootable CD on a computer that is working.

You may need to adjust the computer BIOS settings to use the CD ROM drive as the first boot device instead of the hard disk.  These adjustments are made before Windows tries to load.  If you miss it, you will have to reboot the system again.

When you boot on the CD, follow the prompts:

Press any key to boot from CD...

The Windows Setup... will proceed.

Press 'R' to enter the Recovery Console.

Select the installation you want to access (usually  1: C:\WINDOWS)

You may be asked to enter the Administrator password (usually empty).

You should be in the C:\WINDOWS folder.  This is the same as the 

C:\WINDOWS folder you see in explorer.

The Recovery Console allows basic file commands like: copy, rename, replace, delete, cd, chkdsk, fixboot, fixmbr, etc.

For a list of Recovery Console commands, enter help at the prompt or read about the XP Recovery Console here:

http://www.microsoft.com/resources/documentation/windows/xp/all/proddocs/en-us/recovery_console_cmds.mspx?mfr=true

A good idea before starting things is to first verify the integrity of your file system using the chkdsk command.

From the command prompt window run the chkdsk command on the drive where Windows is installed to try to repair any problems on the afflicted drive.

Running chkdsk is fine even if it doesn't find any problems.  It will not hurt anything to run it.

Assuming your boot drive is C, run the following command:

chkdsk C: /r

Let chkdsk finish and correct any problems it might find.  

It may take a long time for chkdsk to complete or it may appear to be 'stuck'.  Be patient.  If the HDD light is still flashing, chkdsk is doing something.  Keep an eye on the percentage amount to be sure it is still making progress.  It may even appear to go backwards sometimes.

You should run chkdsk /r again until it finds no errors to correct.

Remove the CD and type 'exit' to leave the RC and restart the computer.

You do not have to adjust the BIOS again to boot on the HDD since the CD will not be present.
