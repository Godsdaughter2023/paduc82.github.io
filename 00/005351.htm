You could be seeing this error message for several reasons:

1.  You are reading one or more characters of the product key incorrectly.  For example, in the font used for PKs, the letter "B" and the numeral "8" look a lot alike.  Look closely with a magnifying glass if necessary.

2.  You are using a PK that does not match the product.  For example, if you are trying to activate Office using the PK printed on the Certificate of Authenticity (CoA) for Windows that is affixed to the computer, that is not going to work and will give you the error message you see. Windows PKs don't work to activate Office.

3.  PKs can be very product specific.  For example, the PK for Office Home and Student 2010 does not activate Office Home and Business 2010.  And PKs can be "sales channel" specific, too...for example the PK on a Product Key Card edition of Office Home and Business 2010 will not work to activate a full retail license for Office Home and Business 2010.

4.  Since you stated that you purchased Office separate from the computer, you may have a non-genuine product whose PK has been flagged by Microsoft's activation servers.

I need a Bi-Weekly Employee Schedule with Times Worked & Hours of - Microsoft Community

clarencescott54 say:

I need a Bi-Weekly Employee Schedule with Times Worked & Hours of Pay

We have less than 20 employees.  I'm trying to use excel to create a Bi-weekly Employee Schedule which will calculate approximate weekly hours of pay using daily start/finish times and fixed hour lunch break.  
jllatham say:

You might start here:

http://office.microsoft.com/en-us/templates/biweekly-time-sheet-TC006088876.aspx

A web search for "excel biweekly template" turns up several hits, many of which, like the one above, are free for the downloading.

If you wish to make your own, you would probably want 3 columns to deal with the time involved and a couple more to calculate wages earned.  The example below uses column B for the starting time, column C for the end of shift time, and column D to calculate the working hours in between and take into account a 1 hour lunch break:

Format column B as time such as hh:mm:ss or, using Format --> Cells --> Custom and either enter or choose h:mm AM/PM.  Do the same for column C.  You simply enter the time for the start and end of shift into them.

In column D you would enter this formula, in which the -(1/24) accounts for the 1 hour lunch:

=C1 - B1 - (1/24)

And format those cells in column D as Custom and enter (or choose if it is in your list):

[h]:mm:ss

That will show the hours:minutes:seconds between the times in B and C.  You can then multiply that time value times their hourly payr rate like this:  Assumes hourly pay in column E and formatted as currency.  In column F you would have a formula like:

=(D1*24)*E1

and again format it as currency.

The reason that you see the use of 24 in two of the formulas is because of the way Excel actually stores time.  It stores it as a decimal number where the integer portion on the left of a decimal represents the number of days since January 1, 1900, and the decimal portion represents the portion of the current day that has passed.  So for a calculated 8 hours of work (in column D), Excel actually stores the value 0.33, and .33*24 hours = 8 hours. 

I hope this helps you set up your timesheet to work well for you.  Hopefully none of your shifts go through/across midnight - dealing with those is a little trickier; you have to know the full date and time of the start and end of shift to do it properly.
