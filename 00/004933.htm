Does this work for you?

Sub FilterMe()


y = Range("Table4"): k = 1
ReDim z(1 To UBound(y, 1), 1 To UBound(y, 2))
For i = 1 To UBound(y)
   If Not IsEmpty(y(i, 1)) Then
        For j = 1 To UBound(y, 2)
            z(k, j) = y(i, j)
        Next
        k = k + 1
    End If
Next
Range("Table4").ClearContents
For i = 1 To UBound(y, 1)
    For j = 1 To UBound(y, 2)
        Cells(i, j) = z(i, j)
    Next
Next

Yes there is a way (now).

You need the following software on your iPad:

-Onedrive

-Google Slides

-Chromecast

First create your ppt/pptx file as usual using Powerpoint, save it on your Onedrive. (Make sure the slide transition is set to none)

Then, go to the Onedrive, select up your file by holding over it (don't open it), then at the bottom you should see the option to "Open it in other app", here we pick "copy to Slides".

After that, in your iPad open Google Slides App.

In the app you should now see the presentation file you just copied.

However, don't use it yet, as Google cannot "Chromecast" this file directly, it needs to be converted.

Simply tap and open this file, then the right hand side should exist a "setting" option. On the slide bar that appears, there should be a message saying something like "this files is now viewed in compatible mode, convert the file for ......"

Tap on that and convert the file. The app will then create a converted copy of the presentation file in Google Slides.

Now you may go back to the Google Slides App's main screen, where you can find the new, converted file. Open it, and you should see the option to "Chromecast" it in the top panel.

-No transition, evening just fading

-No virtual pointer, which is very important

-No fullscreen view of slide on iPad while presenting

john mcghie say:
Hi George:

Word has five different kinds of styles.  Which kind of style are you working with?  (If you attempt to redefine properties into a style type that does not contain them, a crash is likely).

Are you certain you are distinguishing between a "line" and a "Paragraph"?  To Word they are very different things.  A "line" is a string of characters that can contain only character properties.  A paragraph is a container that can contain up to 1,200 different pieces of formatting information, plus some text.

Let's say you are dealing with a Linked style that has two components, Character and Paragraph.  If you were to redefine the Font, it could be possible to have Arial in the Character part and Calibri in the Paragraph part.  That's quite likely to cause a crash.

For this reason, I suggest that you turn on your paragraph marks, so you can see what you are doing, when you are redefining styles.  You need to be sure when you have, or do not have, the paragraph mark selected.  If you do not have the paragraph mark selected, the paragraph properties of the style are not available, and this could be the cause of your inconsistent results.

You also need to be aware of "inheritance" when working with styles.  In a default Word document, ALL of the styles in the document inherit their font and colour from the Normal style.  If you change the font in Normal, that change will indeed ripple through the entire document.  If you change a different style, only the styles descending from the style you change will be affected.

We normally recommend breaking the chain of inheritance at strategic points in the style table (and some of us have a little macro to do it for us).  For example, nothing inherits from Normal, because Normal is used by Word for placing graphics, so you want to leave that unchanged.  The Body styles all inherit from Body Text.  Heading 1 does not inherit, Heading 2 through 9 each inherit from the style above.  You may choose to have the special purpose styles inheriting or not, depending on your needs and type of document (I usually choose "not").

Knowing all this:
  1. When updating causes nothing at all, chances are the paragraph mark was not selected when you updated, and the font properties do not represent a change.
  2. When Word crashes, two mutually exclusive definitions are being updated into the same style.
  3. When the paragraph and font changes throughout the entire document, that usually means you have updated the Normal style, but every other style in the document is still inheriting from Normal.
This is a complex subject:  feel free to ask for more information if you need it.

I hope this helps
