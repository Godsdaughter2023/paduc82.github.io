steve rindsberg say:

Like the computers they run on, fonts are cranky about spelling.  Each individual font has a (we hope) unique internal name.  The free Futura you installed most likely doesn't have the same internal name as the Futura installed on the PC where it does work; the PPT is calling for a very specifically named font, and unless that's available on the PC where the PPT is playing, it won't work.

As a test, you could try installing a copy of the font file from the original PC on the other one to see if that at least solves the problem.  Then you'd know for sure what the problem is.  You'd then need to purchase the font to keep things legal.

Other possibilities:

The Futura you're using might be embeddable (if it's a TrueType font).  If so, you could opt to embed it in your PPT file, which'd make it available to any other PCs that open the presentation (though not Macs).

The Futura you're using might not even be installed on the PC; sometimes Windows allows you to use fonts that are resident in the current printer; there may or may not be matching TT or other fonts installed on the PC.  If not, you wouldn't actually even be seeing the true font on the original PC, just whatever Windows substituted for it (though when you print the slides, the correct font would appear).

One way to check this is to use PPT's Replace Fonts command.  Look at the icon next to Futura in the upper list box.  If it shows a TT symbol, it's an installed TrueType font.  If it shows a printer, it could be either a Type1/Postscript font or it could be a printer-resident font.  In the latter case, you'd want to look at Control Panel, Fonts to see if the font is actually installed.
