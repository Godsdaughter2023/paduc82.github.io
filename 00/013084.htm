In order to charge wirelessly, NFC must be enabled.  I talked to Nokia support on the phone.  NFC is necessary to connect the phone to the secondary device, whether it be a charging plate or headset, etc.  When one goes to the settings and turns on NFC, only NFC sharing is enabled.  When the shell that has not been released becomes available, it contains an NFC contact point (square) that must touch with another device to activate (the center of the charging plate for example.  The phone does not charge via NFC, but connects wirelessly to the charging plate through it.

jim gordon mvp say:

The compatibility claims of OpenOffice with Microsoft Office have been wildly exaggerated. I agree with Phillip, that you should choose either Microsoft Word or use OpenOffice (preferably LibreOffice if you are going to use any version of OpenOffice).


What Phillip was trying to get at is, in your relationship with your editor, who wins? If your publisher gave you a Word template to use, then your editor must use Word. If you are self-publishing and want your finished document to look the way it does in Word, your editor must use Word. If your editor has the upper hand, then you must use OpenOffice.


The first problem you will run into with OpenOffice users is that OpenOffice does not come with the fonts you get when you install Microsoft Office. Chances are good that your document now has different fonts in it from the ones you used.


The next problem is that OpenOffice is most compatible with Office for Windows '95. Unlike Microsoft, OpenOffice has kept their code base pretty much the same since 1995. If you want Word 2011 to be more compatible with OpenOffice, go to Word's preferences and choose Compatibility. In the pop-up for choosing Recommended Options for : choose Microsoft Word 6/95. This will make your document behave more like OpenOffice/LibreOffice.


What it comes down to is that you and your editor need to agree on which software and fonts to use.
