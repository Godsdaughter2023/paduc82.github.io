nathanb131 say:

My two cents: 

 

If it's just reference info that you don't plan on editing then I just 'save' them into OneNote because the text within the pdf's are searchable (at least I think they are, you'll want to test that).  It basically saves a little bit of trouble when trying to search for your own information and you'd see all your notes referencing it (assuming you use links within one-note) very easily with a simple onenote search.  Since it saves the full file within and you can always extract it back out to save elsewhere and share with others, I find no reason to keep a copy in both places. 

 

If it's something you plan on editing and/or sharing/collaborating with others a lot on then I'd save it outside of one-note.  The reason for this is the inconsistent save options you have when you want to edit them and it'd be more of a hastle to easily share (unless it's the case that you are sharing that same notebook).  I made an excel sheet as a sort of update log for something I was trying to track in my life.  I thought it would be really simple just to embed that file within one-note so I could pop open that file and quickly add an entry from my windows phone, pc, web, whatever.   I'm not quite sure if the web version does this, but the phone version won't let me open that file, add to it, then save it back where it was originally embedded.  I can open and view it from anywhere, which is great, but just know that if it's a 'working' file then you better only plan on being able to 'work' with it from the full desktop version.

 

As far as size of the database goes, I would consider keeping separate notebooks to separate your relatively light normal note-taking and your space-intensive backup/reference library.  That will give you control over which notebooks you have open depending on which device you are accessing them from.

 

Whether you structure your hierarchy all under one master notebook or 8 notebooks should be functionally seemless to you but will give you the ability to not have to open/sync the entire elephant when all you want to do is mess with your grocery list.

The main dictionary contains most common words, but it might not include proper names, technical terms, or acronyms that you use. In addition, some words might be capitalized differently in the main dictionary than what you want in your document. Adding such words or capitalization to a custom dictionary prevents the spelling checker from flagging them as mistakes." [from the Word help files, using "add to dictionary" as the search term]

 

If you're just concerned about one word, you can simply right-click it and select "Add to Dictionary" and the "flagging" (the red squiggle underline) will go away for good.
