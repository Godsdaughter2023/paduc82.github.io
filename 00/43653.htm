First - I decided to test the size of a couple of simple emails.

    Subject:
    Test of Email Size

    Message Body:
    How big is a message with only this much text (in plain text format) and a simple signature?

   

    --

    Shenan

 

AND

 

    Subject:
    Test of Email Size

    Message Body:
    How big is a message with only this much text (in HTML text format) and a simple signature?

   

    --

    Shenan

 

Neither was sent in italics - that was just for emphasis.  The first email was sent in PLAIN TEXT, as noted.  it equaled approximately 8KB in size.  The second email was sent in HTML but with no special formatting/color/etc.  It equaled approximately 10KB in size.  Now - in case someone says "but the wording was different in the two - the size will be different because of that" - in terms of bytes, you are likely correct, albeit by a small enough margin that if I sent the second email in plain text, it reported back as still ~8KB.  I only looked at it at the KB level - so I am just rounding to the KB.  For this purpose - I don't think much more will be necessary.

So, there you go - a simple one liner email will take up between 8KB and 10KB.  No formatting, nothing special - one was just sent in plain text, the other in HTML format (wven though it did not take advantage of it in any definite manner.)  Although, viewing the source of the HTML email (as generated by Office 2010) was quite frightening in the amount of 'stuff' it added.  Anyway...

Let's go on the high-end of that simple test.  10KB.  How many of those emails could you fit into a 20GB space?  Simple math says...  2,097,152 emails before you fill up 20GB of space.  Yeah - that's a lot of emails.  2 million PLUS emails.

Truthfully - I looked through 6800 items I have in one mailbox and more than half of them were below 35KB in size.  That's not an average - but because most of the emails I receive are without attachments/frills - I would bet I could easily get 500,000 emails - some with attachments of varying sizes and still have a little space to spare.  And that assumes (incorrectly) I would never delete any of those emails.

What if you maximized the size of the message you could send/receive with hotmail?  Again - I could not find the definitive answer.  So I tested.

First I created a 10MB empty file.  Administrative command prompt and used this command:
fsutil file createnew 10MB.SZE 10485760
... and got a file by the name 10MB.SZE to use that was exactly 10MB in size.

Since I was able to confirm GMAIL's attachment size limit, "With Gmail, you can send and receive messages up to 25 megabytes (MB) in size." I used it to do the tests.

  • I sent the 10MB attachment to my hotmail account using my gmail account.  It went through, although it took longer than I think it should have.
  • I sent the 10MB attachment from my hotmail account to my gmail account.  It went through, although it took longer than I think it should have.


I then created a 20MB attachment and repeated.  Same with a 25MB attachment.

  • The 20MB attachment sent and received fine in hotmail
  • The 25MB attachment sent and received fine in hotmail


To go any higher, I had to use a different account - one I knew had a higher limit.  I went just 1MB higher (26MB) since 25MB was the highest I had read about in my quick research for Hotmail.  Looks like 25MB is the maximum!  At 26MB I received: "message size exceeds fixed maximum size"...

So - let's run with that - simplify it to mean the maximum message size is actually 25MB (it must be slightly larger, but...) and thus - if you have a 20GB limit on email you can have ~819 emails of that size before you run out of space.  It is highly unlikely all your emails will have 25MB attachments.

If you think all of your emails will have 25MB attachments - you need to find a better way of transferring files than email. ;-)

considerate_guy say:

The purpose of Movie Maker is to take a series of individual clips and make a single movie.  So it isn't completely designed to do what you want.  You have split your long clip into various segments, but if you save that as a movie from Movie Maker, it will save everything in your project area as a single movie.

Movie Maker is also not designed to easily let you copy/paste clips from one project to another.  It only lets you keep a single project open at one time which makes it pretty hard to do a copy/paste of edited clips.

There is a work-around though, and this is how I would do it:

  1. Make multiple copies of your original project file for each of the movies that you want to save.  For example, make a copy called "Birthday Parties" or another called "Athletic Events".
  2. Open up one of the copies (say "Birthday Parties") and delete all clips that are not related to birthday parties.  Don't worry -- you aren't touching your source files in any way.  You are just removing the clips you don't want to use in this specific project.  Once it is pared down to the clips you want, save the project and save the movie.
  3. Repeat the process for other copies.  For example, open up the "Athletic Events" project copy and delete all clips that are not related to athletic events.  Again save the updated project file and also the movie.

Don't you love error messages that are easy to understand? Here's what your error message means:

You downloaded an installer for SAGE. The installer was a single file (probably with an .exe extension.)

The installer is compressed, which makes the file size smaller, which means it will take less time to download. So the first step in the installation process is to uncompress the installer.

Unfortunately in your case, the installer can't be uncompressed ("unpacked.") This is very sad, of course.

The reason the installer ("the executable you are attempting to run") can't be uncompressed ("unpacked") is because it "has been corrupted." But not from leaked Access Hollywood video.

When files travel through the internet, they are broken up into pieces called packets. This makes it possible for the file to reach your computer faster. Once the packets get to your computer, they are re-assembled into the original file. Sometimes it happens that not all the packets show up, or if they do show up, their re-assembly instructions are all messed up, like the time I had to put together my son's play house. When this happens, the original file has been corrupted.

And the solution is ... delete the original installer and download it again.

I read in one of the forums that Windows 10 32bit does not recognize higher than 4gb memory should I change to Windows 64bit OS.

Thanks for any suggestions that will work

ken blake say:

Three points:

1. All 32-bit client versions of Windows (not just XP/Vista/7/8/10) have a  4GB address space (64-bit versions can use much more). That's the theoretical upper limit beyond which you can not go.

2. But you can't use the entire  address space. Even though you have a  4GB address space, most people can only use *around* 3.1GB of RAM. That's because some of that space is used by hardware and is not available to the operating system and applications. The amount you can
use varies, depending on what hardware you have installed, but can range from as little as 2GB to as much as 3.5GB. It's usually around 3.1GB.

Note that the hardware is using the address *space*, not the actual RAM itself. If you have a greater amount of RAM, the rest of the RAM goes unused because there is no address space to map it to.

If you are seeing 3.4 GB, that's good - more than average.

3. Should you change to 64-bit Windows? That mostly depends on what applications you run. If you don't play games or do much in the way of graphics or video editing, you may find that using the full 8GB of RAM will do nothing for you.

You also should make sure that 64-bit Windows drivers exist for all your hardware.
