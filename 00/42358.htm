a. user say:

Hi,

The root cause may not necessarily be within the Windows 10 OS. To put things in perspective, FAT32 is the oldest file system available to Windows. FAT32 has a 4GB max when transferring files, and an 8TB max for partitions. On the other hand, exFAT has no file-size or partition-size limits, thus making it a preferred format for flash drives.

These limits can be one of the possible reasons why the transferring performance of FAT32 is not as expected. FAT32 and exFAT have different fundamental structures, with exFAT being the more optimized one. Furthermore, there are many possible causes ranging from the hardware's age to a limitation of the code's design.

Another reason, why, after converting to FAT32 and then reverting back to exFAT the transfer speed is faster, is because during the conversion process, the intrinsic "language" of the formats are effectively changed. These are one of the many quirks of advanced technology, but suffice to say, we are aware of this and improvement is underway.

If you have any other concerns, feel free to reply.

matt karns say:

I figured out that there was a SSL certificate problem which I got around by porting the incoming and outgoing mail servers.

This may sound like gibberish but it's pretty easy to try.

In WIN10 Mail click on the gear icon in the lower left corner, then click on 'Accounts' in the upper right.

Click on the account you want to fix and then click on 'Change mailbox sync settings'. Click on 'Advanced mailbox settings' and scroll down.

Add port :995 to your incoming mailserver address thus:

pop.myserver.net:995 where myserver is replaced your mailserver name.

In my case it is pop.secureserver.net:995

Do the same with the outgoing server (smtp) only use port :465

smtpout.myserver.net:465

In my case smtpout.secureserver.net:465

Do a reboot and if you problem was a result of the certificate issue it should work.

Good luck,
