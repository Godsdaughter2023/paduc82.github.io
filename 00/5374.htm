There is only 1 Sources.xml file per user account. It stores all sources the user adds to the Master List. If you send a copy of that it will replace the one s/he already may have. It also will include all sources contained in your Master List, regardless of whether s/he needs them or not.

Unless that's what you want to do, I believe the easiest approach would be to send a copy of the document, itself. Have the other user open the document then use the Citation Source Manager to copy the citations from the Current List to his/her Master List.

Keep in mind, though, that the sources used in a document travel with the document. They need be copied to the Master List only if they are to be readily available for use in other documents.

macropod say:

An alternative is to use the following two macros. The first one writes all the xml entries to a Word document, the second one imports those entries into Word's master list.

 

Sub SourcesExport()
Dim oSrc As Source, StrSrc As String
With ActiveDocument
  .Range.Text = vbNullString
  For Each oSrc In Application.Bibliography.Sources
    StrSrc = StrSrc & vbCr & oSrc.XML
  Next
  .Range.InsertAfter StrSrc
  .Paragraphs.First.Range.Delete
End With
End Sub

 

Sub SourcesImport()
Dim i As Long
'In case the source is already present
On Error Resume Next
With ActiveDocument
  For i = 0 To UBound(Split(.Range.Text, vbCr))
    Application.Bibliography.Sources.Add Split(.Range.Text, vbCr)(i)
  Next
End With
End Sub

    What happens when you upgrade a Office365 license under Enterprise - Microsoft Community

    vincentchoy say:

    What happens when you upgrade a Office365 license under Enterprise Agreement (EA) using Step-UP

    Technical Level : Intermediate

    Summary

    As Microsoft rolls out progressively more features in the Office365 family, there will be instances where users using a lower Office365 plan wanting to upgrade their subscription to a higher plan.

    Due to the various licensing methods Office365 can be acquired, each licensing method would entail different license upgrade processes with different expected impact / outcome in the upgrade..

    This article deals specifically with our experience in upgrading a users E1 license to E3 under Enterprise Agreement using what is known as a Step-Up order.

    We found out that Microsoft adds the new upgraded licenses and removes the old licenses at the same time. This does not give the customer sufficient time to re-assign the user licenses to the upgraded licenses, and results in a user service interruption issue until the individual user licenses have been re-assigned to the upgraded license.

     


    Details

    What happens after the order was placed

    After placing the order with the LSP of record, and once the order has been placed by the LSP with Microsoft, we found that the new licenses were added to the customer tenant, whereas the old licenses were removed from the tenant, at the same time. There was no grace period for customer to unassign the old license and assign the new upgraded license.

    This can prove problematic when the customer to be upgraded a large percentage of their deployed licenses.

    For example

    Customer has 500 E1, with 450 E1 deployed (50 are unused).

    Customer decides to upgrade 250 of the deployed E1 licenses to E3

    Upon placing the order, Microsoft will change the customer tenant to 250 E1 and 250 E3, with 450 E1 deployed.

    This presents a problem, as the users are still attached to E1, and a IT process is required to reassign the E1 users to E3 licenses.There is no grace period where the old E1 licenses and the new E3 licenses co-exist.

    This will result in a insufficient license error and 200 of his E1 users will be unable to access the system.

    The customer will then have to quickly change the license assignment of 250 of his users to E3. 

    Until that happens, 200 of his E1 users will not be able to access the system.

    To make the matter worse, we are unable to understand, which specific 200 users will be affected.

    Under the customer tenant, the customer is basically has 450 E1 deployed with 250 licenses only.

    Thinking this may be human error at operations side, we tried to find out what is the expected behavior of a step up. However, the people that we contacted, including local Microsoft sales, pre-sales, Fast Track, Fast Track Architect, Support Ticket, Answers.Microsoft.com yielded no answers. We were unable to determine what was the expected behavior of the Office365 tenant once a step-up order is placed.

    So the next thing to do is the try to re-create the situation.

    We got the LSP to step-up (with their customer's approval), one user from E3 to E5. 

    This resulted in Microsoft provisioning a new E5 user and removing one E3 user from the customer's license subscription.

    Thereby confirming that when a step up order is placed, Microsoft adds and removes the licenses at the same time.

    We also simulated a insufficient license scenario, by going to our CSP portal and removing some licenses from our test account.

    Removing the licenses from a CSP portal results in the immediate reduction of subscribed licenses from the test tenant.

    This cause an insufficient license error in our test tenant

    The user in the test tenant is then unable to access his Office365 workloads.

    We invite other clients and partners who have experienced this situation to share or feedback their experience with us and how you dealt with this particular problem.
