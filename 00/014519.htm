It is not noticeably faster than 8.1 but that is like talking about two thorobreds when it comes to discussing performance.  The best speed results following a clean installation (fewer services running) so if you upgrade consider doing that second.  If the computer has been accumulating "stuff" for a couple of years it will make a difference.

For those visiting this thread in 2015 (or later):

UNOFFICIAL "OFFICE 2013 FOR BUSINESS" MANAGEMENT PROCEDURE:

I ran into the issues discussed in this thread with Microsoft Office 2013 for Business as well; I support multiple companies as a tech consultant & had to create a manual workflow for this.  It is not a professional move by Microsoft at all, but I'd imagine that it is by design: they want you to buy a monthly 365 subscription, so they're providing the minimum level of effort required to manage anything other than a bulk open-license KMS/MAK mass-activation service.  The bottom line is that you have to manage the license key list manually yourself, sorted by order of activation - otherwise you are screwed.  If you're already past that point, then you're in for a research project, which is annoying, but only has to be done once.  This is what you need to know:

1. For the 2013 Home & Business version, Microsoft requires an Office license key to be activated against an MS Live account.  See #2 & #3 below.

2. Although that email address is required for activation, that account can be de-coupled within an Office program after installation (it will default over to say the local domain account you're logged into), so you don't have to worry about having to manage your Microsoft activation "admin" account email address on every single computer (ex. Word: File > Account > "Sign out").  The sign-out is a one-time requirement & enables the computer to be "standalone".

3. The MS Live account can be based on any email address, so you can do *** Email address is removed for privacy *** to have an in-house "admin account for managing the license keys.

4. You can only activate 30 products (2013 Office family products, that is) per Live account (Office, Project, etc.).  You can, of course, have multiple accounts since accounts are simply tied to a unique email address - so just make more email addresses.

5. I create a generic email address at each company I support (ex. *** Email address is removed for privacy ***).  If they have more than 30 accounts, I created a second account (ex. *** Email address is removed for privacy ***).  Technically they should be going with a MAK or KMS solution for a larger quantity of installations,, but I support many businesses with less than 300 users who don't have the infrastructure or buying workflow to setup app servers or who don't want to buy in bulk.  A lot of smaller companies buy computers on an as-needed basis & have no interest in buying licenses in bulk, especially since the cost for an open license is nearly twice as much as a single one-off license from a reseller like Amazon.

6. The keys are numbered based on the date & time when you registered them at office.com/setup - but Microsoft does not track the order for you, and the order listed in your account has no relevance to the actual order.  The information is hidden in a Microsoft database & it not visible in your account.  Thus, it is YOUR JOB to track them manually.  I use a simple Excel spreadsheet (or if the company doesn't have much in the way of email, just setup a Gmail account to pair the Live account to, then use a Google Docs spreadsheet).  I have the following column headers:

1) Live account email address (if they're planning on having more than 30 keys, since the email address must be different for the second group of 30 keys)

2) Activation number (1 through 30)

3) The license number itself

4) A computer identified (asset ID, username, workstation name, anything to know which computer it was installed on)

If the company has more than 30 keys, I create a new sheet in the Excel workbook to track the additional licenses.  Regarding licenses, you have 3 choices:

1. OEM (comes with the computer, say a Dell computer pre-loaded with Digital Delivery or a keycard)

2. Physical keycard (the ones in the small box)

3. Digital key delivery

Amazon has digital key delivery, which lets you buy a key instantly.  It's nice to have instant delivery, but that Amazon account has to be tied to an email address & managed by somebody, so it may be easier to have physical product keycards.  Price is typically the same as the download version.

7. If you pick the wrong number when activating Office, you can reset it by going into Programs & Features, right-clicking on the Office program, clicking Change, and selecting Online Repair.  This resets it to asking you to sign into to activate again (the old command-line re-arm commands don't work like previous versions of Office).  However, this takes a LONG TIME.  It's MUCH faster to simply un-install & reinstall Office.  Speaking of that, you can download an image of the installer from your Live account.  Find a Microsoft Office listing under My Account, then go to Install from a disc > I want to burn a disc > Download (green button).  It will download an IMG file, which you can either burn to a disc with something like ImgBurn, or mount with something like Pismo File Mount (install, then right-click and select "Quick Mount" to temporarily convert it to a read-only folder).

I drag out the installation folder & just use that, either off the company's file server, or off my personal USB stick.  This is the fastest way to install & re-install; otherwise you have to stream the installation from the Internet, which can take a super long time.  If you need to reinstall or transfer the license to new hardware, but get an error that the key was already activated, you can call in using the number on the Microsoft activation

8. I activate all keys by registering them on the Live account first, rather than when doing setup on the computer.  That way, on the local computer, I just install Office for Business from the folder off my USB stick, sign in with the email account, select the number I want to use, and it's ready to go.  To do that, first login to your Live account:

https://login.live.com/

Once you are logged in, type in your key code here:

https://officesetup.getmicrosoftkey.com/

Then write it down in your personal license tracking spreadsheet - which email address you used (the Live account), which computer it's going on, which number it is, and what the license number is.  This lets you easily reference it later.

9. If you need to transfer the license (or have changed hardware & it freaks out), install it & select the number from the list, then call them to reactivate it.  When it asks you how many computers you've installed it on, say "one", then it walk you through activating it on the new computer or replacement hardware.

All of this could be solved if Microsoft would simply provide you with a numbered list based the date you registered the key in your Live account.  It's also ridiculous that they limit each account to 30 products, and further ridiculous that they require an email address simply to register a standard piece of software.  Fortunately, these workarounds DO work.  You can de-couple your Live account after Office is installed & activated.  You can create a second email account for the next batch of 30 licenses.  You can track the licenses & associated information manually in a spreadsheet.  It's just extremely annoying.

I did not realize all of this the first time I did it.  I had already installed 19 keys, and needed to reinstall Office on a computer that had the original hard drive fail.  Boy, what a nightmare that was!  To add more salt to the wound, you can't just pull a key out using a key finder - the best you can do is find the last 5 digits or so of the key, because of the way Microsoft requires online activation now - it doesn't actually store the installed key on the computer in an accessible manner (not that it would matter - you'd still have to know what number it corresponds to in your Live account anyway, so you HAVE to keep track of it in a personal spreadsheet regardless).

TL;DR: Microsoft makes you register an email for Live account to activate Office & store your license keys in.  Each account can hold 30 license keys, and you can add more accounts by creating a different email address (doesn't have to be a Microsoft email).  However - and this is the core problem identified by this thread - they don't track the order, so you have to do that yourself - #1, #2, #3, #4, etc. - because when you install Office, it asks you for which number it is, not what license key it is (which is horribly stupid).  This is based on the time & date activated, so #1 would be the one you registered on Live last week, #2 would be the one you registered 3 days ago, and #3 would be the one you registered today.  They do not make that information visible, so it's easy to create a big mess if you don't track it.

joan pin say:

Hi,

The Microsoft Upload Center gives the status of the files you're uploading to a server via one location. When you upload a file to a web server, it will first save that file locally to the Office Document Cache before it starts the upload, which means that you can save changes and immediately continue working even when you are offline or have a bad network connection. To fix this issue, we suggest that you follow these steps:

  1. Open Task Manager.
  2. Find the 2016 upload center in the list. Right-click on the Upload Center and click for the details.
  3. You’ll see all the processes that are using the Upload center right now. Make sure you end them all.
  4. Go to %userprofile%\AppData\Local\Microsoft\Office\15.0\.
  5. You'll find a directory named OfficeFileName. Rename it to OLD_OfficeFileName. Do not delete it yet.
  6. Now restart your computer. The Upload Center will recreate this directory.

Let us know if the steps provided above works for you.

keratl say:

I can only give you my opinion/viewpoint as one person who has used Excel and Tableau (no experience with Kipfolio);

TL;DR: Excel is cheaper, and can do most of what most companies need/want. Tableau is much more expensive, there will be a learning curve, but interactive data visualization can change how your leaders absorb and understand the data.

Excel: Reports can be packaged (subsets of data put in mirror copies of a workbook) if the data is confidential and has to be shared with specific target audiences (but after that you don't have control over where it goes). Easy to produce static reports (PDF, etc.). Easy to manipulate data while looking at it with formulas. VBA is the biggest thing that keeps me from switching to any other spreadsheet software- automating the loading of data in the back end, automating the production of reports, etc. Only a one-time cost for the (desktop) license. Slow when handling/analyzing very large data sets. I haven't tried to do this in the latest versions, but "drill-through" capability was historically weak/difficult to set up, at least graphically (click on a slice of a pie chart and have a time series chart update to only show that clicked subset from the pie chart; click on the time series chart to get a table of line item details, etc.). I've seen some examples where Excel scorecards have been integrated into SharePoint (including examples from MS eating their own dog food), but I don't have experience in that area so I can't speak to the additional capabilities that brings to your scorecarding efforts.

Tableau: license is much more expensive per seat (and I don't recall if that is one-time, or annual fee...); many companies also buy a Tableau server license so reports can be made broadly available to internal audiences without having to get an individual license for each person who needs to access the dashboards (which also requires someone to admin the server, but is the only good way to distribute non-public reports within a company; your report developers will still have to have individual licenses as well). It is very good at connecting to multiple large data sources and different types of sources for data, and has more of a drag & drop interface for building out data visualizations. You can write formulas and do bulk data manipulation, but there is a learning curve. It has lots of cool features, but when learning I found myself getting stuck in a couple of places, where it would have taken me seconds to do the same thing in Excel. Good data design is extra important so that your inputs allow you to build the types of data visualizations that are important to you. Once a dashboard is set up, the ability to drill through your data is a game changer. You can invest more time and mimic that in Excel, I've never achieved the same level of user-friendliness that is inherent in software designed from the ground up for this purpose. Prior to Tableau, I'd see monthly operating reviews where a leader would ask a subordinate to explain some variation in the data, and the answer was "I'll find out and get back to you" and they'd go dig through P&L reports, OT reports, etc. With a properly designed dashboard, they can drill down on the spot to get a lot more information about what is going on right there in the meeting. For example, when reviewing a general uptick in expenses among subordinates, a leader clicks around a Tableau dashboard and changes their view from cost center to account- and finds out that T&E is going up across the board. Then they can have a specific conversation around T&E instead of having everyone trying to chase down local patterns in each person's departmental data... that totally changes the conversation in the meetings.

Tableau does have a free version, I believe the limitation is that you can only save your work to their public server, so you won't want to practice with confidential or proprietary data. There are plenty of public data sets (government web sites, etc.) that you can connect to and play with.

I think the two products serve different niches, and would recommend you look at some of the sample visualizations on the Tableau site or your other media site of choice, to get ideas of how it can be used.
