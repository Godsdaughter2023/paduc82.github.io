rohn007 say:

I have been a Database Administrator at a large company. I have never heard or read that logging in to a DB is "discouraged". 

Yes, there are techniques you can use to often get enough information from a device to uniquely identify a computer, and user, assuming they are the only person using it. The general techique is called "fingerprinting". I have never seen "fingerprinting" suggested as the primary method of logging in.  On the contrary, I have seen suggestions of using fingerprinting to limit the specific devices a person can log in (using a unique identifier like userid and password) from. One place you may have encountered fingerprinting is on your credit card.  If you travel and use your credit card, some credit card companies may block the transaction, or request additional confirmation if they do not have any other purchases to confirm the travel.  ie if they don't see an airline ticket or appropriate gasoline purchases to get you to the destination.  That is a type of fingerprinting based on (geo)location.  In the past, logins could be limited by device "MAC" ID, a unique identifier associated with your network card.  But that technique has become too easy to spoof ("hack") to be considered reliable any more.

You are right, you have to require users to be uniquely identified. That means they have to uniquely identify themselves to the database. If they are using PC's that can be shared, then you will have to require them to log in to the database. Using their MSAccount and password should be good enough. 

The generally accepted minimum is a unique userid and secret password. You say they already

As well each individual's access rights should be limited to their own records only (based on their logon id). It is up to you, actually HR, to decide if people should be allowed to look at each others information. But I don't think it is reasonable for the average user to be allowed to change other people's information, causing the problem you described.

While it may be reasonable to use a shared ID to access a specific PC, from a security point of view it is not reasonable to grant them access to this type of timekeeping DB.  Shared ID's eliminate responsibility for actions.

talking moose say:
You install fonts for your Mac not for Word specifically. Word will use most types of fonts you have installed on your Mac.

If your Mac has multiple users then place them in the /Library/Fonts folder. This is the top-level Library folder on your hard drive. You can place your fonts into a sub-folder to keep them separate from the others and they will still work.

You can also place fonts into your own Fonts folder in your home folder. Click on a blank area of your desktop so that you're in the Finder and press the Option key as you select Go menu > Library. That said, it should be noted that the "simple" change that you made is significant.  In general, it can indeed make the calculation of an IRR difficult, ambiguous, or impossible.

Generally, the IRR algorithm works best when there is only one sign change in the cash flow sequence; that is, a positive CF followed by all negative CFs, or a negative CF followed by all positive CFs.  See the wiki IRR webpage.

[1] [EDIT] You might wonder why I don't use the following simpler NPV formula:
=SUMPRODUCT(B1:B24/(1+E1)^((A1:A24-A1)/365))
So did I. :-)  The answer is:  I developed the habit of pulling CF0 out of the NPV "function" because Excel NPV requires that. :-(  In theory, the simpler formula should return the same result because the first term is divided by (1+E1)^0, which is exactly 1.  But in practice, I discovered there is an infinitesimal difference with these particular examples.  I could explain the difference; it has to do with the internal 80-bit binary floating-point implementation.  But it does not matter.  The point is:  when NPV is calculated by either SUMPRODUCT formula, the result is zero or infinitesimally close to zero.
