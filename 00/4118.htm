Hi Chris,

We're sorry you had deliverability issues.  Please let us know if you're still having problems.  I'll need you to PM one of us with your IP address at a minimum.

To clarify and give steps for anyone who is in Chris's shoes and having issues sending mail to a Microsoft customer:

  1. Always start by reading the full bounce message.  Under most circumstances, there will be instructions on what to do and/or a link to documentation on how to fix the problem.  This is also critical to making sure that it isn't a problem somewhere else.
  2. For now, it is important to note that Outlook.com (formerly Hotmail.com) and Office 365 are two different entities with different delisting processes and different support teams.  Both sets of email servers will have domain names ending in *.outlook.com, but don't let that mislead you.  We're working to streamline this, but this is another reason why you must read each bounce message for instructions.
  3. It is possible to have problems sending to one and not the other, or have problems sending to both.  If it is the latter case, you may have to go through two different sets of procedures.  You may fix one problem and still need to take further action to fix the other. Again, this is something we want to streamline, but we are not there yet.

Do keep in mind that it is your responsibility to maintain a good reputation and ensuring that your server(s) are not sending spam.  Establishing and maintaining good sending reputation is an ongoing process, not a one-time fix. It takes time to improve IP reputation and careful vigilance in monitoring your email systems to maintain it.  Here are some additional security measures for you to consider which may help you detect and address compromises to your mail systems or abnormal sending behavior more quickly and therefore reduce the likelihood of your mail traffic being negatively:

 • Review outbound mail logs – It’s recommended that you have network and email admins review server logs for anomalies and confirm all of the email sending is intentional. Be aware that not all compromises immediately result in a high volume of mail. Some attacks may be more subtle, and may only periodically send mail from infected mail servers.  Therefore, careful log review may be necessary to detect and address potential compromise issues. As a best practice, set up monitoring on outbound sending volume to ensure that sudden, unexpected peaks are quickly identified and reviewed.

 • Security review of sending servers – In addition, it’s recommended that you ensure all sending servers are up-to-date with the latest security patches, updates, etc. and that security and anti-virus scans are regularly executed against all sending servers.  Do not share IP addresses with client workstations which may be compromised or with bulk/grey mail systems.

 • Implement Sending Limits - It’s a recommended best practice to enforce sending limits on your domain accounts; this is a simple method to prevent users who may be compromised from sending spam emails and helps better protect the reputation of your IPs.

Outlook doesn´t support shared categories in meaning of one list of categories that can be accessed by everyone for every folder. The master category list (mcl) is stored in each mailbox. The steps described by Devlin let you use the categories of another person only if you look at a folder of that person´s mailbox.
