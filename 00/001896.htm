jay freedman say:
When you just scan directly to a PDF, the content of the PDF file is just a picture of each page. The "Open a PDF" feature in Word 2013 relies on there being actual text in the PDF file; anything that's just a picture in the PDF becomes just a picture in the Word document.

To get text from a scanned image, you have to run it through Optical Character Recognition (OCR) software. That software "reads" the picture and creates text. Some OCR programs are better, making fewer mistakes, than others.

You can try using OneNote to do the recognition: Copy/paste a scanned picture into OneNote, right-click it, and choose Copy Text from Picture. Then paste from the clipboard into Word (or just into another page in OneNote). You'll have to proofread the result and fix any errors.

Third-party OCR programs may be better, but at extra cost.

Yes and No.

The prerequisite is there: If you right click on a page in OneNote and select "copy link to page" you get the URL that you need in the clipboard. Like in Windows there are in fact two URLs. One starts with https:// and would open the page in OneNote Online in the browser. The other one starts with onenote:https://.... and would open the page in OneNote. If you paste only that second URL (extract it with a text editor first) in the URL field of a browser it starts the OneNote app and opens the correct page.

Unfortunately its not as easy to create a shortcut containing any URL format on the Mac desktop as it is in Windows. Dragging it from Safari (the usual way to create Weblink shortcuts on the desktop) just creates a shortcut which opens OneNote in the browser instead of the app.

tell application "Microsoft OneNote" to open location "<your long OneNote page URL>" and export it as "Program". This "application" does what I want: Open the OneNote app and the correct page. 

There must be more elegant ways to do this however.
