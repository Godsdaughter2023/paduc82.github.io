John,

Windows 10 will include a feature for "rolling back" to the previous OS [Windows 7, or Windows 8/8.1 on your other computer].  I suggest that you wait until after 29th July to search the forum for advice on this as by then there will be posts from users who have tried it.

However, you would be taking a very great risk by upgrading without taking the appropriate precautions first so that you have alternative means of restoring your computers to their current state.  You need to do each these tasks on your Windows 7 computer and also on your Windows 8 computer

  1. Make a Windows 7 "System recovery disk" / Windows 8 "Recovery drive" unless you have the installation disk
  2. Test that the computer can boot from its Windows 7 "System recovery disk" / Windows 8 "Recovery drive" or installation disk [they can be tested without then doing anything risky - if you can't find a safe procedure for this elsewhere in the forum then say so & I'll give you appropriate links]
  3. If your PC maker included a "factory restore" partition then you need to find out how you can transfer that to a USB stick as the Windows 10 upgrade will probably* destroy it otherwise.  The facilities provided differ between PC makers so you need to check in your computer manuals or on the PC makers' support sites or in their user forums. [* installing the Win10 Preview version does so]
  4. Make a "System image" of your computer's main drive - the drive that Windows is installed on
  5. Make a full file backup of all your own files, all downloaded software installation files, all Product Keys needed to activate your applications, all notes of login information / passwords etc

There is guidance in Windows Help for each of these tasks and there are also helpful threads in these forums about them.  As you can guess, the preparations will take you longer than installing Windows 10.  All these preparations are things you might be doing routinely anyway [even without expecting to be doing any upgrades]; I hope so.

I suggest that you look through descriptions of what Windows 10 does.  I have been experimenting with it for several months and there is nothing to get excited about.  If there is something that you cannot do currently or that Windows 10 does better then consider upgrading.  If not, you can stick with what you have.  Windows 7 will continue to be supported with security-related updates as it is now and this will continue until 14th January 2020.  Windows 8 will continue to be supported with both functional & security-related updates until 9th January 2018 and will then be supported with security-related updates until 10th January 2023. 

Just by way of example & nothing more, I have decided to upgrade to Windows 10.  I have two computers and there are things I cannot do on the older one as it has Windows XP - because my needs/wants have grown as a result of getting used to what I can do with Windows 7 [and because it is now too risky to connect WinXP to the internet].  In addition to wanting to be able to do more things with the older computer, my Win10 experiments on it [using the Preview version] have persuaded me that there are lots of things I would find less confusing if I had the same OS on both computers.  That's why I will pay to put a new OS onto the WinXP computer and why I will upgrade my less aged Win7 computer to Win10 as well. 

If you do not find any compelling reason to upgrade then consider if there might be a compelling reason in the future.  If you are ever going to upgrade the two computers then do so by 29th July 2016 so it does not cost you anything.  If there is no compelling reason to upgrade then don't bother.

All the best,
