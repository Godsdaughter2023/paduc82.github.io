So, at the present state of the technology, we don't have the ability to make use of any storage space saved by removing any factory installed programs, though we can fool ourselves by removing their shortcuts, to make things a little cleaner.

rohn007 say:

I'll try to break down Johns point, even though it is an extreme example.

The development of a computer program, like any of the Office applications, is a long term project.  After it is developed initially, adding new features is a complex process. In big companies like MS dealing with complex applications like Word / Excel / Windows, the process goes something like this:

#1 Idea

Some genius submits a "new idea" in the "suggestion box

#2 collect

The suggestion box is reviewed, ideas are aggregated and listed. Many people may suggest variations of the same idea, so those suggestions are pooled into one common suggestion

#3 Evaluate

The current pool of suggestions is submitted for initial review by 'experts'.  Experts in programming, in user interaction with program and marketing (who in theory know what users want / need)

#4 Priorize Suggestion

A committee of those experts meet and come with a way of ranking the suggestions, either simple numeric priority, or categories like "MUST" have, maybe have, forgetabout it.

#5 Analyze

The developer analysts look at the priorized list of suggestions and comes up with estimates of cost (man hours and other resources) and time duration to make the suggestion real

#6 Decide development priority

Back to committee. This time to look at the results of analysis and decide which ones provide the best "cost"benefit" results.  This committee may decide that a "high priority" feature (like a Mac version of right to left language support is simply too expensive for the return it would give. This pushes the feature down or out of the priority list.

#7 Fit the development priorities against the current budget available

Now that the committee has decided which projects are doable, they have to apply the costs from the analysis against the money that has been budgeted by the corporate executive for new development.  Even though the committee may decide that there are 10 "high priority" projects, the executive may have only budgeted enough money for 6 of them to be worked on.

#8 Assign development priorities

Every company has a limited number of programmers available, and limited computer resources, so developer managers have to come up with specific schedules when  each project will start and run.

#9 Do the actual programming

#10 Testing

Often testing takes longer than the time it takes to code the program changes.

So while it is trivially easy to make a suggestion, the time and effort to make it a reality can be surprisingly large. In a big company like MS, suggestions have to make their way through MANY committees. And everyone knows how efficient committees are.
