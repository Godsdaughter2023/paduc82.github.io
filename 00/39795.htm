a. user say:

With most web browsers, every time you start the browser, it will try to become your default web browser, even if you have already specified a default browser. In order to become the default, a web browser needs Administrators permissions, which is why you are being prompted.

So here's what to do:

If you have already chosen a default web browser other than Chrome, and you want Chrome to stop trying to become the default, open Chrome settings (click on the three vertical dots in the top right corner of the browser window) and go to Settings -- Default browser. That's where you tell Chrome to stop trying to become your default web browser.

In any case, don't lower your User Account Control settings - that increases your computer's exposure to security threats while doing nothing to stop Chrome from trying to become the default web browser.

As I understand it the multiple sound channels from digital outputs only exist in the form of an encoded, compressed audio data stream. The alternative default is 2 channel PCM (uncompressed) audio. It requires a decoder to extract the individual sound channel from other, non-encoded, sources (ie. microphones, game sound tracks etc.) would need to be encoded into a compatible multi-channel digital format first before it could be passed through to the speaker system. Without that extra encoding function 2 channel PCM output is the only option.


I'm inferring from the various descriptions  given in this thread that typical audio card utilities seem to have an inbuilt function to send properly encoded individual speaker test signals through the spdif or optical outputs but they do not usually include the necessary hardware or software to convert other audio sources into multi-channel digital format.

The sound test may well be encoded for 5.1 (or whatever number) digital speakers but practically no other sound source that the computer normally handles is encoded that way.  Since the speakers themselves will decode a multichannel audio track into discrete channels it is pointless to have the sound card default to an output setting it cannot actually use. If your audio card were capable of encoding ordinary audio sources into a multi-channel digital format that the speaker system can recognise that would be different.


WRT players etc., you've got a lot of interacting variables there.


Someone may correct me on this but AFAIK you would only want to enable the multichannel audio decoding  functions in your assorted software if you were outputting the audio to an analog speaker system. Headphones for example or 5.1 analog speakers. For digital 5.1 speakers you'd want to just pass through the (DTS/Dolby) audio track unaltered since the speaker system itself extracts the individual audio channels from the source data.


Some of the player and system codec or filter settings can conflict with each other. MPC and VLC have their own internal codecs but they can also interact with Windows system codecs and audio filters.   I'm only using analog 5.1 speakers but even with mine the Realtec "Speaker Fill" feature conflicts with the Logitec 5.1 speakers "Matrix" mode (both simulate multi channel audio from stereo sources). When those features are activated some of the channels from actual multi-channel audio sources can get wiped out completely as well.


Codecs or filters might come into play, say for example if an encoded audio track was being passed through AC3 Filter and it uncompressed and separated the multi-channel track into discrete audio channels or down-mixed them to stereo before the audio gets sent on to the sound card.


If all of your player software, audio codecs and filters are configured correctly to pass through the DTS or Dolby multichannel audio then the encoded audio track should get sent intact to the speakers from the audio card output. Your speakers should automatically decode and output 5.1 audio whenever the audio source reaching them is encoded in an appropriate 5.1 digital format. All other multi-channel audio formats would either be down mixed to stereo or the back channels /center/sub might just be dropped entirely. 


As far as that Advance settings section you mention, that section refers to how the system selects the default audio quality when multiple sound sources are played simultaneously, ie.  playing Windows system sounds while listening to Mp3 files, or listening to music tracks or voice chat while gaming. IMO you'd pick the setting which either best reflects the quality of the type of audio source you listen to most often or else pick the setting which reflects the highest quality audio source that you actually listen to at all. You cannot improve low quality audio sources (ie. low bitrate MP3s) with this setting but you can degrade high quality audio.


 In that setting on mine there is no specification or limitation for the number of speakers/channels the setting applies to.  When I click the test button it sends a test sound to each of my 5.1 speakers in turn. Speculating here, if the speaker test doesn't do the same on yours it suggests that this particular function isn't using an appropriately encoded multichannel digital audio track for its channel test signals so your digital speakers only see the default 2 channel PCM output from the audio card.
