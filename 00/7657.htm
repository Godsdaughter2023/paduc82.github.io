PS....  Simply "multiplying by 24" does not "remove any odd rounding issues" unless you explicitly round the multiplication at least to an integer.

For the same reason, you might also find that =A1-B1=0 is FALSE, even though =A1=B1 is TRUE.  These differences are often invisible, due to arbitrary limitations in Excel formatting.

I confirmed that simply adding TIME(1,0,0) to previous date/time almost never (!) equals the binary representation of the apparent date/time.

I entered 3/25/2016 13:00 into A2.  Then the formulas are:

A3: =A2+TIME(1,0,0)

B2: =--TEXT(A2,"m/d/yyyy h:m")

C2: =A2=B2

D2: =A2-B2=0

E2: =MATCH(A2,B2,0)

F2: =SUM(A2,-B2)

Of course, there should be no difference between C3 and D3.  The difference is due to "games" that Excel plays in an attempt to hide these sorts of infinitesimal arithmetic differences, which arise because most decimal fractions (including the representation of Excel time) cannot be represented exactly in 64-bit binary floating-point.

For the same reason, I use SUM(A2,-B2) in column F instead of simply A2-B2 in order to avoid Excel's "games".

In my example, MATCH fails all the time.  It might failed less often in your case because (wild guess) both of your date/time values are calculated; sometime the binary representations of the calculations match and sometimes they do not, by coincidence.
