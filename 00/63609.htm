a. user say:

If you want your WiFi to operate on the 5 GHz band you need a both a router and a WiFi adapter that operate on the 5 GHz band. Keep in mind that while the 5 GHz band has more bandwidth, the signal doesn't travel as far as a 2.4 GHz signal.

Something to know: The speed of your internet downloads and uploads is determined by your Internet Service Provider (ISP) not by the frequency band. If your ISP provides you with X Mbps of download speed, nothing that you can do is going to increase that to more than X Mbps.

The 5 GHz band has greater bandwidth than the 2.4 GHz band. That means the 5 GHz band can transmit more data per second, but it can't move that data any faster. It feels faster because more data is moving at the same time. Unless you're moving piles of data - for example, you're transferring huge files to someone else on your network, or you're streaming TV or video - you're not likely to notice much difference between the 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz bands, if any.

If the internet feels slow to you that could happen for several reasons:

1- Ads on web pages suck up bandwidth which causes the page to load slower. An ad blocker helps a lot.

2- You could be unknowingly infected with malware which is sending out or receiving data over your internet connection without giving you so much as a clue as to what's going on. Even if you're not infected, if you aren't careful about WiFi security, your neighbors or passers-by could be using your WiFi signal.

3- The farther your computer is from your router, the signal weaker from the router will get. The signal can get deflected if it has to go through walls or if it encounters interference from metal objects like file cabinets.

4- If there are other devices or other users operating on the same channel as you are, that could cause interference which reduces the available bandwidth of the channel. Trying a different channel might help.
