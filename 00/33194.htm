a. user say:

The HDD in my laptop has failed and I want to replace it

Does 'failed' mean physically failed, as in no longer spins, or parts broken off? If that's the case, you'll have to reinstall everything from scratch on a new drive. I hope you have a recent backup of your personal files.

But if 'failed' means 'doesn't work right' you should think carefully before you replace your hard drive. Because if the drive is not physically damaged, there's probably nothing wrong with it, and it doesn't need to replaced. The software on the drive may be totally messed up, but if that's the real problem, and you aren't inclined to fix software problems, you can always reinstall everything from scratch on the same hard drive.

Don't think that you need to buy a new hard drive just because Windows or your other software isn't working properly. Software problems don't cause damage to hardware.

If your drive is not obviously physically damaged, you owe it to yourself to go to the manufacturer's website and use their diagnostic software on your drive. That will tell you if the drive needs to be replaced.

a. user say:

Thank you all for your help.  Yes i believe  the hdd has failed.   Ran hdd scan, results say over half the drive has bad sectors.   I was able to back all my personal data up.  

It's normal for a mechanical hard drive to have a few bad sectors ... but when it gets to be a lot of bad sectors that's a sign that the drive has to be replaced.

There's nothing that you (or your software) can do to cause bad sectors. That's a problem with the surface of the disk. If you're still under warranty you can get it replaced, if you want.

Moving to a new drive is very easy. You don't have to reinstall anything. Most retail hard drives come with all the instructions, parts and software you will need. The software makes an image backup of your hard drive. You might already know that an image backup is a complete backup of your entire hard drive from beginning to end.

Here's the procedure you'll follow in a nutshell:

1- Make an image backup of your existing hard drive. The software will ignore (i.e., not copy) the bad sectors. You can use the software from the manufacturer or your own image backup program if you've got one. Store the image on an external hard drive.

2- Swap the drives.

3- Restore the backup image to your new drive. Ta da!

That's all there is to it. You end up with exactly the same computer as you had before, only with a new hard drive. Still activated.

The manufacturer of your new hard drive probably has the instructions on their website, if you want to see in advance what you'll be doing. Might even have videos, too.

You do realize that any files that were written to bad sectors are possibly unrecoverable. Hopefully that won't affect you much (or at all.)
