john mcghie say:
Hi Deb:

There are three ways to do this.  Find/Replace is the simplest (and least error-prone, in the hands of unskilled users)

The name you are looking for, by the way, is "Variable Text" or in FrameMaker terms, "Conditional Text".

A Document Property is the mechanism built for this purpose.  If you go into File>Properties>Summary, you will find that "Company" is one of the pre-existing properties.  You can set that to the company name.  You can then use that value in the text with Insert>Field>Document Information>DocProperty>"Company".  Put it in once, copy it, then paste it wherever else you need it.  You can even put it in with a Search/replace :-)

To change it, type a new value in File>Properties>Summary, then select the document text and hit F9 to update fields.  The value will appear wherever you placed the field and will update whenever you update the fields.

If you go to File>Properties>Custom, you can create fields of your own, as many as you like.

  Problems:  1)  It will only update if you type a new value in the Company field, then select all and F9 to update fields.
  2)  Most users are so clueless about fields they do not even recognise them and type over them, which leads to some embarrassing breakages.

Sadly, Mac Office has not bothered to document this very powerful feature: the best I can find right now is this from Word 2003: http://msdn.microsoft.com/en-us/library/office/aa537154(v=office.11).aspx  Word 2011 is exactly the same.

Another mechanism you can use is a cross-reference.  Simple, but extremely easy for unskilled users to damage.  You simply type the company name the first time and bookmark it.  Then in the text wherever you want it to appear you set a cross-reference to the text of the bookmark.  Again, you need to remember to update it each time the value changes.  Again, you have to be very careful not to damage the bookmark when updating the name.  Some people use a one-cell table to contain the name.  You then bookmark the table.  This is a little less easily broken.

For extra credit, you can use a Document Variable.  This operates exactly the same as a Document Property, Insert>Field>All>DocVariable...  However its benefit is that the value can be changed ONLY by a visual basic macro.  It is described here http://support.microsoft.com/kb/306281

While the variable is much more protected from being changed wrongly by users (they would have to know about it, know where to find it, and know how to create a macro to change it) it shares the document property's disadvantage that it's too easy for an unskilled user to delete the DocVariable field while editing the document.

I always used to use Document Properties and Document Variables for this.  But that was back in the days when professional long documents were only ever edited by professional technical writers who were aware of properties, fields, variables, and macros.

Recently, I have stopped bothering, because it is now impossible to get normal users to be interested enough to learn about these mechanisms, and thus inevitable that they will damage them in use.  So these days I use Search/Replace.  It is very fast (for me...) and the result is impervious to user damage.

Let me know if you need more detail.

Hope this helps.

bob jones aka: cybertaz say:

Re: "... i do not have any space left in my mac at all"

If you're referring to free disk space on your primary HD, that's the most likely issue... All software, including the OS, requires available disk space in order to function. The more powerful the software, the more free space it requires, & the Office apps are toward the higher end of the spectrum. The [admittedly arbitrary] 'rule of thumb' is that your HD should have at least 20% free space at all times.

I'd suggest that you first remove any programs & user files you don't really need. Use a utility* such as OnyX to clear caches, delete old email & remove all the stuff you've likely accumulated in the Downloads folders.  Also, anything you want to keep but don't have to access routinely can be moved to some other storage media... CD, DVD, flash drive, external HD, etc. [* I'd be careful if using utility software that offers to 'clean up your Mac', though.]

Alternatively, you may be able to replace your current HD with one of greater capacity or add a 2nd internal HD (depending on your Mac) or an external HD.

I see that your installation of Office 2011 isn't fully updated (14.3.9 is the current version) but I doubt that is the cause based on your version of OS X. It would still be a good idea to update as soon as you're able.

jim gordon mvp say:

The error message you are getting is displayed when you double-click a Word document in Finder, and your Mac has a version of Office older than 2008. Yes, you have Office 2008 installed, but there is an old version lurking around your Mac somewhere. 

This web page describes how to completely remove Office v.X and Office 2004. These are the two editions of Office that were around Mac prior to 2008. Following the instructions will free up at least a little space on your jam-packed hard drive.

Another place to look for old stuff is your Documents > Microsoft User Data folder. If you use Entourage 2008, you should have a folder for Office 2008 Identities. If you have folders for identities of older versions of Office you can delete those unless you are saving them as backups. If you are worried about deleting something important, burn the old version identities to a CD and then delete them. 

You might do a search on your drive for old system folders. They take up a lot of room, and chances are you don’t need them since your current system has probably been running OK for years.

And don’t forget to empty the trash!  So many people put things into the trash bin and let them accumulate but never empty the trash. Things in the trash are still taking up space until you empty the trash.

Once you have made some room, download the Office 2008 updates and install them. That should clear up the error messages. If it doesn’t, please reply and we can offer additional suggestions.
