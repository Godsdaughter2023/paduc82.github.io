andre da costa say:
Windows 10 Digital License
When you upgraded from a previous version of Windows or receive a new computer preinstalled with Windows 10, what happened is the hardware (your PC) will get a digital entitlement, where a unique signature of the computer will be stored on Microsoft Activation Servers. The Windows 7 or Windows 8 genuine license you were previously running will be exchanged for a diagnostics key.

Anytime you need to reinstall Windows 10 on that machine, just proceed to reinstall Windows 10. It will automatically reactivate. You will prompted to enter a product key a couple times through the installation, click I don't have a key and Do this later.

There is no need to know or get a new key, but if you have issues with Digital Licensing or the Activation Troubleshooter, you can utilize an existing Windows 7 or Windows 8/8.1 product key you already own to reactivate if necessary. or use the reset function in Windows 10.

Windows 8.0, Windows 8.1 or Windows 10 came preinstalled on my computer, how do I find the product key?

Press Windows key + X
Click Windows PowerShell (Admin)
Enter the following command:
wmic path SoftwareLicensingService get OA3xOriginalProductKey
Hit Enter

The product key will be revealed, copy the product key then enter it
Click Start > Settings > Update & security > Activation > Change the product key then enter the new product key then click Next to Activate

If you are unable to activate Windows 10 after all the above and you are certain you have the correct edition installed, then please attempt phone activation:

Activate Your Windows 10 License via Microsoft Chat Support
https://partner.support.services.microsoft.com/...

Windows 7 came preinstalled on my computer, how do I find the product key?
The product key is usually located on the COA sticker attached to the bottom of your laptop or in the battery compartment. In some rare cases it can also be found in the memory compartment. For tower systems, look at the top or the side of the system unit.

anannya podder say:

Hi,

 

 

Unfortunately, it is not possible to resume Windows backup after you shut down the computer.

In Windows 7, you find the Stop Backup button by clicking the View Details button in the Backup and Restore window while a backup is taking place. In the window that appears, you see a progress bar for the current backup as well as the Stop Backup button.

  • There’s nothing wrong with stopping a backup; it doesn’t destroy any data that’s already on the backup hard drive. Stopping the backup does, however, prevent the backup program from making copies of all the files in need of backing up.
  • You can’t resume a stopped backup, but you can start a new backup when you are done using the computer.

techfreak_ say:

The Stereo mode you are referring to would be A2DP (Advanced Audio Distribution Profile).  That Bluetooth profile is playback only providing no recording capability.  In order to use Bluetooth as a recording and playback device you have to use a monaural (HSP) Headset Profile or HFP (Hands Free Profile).   Neither of these Bluetooth profiles support stereo.  The limitation would be the current Bluetooth protocol revision itself and the corresponding Bluetooth hardware used, not Skype or the Microsoft OS.

The only solution at this time would be to use a wireless technology that is not based on Bluetooth or hardware that utilizes a proprietary dongle that might provide you with a proprietary implementation of Bluetooth, other  wireless technology, or better implementation of an existing HSP/HFP profile than your Bluetooth stack software or hardware chipset.  You can continue to monitor the revisions of Bluetooth (5+) to see if newer hardware (client and devices) are released that might support a stereo headset profile.

Hello Gwen,

Thank you for visiting Microsoft Community.

As per the issue description, I understand that you’re experiencing dropping signal strength issue on your fresh installed Windows 8.1 computer after downgrading from Windows 10 Technical Preview.You have already performed a number of troubleshooting steps but none of them helped you enough to get this issue resolved.  

I can understand the inconvenience that you have to go through due to this dropping signal issue, however, since now we’re here let’s work together to get this issue resolved with our combined efforts.

I would appreciate if you can provide us the following information to help us understand the issue better.

  1. Does the connection drops when you’re away from the computer for some time and screen saver comes up or it drops randomly?

  2. Are you using a dual band router?

  3. Have you checked the MTU (Maximum Transmission Unit) settings of your router?

There can be a number of reasons for dropping/limited wireless connection in Windows, some common issues that can cause these problems are:

  • Corrupted or incompatible drivers.

  • Missing updates.

  • Network connection settings.

  • Hardware or software problems.

  • Inappropriate distance from the router or obstruction.

  • Interference from other devices.

Let’s try these methods below and check if that solves the problem.

Before trying any further troubleshooting I would suggest you to perform Power Cycle on your computer to release the static and check if that works.

To perform Power Cycle:

Simply shut down all your hardware – ie your PC, your modem, your router, your laptop, etc. – anything on the network. Then power them back on in the following order: modem then router and then PC and see if that fixes the issue. If not, proceed to more potential solutions below.

Method 1: Adjust your wireless router settings

Lower the MTU (Maximum Transmission Unit) from 1500 to 1492 or less (usually found on your router’s main/ basic setup page).

Adjust the following advanced wireless settings:

  • Lower the beacon interval from 100 to 50
  • Lower the fragmentation threshold from 2346 to 2306
  • Lower the RTS threshold from 2347 to 2304
  • That should solve your wireless connectivity problems and should prevent your wireless connection from dropping.

Method 2: Check if there is any auto power management settings are enabled

Let’s check if your computer is managing your wireless card’s power. It may be instructed to shut off your wireless connectivity after a certain amount of idle time goes by. If you have system specific utilities, make sure that the wireless power management isn’t set to shut off the card if your computer is idle for a certain period of time.  Second you’ll want to check the settings on your wireless card via your device manager.

  1. Press Windows+R key to open run box.

  2. Type devmgmt.msc in run box and press Enter to open Device Manager.

  3. Find your wireless card under “Network adapters” and double-click it.

•Verify that there aren’t any auto power management settings enabled that might be shutting your card down prematurely.

Method 3: Update your computer firmware and wireless card drivers

If nothing suggested above works, I would suggest you update the firmware of your wireless router, and your wireless card drivers.  This should solve the problem most of the time. With any firmware or driver update, please make sure you are retrieving the update directly from the manufacturer’s website.

You may also refer the article below for help in updating drivers.

Downlaod and install Drivers

http://windows.microsoft.com/en-us/windows-8/all-drivers
