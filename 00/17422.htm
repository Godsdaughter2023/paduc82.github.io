Active Directory Account Lockout/ActiveSync Redesign - Microsoft Community

the sage say:

Active Directory Account Lockout/ActiveSync Redesign

 I I recently had the unfortunate experience of persistently numerous instances of Account Lock Outs in active directory. My initial thought was that we were experiencing a DoS attack. Further investigations revealed my a number of my numerous client ends may have old devices having expired passwords and still trying to connect to exchange server channels (ActiveSync). Some meditation on ActiveSync Design required.

Research on the internet led me to quite a number of people with similar experiences in the past; Very likely others are encountering this or will encounter it sometime in the future.

Recommendations from Microsoft support team was to copiously increase the Account Lockout Threshold, 50 seem to work fine for us so far.

My concerns:: with an average of 200 lockouts every 10minutes from 5,000 active accounts, when our active accounts double withing the next two years i definitely will need to keep pushing the lockout threshold up at the expense of security.

There seem to me to be "Design Limitations" in both Active Directory and ActiveSync-Exchange 2013 interrection that allows the possibility of both malicious and unintended denial of service via account lockout for exchange mail clients.

In my opinion, AD should be able to separate lockouts based of frequency of login attempts (Brute force attack) from legitimate user typing a wrong password more than established threshholds, and this two incidences should have different Event ID in the logs.

Could you guys please share your thoughts with me on the above.

alan wu msft say:

Hi The Sage,

I quite agree with your description about the AD (active Directory) account lockout. But as an local system admin can take more glance at the local account's activity. That is you mentioned here "the local account's password has expired". If the admin can make a notice to the end user that their accounts' passwords might be expired in the future (something like that). The end user can avoid the failed logon. As a result, the ratio of the account being locked out will be less. 

On another hand, your understanding is right that the higher the lockout value, the higher the risk that a hacker may be able to break into a system.

For more information, you can refer to https://technet.microsoft.com/en-us/library/dd277400.aspx.
