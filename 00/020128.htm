d.i.v. say:

Hello, Doug &c. 

In my case I was specifically interested in the time component of the "date" field, as actually the last-save date & time were already known from the timestamp on the original files (visible in File Explorer etc.). 

However, there is not necessarily a correspondence between save date and either printing or

The date (and time) saved in the DATE field that is visible in WordPad (or Notepad, etc.) is that corresponding to the last time the field was updated prior to saving.  Updating the DATE field may be triggered by opening the document, and printing (or print-previewing) the document. 

In my case the most likely scenario is that

1. the file was created; 

2. the file was iteratively edited and saved;  closed and re-opened; 

3. the file was print-previewed and printed (triggering an update of the DATE field, visible in WordPad, Notepad, etc.); 

4. the file was saved (which will be the time and date shown by the SAVEDATE field (corresponding to the timestamp visible in File Explorer etc.). 

In this and every other scenario, examination of the DATE data in WordPad/NotePad appears to answer Jeff Bowman's question:  "what was their value(s) when the document was last saved?" 

Of course, in other scenarios, other things may be of interest.  For example:

* If the file is saved, then closed, reopened sometime later, printed (with automatically updated DATE field), and finally closed without saving, then it will be impossible to recover the

* If the file is printed (with automatically updated DATE field), and then edited, then saved (i.e. overwritten), and finally closed, then it will be impossible to recover the the practical issue raised by user maverik.) 

* In an unlikely (but confirmed) scenario, after the original editing, printing and saving within Word, after closing the document, the DOC file may be modified in a hex-editor (such as HxD), then saved again;  the etc.) is updated to that of the modification made in the hex-editor, but this is not reflected in the SAVEDATE when the modified DOC file is reopened in Word.  Presumably this deviation of the 'updated' timestamp and the nominal SAVEDATE would occur whenever the file's timestamp is changed outside of Word (not confirmed), which would be more likely to occur (e.g. in some backup operations) than only for modification in a hex-editor. [I presume that the SAVEDATE is stored by Word in binary format along with other document properties such as 'Total editing time'.] 

NOTE:  When dealing with legacy documents, it is important to remember the superior importance often associated with the
As stated by user kmurdock:  "The create date or the last save date are not necessarily the date the memo or letter or whatever was finalized and sent out by your clients. Unless your clients maintain logs (or have photocopies), they themselves don't have an accurate record, either."

d.i.v. say:

Hello, Stefan & Doug &c.

I agree SAVEDATE is better than CREATEDATE.

I also agree that the file's timestamp (as seen in File Explorer etc.) is not always reliable*.

In my long post above, there were a few key points I was trying to make & illustrate:

  1. The relevant print-date is probably the most relevant date of all, for legacy documents.
  2. It cannot be guaranteed that the relevant print-date of a document can be recovered from any electronic file, let alone a legacy file of uncertain heritage/provenance.
  3. Nevertheless, the date saved in plain text within the file (visible in Notepad etc.) might be a better surrogate for a print-date than any of the other options suggested up to now.
    Furthermore, this seems to be the best (and only) way of answering Jeff's original question in a literal sense regarding:      "value(s) when the document was last saved".  

Anyway, as I looked into this matter, I discovered there is yet another field — which I have never used — called PRINTDATE.

I wondered whether this was a new feature.

It is present in Office 2013 (as I have installed), and confirmed back to Office 2003 (per official MS documentation**), and indeed seems to go back at least to Office 2000† and Word 97†† (per archived forum posts). 

Therefore, it is reasonable to expect that it would have been available in 1999 for the documents I've been looking at.  

Unfortunately....

it seems that the wdPropertyTimeLastPrinted property was not set/saved in any of my old Word documents (originating from 1999).  Therefore in the document's Advanced Properties the "Printed" entries under "Statistics" all seem to be blank, and so the PRINTDATE field is useless for these files:  PRINTDATE displays something like "0000-00-00 00:00:00" (depending on your date/time-string formatting). 

I am not sure whether PRINTDATE was flawed in old releases of Word, or whether those files were never saved after printing‡. 

One reason for 'failure' to save after printing is that if the document is saved after all manual editing is completed, then the updating of the PRINTDATE field that occurs upon printing may be (probably) insufficient to trigger a warning to the user to save the document, and indeed may even make it impossible to re-save the file without manually making further (trivial) edits‡‡.
