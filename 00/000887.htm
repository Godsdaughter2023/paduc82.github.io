bernie deitrick say:

Let's say that you have this formula to create your unique keys, in cell A2:

=B2&C2

copied down to match your data.

Then, in another column, use

=COUNTIF(A:A,A2)

copied down to match your data set.  That COUNTIF will return the count of the cells in column A that match the value in A2 - it will always be at least 1, since A2 is in column A, but if it returns 2 (or more!) there are duplicated keys in column A.

bernie deitrick say:

Before data de-duplication, in the old section, 1 will indicate that the record has been removed and is no longer in the new data. In the new section, 1 will indicate that the record was added and never appeared in the old section.  2 in either section indicates just that the record appears in both, but does not indicate if it was changed or not.

After de-duping, 2 in either section will indicate a changed record - but 1 in the old can indicate either an unchanged record or one that was  deleted from the new records.  A 1 in the new section will indicate a new record.

Some thoughts that may help: Scanners produce picture files as a starting place. Thus while a picture of an automobile can be quite engaging, trying to drive it anywhere will leave you frustrated. Likewise, a picture of text is just that, a picture.

Word does try to interpret that picture and turn it into text. This is called optical character recognition (OCR). There are other programs, probably one that came with your scanner, that are better at this than Word. None are perfect. If you are using scanned text, you need to proof-read.

I am a lawyer. I use scanned text often. When I do, I scan into a pdf form that is searchable. This means that an OCR process has been applied to the text. Then I copy the scanned text into a Word document as plain text (without formatting) and apply formatting using Styles in Word.
