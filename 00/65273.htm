Pretty much.  Here's the gist of it.  That site has a tool that lets you make your own "Windows installer drive" (it can be a USB 'thumb drive stick' or a CD).  I prefer USB but that's because most of my computers don't have disc drives.  You just need a USB stick that can hold at least 8 GB of data (they cost about $8 dollars at Walmart if you don't have one).

  1. Run that tool and it puts all the installer files on the USB drive.
  2. Plug the drive into the troubled computer and boot up from the USB stick, choosing to install when prompted.
  3. When it asks what drive to install Windows onto, I like to delete all the listed partitions until nothing is left except for "Unallocated space", then click "Next".  Windows will create all the drive partition setup automatically and install (takes about 15 minutes).
  4. Then you'll get the setup part where it asks you to choose your time zone and wifi settings, and you're done (well, except for reinstalling your favorite other things like browsers or whatever).

This makes the computer basically "factory fresh" and just like you took it out of the box, ready to use and should be in perfect working order.

I use a lot of experimental software so I tend to go through these steps more than a regular person would, and I keep all my important files in OneDrive so that they are already available as soon as I login to the restored computer (plus it syncs those files to my other computers).

anacunga say:

Just to clarify: the question here IS NOT what caused the BSOD, but what the programmers were smoking when designing such a kind of unreadable BSOD and how that unreadably small font can be avoided and the info be displayed in a readable font size!

 

The Problem to discuss here starts to exist from that point in time when the BSOD-managing software (that one that collects the information AFTER the real crash) has found out what the crash could have caused (long long after the crash itself), and it ends at that point in time when that information is written to the screen in an unreadably small font; i.e. the issue in the system is at the point when the information with the reason is "written" onto the BSOD and that BSOD is given to the screen to be displayed. In that process of "having got the information with the appropriate word, writing it onto a BSOD template and sending that template to the graphicscard for displaying it onto the screen". That's the range of the issue here – and not the cause of the BSOD itself!

 

It is an issue by itself to create a design for a BSOD that is not readable on EVERY monitor display possible (as long as anything can be given to the monitor to be displayd and as long as the display is still displaying it)! A BSOD of "old fashioned" Windows comes in 80x24 characters and would even be readable on a line printer. Btw: the BSOD is given out in 640x480 VGA size!

 

What did the programmers think when they had designed a BSOD that delivers such an unreadable font? And how can that be avoided so that the info gets readable? That's the question here – and not what was causing the BSOD; that's another issue (to come to later on)!

 

First solution to find here is how the info line can be displayed in a readable form – and only if that is done (and not before), a second step would be looking after the source of the BSOD. As it is reproduceable here for the moment, it can be used for testing several options you might offer how to improve the design of the BSOD and specially that too small info line!

a. user say:

is 4GB ram enough for windows 10 after update. my dell laptop became quite slow

Most users have more RAM than they they need. Their computers are not slow because they lack RAM, but rather because they have too much software running in the background, or their computers are not properly setup, or they have been attacked by malware.

The truth is: The amount of RAM you need is determined by the software you install.

Windows 10 by itself will work well with as little as 2 GB of RAM, but you're surely going to install additional software, which will require additional RAM. You have to take that software into account when computing how much RAM you should have.

Your calculation should be based on the amount recommended by the software developers, which you can easily find on the developers' websites:

amount recommended for Windows 10

amount recommended for program #1

amount recommended for program #2

etc.

--------------------------------------------

total recommended amount of RAM

--------------------------------------------

You should round up to the nearest gigabyte, then add a little more to take into account software you may install in the future. RAM is always installed in multiples of 2, i.e., 2 GB, 4 GB, 6, 8, etc.

This is how you determine how much RAM your computer should have. Anything else is a guess or a 'rule of thumb.'

Regardless of any calculation, the most RAM you can use with 32-bit Windows is 4 GB.
