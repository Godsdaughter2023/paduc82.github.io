Hi,

Bluetooth is a high-speed, short-range wireless communication protocol. It's fast enough to transmit high-quality audio signals to audio devices, like headsets or speakers that are within 30 feet. Turn on the Bluetooth enabled device, or other wireless device, and then make it discoverable. Methods for making it discoverable can vary widely depending on the device. You won't see a Bluetooth settings page if your PC doesn't support Bluetooth.

 

To learn how to make a Bluetooth enabled device discoverable, check the info or the manual that came with your device or go to the manufacturer’s website.

 

Refer to these articles to know more.

Why isn't Windows finding my Bluetooth or other wireless device?: http://windows.microsoft.com/en-in/windows-8/why-isnt-windows-finding-device

 

Follow these steps to setup a Bluetooth device with the computer:

-          Put your audio device in "discovery" mode. Most devices are put in discovery mode by holding their main control button until their indicator light turns a solid blue, but this process may differ depending on the type of Bluetooth audio device you are using. Check the device's owner's manual for specific instructions.

-          Open "Devices and Printers" from your computer's "Start" menu and click the "Add a device" button. Your computer then searches for and lists any devices available to connect to; your Bluetooth audio device should be shown.

Article for reference.

Add a device or PC to a network: http://windows.microsoft.com/en-in/windows-8/add-device-or-pc-to-network

-          Select your Bluetooth audio device and click "Next." Enter the device's security passkey if prompted. Most devices use "0000," but yours may differ. Again, check the owner's manual if this code doesn't work for your device.

-          Open "Control Panel" from the "Start" menu and click "Hardware and Sound," then the "Manage audio devices" link in the "Sound" section. You should see your Bluetooth audio device listed under the "Playback" tab.

-          Select the Bluetooth audio device and click the "Set Default" button near the bottom of the window. Your Bluetooth device should now play any sounds produced by your computer.

samir gunic say:

Printer installations have historically been problematic, due to a number of different factors, such as device driver design, vendor specific control commands, varying degree of adaptation to industry standard printer and scanner protocols, and so on. But let's not dwell on the past.

How do you plan on using the printer? As a network printer I presume, connecting to it wirelessly? In that case, you should not really be connecting to it with the USB cable during installation. If you are following the installation wizard provided by HP, it may tell you to connect the printer to a USB port, in which case you should ignore that. If you have already done so, the printer may now be configured in Windows or the HP device software to be used as a USB connected device, in which case it will be difficult to connect to it over the network. The configuration needs to be undone and redone.

It's unfortunate but these installation wizards that are provided by the vendors are often poorly made, and they are often outdated, being made several years before the actual printer it comes with. So they may not be adapted to the latest version of the operating system, nor to the printer model it comes with, or the use cases such as wireless networked printer. In general, whenever possible, I would advise you to always use the native facilities for adding devices such as printers to the operating system you are using.
