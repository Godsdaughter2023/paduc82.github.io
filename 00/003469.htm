scottgem say:

Instead of looking up against the whole eval table, try creating a query:

SELECT [dx code 1] FROM [eval table] WHERE [p id] = Forms!mainformname!Patient;

Then do your lookup against that query.

On a separate note I see 2 potential problems with your database. First, the use of spaces in object names can come back to haunt you. It is recommended that you use camel notation (EvalTable) or underscores (eval_table) instead. Second, I'm bothered by the use of a field name dx code 1, this indicates you might have multiple fields in your table named dx code 2, etc. This is not proper design. You should have a child table:

tblVisitDiagnosis

VisitDiagnosisID (PK Autonumber)

VisitID (FK)

PatientID (FK)

DiagnosisID (FK)

scottgem say:

PK means Primary Key and FK means Foreign Key. This is how you link relational tables. Before yyou do anything more on this database you should read up on Normalization. Being self taught is not the problem, The resources you used to teach yourself may be the problem. I would suggest you go through Crystal's Access tutorial before you do anything else. 

I'm not sure what you mean by "my method to create tables". The primary way tables are created is going into Table Design mode, adding the fields and adjusting the properties for each field as necessary. If you are not creating tables this way, then you are not using Access the way it was designed to be used. 

If you already have a table that lists the Diagnostic codes, then just add the new ones to that table. Add a field (Yes/No) to the table that indicates if the code is obsolete or not. Then you can modify your Combobox's RowSource to not allow the user to select Obsolete codes. 

If you need more help with your project, please feel free to ask.

a. user say:

Hello there, AS#18! Thanks for posting your query in Microsoft Community.

About your concern, if your phone stops working or responding like it used to, restart it to get back on track.

To restart, press and hold the Power button until a Slide down to power off message appears on-screen, then swipe down. (It usually takes about three seconds for the message to appear.) To turn your phone back on, press the Power button.

If that doesn't work, press and hold the Volume down and Power buttons at the same time until you feel a vibration (about 10 to 15 seconds), then release the buttons. Your phone will restart automatically.

Please be reminded that:

Your phone may vibrate as soon as you press the Volume down and Power buttons because it's changing your ringtone to vibrate. Wait to release the buttons until you feel the second vibration 10 to 15 seconds later.

If you don't let go of the buttons immediately after the vibration at 10 to 15 seconds, your phone may show a black startup screen instead of restarting normally. If that happens, try pressing the button combo again.

Not all phones restart this way, so check with your phone's manufacturer to find out how to force a restart.


If your phone has a replaceable battery (it's often under your phone's back panel), you can also restart your phone by removing and reinserting the battery, and then pressing the Power button to turn on your phone. The date and time may be off after you restart this way, but you can fix that by swiping over to the All apps list from Start, then selecting Settings > Time & language > Date & time.

Feel free to reach us again for further assistance.
