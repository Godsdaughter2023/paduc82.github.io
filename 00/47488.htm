aileen alf say:

Hi Jay,

If you know your PC has Bluetooth hardware but you don’t see it in Device Manager, or you don’t see any unknown devices under Other devices in Device Manager, there might be a problem with a related device. We suggest that you follow the steps below to get Bluetooth working:

  1. Right-click on the Start menu and select Device Manager.
  2. In Device Manager, select View > Show hidden devices.
  3. In Device Manager, select View > Devices by connection.
  4. Find your Bluetooth hardware.

    As shown in the following image, your Bluetooth hardware might be under a related parent device which indicates there’s a problem. (You may need to expand devices that are collapsed and shown with an arrow next to them.)

    1. Parent device
    2. Bluetooth device
  5. To try to troubleshoot and fix the problem, do one or more of the following:
    • Update the driver. To do this, right-click the parent device, then select Update driver > Search automatically for updated driver software.
    • Uninstall the driver and then restart your PC. To uninstall the device, press and hold (or right-click) the parent device, then select Uninstall device. To restart your PC, select the Start  button > Power  > Restart.

      Before your PC restarts, you might temporarily lose the connection to other devices. For example, you might temporarily lose the connection to other USB devices.

      After your PC restarts, Windows will automatically install the default driver.

    • Download an updated driver from the PC manufacturer’s website, and then install it manually. To install a driver you’ve downloaded from the manufacturer’s website, right-click (or press and hold) the parent device, then select Update driver > Browse my computer for driver software and then find the driver you downloaded on your PC.

Let us know if you need further assistance.

Hi,

The Media Creator can NOT create an install disk on USB if the drive has GPT (Win 10 favored native) partition... It needs a MBR (Master boot record - old style more generally supported (read: good for general support of most computers) partition.

This is a "bug" that Microsoft seems to miss...

Most USB-pendrives have MBR initialy but Win10 prefers to set it to GPT unless you force MBR when reformatting... (but after being GPT:ed you will not be asked again... it stays GPT)

Use Gparted on a bootable DVD  or any other diskpartioning software that allows you to set the USB-pendrive to have a MBR boot record.

You can also use diskmgmt.msc under Win 10 to do this... Instructions can be found on internet... search for "change format disk gpt to mbr"

slance310 say:

Hi,

I didn't look at any of your links, but I am almost 100% certain that the Fast Startup feature in Windows 10 caused your problem... I doubt that many of your missing files are savable, as they probably were not "saved" in the first place...

I switch OS's on SSD's as you describe also, and have encountered this... Even knowing of the issue, I sometimes forget to turn off Fast Startup and end up with issues like chkdsk, damaged missing files, and etc...

The reason being is that when Fast Startup is turned on, your machine does a "Hybrid Shutdown" to generate a faster bootup... Unfortunately, part of the process is to not "Close" HDD\SSD's normally...

So... Unhooking your Windows 10 SSD after powering off while Fast Startup is in use, or disconnecting the computer from the wall plug, removing the battery, or similar, has about the same effect as pulling the plug on a running computer..

Fast Startup only works when you do a SHUTDOWN and then Boot.
It doesn't effect a RESTART.

To turn it off or on...
Go to Control panel...Power Options, and select Choose What the Power Buttons Do on the left.
Then select Change Settings That Are Currently Unavailable near the top center of screen...
Lower down on the window, uncheck Fast Startup.

Hi,

I don't know what a lot of that means unfortunately...

It mentions only one partition must be bootable...

It may be that one of your OS's has added a boot partition by setting it active...

If more than one drive is attached when Windows is installed, it sometimes creates an extra boot file on it, but does not make the location "Active"...

That might just be a note...

The drive with the bad sectors... my guess is that fast Startup caused this...

My understanding of your log is that the MBR, and possibly some files, got corrupted...

I had a drive that appeared to loose everything... a 8TB USB drive...

It was being viewed as a "RAW" drive...

I was able to save it running some repair functions, but I'm not sure which one actually did the trick..

I have had other instances where all four of my internal drives needed to run CHKDSK before Windows would boot... This pretty much always happened when switching from my Windows 10 SSD to the Windows 7 SSD...

I always try to make sure to do a Restart when using any type of bootable repair or imaging media... A Shutdown and restart causes problems...

If I am switching drives after a shutdown, I try to make sure to turn off Fast Startup, which I sometimes forget...

Fast Startup has about the same effect on a drive as unhooking it while it is running, without properly ejecting, or using Safe Removal... The damage caused can range from none, to loss of everything, depending upon the drives status at that exact moment...
