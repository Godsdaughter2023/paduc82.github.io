I think Exchange basics will solve most of your issues. If you need to archive any data you can create a smart folder using dates. Assign those messages a category you create the under File > Export select to export by category and delete after exporting.
Exchange Basics

The Inbox syncs more frequently than any other and should be kept as clean as possible. (this is the number one cause of problems with sync). Do NOT make subfolders under the Inbox.

Any folder with more than a few thousand messages is going to take some time to fully come down when you first sync your account. Due to the design of Outlook's sync engine (and how exchange works), you will see the 512 newest messages in a folder at the initial start of sync then the rest of the folder's contents will be back filled before you see any mail that arrives after this point. This is why it will look like you are not getting new mail when a folder is still going through initial sync. This is also further exacerbated by an Exchange issue where it gets unnecessary change events before the newer mail arrives. If you carefully watch the progress during the sync of a folder with greater than a couple thousand messages, you will see "Updating local..." a lot before the newer mail starts arriving (newer than the initial 512 that come down).

Outlook checks the server for updates every minute, any folder that has updates will subsequently be synced. Since Outlook has a limit on how many folders can be synced at a time, there can be a queue of folders waiting. The Inbox does get high priority so it will generally sync before other folders that also need to sync.



No! "On My Computer" folders are for storing POP messages and only exist on your computer and not on the server (exception if you select to "leave on server"  for POP)

Outlook Mac can only import a .pst file. That's all.

If your data source is a Word document, you can sometimes retain some of the formatting from the data source by
 a. using Alt-F9 to display the merge field codes (e.g. so you see { MERGEFIELD myfield }
 b. for each field where you want to retain formatting, change that to

or possibly even just
{ myfield }

If you see "\*Mergeformat" in the field code, delete it.

That is a prescription for a disaster.  You might not want to read this but this approach to retaining data is fundamentally wrong.  In the coming weeks, months, or years, you will have a disaster on your hands.

In addition, it is extremely unfriendly when it comes to doing any kind of data analysis or data mining.  How have patient X scores evolved over the last 2 years?  How does patient X's performance compare with other patients who started with a similar score?  Are there any patterns among our patents -- by age? by gender? by marital status? by lifestyle? by job type?

How do you think large organizations store data?  Take a bank like Citi, or Bank of America, or Chase.  Do you think they have 1 Excel file per customer per account per day?

I would reconsider the entire approach.  Use a system that is consistent with a relational database.  Either use a real database system (MySQL is free) or use Access or even a single Excel workbook with separate worksheets (one for patient information, one for visit, one for each set of clinical tests).  For an example see

RDBMS in Excel

lord kilborn say:

To answer your question about types of data, let's keep it simple:  Diastolic BP is an indicator of heart health.  90+ is considered hypertension and that is a negative indicator of health.

My equation for Diastolic BP is as follows (here is the train of information and formulas):

Data entry for all factors we consider is on one sheet currently titled "ALL DATA ENTRY".  Data for D-BP is in B9.

I have a separate sheet entitled "Calculations- Do Not Change" that is usually protected from changes.  The answer from All Data Entry is imported into E7 via "='ALL DATA ENTRY'!B9" (outer quotes not included in the formula of course).

The calculation I use to "grade" this is "=((100-((E7-70)*1.5))*0.01)" in F7.  The calculation to cap the score at 110% maximum is "=IF(COUNT($F7),MIN($F7,1.1),"")" in G7.  The calculation to move that result over to the "results" column and its conditionally formatted cell is "=G7" in K7.

The calculation used to transfer this grade over to a presentably-formatted results page for delivery to the patient is on a sheet titled "PRINT THIS PAGE"; the calculation is simply "='Calculations - Do Not Change'!K7" in B13.

I hope this is not too confusing.  In creation of this tool, several things were mixed around and matched, and then we needed it in a presentable form for the patient to clearly understand what they were looking at.

There is another sheet involved that calculates a patient's perception of their physical and mental well-being entitled "SF-36 Calculations", but it has no bearing on the Diastolic BP calculation.
