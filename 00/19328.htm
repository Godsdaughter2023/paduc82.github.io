rob koch say:

Excellent questions Michael and it shows you have a deeper understanding than not only most consumer users of Windows, but even most of those also typically consumer volunteers or even the now mostly contract workers with the Microsoft tag under their avatar within these forums.

That's not surprising, since unlike in the past where helpers here were often working in the computing profession in business, today most are less highly trained consumer users themselves, though still dedicated to helping others understand what most consumers need to know to have basic protection and safety using a home PC.

The primary difference in my case isn't current status, since my own use now is as a consumer, but rather my past history working as a computer tech, network manager/administrator and eventually a security professional in education, business and as a consultant for businesses of all types including national, international and government customers.  The key difference though is that I was one of only two people that have been involved in helping with the Microsoft security products in these and previous versions of these Microsoft forums since the early beta versions back in 2005-6.  We also physically met with the developers of the first Windows [Live] OneCare Antivirus commercial suite product during MVP Summits, as well as regularly via teleconference or other methods with the Technical Product Management lead for OneCare, it's immediate descendent Microsoft Security Essentials, as well as most of the parallel business products from that time period.

Sorry for the excessive history, but it may aid in understanding why though all of the information I'm about to tell you is documented, few are actually aware or even know what they're looking at when they view it.  Sorry, but this will get long, since it would take more time to be brief than I wish to spend.

Starting with your key queries, the Windows Defender Advanced Threat Protection (ATP), as you mentioned is really designed for business and requires not only a server, but also certain network management products in order to facilitate its operation.  The reason for this is ATP isn't so much a product itself as a toolkit intended for use by security professionals within enterprise business to use in order to examine logs, alert notifications and other time synced data relating to potential security events.  This process also requires security analysis professionals, both within the business as well as Microsoft in some cases, which is why Windows Defender ATP is limited to use in this fashion.

It should be noted though, that in most cases the types of targeted attacks that ATP is intended to detect and analyze are commonly made against large business or government customers, while consumers are more often treated as mass market, low value targets using the common tools (e.g. malware, spam blasted phishing email, SEO, website hijacks, etc.) that you'd expect.

The Potentially Unwanted Application (PUA is Microsoft's term, many 3rd-parties use PUP or Program instead) protection is actually built into every Windows Defender client, it's simply not enabled by default on any of them and is most easily managed using the management consoles used by mostly larger business.  However, it is possible to enable this using other tools, including a simple set of PowerShell commands documented at the following page.

Block potentially unwanted applications with Windows Defender Antivirus | Microsoft Docs

The problem with the Microsoft PUA protection is that it's tuned for business, so it contains no true granularity and thus the only choice is to block or not, which for a business might make sense.  However, for consumers, where the lines between what's desired or not is often less clear, the complete blocking and removal of something like an adware program that gives you something "free" in return for watching ads may not be a desired result.

That's why you'll often see many here recommend the Malwarebytes Anti-Malware product, since it was designed first for the consumer, with both simple, granular control, and a greater range of optional methods for dealing with these less malicious, questionable applications I like to call annoyance-ware.  This is also something that most careful users, consumer or not, will never need if they aren't downloading and installing questionable files or programs, since that's how PUPs are typically encountered.  I know, I've never had one I wasn't purposefully aware of in over 20 years.

Ransomware protection via Controlled Folder Access is another special case, which is more similar to the User Access Control configuration first provided with Windows Vista.  The issue that resolved was one of (mostly 3rd-party) apps requiring administrator access to operate, when in most cases this should have been unnecessary, so a popup "nag box" was added to require authentication with an Admin account in order to run these requests, eventually causing most apps to resolve these design issues.

With CFA, the issue is requiring legacy access to specific Windows system and other app folders via archaic, direct file system access techniques that Microsoft has been attempting to wean developers off of for years, so similar to UAC, the CFA configuration allows manual (e.g. annoying) configuration of a workaround to allow those badly behaving apps still requiring this access to operate despite these design flaws.

Along with reporting these flaws via telemetry back to Microsoft so they can contact offending developers, the annoyance of this requirement itself is enough to get some to solve the problems due to the customer support issues this creates.  Moral of the story here is that if you must use CFA to get an app to work, either update to a newer version that fixes the problem, contact the vendor of the app to have them fix it, or do what I do and dump the app for something more modern that's being kept up to current security standards.

Finally to address your general question about how enterprise focused security initiatives like ATP affect the consumer user's PC.  This isn't clear because most see this as a product, which though portions of the client piece are included in certain business versions of Windows, you've already seen above are really just part of the tools required to make the server aggregation and logging work in order to allow central access and analysis, whether automated or by a security professional.

The point is that ATP isn't really the issue, since it's clearly not useful without all of the above.  However, if you think about what it allows, the results of the analysis of new, unknown attacks will often identify new malware, servers and even techniques being used in truly sophisticated attacks against high profile targets.  The final result of this will likely include new malware detection and removal definitions, detection engine code and malicious web page or server identity additions to the SmartScreen Filter system now integrated into Windows 10 as well as the Microsoft provided browsers where it's existed for years.

So even if a consumer can't themselves use the enterprise focused security extensions, they benefit indirectly via general intelligence and directly when new specific malware or malicious systems are identified and added to Defender or other security products, including those Microsoft supports through industry partnerships and 3rd-party agreements.

I'll also quickly mention that Microsoft has integrated portions of what they term ATP into their Outlook Email, Office 365 and certain other online products like Azure, though from what I've seen identified, I really just think of these as parallel portions to SmartScreen, though they include more sophisticated items like the wrappers around URLs in email messages which detect malicious script on-the-fly.

BTW, this is only a partial picture, since I finally got tired and decided this is where I should stop.  I do think it covers most of your questions though, even though as I guessed it got quite long.

I will say that it's nice to see that the testing groups are finally catching up with the Microsoft security apps to some extent and can identify what those of us with inside knowledge always knew, that Microsoft is playing a different game of trying to protect their customers, while 3rd-party products are trying to sell themselves, so they tune for the tests.  Maybe some day the tests will be better than the products, but in truth that's unlikely, since technical testing always requires better technology than the products being tested, which with something as sophisticated as cloud-based, dynamic anti-malware protection performing heuristic, behavioral and emulation based analysis on the fly both on the client and in the cloud, there's really nothing available that's sophisticated enough to test it thoroughly.

I gave up on the tests years ago other than as a basic sanity check for something in the top 10 or 15, which is about the best granularity you can expect.  Everything else they determine is really just random noise, since the malware world changes too fast to test in a coherent fashion.
