entegy say:

I realize how much better social was in WP8, but there are sadly a lot of caveats to that approach that ultimate hurt Microsoft and Windows Phone.

With the built-in integration, updates to said integration relied on OS updates, those pesky things that the carriers would rather avoid in order to sell you a new phone. Facebook chat became horribly out of date with no ability to share files, send stickers or participate in group chats. You couldn't like statuses from the People Hub. Windows Phone's best feature slowly became one of its worst.

The new model is everything the social integration should have been. It's now possible for apps to tie themselves into the People Hub without Microsoft from what I understand. This means integration can now be improved without waiting for OS updates. But this comes at a cost. Companies like Facebook gain their revenue from ads and other things, not just you existing on their platform. So while the integration can be a choice, it's not viable for Facebook to have all their content pre-formatted away from their own website and apps.

Facebook Messenger is better than the built-in Facebook chat ever was. Notifications are reliable for one.

One example is the Photos hub. OneDrive and Facebook albums used to show right next to your normal albums. But now they're in an "app" album. This is an extension of the new way of integrating social services. It may be one extra tap away to access those albums, but what you see is still native Windows Phone, showing what is possible when developers go all the way with the integration.

And of course this means faster updates. For example, the new way to share links to Facebook is not just bad, it's insulting to the user. Put a dumb link with no preview? That can be fixed to do the normal previewing stuff Facebook does in an app update.

Breaking out a lot of this integration sucks in the short term, but is better for the platform in the long term. And now that Microsoft is heavily dogfooding its own public APIs for a lot more (the Calendar app is a good example of that), that means anything that can't be fixed with an app update will likely get more attention to be improved since it's part of the core experience.

The idea is solid, but let's hope it doesn't take as long as Xbox Music to be good.
