I found this bit of code (http://www.ozgrid.com/VBA/validate-numbers.htm).

A better way is to disable alphanumeric keys using the keypress event:

Private Sub Textbox1_KeyPress( _
    ByVal KeyAscii As MSForms.ReturnInteger)
  TB_KeyPress KeyAscii
End Sub

Call this sub as shown in all your numeric textboxes:

Private Sub TB_KeyPress(ByVal KeyAscii As MSForms.ReturnInteger)
  'Läßt nur Zahlen in der Textbox zu
  Select Case KeyAscii
    Case Asc(Application.International(xlThousandsSeparator))
      'Erlaube Tausender Trennzeichen/ändere zu Dezimaltrenner
      KeyAscii = Asc(Application.International(xlDecimalSeparator))
    Case Asc("0") To Asc("9")
      'Erlaube Ganzzahlen
    Case Asc("-")
      'Erlaube Negative Werte/Berechnungen
    Case Asc(Application.International(xlDecimalSeparator)), _
        Asc("E"), Asc("e")
      'Erlaube Dezimalzahlen
    Case Asc("+"), Asc("*"), Asc("/"), Asc("^")
      'Erlaube Berechnungen
    Case Else
      'Nicht erlaubt
      KeyAscii = 0
  End Select
End Sub

Customize it as you need and if you like to have a calculation feature inside that box use the BeforeUpdate event:

Private Sub Textbox1_BeforeUpdate( _
    ByVal Cancel As MSForms.ReturnBoolean)
  TB_BeforeUpdate Me.ActiveControl, Cancel
End Sub

Call this sub as shown for all those textboxes:

Private Sub TB_BeforeUpdate(TB As Control, _
    ByVal Cancel As MSForms.ReturnBoolean)
  On Error GoTo CancelIt
  'Berechne den Wert oder Formeln
  TB = Evaluate(Replace(TB.Text, _
    Application.International(xlDecimalSeparator), "."))
  Exit Sub
CancelIt:
  Beep
  Cancel = True
End Sub

doug robbins - mvp office apps & services (word) say:
The NAMIKON 2001 logo is an AutoShape which in Word documents before 2007 were inserted inside { SHAPE } fields.  The reason that the logo does not print is because when the fields in the document are updated in later versions of Word, the contents of the { SHAPE } field are deleted.   The way around the issue is to access the header of the document and select the logo and then press CTRL+SHIFT+F9 to unlink the field and convert it to "ordinary text"

theamochor say:

I did not really want a discussion-but there is no category for the voicing of opinions I have encountered

I, as a retired person having been involved in computers since the early 70's, am still very involved with computers, their set-up and the training of semi-familiar and new users.  I also encounter comments from people that are extremely heavy computer users.  There are issues that I have seen that have not been resolved.  Here is a summary of feedback that I receive (and it includes, in some part, my feelings as well:

Sliding, fading and transparent windows are pretty.  Most of us want functionality and a familiar environment instead.

The added eyewash features bloat the operating system.  I could install WXP in less than 2GB.  All Windows OSs after XP have been extremely bulky and use up progressively more hard drive and system resources.

Up to XP the framework and progress of Windows had built upon the previous software and was intuitive.  Starting with Vista, utilities and other parts of windows have been re-named and moved to strange locations within the Windows framework.  That makes troubleshooting frustrating.

The new Windows networks beautifully: The most frequent complaint I hear is that the look and feel of XP was the best and seems that it is lost forever.  My contacts want XP looks with the networking ability of W7, W8 and W10.

I recently helped a friend with problems on his Mac computer.  I was pleasantly surprised that the look, touch and feel was the same as in the early 80's versions (system 7).  The OS is not even close to the original but fairly similar at the file-level maintenance/naming level.  That's what techs like.

The sorting of files in the "start menu" in W10 is totally confusing even though getting the "Start" menu back is nice.  Having "Programs" and "Accessories" was also somewhat intuitive through the system evolution.  The new layout does not seem to follow an alphabetical, intuitive or categorical (is that a word?) level.  Perhaps I have missed something in the user-sort of menus.

The updates are frustrating.  Walking away from the computer can result in dumped files because there was a time-delay re-boot during the interim after an update.

Two business people that I'm dealing with are angered with the "upgrade W7 to 10 notices" that keep popping up.  They don't want their focus destroyed during work.  At present I am only dealing with two business people :) on a tech-level. 

Most users that I know have turned off features such as UAC due to nagging pop-ups.

I am not interested in stirring up arguments and not a discussion per sec either.  I am hoping, instead,  that there will be a new self-awareness of what customers want and need rather than in presentation slickness.  Yes, we want a good-looking system:  We need a skinny, healthy operating system that functions with a minimum of memory, hard-drive usage and software. 

Can we, in closing,  get a detailed survey sent to every known owner and/or user asking what is specifically wanted and needed?
