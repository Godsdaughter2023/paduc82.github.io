joeu2004 say:

You are encountering a common anomaly in the internal arithmetic, which uses 64-bit binary floating-point.  This is demonstarted and explained below, together with a work-around.

(At the moment, I am only able to copy-and-paste your data and formulas in A1:J10.  I assume that the same problem and work-around apply to your other examples.  If I can work around my copy-and-paste difficulties, I will post an update later.)

The formula in B12 is =B2-TEXT(B2,"0.00000000000000E+0")-0 . 

That is 14 zeros after "0.".  The redundant -0 is necessary to work around an Excel "optimization" that hides the actual arithmetic result.  Copy the formula into B12:J16.

The formula reveals the difference between the exact internal binary value and the decimal representation, which Excel limits to 15 significant digits (rounded).

The point is:  there is an infinitesimal difference between the decimal presentations and the internal binary values in D4, G4, H4, D5 and I5.  And MATCH compares the internal binary values.

The work-around is to change the formulas in rows 4 and 5 as follows:

B4: =ROUND(CEILING(B3,0.1), 1)

B5: =ROUND(ROUNDUP(B3,1), 1)

I am not surprised by the difference in the results from CEILING.  But I am surprised by the difference in the results from ROUNDUP.

The problem arises because most decimal fractions cannot be represented exactly in 64-bit binary floating-point.  For example, 0.3 is 0.299999999999999,988897769753748434595763683319091796875 , and 0.1 is 0.100000000000000,0055511151231257827021181583404541015625.

(I use period for the decimal point and comma to demarcate the first 15 significant digits.)

Consequently, CEILING(..., 0.1) does not always return the exact binary representation of the decimal value that we expect.

AFAIK, ROUND(..., 1) always returns the exact binary representation.

I thought ROUNDUP and ROUNDDOWN did, as well.  Apparently not, sigh.

albert d. kallal say:

You are quite much on the right track. You want to loop + open each record separate.

The way you "restrict" the report to the ONE record is execute an open report FIRST and THEN send it out to pdf.


   Dim strR       As String
   Dim strWhere   As String
  
   strR = "my invoice report name goes here"
  
   Set rs = db.OpenRecordset("Cases WIP")
  
   Do While rs.EOF = False
      strWhere = "id = " & rs!ID
  
      DoCmd.OpenReport strR, acViewPreview, strWhere, , acHidden
     
      InvoiceNumber = InvoiceNumber + 1
 
      Number = CStr(InvoiceNumber)
 
      DoCmd.OutputTo acOutputReport, strR, acFormatPDF, "E:/Temp/" & Number & ".pdf", False

      DoCmd.Close acReport, strR < --don 't forget to close the report!
     
      rs.MoveNext
     
      etc...

> very operation of the DoCmd causes Adobe, my default PDF Reader, to open

 

That is what the "autostart" option in the output command is for (you have "true" which means autostart pdf). Change it false as the above smaple code shows – and your pdf will not launch.

Hi Tony,

No, it’s not feasible to schedule a Skype for Business Online meeting in Outlook using a group address or an alias. Based on my test and research, the answer is no.

It's possible to add the group address in the To field when sending a Skype for Business meeting request on a user's calendar. Then the event will appear on the group calendar. The organizer of the meeting will show up as the user's name.

Public Sub SPCCharts()
    Dim fileA As Workbook
    Dim fileB As Workbook
    Application.ScreenUpdating = False

    fileB.Sheets.Copy After:=fileA.Sheets(fileA.Sheets.Count)
    fileB.Close SaveChanges:=True
    Application.ScreenUpdating = True
End Sub
