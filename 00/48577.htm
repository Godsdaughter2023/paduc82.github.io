a. user say:

You might have a misconfigured computer. It might have been something you did to it last week or last month ... or last year. The mistakes you made might have had no effect at all at the time you made them, but now they're coming back to haunt you.

This is why it's so important to consider the longer term consequences of advice that you're given to solve some problem or make your computer run better. Without a full understanding of what you're being asked to do, you could be fixing a symptom while setting your computer up for failure down the road. This is a lesson I learned the hard way.

Or your computer might be infected with any of the many types of malware that aren't detected by antivirus software because there's nothing intrinsically malicious about them. For example: There's nothing malicious about a computer doing mathematical computations, unless those computations are mining cryptocurrency, which can overwhelm your computer's resources and cause it to freeze or lock.

You'll need more than scans with anti-virus software to catch cryptominers or botnets or peer-to-peer networks, among the more common exploits that are unlikely to be noticed by antivirus software. You'll need to have the computer examined by someone trained to spot these problems.

Ultimately, you need to have an understanding of how your computer works, and an awareness of threats found on the internet, in order to operate a computer successfully. You don't need to be an expert or have lots of fancy-sounding titles to do this; but you do need to keep current with information available in the popular media. That's far more effective at keeping your computer running well than generic troubleshooting or guesses.

In your current situation you have two options:

1- Find out what's causing the problem. Everything that goes wrong with a computer happens for a reason, and by the way, system files don't spontaneously become corrupt. Corrupted system files are truly rare nowadays. To find the cause you'll probably need the help of an expert. That costs, but if you come away with a knowledge of what went wrong then you can avoid the same trouble in the future.

2. Start over. That means completely reinstalling everything from scratch, which needs plenty of preparation if you're doing it yourself and will take a good chunk out of your day, but you'll end up with a pristine software setup.

The easiest and most reliable way to do this is to perform a factory reset which is so-named because it restores your computer to exactly the way it was when it left the factory. The two advantages to this is are 1) it's much faster than doing a clean install because you'll be restoring an image rather than reinstalling everything; and 2) you know it will work right because everything was previously setup and tested at the factory. Your computer manufacturer has complete directions for doing this.

One habit you can adopt that will save you a lot of trouble: Backup every day. With a daily backup of your personal files you'll never have to worry about losing your personal data, no matter what happens to your computer. And when you learn to make daily image backups of your C: drive, you'll never have to reinstall anything ever again. Instead, just restore a backup and in a matter of minutes your computer goes back in time to when everything was working well.

patrick barker say: Hi,



If you don't know where DMP files are located, here's how to get to them:

 1.    Navigate to the %systemroot%\Minidump folder.
 2.    Copy any and all DMP files in the Minidump folder to your Desktop and then zip up these files.
 3.    Upload the zip containing the DMP files to Skydrive or a hosting site of your choice and paste in your reply.

If you are going to use Skydrive but don't know how to upload to it, please visit the following:

http://www.wikihow.com/Use-SkyDrive

Please note that any "cleaner" programs such as TuneUp Utilities, CCleaner, etc, by default will delete DMP files upon use.

If your computer is not generating DMP files, please do the following:

1. Start > type %systemroot% which should show the Windows folder, click on it. Once inside that folder, ensure there is a Minidump folder created. If not, CTRL-SHIFT-N to make a New Folder and name it Minidump.

2. Windows key + Pause key. This should bring up System. Click Advanced System Settings on the left > Advanced > Performance > Settings > Advanced > Ensure there's a check-mark for 'Automatically manage paging file size for all drives'.

"PromptOnSecureDesktop"=dword:00000001

Now some explanations here. The “PromptOnSecureDesktop” DWORD key is the
value holder for the “User Account Control: Switch to the secure desktop when prompting for elevation” setting in Local Security Policy (LSP).

@The_lookup: Please note:
[HKEY_LOCAL_MACHINE\SOFTWARE\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Policies\System]
“EnableLUA”=dword:00000000

The “EnableLUA” DWORD key is the value holder for the entire User Account Control (UAC) status,
if you set the “EnableLUA” DWORD to equal from 1 to 0, Action Center will directly prompt with a message.

The second DWORD key which you posted is:
[HKEY_LOCAL_MACHINE\SOFTWARE\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Policies\System]
“ConsentPromptBehaviorAdmin”=dword:00000000

Ths “ConsentPromptBehaviorAdmin” DWORD key is the value holder for the “User Account Control: Behavior of the elevation prompt for administrators in Admin Approval Mode” setting in Local Security Policy (LSP).

The “ConsentPromptBehaviorAdmin” has to do with the behavior of the elevation prompt for administrators.
