wade c.campbell say:

what excel formula would you use for a timesheet?

What formula is used to compute a timesheet?  I wondered if there was one that you could enter the hours you worked (in at 8 AM and out at 4:30 PM) that would compute it as an hourly measure (tell me it is 8.5 hours)?  I'd like to use it for a payroll spreadsheet for a small business.

thanks
hansv mvp say:

With start time in B2 and end time in C2:

 

=C2-B2+(C2<B2)

 

formatted with the custom format [h]:mm will return worked hours as for example 8:30.

If you need to subtract time for a lunch break of half an hour, it becomes:

 

=C2-B2+(C2<B2)-1/48

 

If you want the result in decimal hours, e.g. 8.50:

 

=24*(C2-B2+(C2<B2))

 

or to subtract half an hour:

 

=24*(C2-B2+(C2<B2))-0.5

 

formatted as General, or as Number with 1 or 2 decimal places.

rwswan say:

This issue has been around for years.

I, too, am being inundated with *.*.io emails in my junk folder.  In the past, it was *.*.date emails and many others. 

Outlook.com uses Smartscreen to filter incoming emails.

It works like this:

Smartscreen determines an email is SPAM and the email is blocked.  The user never sees it, and this is good.

If the email passes the SPAM test, Smartscreen takes another look.  If the email is determined by Smartscreen to be JUNK, the email goes to the Junk folder, and this is bad, because  none of the user settings, including rules, work for emails sent to the junk folder by Smartscreen.

If the email passes the SPAM and JUNK test, Smartscreen allows the email to be controlled by the user, as one would expect. The email goes to the destination of your choice, depending on your settings (Standard, Exclusive, Rules).  The user is in control, and this is good.

A related issue is the inability of users to block Top Level Domains (TLD').  (In your case, .io is a TLD.).  The user cannot block *.*.io, for example, where * = anything (a "Wildcard").
