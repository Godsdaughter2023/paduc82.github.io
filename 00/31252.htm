    I have found through experimentation that some windows update files (and possibly other self-extracting files), when downloaded manually and run, will extract themselves to the local drive with the most free space (not necessarily where they were downloaded to).

    This seems completely unexpected.

    What I have discovered:

    I downloaded two windows updates manually as they were failing to install.  I got SQLEXPR.EXE and SQLEXPR_ADV.EXE from http://www.microsoft.com/download/en/details.aspx?id=184

    I saved them on the C: drive of a SBS2008 server and ran each one separately.   Without any prompting, both of them extracted to a USB-connected backup drive (which happened to be the I:\ drive).  This was totally unexpected and sent me off on a quest to find out why they extracted there.  The environment variables have no reference to this drive and this is not defined as a temporary location.

    I then tried this on an independent pc (XP), at a separate location, to see what happened.  I downloaded one file to a local drive G, and one to a network drive W.

    Then I moved them both to the C drive.  When I executed them, both extracted to the G drive.  This proved to me that the original download location was not relevant.

    After examining the free space available on the various drives, I was able to conclude from this that, despite there being plenty of free space on C: (5GB) and loads of space on W (terabytes), the executables decided to extract to the local (ie non-networked) drive with the most free space (which was G).

    This seems very odd.  The self-extracting files are choosing where to extract their contents by using the local drive with the most free space - without even asking the user.  Since Microsoft have no control over what drive is what, they should either be prompting for a location, or just extracting to the drive where the file currently resides.

    In the circumstances, the I drive on the server was being used for backup purposes and we definitely did not want any unexpected content on there, or any interference with the I/O speed, even it if was tidied up after completion of the update.

    I just wonder how many other updates are extracted unseen on to unexpected drives.

    Does a Microsoft expert have any comments on this, or a way to ensure the System Admin can control which locations are permissible for the extraction of Microsoft created executables?
