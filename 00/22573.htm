charles kenyon say:
See Understanding Styles in Microsoft Word and Tips for Understanding Styles in Word.
  1. Styles are document-wide, not limited by sections. You can use different, related, styles in different sections if you want, but I wouldn't.
  2. Whether applying a style to a paragraph will changed direct formatting depends on how much direct formatting is there. I believe the general rule is that if more than 50% of the paragraph is directly formatted, that formatting will be lost. I believe this applies to all character-level formatting including character styles.
  3. You should be using different styles for different looks, not direct formatting. While it takes a bit of time to set up initially, it will save you time in the long run.
  4. You can have all of your body text styles based on one style that controls say the font used.
  5. A style based on that could change your alignment or size (or both).

Also, very important, is to use the built-in heading styles for your headings. Why use Microsoft Word’s built-in heading styles?  You can change the look of these heading styles to look just the way you want.

Word is very much based on use of styles. The sooner you start using them, the sooner you'll find Word working for you to accomplish what you want rather than sabotaging your work behind your back.

The renaming of a sheet is not critical, because you can check the Codename property of the worksheet.

The only way to detect Cut & Paste is to use the Workbook_SheetChange event, this event is raised twice if the user paste.

The 1st call is the old range, the 2nd call is the new range.

VBA example in module ThisWorkbook:

Private Sub Workbook_SheetChange(ByVal Sh As Object, ByVal Target As Range)
  Debug.Print Sh.Name, Target.Address
End Sub
