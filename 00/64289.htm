bossdweeb say:

TDR errors always blame the driver. This is often only because the driver is the last link in a chain of event that leads a game to

lagging too long, or crashing.

The TDR error concept, introduced with Win7, is a failed attempt at preventing a BSOD, but is no less exacting at determining

the cause than a BSOD is.

The dread TDR is not as common on Win10, but it can still be notoriously difficult to troubleshoot when it does happen.

In addition to driver, it could be caused by a failing hardware , or software conflicts.

- Regarding drivers. The 15.7.1 driver is the last WHQL driver provided for your card by AMD, and should be a good option.

https://www.amd.com/en/support/graphics/amd-radeon-hd/ati-radeon-hd-5000-series/ati-radeon-hd-5570

- Due to the age of your HD5570 and the fact you are getting TDR when booting into Windows, this would be a likely source of the TDR

in your case. If you have access to another machine you could try the card in that machine, or if you have another card you could

try that to verify if your card is failing (more hardware at bottom of post).

------------

(my old notes from Win7 regarding other potential causes of TDR - Not all would be applicable in this instance.)

Some examples of software causes ....
- When TDR occurs when using the internet it is sometimes due to browser add-ons, or the
version of the browser you are using.
- Anti-Virus software, particularly AV suites that run a number of programs in the background
are another possibility. Running more than one AV program in resident (always on) can cause issues too.
- Malware, Spyware or Virus are possible. Run a full boot-time scan with your AV.
A virus called iehighutil.dll  that come as an added bonus from some torrent downloads is one example.

- System 'optimising' software is another possibility.

- Games often cause TDR. In some cases this is due to using too high graphics settings, but can also result from mods.

- Are your BIOS settings at default, or have you used some manual settings, specifically a RAM or cpu overclock ?
- Have you checked for a BIOS update for your motherboard ?
- Did you install anything around the time the crashes started ?
- Conflicts with other drivers can also cause TDR
One such driver comes with Daemon Tools.

Running in Clean Boot to troubleshoot potential conflicts from Services or Startup programs can sometimes help.

How to troubleshoot a problem by performing a clean boot in Windows Vista or in Windows 7

[EDIT: The Registry key below may not be relevant to Win10 any longer. There is a new folder in the Registry relating to TDR ]

- In Win7 TDR (time our detection) is set to 2 sec , but Monopoly$oft has advised in recent times
to use an 8 sec setting.
- The registry entry below can help prevent TDR's in some instances -
Open - Regedit - and Navigate to -

H_KEY_LOCAL_MACHINE \ System \ CurrentControlSet \ Control \ Graphics Drivers.
Right click on the open field and click on New / DWORD (32bit) Value,
Then name it:  TdrDelay -> double click it -> Set the value to 8.

Hardware:
- RAM... Run  memtest to verify the RAM is not producing errors
- HDD... Run  chkdsk to test the HDD for errors
- Run a temperature monitor on the cpu and graphics card to verify they are not overheating.
- Inadequate or failing power supply... Can cause lock-ups or random re-boots, but more often when
under load from gaming or similar system stressing software.
