albuquerquefx say:

If you are trying to share data directly between 2 computers without a router or modem you need a type of cable called either a "crossover" cable, or a "null modem" cable.

To be clear, this isn't entirely true.

If either of the computers in question has a gigabit Ethernet adapter, then a direct-connect cable will work. Gigabit network interfaces are required to support Auto MDI-X, which is why it works. Simply plug the cable between both machines, wait about 30 seconds for Windows to assign an automatic personal IP address (APIPA) and then perform the following:

From one of the computers, click the Windows+R keys on your keyboard, type the word CMD in the ensuing Run window, and press enter. You will get a black command line window with white text. In that window, type ipconfig and press enter.

One of the lines will say something to the effect of:

Ethernet adapter Local Area Connection:

   Subnet Mask . . . . . . . . . . . : 255.255.0.0

- it will always start with 169.254 and the last two numbers will be more than 1 and less than 255. That set of four numbers is THAT computer's IP address.

Now, from the other computer, click the Windows+R keys on your keyboard.  For example purposes only, let us consider that the IP address I showed above was accurate for your computer. Using that IP address, you would type \\169.254.12.109\c$ and press enter.

It may prompt you to log in -- you will need to log in with the name and password that would use on the first computer, not the one you used to log into the computer you're currently on.  After a few moments, it should pop up with a windows explorer of the remote machine.

For those who want more detail on gigabit MDI-X:

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Dependent_Interface

If you plug a traditional crossover cable into gigabit-capable network cards, you'll actually slow down your transfer rate. This is because gigabit interfaces use all four pairs of wire for TX/RX, and a crossover actually forces half of the pairs to go unused -- making it a standard 100mbit interface speed.

It's highly unlikely (albeit, not impossible) that the person above needs a crossover cable.

At least, it worked for me tonight and I pray it works for others in this situation.

I had exactly the same problem as you, except I wanted to replace Vista with a clean install of Windows 7 on an EI System 32-bit computer.  But, like you, it just wasn't recognising that there was a disc in there and then after a lot of 'clicking' it would say tell me to insert a disc or that there was a disc failure.  I bought this from new from a retailer and so was not very impressed and neither am I impressed with the response from Microsoft about this problem and shall be sending them a letter expressing my dissatisfaction.  Problems such as this should be properly investigated and perhaps added to the 'specifications' of the software to warn buyers of potential problems with certain brands or models of PCs.  In my opinion being told to go down the USB route with a process that most of the public would find near impossible to understand and follow and with the likely added expense of having to purchase a portable hard drive, DVD drive or large enough USB is unacceptable, particularly as Windows 7 is not a cheap item to purchase as it is.
