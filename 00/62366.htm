I would like to let you know that many different factors affect the high speed connection you can achieve with a Wi-Fi connection. Some of these factors you can control are the type of wireless router you are using or the positioning of the router and your computer. However, other factors, such as wireless interference from nearby wireless networks and cable Internet congestion, are hard or impossible to control.

Here are certain points to remember while using a Wi-Fi internet connection:

Distance from Access Point : Your Wi-Fi connection will be stronger if you are closer to your wireless access point. When you connect using a wireless device, you should see an indicator that shows in bars or waves how strong your connection is. A weak connection will be much slower than a strong one. If possible, move closer to your router.

Users on the Network : our network only offers so much speed, and the more users on your network, the less there is to go around. As a corollary to being farther from the router, users closer to the access point will have better speeds and interfere with the signal reaching devices farther from the access point – sometimes entirely blocking farther devices from connecting.

Bandwidth Use : The more bandwidth-heavy activities taking place over the network, the slower the overall connection becomes. If someone on a wired computer is downloading large files or playing a bandwidth-heavy game, wireless devices may see a noticeable slowdown in their Internet speeds. Streaming video, VoIP phones and online gaming all use bandwidth.

Device Interference : Other wireless or radio-based devices in your household can interfere with your Wi-Fi connection. Placing your router too close to a wireless phone receiver or a baby monitor can muddle your connection. Standing too close to a device like the microwave can also cause speed-hampering interference over your network. Avoid other wireless devices that use the 2.4 GHz frequency, and consider changing the wireless channel to cut down on interference.

Environmental Barriers : The space between your router and your wireless device is one aspect of signal strength; the barriers present between the access point and device is another often-overlooked one. The more walls, large furniture or appliances between you and the router, the weaker your signal strength. Some elements, such as wood or glass, make little differences; other elements, like metal or water -- including the water present in your body -- have a higher impact in hampering your Wi-Fi signal. It's best to place your router toward the middle of your home or office; using a signal booster can help ensure a stronger connection in larger areas.
