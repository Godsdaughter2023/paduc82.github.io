8.1 Update Causes "Cracking" Sound immediately after songs - Microsoft Community

kchm say:

8.1 Update Causes "Cracking" Sound immediately after songs during music playback

(This problem has been isolated to only happening with the 8.1 update, and not being limited to Xbox Music)

"Cracking" is the common name for a fraction of a second of music, seemingly chosen at random, in audio playback after a song ends but before the next song.. It also occurs if you choose to play only one song (at the end of the song) in your playback. You can think of it as the "opposite" of gapless playback in that regards. It's generally characterized by always being present so long as the same method of playback is used--in my case, not streaming music (so, not Pandora or presumably Xbox Music streaming), played through headphones. If you don't have such a "crack" when you normally do, it's not because the problem isn't occurring--you've simply finished a song that had moments of very low sound, and as such, the "crack" just happened to play an inaudible fraction of a second.

What makes this problem interesting isn't all the various strange issues with Xbox Music, some of which have been addressed. The problem manifests on multiple music playback apps (and for some very unfortunate people, also streaming apps). It's that there don't seem to have been any cases of this occurring back in Windows Phone 8.0. Which is sensible--how would Microsoft hope to sell a smart phone where music playback is always characterized by a often-loud CRACK between songs? That's about as attractive as a badly-skipping CD player. 

This problem, instead, seems to be the consequence of something in 8.1 being incompatible with the playback system in many or most 8.0 phones. I'd like to hear if anyone who had phones immediately launched with 8.1 (like the Lumia 930) have the same problem--I suspect they don't. And there isn't really any indication that Microsoft is aware of the issue (I'd love to be proven wrong, particularly here).

So, that's the gist of it. A particular bug of the release of 8.1, that hasn't been corrected with Cyan, is a playback glitch with music stored on the phone itself. It seems 8.1 has a long list of problems in introduces into the ecosystem (most of the high-profile post-8.1 patches seem to be corrective in nature), so here's another one to add to the list. Windows Phone Support's Twitter account recommended I post this problem, as they couldn't think of a solution (nor could their normal tech support reps on chat). 

I'm pretty sure I've stopped the Microsoft WP team, but I'd like to be proven wrong.

anthony.b say:

I think that perhaps you meant GB and not MB in your question. Assuming that this is the case,

Microsoft's web site states that for MS Office 2013 you need to have:

[1]  A processor with at least speed of 1 gigahertz.

[2]  At least 1 GB of RAM for 32-bit computers, or 2 GB of RAM for 64-bit computers.

[3]  3 GB of hard disk space available.

[4]  Graphics hardware acceleration requires a DirectX 10 graphics card.

(the above four items are quoted from Microsoft's web site, for "Office Home & Student 2013" - for further details, refer to Microsoft's website www.microsoft.com)

I think there are five factors that would affect how large of a file MS Word can handle:

[a]  I think one of the key factors is HOW you have built in your MS Word document.  Lots of factors are a possible issue, such as number of styles used, number of pictures, etc.  If you Word document has become partially corrupted, that will also probably result in issues when the file gets very large.  Or if the corruption is really bad, your Word document will not open at all.

[b]  The number of processors on your computer, and how fast each processor is, will be of help for working on a large Word file.  But if "[a]" is a problem, then a faster computer might not help much.  Sometimes shutting down various other tasks, and letting your computer mostly use its processor(s) for Word can help.

[c]  The amount of RAM memory on your computer will be of help (the more RAM, the better your Word document will work, up to a certain point, where your extra RAM will not make much difference).   But if "[a]" is a problem, more memory might not help much.

[d]  If you are editing your document on the network or via the internet, then your network and/or internet speed will be an issue.  I would recommend editing your document on your hard drive, if possible.  However, the faster the speed of your network and/or internet connection, this could make even Word files on your hard drive run faster, since other tasks that the computer is doing will run faster (such as behind-the-sceens updates to your virus protection software, over the internet, at the same time Word is running).

[e]  Sufficient free hard-disk space is needed to open a large MS Word file.

As an example, one of my computers has a dual-core processor, which has speed of 2 gigahertz per processor, no video card, and has about 8 GB of free hard disk space on it.  This computer is running Windows 7.  This computer has 2 GB of memory.  I started a new blank Word document in Word 2013, and typed:

=rand(99,99)

and then pressed the Enter key.

This generated 195 pages of test text.  I used copy/paste to replicate this text 4 more times, and now I have a 1166 page Word document.  I saved the file, and it used about 115 KB of file size.  The file takes about 3 seconds to open, but other than that, I see no issues with that Word document on this computer.  However, that word document is very simple, as I have only added text to it, and I added no new pictures, styles, etc., other than what the blank document started with.

As a second example, one of my other computers has an 8-core processor, 12 GB of memory, a video card, and has about 1600 GB of free hard disk space on it.  That computer is running Windows 7, and has Word 2007 on it.  That computer has a high-speed internet connection (Comcast cable modem).  This computer opens the 1166 page word document in about 1/2 second.

As a third example, if I started a new blank Word document, on ANY computer, and started going to various web sites, from many different publishers, and copying pages from the internet, onto this Word document, I would expect that eventually, even the fastest computer available, would no longer be able to open that Word document, and the total pages would not be that high (maybe 200 pages or so) due to the extreme variety of styles, pictures, tables, etc. that would be copied into that word document.  By the way, I don't think it is a very good idea to copy stuff directly from the internet, or even from other Word documents (sometimes is better to copy into Notepad first, and then copy only the text into your Word document).  In other words, it is good to try to keep your Word document simple, with a minimal number of styles, and avoid copying in unneeded pictures, styles, fonts, tables, etc. that can cause the Word software to get over-worked, and the file to get oversized (that could lead to various problems).  It is OK to put pictures, styles, tables, and various fonts into your document, but do so carefully, and purposely, and not to an extreme level.  It is a good idea to keep multiple backup copies of a Word document, for example to end the document with v0002, v0003 and etc.  Then if the document ever has that slow open (or no open) problem, go back to a prior version, and try re-doing your changes in a different way - hopefully the the problem will not repeat itself.  It that problem does repeat itself, then at least you have some idea of what might be causing the document to have issues.  When you said:

"I can't work with this file or save it in Word.

I have had to break it into small parts to work with.

However, I now have to send it as one contiguous file to a commercial printer to print it."

that could be a problematic approach, since it might not be possible to re-build the parts into a single Word document on your computer.  IF THE FINAL RESULT MUST BE IN A SINGLE WORD DOCUMENT, then I would think it would be better to work on a single Word file, using some of the techniques that I described above, to allow the larger Word file to work properly on your computer.  If need be, re-do your entire document, starting with only the unformatted text (use Notepad to strip out all the formatting), and carefully add the tables, pictures, styles, making backups often, being sure to NOT copy the formatting from the old document (rather, select the formatting in, from scratch, in your rebuilt Word document, using various MS Word tools).  If need be, get ahold of a large book on Word, and learn, and follow, the recommended techniques for building/formatting a large Word document (for example, this will help you to understand styles, paragraphs, headings, tables, paging, etc. and how to use these things properly).  Two Word books I would recommend are Microsoft Word 2013 Inside Out and Microsoft Word 2013 Bible.

I hope that the above might be of some help.
