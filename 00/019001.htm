john w. vinson say:

I think you may be misunderstanding the purpose of Forms in an Access database. Forms do not contain any data! Data is stored in Tables, and only in Tables; a Form is just a tool to manage data in a table (its Recordsource table to be specific). If your form is not connected to the demographics table, or to some other table in your database (or a linked database), it's certainly not going to work!

You should never need to "go to each table, search the right cell and input the data" - in fact except for design and debugging, you should never need to open a Table directly at all. Instead you would use the Form as an interface.

Could you describe your tables, and their relationships? THAT is the foundation of any database, and you need to get it right before you even start working on forms.

Incorrect xirr - Microsoft Community

a_805 say:

Incorrect xirr

Microsoft Excel 2010 is calculating incorrect xirr. Two completely different cash flows are giving the same xirr, which logically cannot be correct since once has a net positive value and the other has a net negative value. How do I resolve this ?
joeu2004 say: A805 wrote:
Microsoft Excel 2010 is calculating incorrect xirr. Two completely different cash flows are giving the same xirr, which logically cannot be correct since once has a net positive value and the other has a net negative value. How do I resolve this ?

You start by giving us enough information that would allow us to offer an educated response.  For example, what are the dates and the cash flows?

The proof of whether or not the value returned by XIRR is correct is whether XNPV is close to zero when you use the XIRR as the discount rate.  Is it?

If so, there can are at least a couple possible explanations.

First and foremost, there is no reason why two cash flows, one net positive and the other net negative, cannot have the same (X)IRR.  For example, IRR({10000,-2000,-4000,-6000,-8000}) and IRR({-10000,2000,4000,6000,8000}) are both about 27.2732%, but the first net cash flow is -10,000 and the second net cash flow is 10,000.

Second, if your cash flows have multiple sign changes, there can be multiple IRRs.  Which one XIRR finds depends on the design of the internal algorithm.  By coincidence, it might find the same IRR for both cash flows, even though there might be different IRRs for one or both cash flows.

There is little point in explaining further without having concrete data to work with.

Finally, I do know that XIRR sometimes returns bogus results (instead of a #NUM or #DIV/0 error).  That is, it returns a rate that does not cause XNPV to return close to zero.  This is a defect in XL2003 XIRR.  I suspect the same defect exists in XL2007 XIRR.  But the XIRR algorithm was "improved" in XL2010.  I do not know if the same defect exists in XL2010 XIRR.  TBD.
