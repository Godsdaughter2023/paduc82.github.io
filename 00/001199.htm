You can use  ipconfig  in a cmd window to find out what is going on in terms of the dnscache.   E.g. before starting a test use  ipconfig  /flushdns   to empty the cache and then after a test use   ipconfig  /displaydns   to see what happened.   Doing that could give you an IP address for IE to try to as a connectivity check with a real HTTP server (e.g. intead of just a ping request which is only a connectivity check with an ICMP server which is often blocked because of that protocol having been misused by moronic "denial of service" "exploits".)

For an example of the  technique, since you said "when I attempt to access Facebook" let's take "facebook" as an example.    What are you doing then?   What are you seeing?

 

FWIW I just entered   facebook.com  in my IE Address bar and instantly got redirected to  http://www.facebook.com/

complete with favicon in the Address bar.   I am actually using IE10 which like IE9 has significantly hidden the previously more apparent available cues (e.g. no more hints from either the Status bar or Title bar which you have available in XP) but there are some analogous cues from changes in the Tab label which now says "Welcome to Facebook - Log in, Sign up or Learn More" and then repeats the URL in the Address bar.   The tab label (which I suppose is probably the page Title and would replace whatever you as an XP user would see in your XP IE8 Title bar) is prefixed by the favicon in the Address bar.  The display area contains what you would expect to see as a result of rendering the page provided by that URL but the favicon alone could prove that there is connectivity to the host.  .   In place of the cues in the Status bar in IE9 I could if necessary enable the new Developer Tools Network Capture tool for a much clearer picture of what was happening in terms of redirection and rendering than the flashing changes in the XP Status bar ever could.

Now let's look at the clues provided by  ipconfig  /displaydns  after that redirect.

The most significant one is:

<extract  command="ipconfig /displaydns">

www.facebook.com
----------------------------------------
Record Name . . . . . : www.facebook.com
Record Type . . . . . : 1
Time To Live  . . . . : 47
Data Length . . . . . : 4
Section . . . . . . . : Answer
A (Host) Record . . . : 69.171.224.69
</extract>

 

Surprisingly there is no record of   facebook.com   which I used to start my request with.   Maybe I was too slow typing my command and would have to have it ready to use as soon as I had entered it.    Sometimes the Time To Live  values are very small and force us to be on our toes to let them be captured as diagnostics.  Nevertheless this shows that a redirect had occurred and than IE had gone on to issue a request for the redirected host name.   It has a more usable TTL and of course it was done later than the original lookup so that is why I was able to capture it.

 

Let's say that that this Host record would have been the only clue I would have been given about my request.   E.g. IE would be indicating that it could not find it etc.   Then copy the IP address given here and paste it into the Address bar and press Enter.   Result (unexpected but not uncommon): it redirected to the above URL I referred to as having seen instantly.  In a more general case I could expect to see some kind of HTTP error message, indicating that yes there was connectivity at that address with an HTTP server but that I needed to know a proper URL in order to proceed further with that host.


Let's say that instead of the first redirection I only got a clue that the initial lookup had succeeded, e.g. that I had seen a Host record for  facebook.com.   Well, I can simulate that result by doing an  nslookup  for it and picking one of its addresses, then doing the same test of using an IP address in the IE Address bar.   Result (even more surprising):  another instant redirect.   E.g. still no sign of a  lookup for  facebook.com (that's not surprising) but a switch to one of the www.facebook.com  IP addresses and another lookup done for it.   (That is surprising since I thought that once IE had done a lookup it held onto it.  E.g. in order to force IE to do another one and hopefully find a different IP address to use, it could have been necessary to close all instances of iexplore.exe and start over.   I wonder if that is a new feature of IE10?  <w>)


Hopefully this will give you some ideas about how you can diagnose one of your own cases, even if you can't describe it.   <eg>

Outlook.com contacts with Company Name and Email only items do not - Microsoft Community

mark krisburg say:

Outlook.com contacts with Company Name and Email only items do not sync correctly with Outlook 2013

A contact in Outlook.com that contains a First Name, Last Name and Email address syncs to Outlook 2013 without difficulty.  In Outlook 2013, this contact can be found by performing a "To:" filtered search, using the characters of the first or last names.

However, a contact defined in Outlook.com only with a Company Name and Email address can not be found in Outlook 2013 with a "To:" search.  The contact exists, but the "Display as" field only contains the email address from Outlook.com surrounded by parenthesis.  The "File As" field appears to contain data, but it does not propagate to the "Display as".  Only the email address is found in the "File as" field.

If you find such a contact in Outlook 2013, and open its properties, and then re-save the "File as" item, and delete the "Display as" item and force it to be regenerated by tabbing out of the field, THEN the contact can be found using the "To:" filtered search, by searching for either "ABC..." or "John Smith".

I have many items defined only by company name and email, because I wanted to locate various persons by searching for their company name.  Examples are:

Company Name:     ABC Broadcasting - John Smith - TV Advertising

Company Name:     ABC Broadcasting - Susan Jones - Communications

In Outlook 2013 if you use the "To:" search for  a company name that was entered in Outlook.com and synced over to Outlook 2013, you will not be able to locate this company.  On the other hand, if you ENTER the contact Company Name and Email from within Outlook 2013, then you will be able to find it with a "To:" search.
