NAT error solution

"I can't hear my friends online, or I can't join or host a multiplayer game with my Xbox One"

Your Steps
1
Welcome to the Xbox One Multiplayer Game Solution
Step 1: Welcome to the Xbox One Multiplayer Game Solution

Can your Xbox One connect to Xbox Live, but you can’t hear your friends online within a game or a party? Or are you unable to host or join a multiplayer game? If so, then you may be encountering an issue related to Network Address Translation (NAT). Answer a few questions, and we’ll try to fix the problem.

First, we’ll want to check your current NAT type. To do this:

  1. Scroll left from Home to open the guide.
  2. Select Settings.
  3. Select All Settings.
  4. Select Network.

On the Network settings screen, in the center column under Current Network Status, the NAT Type field displays your current NAT type.

Why NAT is important

Your NAT type, combined with the NAT type of other players, determines whether you can successfully use multiplayer gaming or party chat. The tables below illustrate the differences between the NAT types and, based on your NAT type, who you can play multiplayer games with and hear in a party chat.

OPEN NAT

MODERATE NAT

STRICT NAT

UNAVAILABLE NAT

You can chat with other people, as well as join and host multiplayer games with people who have any NAT type on their network.

You can chat and play multiplayer games with some people; however, you might not be able to hear or play with others, and you typically won’t be chosen as the host of a match.

You can chat and play multiplayer games with people who have an OPEN NAT type. You can’t be chosen as the host of a match.

You’ll be unable to use party chat or connect to multiplayer games for some titles on Xbox Live.

To resolve your multiplayer game issue, you’ll need to recheck your NAT type after each solution we try. To retest your NAT type:

  1. Open the guide by pressing the Xbox button  in the center of your controller.
  2. Scroll right in the guide to the System area
  3. Select Settings.
  4. Select Network.
  5. Select Network Settings.
  6. Select Test NAT Type

After the test has completed, you’ll see the results of your NAT type just below All done. If your NAT type is now Open, you can select Continue to return to the Network Settings home page. If it’s not Open and you’re going to continue to troubleshoot, you may want to leave your console on this screen while trying the next step. This way, you can select Test NAT type when you’re ready to test again.

Note: If your NAT type is Moderate NAT and you immediately retest your NAT type without power cycling your home networking devices, you may see a false positive where your console displays an Open NAT even though your network isn’t Open. This false positive is because your network device has already mapped that port based on the prior test. Restarting your network devices first is the only way to ensure an accurate test.

Here is some information I found on Superfetch that might be helpful to you. One of the options is to edit the registry so I wanted to make sure that you understand that Modifying REGISTRY settings incorrectly can cause serious problems that may prevent your computer from booting properly. Microsoft cannot guarantee that any problems resulting from the configuring of REGISTRY settings can be solved. Modifications of these settings are at your own risk. You should backup your registry before making changes. You might also want to create a restore point in case a system restore needs to be done for any reason.

 

Superfetch settings are configured in the registry at the following location:

 

HKEY_LOCAL_MACHINE\SYSTEM\CurrentControlSet\Control\Session Manager\Memory Management\PrefetchParameters

 

Find the EnablePrefetcher key on the right-hand pane, and change the value to one of these:

  • Disable Caching: 0
  • Cache Applications Only: 1
  • Cache Boot Files Only: 2
  • Cache Everything (default): 3

If you decide to change these settings you may want to try different settings to see which one performs the way you want it to.

You will also want to clean out the history of Superfetch if you modify the registry setting or even if you don’t modify the registry you may want to in case a file has become corrupt. It may take a little longer to reboot because the boot files will not be precached until the Superfetch files are rebuilt.

I hope this information is helpful in resolving your issue.

nik3r say: The random trashing you describe is more likely Windows making system restore backups. There is a simple way around, just resize the maximum available storage for backups and it will only take 1-3 minutes to perform this which is no big deal really.

run: "cmd"

type for notebook:
vssadmin resize shadowstorage /For=C: /On=C: /MaxSize=1024MB

for desktop PC:
vssadmin resize shadowstorage /For=C: /On=C: /MaxSize=2048MB

The lesser the size, the less time the trashing takes BUT you will have less restore points in case something goes wrong, at 2048MB I can usually go back by 3 days at least with heavy installation activity.

You can verify if this is your problem by monitoring the files being written/read at the time of trashing, it should be acessing \$System Volume Information.
