scottgem say:
You simply create multiple grouping levels. Group by fruit, THEN group by date.

jolenamiller say: Thanks Scott - I do this, but then my only option is to sort group by A or Z; however, I want to sort group by earliest date.  So if Plum had an earlier date than Apple, it would list Plum first....

qa guy commelec say:

Do the sorting within the report itself.

P.S.  A to Z is the same as lowest date to highest

jolenamiller say: So there is no way to sort by date FIRST, but keep group together even if some dates are later than others in other groups?

john w. vinson say:

You would need to add a calculated field to the query, such as

(SELECT Min(datefield) FROM yourtable AS X WHERE X.fruitfield = yourtable.fruitfield) AS FirstDate

You could then use FirstDate in the Sorting and Grouping property of the report, rather than the fruit name. You would still have the datefield as the second level sort.

kathy sims say:

Macros, building blocks, or quick parts?

I am a former Word Perfect power user.  I made the switch to Word 2010 about a year ago.  I relied heavily on Word Perfect macros to perform many tasks from creating form letters and asking me to insert specific text to customize the form to simply inserting a signature block.  I have figured out the basics of Word, but am struggling (among other Word/Word Perfect adjustments) with trying to determine whether Word has any type of equivalent to Word Perfect macros.  For example, I previously had a macro that would insert letterhead, adjust the top margin of the first page and replace the date code of the letter with date text that would not change when I finalized a letter simply by clicking 2 keystrokes.  Can someone point me in the best direction for an equivalent in Word, if such exists?  Thank you for your help.

jay freedman say:
The equivalent in Word is mostly "set up a template with the parts you need, and base documents on the correct template". Certainly, all the items you mentioned should be handled that way.

For setting up a letterhead in a template, see http://wordfaqs.mvps.org/Letterhead.htm.

To change the space at the top of only the first page, you would not change the margin, but instead set the size of the first-page header by adding "Space Before" to the first paragraph of that header. That's also covered in the cited article. (Note that in Word, a "margin" is a per-section property rather than a per-page property.)

In the template, at the location for the date, insert a CREATEDATE field instead of a DATE field. In each document based on the template, that field will display the date on which the document (not the template) is created, and that will not change afterward.

There are many things you can do with building blocks, especially for inserting blocks of text, preformatted tables, signature blocks, and so on. (I've always thought that Microsoft should not have made any distinction between the terms "building blocks" and "Quick Parts" -- it just confuses people.) A building block can contain just about anything you can put into a document. Once you've inserted a building block into a document, you can edit the result as you like. It's up to you to balance the proliferation of similar building blocks against the work needed to modify one after it's inserted.

Macros in Word can do anything you could do from the keyboard and much more, but they're really overkill for most reasonably simple tasks. Their real utility is in working around Word's inability to do certain things, or to customize parts (such as dialog boxes) that were never intended to be customized. In this, they're very unlike WordPerfect's macros, especially those that mimic keyboarding.
