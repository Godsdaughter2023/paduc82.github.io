carolina calling say:

You haven't told us about the keyboard itself.  What manufacturer and model?

On some keyboards (not laptops), where each key has its own set of metal contacts, you can clean them.  This is not the case where the key presses two  layers of film together.  These cannot be cleaned without taking the keyboard completely apart and they often are hard to put back together (read align) afterward.  Also, the contact pads where the film is deformed often just wear out.  With these types of keyboards, its often easier to just buy a new one.

If there are contacts to clean, you can make a "keycap extractor" out of two paperclips.  Bend the bottom of the small end of each paperclip ninety degrees so that there is about an eighth of an inch (three millimeters) sticking out perpendicular at the bottom.  You then can slip a paperclip under either side of the key and pull gently straight up.  If you see contacts to clean, proceed as below.  Otherwise (where there is just a hole) put the key cap back.

Clean all the fuzz, flakes, and whatever out of the well under the key.

Cut a stiff piece of lint free paper (nothing abrasive) to twice the width of the hole where the key contacts are, fold it in half, lightly wet it with alcohol and place it in the hole between the contacts.  If the mechanism of the key allows, press it down to close the contacts then withdraw the paper to wipe the contacts.  Do this twice.  Do not generate lint!

If the mechanism to close the contacts is not there, wipe both sides carefully as not to create any lint.

I have on occasion, where I thought it appropriate because of excessive wear and the gap not too narrow, put atiny speck of graphite key lubricant on the end of a toothpick and placed it between the closed contacts.  I use graphite because it is a conductor.  Most common lubricants will just attract dust and gum up the works.

Put the key cap back on and test.

Good luck!
