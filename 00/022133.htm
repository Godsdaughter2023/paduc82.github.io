To select the type of tab stop you want to set on the ruler, click the tab symbols at the far left of the horizontal ruler (below the ribbon). Locking chart additions in Word - Microsoft Community

murrayv3 say:

Locking chart additions in Word

I frequently import charts to Word and then add trend lines or text boxes. How can one then lock the additions so the chart can be shrunk or enlarged with the additions staying in correct relation to the original?
philipradler say:

I'd suggest the following, based on what the real experts on this forum have taught me:

  1. Right-click on your chart.
  2. From the resulting menu, choose Wrap Text, then pick any wrap mode except "In Line with Text."
  3. Draw your lines, text boxes, whatever. (Lines and inserted text boxes may disappear behind your chart, but you can bring them to the front by using the Selection Pane to select them, and clicking "Bring to Front" from the right-click menu.)
  4. Select the chart with a single click, then Shift+click each additional item you wish to group.
  5. Click on the Drawing Tools>Format tab.
  6. Click on the Group button and select Group.

I believe the default mode, at least with inserted Excel charts, is "In line with text." That puts them on the same layer as your text (duh!); all of your drawing objects (lines, squiggles, text boxes, etc.) are going onto the drawing layer. You can't group across those layers. By selecting a wrap style other than "In line with text" (square, tight, in front of text, behind text, whatever) you will put your chart on the drawing layer. Then it can be grouped with your drawn additions (including text boxes), and the whole mess can be sized, moved, copied, or deleted in one fell swoop.

Hope this helps!

-Phil

The one-click functionality offers beginners easy approaches for the most commonly used concepts. Highlighting Hi/Low values in a chart is not something absolute beginners in Excel typically need to do. As with any software, there is a learning curve for more intermediate and advanced topics. 

Fortunately even these more complex topics are well documented and an article like Jon Peltier's tutorial above should be mastered by anyone who knows how to select a cell and read instructions. 

On the other hand, VBA can be used to create specific charts with a single click, but even then data might need to be arranged in a specific way. Also, running VBA will require at least that the user allows running VBA or setting up trust center parameters. Again, not stuff for absolute beginners.
