A lot of problems with Office 2013 are caused by third party (non-Microsoft) applications.  This is my (expanding) list of things to try when Office 2013\365 won’t start or crashes:

See Office 2013 install woes - http://office-watch.com/t/n.aspx?a=1834

See the KnowledgeBase article “Office 2013 applications crash on launch at the splash screen” at:

http://support.microsoft.com/kb/2813143

Or see the KnowledgeBase article Error: “Something went wrong and we can't sign you in right now” when trying to install Office 2013 at:

http://support.microsoft.com/kb/2811180/en-us/

Also see my response in

http://answers.microsoft.com/en-us/office/forum/office_2013_release-word/microsoft-word-2013-has-stopped-working/67b81016-849f-481a-bce5-bf04797ac03b

Do you have Abbyy Finereader installed on the PC? A number of people have reported that removing it overcomes problems with 2013

Do you have “Fun Tools” add-in? A number of people have reported that removing it overcomes problems with 2013

Do you have Toshiba Book Reader installed on the PC?  It has been reported that uninstalling that brings Office back to working status.

Do you have the Acrobat PDFMaker add-in.  It has been reported that uninstalling that overcomes an issue with a message appearing that states "The document contains macros. Macro support for this application is disabled. Features requiring VBA are not available. Would you like to open this document as a read only?"

http://answers.microsoft.com/en-us/office/forum/office_2013_release-word/microsoft-word-has-stopped-working-when-i-close/8375b5b6-4f7e-46ed-a02b-90152ecad287#LastReply

You'll have to substitute your actual paths if you have renamed your profile or installed Outlook in a different location.
f. Reindexing will take some time to complete. After the process is complete, exit and then restart Outlook.

How to interpret the output of XIRR function in excel? - Microsoft Community

david a57 say:

How to interpret the output of XIRR function in excel?

I am using an example where cash flows are quarterly over 10 years. Does that mean that the output of the XIRR function provides a quarterly return that must be multiplied by 4 to get an annual return? Or does the function automatically annualize the result? So if the output from the data using the XIRR function is say 5% based on quarterly cash flows does that mean there is a 5% quarterly return or a 5% annual return?
financial engineer say:
I am using an example where cash flows are quarterly over 10 years. Does that mean that the output of the XIRR function provides a quarterly return that must be multiplied by 4 to get an annual return? Or does the function automatically annualize the result? So if the output from the data using the XIRR function is say 5% based on quarterly cash flows does that mean there is a 5% quarterly return or a 5% annual return?

XIRR = AEY or APY or APR

AEY - annual effective yield
APY - annual percentage yield
APR - annaul percentage rate

The financial regulators worldwide sanction the lenders to quote the interest rate on loans and investments as an APR so the customer is able to make comparisons with alternative products on the market

The actual interest rate (return) is often quite different from the APR, depending on the compounding frequency of interest.

The actual return from an investment will be the periodic interest rate, the IRR. An annual rate may be found using the product of the periodic rate with the number of such periods in a year.

nominal rate  = periodic rate * m
m is the compounding peirod in a year

AEY = ( 1 + nonimal_rate/m )^m - 1

XIRR = AEY

Thus if your quarterly cash flows return an XIRR of 5% then this is the APR (AEY). To find the quarterly return, you may use the nominal function outside XIRR and divide it by 4

=NOMINAL ( XIRR(B1:B40, A1:A40), 4 ) / 4

Same may be found by rearranging the AEY formula given earlier

XIRR = (1+i/m)^m - 1
(1+i/m)^m - 1 = XIRR
(1+i/m)^m = XIRR + 1
1+i/m = (XIRR + 1)^(1/m)
i/m = (XIRR + 1)^(1/m) - 1

i/4 = (5%+1)^(1/4) - 1
i/4 = (1.05)^(0.25) - 1
i/4 = 1.01227 - 1
i/4 = 0.01227
i/4 = 1.227%

Thus for an APR of 5% compounded quarterly the quarterly interest rate is 1.227% whereas the annual quarterly compounded rate is given by

i/4 = 1.227%
i = 1.227% * 4

nominal_rate = 4.91%

Had you used IRR instead of XIRR for your 40 quarterly cash flows, IRR would have returned the periodic quarterly rate closer to 1.227%. The slight difference in IRR vs XIRR is due to the fact that 30/360 day count is applicable in IRR calculation whereas an Actual/365 day count is applicable in XIRR calculation.
