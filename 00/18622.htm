steve belleguelle say:

I have just fixed this problem for a customer of mine who had a new Dell PC with Windows 10 and was trying to use Office 365 with iCloud.  He was getting the "no default profile" message.  After a few dead-ends I found the following worked for his machine.

The problem was due to the pre-installed version of Office on the system.  Once I removed this and reinstalled Office 365, the problem cleared.

One thing I didn't get a chance to try was to simply check that the correct Outlook was selected as the default mail program in Windows.  I don't know if it will clear the problem, but it may be worth checking first.  This is because of the nature of the problem and the way iCloud looks for Outlook.

The procedure I followed may contain unnecessary steps, but I include them in case they are required.

1. Open a Powershell window with Administrator privileges  (Thanks to Nick001Aye for his instructions on how to do this which I've pasted below)

To open an elevated PowerShell prompt, in the taskbar search, type powershell. Now see the result Windows PowerShell which appears on the top. Right-click on it and select Run as Administrator. The UAC prompt will ask you for your consent.

 From the Powershell window run the following to remove the Outlook component:

Get-AppxProvisionedPackage -Online | where-object {$_.packagename –like "*Outlook*"} | Remove-AppxProvisionedPackage -Online

2. Next run this in the same Powershell window - not sure if it's necessary or not:

Get-AppxPackage "*Outlook*" |  Remove-AppxPackage

3. Finally, in a non-admin Powershell window, run the command from 2 again.

Get-AppxPackage "*Outlook*" |  Remove-AppxPackage

This should remove the installer for all new accounts and the folder that iCloud is using.

I would suggest restarting the PC now and then either reinstalling or running a repair on your regular installed version of Office.  Hopefully this will clear the problem.

TC Fields are user-inserted for the purpose of including TOC content that isn't formatted using a qualifying Paragraph Style.

One way to determine if they have been used is to toggle on the display of field codes. Any TC fields would appear in the document as something like { TC "Last Days of Pompeii" \l 2 } adjacent to the actual text. [That example would identify Last Days of Pompeii to be included as a Level 2 entry in the TOC regardless of how the text was formatted or where it appeared.]

Even easier is to check in Insert> Index & Tables> Table of Contents - Options to see if Table entry fields is checked... In order for the marked text to be included in the TOC there must be a check in that box. TC fields can be used in place of or in addition to Styles for identifying what should appear in the TOC.

john mcghie say:
Since Mr. Jones has punctured a very pleasant nap that I was having, let's go for the complex issue:

You have a Bullet formatting applied to the TOC styles.

The period is not in the text, it is being generated by the style.

Go to Format>Style>TOC 1>Modify>Format>Numbering...

You will find that this particular format has been set in the "Outline Numbered" tab, not in the "Bullets" tab.  This is a little confusing, but "Bullets" and "Numbering" are the same thing to Word.  Outline Numbering is the only place where you can set more than one character as part of your Bullet format.

The alignment problem is caused by the tab being set to far to the left (!).  Word needs two tabs in the paragraph, one to align the text, one to align the page number.

Choose the format that has the Globe in it, and click Customise.

Now look in "Number Format".  Chances are you will see a period after the globe.  Remove it.  While you are in there, consider the setting of the "Text Position" -- "Add tab stop at" field.  Chances are, now you have removed the period, you won't have to chance that.

I suggest that what was happening was that the period was pushing the text to the right of the tab setting, so the text was aligning on the right-quad tab that was intended to align the page numbers.  You could increase the setting a little if you like, to give you some safety room.

The FONT of the bullet character becomes a little critical if you run into a computer that does not have that font installed.  The Globe is indeed in Webdings, character F0FE (224 in Decimal).  The problem is that its up in the high-order part of Unicode where the font foundries tend to behave very badly: what is a globe in one font can be something else entirely in a different font.

Under Unicode, font substitution is automatic: if the computer doesn't have the font, it will "fall through" to the next most similar font it does have.  But in the high-order encodings of that substituted font, character 0224 may not be a globe.

If that's what has happened, you may have to go delete the globe also, and re-add it as a "New Bullet" making sure you specify the Webdings font.

You will notice when you go into Modify Style that the TOC series of Styles are set to "Automatically update".  That's the default setting which enables Word to change the style definition to produce its different TOC formats.  In your case, you should disable that for TOC 1 through 9, so users futzing around can't do this to you again.

I think that should solve your issue...

I'll go back to sleep now.  Any further issues, don't hesitate to ask --  Bob! :-)

Hi IanRiches,

 

Please do the following steps to minimize or to stop receiving unwanted emails.

 

A. Scheduled Sweep - automatically lets you keep only the latest mail from a sender or have their emails be moved to deleted folder after 30, 60, or 90 days.

1. Open the email you would like to filter.
2. Click Scheduled Clean-up beside sender's name
3. On the pop-up window, choose your preferred option
4. Click OK.

B. Rules - we can create a rule that can block a specific word from the subject of the spam email.

1. Sign in to your Microsoft account. Go to Inbox.
2. Click on Gear icon then More mail options... at the upper right corner of the page.
3. Under the Customizing Outlook section, click on Rules for sorting new messages.
4. Click on the New button.
5. Under Step 1: Which messages do you want this rule to apply to, 
    a. First drop-down menu - choose Subject
    b. Second drop-down menu - choose contains
    c. Third field - input the word you would like to block
6. Under Step 2: What action do you want to apply, click on Delete these messages
7. Click Save button.

C. Sweep - This will allow all received or even future emails coming from the specific sender be deleted.

1. Sign in to your Microsoft account. Go to Inbox.
2. Check the specific sender you would like to filter.
3. On the upper part, click Sweep
4. Choose Delete all from
5. On the pop-up box, just click Delete all.

Note: You can also block future messages from that sender by checking the box beside Also block future messages.

 

 

We also recommend that you follow these guidelines to help reduce junk e-mail:

 

* Don't reply to junk e-mail, even to ask to be removed from the sender's mailing list. The sender may simply use that response to confirm that your e-mail address is valid, and continue to send you junk e-mail. To report the message as junk e-mail, select the message, click Junk.

 

* To report the e-mail message as a suspected phishing scam (Falsely claiming to be a legitimate business to send you to a fake website or scam you into giving out private information.), click Mark as - Phishing scam.

 

* Remove your e-mail address from any web page, newsgroup, or bulletin board available on the Internet.

 

* To remove your e-mail address from newsletters or mailing lists that are on your safe senders list, you can click Unsubscribe. Microsoft notifies the people who created the newsletter to stop sending it to you. 

 

Note: If you receive junk e-mail that looks as if it was sent from your Outlook.com account, someone may have spoofed (Sending messages with fake or stolen account information) your account information.

 

If you have any question or clarification, don't hesitate to reply.
