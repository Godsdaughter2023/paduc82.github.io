jim aksel say:

Sorry for the bump in for my peers...

What is a best practice?  Use as many baselines as you feel are required to accurrately display the progress of your project. Generally you only use one baseline at a time.  However, you may want to hold your team to one internal baseline and have a contractual baseline of something a little bit different (that's what we do).

Actually, there are no special formulas required-- use of Number1, etc is one method and is used if you need to display variances against multiple baselines simultaneously. Here is another choice:  Project cacluates the variance fields (ie: Start Variance, Finish Variance, Duration Variance, Cost Variance.....) against the baseline that is used for Earned Value calculations. By variance field, I mean any field with a name that starts with "variance"

Example:  I have a task that is 3 days in duration.  I set a baseline.  At this point finish variance is 0 days.  I decide the task will take 5 days, finish variance become +2 days.  I convince management to allow me a baseline change becuase of the change in scope and increased effort.  So, I save a new baseline to Baseline1 and my task now has Duration=Baseline1 Duration = 5 days.  However, my finish variance is still +2 Days.

Since I was authorized a change to my baseline, I switch my earned value calculations to use Baseline1 as my basis for EV calculations.  I do this as Tools/Options... pull the Calculation tab and then click on the "Earned Value" button.  In the Earned Value dialog box, change the reference baseline from Basline to Baseline1 and click OK.  Now you will see your [Finish Variance]=0 days.  You decide the task will actually take  6 days instead of 5.  [Finish Variance]=+1 day (with respect to Baseline1).  If you switch your EV baseline back to "Baseline" from "Baseline1" then [Finish Variance] changes to [Finish]-[Baseline Finish]= +3 days  (the original baseline is 3 days after start and the current task finish is schedule for 6 days after start).

Subtle, but useful.  What we do is hold an internal baseline to what our people have estimated and save that as Baseline1.  We then use a schedule risk analysis to establish longer task durations and use these durationas as Baseline.  We measure our EV against Baseline and submit that to our customer.  Internally we "beat each other up" to Baseline1 as it shows a tighter delivery.

greg maxey say:

Sub ScratchMacro()
'A basic Word macro coded by Greg Maxey
Dim oFF As FormField
For Each oFF In ActiveDocument.Range.FormFields
  Select Case oFF.Name
    Case "ProjectNumber", "StartDate"

       'Do nothing. Answer 1.

    Case Else

      'Answer 2

      Select Case oFF.Type
        Case 70: oFF.Result = vbNullString
        Case 83: oFF.DropDown.Value = 1
        Case 71: oFF.CheckBox.Value = False
      End Select
  End Select
Next oFF

'Answer 3

ActiveDocument.Fields.Update
End Sub

jay freedman say:

It seems you might have a form field that's reporting a Type value that isn't one of the three cases. That shouldn't be possible -- there are only three types of FormFields -- but the symptom is that of a Select Case that doesn't know what to do.

The quick fix is to insert another Case Else statement between the "Case 71" line and the "End Select". That might leave one or more form fields still filled; you could delete those fields from the document and reinsert them to see if that helps.

The more definite solution is to find out what VBA thinks the field is. When you stop the macro and click Debug, select the expression oFF.Type, right-click it, and click Add to Watch. Don't change anything in the dialog box that appears, but just press Enter. A window will open at the bottom of the VBA editor, and it will show the current value of oFF.Type. If that value isn't 70, 71, or 83, what is it?
