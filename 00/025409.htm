bob jones aka: cybertaz say:
Just a more 'general' observation, FWIW :-) Toolbars as you've known them in the past are on the way out... Both the Windows OS & the Mac OS are trending away from them altogether. Right now, though, we're going through a transition period where alternatives such as Palettes, tabbed Ribbons & other contextual control panels are being explored. IMO, many software developers are either unable/reluctant/resistant to make the radical & abrupt UI change that occurred with the introduction of the Ribbon in Office 2007 for Windows.

What is currently offered in Office 2011 is a prime example of that transitional situation. MacBU has struggled to comply with the new age demands Jim referred to while attempting to hold on to as many elements of the "Classic" UI as they could. For better or worse, that's resulted in a potpourri intended to soften the blow for their Mac-oriented clients. I have absolutely no factual information on which to base my expectation, but I wouldn't be surprised to see toolbars removed altogether in the next release of Mac Office. Instead, the Ribbon would be the main control panel but would have to provide a full range of customization by the user. IMHO, if that level of customization can be delivered in 'Mac Office Next' toolbars - floating as well as docked - either will be gone or constrained to a single bar locked into the document window.

BTW: Just in case Jim's point wasn't clear :-) You cannot dock a toolbar in a document window - which is what I believe you might prefer to do. The 'docking' Jim suggested has to be outside the document window at the bottom edge of the main Menu Bar. Or, if the custom floating bar is stretched to be vertical it can also be docked on either the left or right edge of the screen. Also, if you position the OS X Dock on the left or right you can dock the floating toolbar horizontally at the bottom of the screen.

There is no single "right answer".  But generally, you should include in Net Profit any costs associated with the business operation.  I do not understand the difference between "borrower's investment" and "lender's investment" [1].  But if "Loan Payments" are for funding operations that generate revenue, I would use "Net Profit After Loan Payments" for the numerator.

Some other ancillary comments, if I may.

1. I would use simply NetProfit / Investment, formatted as Percentage.  Do not multiply by 100.

Multiplying by 100 represents the true percentage (e.g. 0.3456 or 34.56%) as a "number" (34.56).  My terminology is poor here; but the point is:  the latter is difficult to use in any dependent calculations.

2. There are many ways to calculate ROI.  The formula above is the simplest and most common; and it is appropriate for a single year.  Nothing wrong with it.  Just a head's up that the customer might have something else in mind, or that others might suggest a different formula.

It is debatable, for example, whether the "average ROI over several years" should be a simple average of the annual ROIs calculated as above, or a geometric average.  Again, there is no "right answer".  It depends on the statistic that the customer wants to see.  Both have their purposes.

The code works for me, provided that

  1. I copied something to the clipboard before running the macro, and
  2. That text occurs on the active sheet.

If the text from the clipboard doesn't occur, you'd get an error message. Here is a way to avoid that, and to display a custom message:

    Dim txt As String
    Dim find As MSForms.DataObject
    Dim rng As Range
    Set find = New MSForms.DataObject
    find.GetFromClipboard
    txt = find.GetText
    Set rng = Cells.find(txt, ActiveCell, xlFormulas, xlPart, xlByRows, xlNext, False, False)
    If rng Is Nothing Then
        MsgBox "Text not found!"
    Else
        rng.Activate
    End If

Should do it. Just expland the array to include other sheets.

Option Explicit
Sub GetDataFromShtsSAS()
Dim ds As Worksheet
Dim s As Variant
Dim slr As Long
Dim dlr As Long

Set ds = Sheets("Prior Week Detail")
ds.UsedRange.Clear

For Each s In Array("543", "546")

      Columns.Count), , , , xlPrevious).Row + 1
