pranavmishra5582 say:

Hi brucefl,

 

Thank you for opting for Windows 8.

 


You can transfer your files between two computers using a Homegroup network.

 

A homegroup makes it easier to share files and printers on a home network. You can share pictures, music, videos, documents, and printers with other people in your homegroup. Other people can't change the files that you share, unless you give them permission to do so.

 

You can prevent specific files or folders from being shared, and you can share additional libraries later. You can help protect your homegroup with a password, which you can change at any time.

 

Please follow the steps given below to create a home group:

 

a) From the Control Panel settings click HomeGroup. Then in the left panel under Libraries and Devices, switch on the types of files and devices you want to share.
b) Under Membership, a password is automatically generated for you. You’ll need this to connect your other Windows 7 computer.
c) You can change the password to something easier to remember — which will make things easier when joining other systems. Press Win Key + W to bring up the Search box for Settings. Type: network and sharing then click on Network and Sharing Center from the results field on the left.
d) The Network and Sharing Center window opens on the Desktop. On the left side click HomeGroup under See Also.
e) Now under Other Home Group Actions click Change the Password.
f) Make sure any other machines that might be already joined are powered off or sleeping, then click Change the Password.
g) Here you can type in your own password that’s easier to remember. Click Finish.
h) After a few seconds, you’ll get the following screen letting you know the Homegroup password has been successfully change. Click Finish and close out of the remaining open screens.

 

Join Windows 7 to Windows 8 HomeGroup

 


a) Now go to the other machines running Windows 7 to join the Homegroup. Here I am using a Windows 7. Click Start and type: homegroup into the Search box and hit Enter.
b) On the next screen click on the Join Now button.
c) Then decide which files and folder you want to share on the Windows 8 HomeGroup. Click Next.
d) Enter in the password and click Next.
e) Now you’re successfully connected to the Windows 8 Homegroup. Click Finish.
f) Now you can easily share document, files and printers between the Windows 8 and Windows 7 computers over your home network.

You've got a couple of misconceptions there. Firstly, RAM being used is not a bad thing. RAM is meant to be used. If there is not enough of it then Windows will use harddrive space as virtual RAM and put the program data from apps which aren't currently active into the virtual RAM to free up real RAM for the apps that actively need it.

 The second thing is that it does not matter at all which Windows version gets upgraded to Windows 10. Once Windows 10 is installed there is nothing left of the old operating system. This computer I am on right now went straight from Windows 7 to Windows 8 to Windows 8.1 to Windows 10, one OS right after another on the same day.

What is a frequent cause of problems during and after upgrading comes from all the other stuff that is pre-installed on the computer on top of the original operating system. In an upgrade install most of these things are carried over into Windows 10 but they are not always fully compatible with Windows 10. Often the Windows pre-installation checks will give warnings that this or that software or driver or even hardware device should  be uninstalled before upgrading. The hook is that only products which have already been tested for compatibility can generate these warnings but there are many more where compatibility with the new OS is unknown. That's one of the reasons why a "clean install" is so frequently recommended in these forums, to eliminate the unknown incompatibility factors as well as any known compatibility problems.

So now for the rest of the story.  The root cause ended up being a CryptoLocker (new 2.0 TorrentLocker variant).  The reason my email looked "corrupt" was because it was encrypted.  That is why when I got a NEW email it looked fine for a while (it later was encrypted like the rest).

It started by encrypting my WindowsLiveMail storage folders and moved on from there through my other "AppData" and then on to MyDocuments.  I was fortunate to #1 realize I had some bad malware, #2 keep my PC off while I wasn't able to be actively investigating things, #3 stay off the internet and/or in safe mode, #4 buy the premium version of Malwarebytes that monitors continuously.

#4 ended up being the big clue as it started blocking dllhost.exe to some various specific web pages.  Quick google search lead to the malwarebytes forum where a very kind expert guided me through removal.

The other big break was that while the code encrypted up through MyDocuments starting with "A-L", I was able to use Shadow Restore to get back all but a handful of documents.  My old emails were lost for good as I decided not to pay the $500 requested to decrypt them (yes- you read that right - $500).
