arminatorx say:

The problem with this is: Since your account is an adult account, even if you could put time restrictions on that, your kid using the account would be regarded as an adult thanks to the adult account, and the kid could just go to the account settings themselves and revert the changes. 

That's why child accounts can't make such settings and require a separate adult account to do it. 

Let's make a real world analogy:

You as a parent could go to a bank and tell the cashier that your kid has a "child" account at their bank, and you want to limit the ability to withdraw money to 100$ per month. 

Since the kid is unable to sign contracts and stuff, if the kid goes to the bank and says "I want the 100$ limit lifted because I want to buy Disneyland!", the cashier will say "Sorry kid, due to your age you can't make or change contracts. Get your parent here to lift the limit."

If you as an adult go to the bank and say "I want to limit my spending to 100$ per month", and a few days later come back and say "I as an adult want to buy a boat, a house and a new car, so lift my 100$ spending limit", the bank will comply as you are the adult. 

So unfortunately, you can either leave the kid unable to be restricted, or create a new child account where the kid can then be restricted, but loses their game progress. 

Also be aware: The older the kid gets, the more they want their own account. And the more YOU want them to have their own account, as the older kid probably then could see your emails and use other Microsoft Account services on your account. 

So personally, I can only recommend to create a new account for the kid, and have them replay all the fun games again. With time restrictions, if needed.

  • Step 4: How to get the <id> for the next section:

Supplementary information  from the site:

Occasionally you may need play with different TAP-driver versions. In this case you can use devcon.exe from the Windows command-prompt. Below are some common commands you can run from an administrator Powershell or cmd.exe console:

List available TAP-Windows adapters:

$ openvpn --show-adapters
Available TAP-Windows devices:
[1] 'my-tap'

List network interfaces (incl. TAP-Windows adapters):

$ ipconfig

View information about an installed driver:

> tapinstall.exe hwids <id>
ROOT\NET\0000
    Name: TAP-Windows Adapter V9
    Hardware IDs:
        tap0901
1 matching device(s) found.

Uninstall a TAP-driver:

> tapinstall.exe remove <id>
ROOT\NET\0000
1 device(s) were removed

To verify TAP-driver removal:

> tapinstall.exe hwids <id>
No matching devices found.

Install a new TAP-driver:

> tapinstall.exe install <something.inf> <id>
Device node created. Install is complete when drivers are installed...
Updating drivers for <id> from C:\Program Files\TAP-Windows\driver\OemVista.inf.
Drivers installed successfully.

Update a TAP-driver:

> tapinstall.exe update <something.inf> <id>
Updating drivers for <id> from C:\Program Files\TAP-Windows\driver\OemVista.inf.
Drivers installed successfully.

Notes:

  • <id> refers to the driver identifier which is tap0901 for OpenVPN 2.2+, but may be different in older/newer OpenVPN versions.
  • <something.inf> is typically OemWin2k.inf (old tap-drivers) or OemVista.inf (newer tap-drivers). You need to specify the full path to this file, e.g.C:\Program Files\TAP-Windows\driver\OemVista.inf.

By installing multiple times, you will create additional TAP-Windows adapter instances, which can be used for multiple concurrent VPN tunnels. It is also possible to install using Control Panel -> Add New Hardware, and it is possible to uninstall using Control Panel -> System -> Hardware -> Device Manager.
