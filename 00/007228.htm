Creating outlines using multilevel list feature - Microsoft Community

nuala7 say:

Creating outlines using multilevel list feature

When I was in law school using Word 2007, I did a ton of outlines.  I would just sit down, select the outline format I wanted from Bullets and Numbering, and go to town.  Word 2007 realized I wanted my outline to last the entire document, and when I hit "return" it would automatically go from "I." to "II." or "A." to "B."  And if I wanted to switch from the outline format to the regular paragraph format, I would hit "return" twice and Word knew to drop out of the numbering.  It was awesome.  Now that I've "upgraded" to 2010, this feature seems to be broken for no reason.  Whenever I hit "return" to go from "A." to "B." in the outline, Word reverts back to the normal paragraph style without numbers.  I know I can click on the Quick Headings bar or hit Ctrl-Alt 1 to get back into the outline, but this extra step takes too much time.  Can someone please tell me how to make "return" mean go to "B" rather than kick me out of the outline?  Thanks.   

suzanne s. barnhill say:
The list you selected was probably one in which the levels are linked to the
built-in heading styles (in the gallery, note that the only I, A, 1 style
says I. Heading 1, A. Heading 2, 1. Heading 3). The "style for following
paragraph" for all heading styles is Normal, so when you press Enter at the
end of a heading, you get a Normal paragraph. There are three ways around
this:
 
1. Work in Outline view. In that view, you will get a new paragraph at the
same level when you press Enter.
 
2. In the Modify Style dialog, change the "Style for following paragraph"
for each heading style to the same style instead of Normal.
 
3. Use Define New Multilevel List to link the multilevel list levels to
different styles, such as List Number, List Number 2, List Number 3, etc.
 
If you are making an outline that will not include Body Text below the
heading level, then I would strongly recommend option 3. The reason for this
is that all heading styles by default include "Keep with next" formatting.
This means that Word will try to keep your outline together in a single
block because every paragraph wants to stay with the following one. With no
breaks, the document will be unstable, and if you ever do break out of a
heading style, you'll get a page break at that point, possibly leaving most
of a page blank.
 
If you're setting up an outline first and will later go back and fill in
body text under the headings, then I recommend Option 1. I don't really
recommend Option 2 in any situation.
