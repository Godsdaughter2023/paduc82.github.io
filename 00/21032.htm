ken sheridan say: You might like to take a look at RowNumbering.zip in my public databases folder at:

Note that if you are using an earlier version of Access you might find that the colour of some form objects such as buttons shows incorrectly and you will need to  amend the form design accordingly.

This little demo file illustrates a number of queries for both numbering and ranking rows.  It uses simple financial transactions for the examples, but think of customers as analogous to your students and transaction amounts to their scores.  It doesn't include a query for ranking averages, but one can easily be created by joining two subqueries as follows:

SELECT T1.CustomerID, T1.AverageAmount,
COUNT(T2.CustomerID)+1 AS Rank
FROM  (SELECT CustomerID, AVG(TransactionAmount) AS AverageAmount
              FROM Transactions
              GROUP BY CustomerID) AS T1
LEFT JOIN (SELECT CustomerID, AVG(TransactionAmount) AS AverageAmount
              FROM Transactions
              GROUP BY CustomerID) AS T2
ON T2.AverageAmount>T1.AverageAmount
GROUP BY T1.CustomerID, T1.AverageAmount
ORDER BY T1.AverageAmount DESC;

You'll probably find that there are no ties, but if you amend the amounts in the Transactions table so that a couple of customers tie for first or second place you'll see how it correctly ranks them and other customers in the event of a tie.

ken sheridan say:

But the author would still need to define HOW they want to break ties. There is no way it's a one-size-fits-all process.

Why would they want to 'break' ties?  In statistics rank is defined as a position in a numerically ordered series.  If two values in the series occupy the same position in the series, they have the same rank.  The descending series 8,6,4,4,2 for instance has ranks 1,2,3,3,5.  This is how the 'problem' of ties is 'solved'.

Sequentially numbering a sorted series is not ranking, and requires an arbitrary determinant in the event of a tie.  My demo includes methodologies for that also, using an autonumber key as the determinant.  In the current context 'breaking' ties between two or more students of equal attainment  in such a way would not only be statistically incorrect, but manifestly unfair.

scott mcdaniel say:

In the current context 'breaking' ties between two or more students of equal attainment  in such a way would not only be statistically incorrect, but manifestly unfair.

But we don't know the context. While I grant that the concept of grading is generally a single-root data point, it may not be. Business rules may define how the "ranking" is determined, which may include root elements that would be used to calculate the final data element used for the ranking. If there is some method to rank some "root" elements higher than others - or give some more weight than others - then the ranking system could certainly take those into account when determining ties, since you could potentially arrive at the same final ranking through different combinations of the root elements, and if those elements are weighted, the scores with the higher count or "weighted" elements would win.

While breaking ties might not fit into the theoretical context of ranking, it most certainly fits into the real-world usage.

kay_c. say:

Hi breconbeaconpeaks,

 

Welcome to Microsoft Community.


I know how important it is to receive emails and I am here to assist you. We are glad you contacted us so that we can find solutions and fix this issue immediately.


It is possible that your incoming emails are triggered by Junk mail filters. I suggest that you check and modify your Junk mail filters for us to make sure that it is not contributing to this issue:

Check your Junk folder
1. Sign in to Outlook.com.
2. Click the "Junk" folder on the left pane.
3. Check to see if the wanted emails are in this folder.
4. If you see any messages that should have been delivered to the Inbox folder, click the box on the left of each message to put a check mark and then click the "Not Junk" button.

Check your Blocked sender list
1. In your Outlook.com Inbox, click the cog icon beside your Profile name.
2. Click "More mail settings".
3. Under Preventing junk email, click "Safe and blocked senders".
4. Click "Blocked senders".
5. In the Blocked senders list, select the name or domain (the part of an e-mail address that follows the @ sign, such as hotmail.com) that you now want to receive mail from, and then click "<< Remove from list".

Add the email address to your Safe senders list
1. In your Outlook.com Inbox, click the cog icon beside your Profile name.
2. Click "More mail settings".
3. Under Preventing junk email, click "Safe and blocked senders".
4. Click "Safe Senders".
5. In the text box, type in the email address or domain you’d like to always receive email from, and then click "Add to list >>".

Check your Email Rules
1. In your Outlook.com Inbox, click the cog icon beside your Profile name.
2. Click "More mail settings".
3. Under Customizing Outlook, click "Rules for sorting new messages".
4. Perform one of the actions as per your requirement:
Click "Edit" next to the rule that you want to edit.
Or
Choose the rule that you want to delete by ticking the check box and click "Delete".

Note: Not all international characters are supported as filters.

Check your Junk Mail Settings
1. In your Outlook.com Inbox, click the cog icon beside your Profile name.
2. Click "More mail settings".
3. Under Preventing junk email, click "Filters and reporting".
4. Under Choose a junk e-mail filter, choose the level of protection that you want, and then click "Save". If the Exclusive level was set, messages from senders not on your Contacts or Safe senders lists will have been sent to the Junk e-mail folder.

Check your Reply-to address
1. In your Outlook.com Inbox, click the cog icon beside your Profile name.
2. Click "More mail settings".
3. Under Writing email, click "Reply-to address".
2. Select "Current e-mail address" radio button.
3. Click "Save".

Check Email Forwarding
1. In your Outlook.com Inbox, click the cog icon beside your Profile name.
2. Click "More mail settings".
3. Under Managing your account, click "Email Forwarding".
4. Choose Don't forward, if you have enabled email forwarding for you to receive incoming emails to your Inbox Folder.
5. Click "Save".

Once done, kindly check if you can already receive incoming emails from Groupon.


Get back to us with the results.

 

Thanks,
Kay
