No, I do not believe that web page describes the math.

As I explained before, according to Excel Help, YIELD uses the PRICE formula to solve for "yld" using the "Newton" method (aka Newton-Raphson method). [1]

And as I explained before, see http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_method to learn about Newton-Raphson.

Yatie wrote:

what is the math formula to proof excel calculation 3.426 as my example above.


Alternatively, note that the yield to maturity is the IRR of the cash flows.

So we might use the Excel RATE, IRR or XIRR function to convince ourselves that the YIELD function is returning the correct value.

Here is how....

Yatie wrote previously:

Face value - 100
Price - 103.05
Coupon - 4.012%
Freaquency - semiannual
Yrs to maturity -  5.80
Excel calculation for yield show: 3.426%
(using YIELD(settlement,maturity,rate,pr,redemption,frequency,basis)


When you ask a question about Excel usage, it is important to show us how you are using Excel -- the
You neglect to tell us the actual settlement and maturity dates that you used, as well as the
5.8 years is an odd number of "years to maturity".  0.8 years is about 41 weeks and 4.2 days(!). [2]

I will assume you mean 5 years 41 weeks, since then the result from YIELD is closest to 3.426%.  Also, I will assume a 30/360 basis.

With those assumptions, YIELD results in about 3.4259%.  My set-up is as follows:



B2 contains the formula =EDATE(B1,5*12)+41*7.

C2 contains the formula =YIELD(B1,B2,B3,B4,B5,B6,0).

The cash flows are set up in E1:E13 (dates) and F1:F13 (amounts).

The formula in E14 is =EDATE(E12,6), the next coupon date after the redemption date.  The formula in F2:F12 is
=100 * $B$3 / $B$6.  The formula in F13 is =B5 + 100*B3/B6*(E13-E12)/(E14-E12).

G1 contains the formula =XIRR(F1:F13,E1:E13).

Note that the XIRR result is significantly different:  3.4544% instead of 3.4259%.

In part, that is because XIRR assumes compounded interest, whereas semiannual coupons are computed using simple interest.  That is, the annual rate is divided by 2.

H1 attempts to adjust for that with the formula =2*(SQRT(1+G1)-1).

Note that the result is "close":  3.4251% instead of 3.4259%.  There are many possible reasons for the difference.   But hopefully that is close enough to demonstrate the math and "prove" the YIELD result.

[2] [EDIT] Arguably, 0.8 years is 41 weeks and 5 or 5.2 days.  I rely on the US Truth in Lending law, which specifies that a year consists of 52 weeks, not 365/7 or 365.25/7.  But I cannot say with impunity that that law applies to US bonds, much less to whatever bonds Yatie is talking about.
