lemp say:
That's a difficult call.  Mostly, these scammers are just interested in charging you to fix your non-existent problems and then selling you an expensive "service contract" rather than actually stealing information from you. 

On the other hand, you did let someone have full control over your computer, and although you probably could see everything that was being done, there might well have been activity going on in the background copying your files.  Usually, however, all they do is look at Event Viewer where they can amaze you by pointing out "errors" and "warnings" (which virtually every Windows computer will show in Event Viewer) and thus convince you to purchase their "repair" services.

At a minimum, I suggest changing all of your userid/password logon information for whatever sites you visit, especially for any banking or credit card sites.  If you're really concerned and you keep financial information on your computer, you might want to act as if your wallet including your credit cards was lost or stolen:  contact your financial institutions and change your account information.

IMO, it's unlikely that any "backdoor" or other malware was left behind from this event, but if you are concerned about that, really the only way to be sure is to do a complete "clean" install of Windows.  For most people who have computers that were purchased with Windows pre-installed, this means (a) backing up your personal files, (2) restoring the computer to its "fresh from the factory" condition using whatever mechanism for doing so provided by the manufacturer; (3) tediously updating Windows to the current service pack and post-service pack updates; (4) reinstalling all programs that were installed after you bought the computer (using original installation media) and then updating those programs as necessary; and (5) restoring your personal files.

        You can confirm this by pressing the "Windows logo" key and the "Pause/Break" key simultaneously (or right click My Computer and select "Properties").  At the bottom of the "System" window that opens, you'll see your "Product ID."  Look at the 3 characters after the first 5.  If they are anything other than "OEM" you may be OK; what are those 3 characters?

        Microsoft's license for OEM versions of Windows "is permanently assigned to the computer with which the software is distributed. That computer is the 'licensed computer.'" (Paragraph 5 of OEM EULA).  Although you may see articles suggesting otherwise, AFAIK, it is still Microsoft's position that installing a new motherboard -- unless it is a replacement because the original was defective and is the same make/model as the original -- results in a "new computer" which is thus no longer licensed.

        From Microsoft's Licensing FAQ (emphasis added):



        Q. If my customer asks me to upgrade a PC with new hardware components, when is a new operating system needed? When would the PC be considered "new"?

        A. Generally, an end user can upgrade or replace all of the hardware components on a computer—except the motherboard—and still retain the license for the original Microsoft OEM operating system software. If the motherboard is upgraded or replaced for reasons other than a defect, then a new computer has been created. Microsoft OEM operating system software cannot be transferred to the new computer, and the license of new operating system software is required.

        If the motherboard is replaced because it is defective, you do not need to acquire a new operating system license for the PC as long as the replacement motherboard is the same make/model or the same manufacturer's replacement/equivalent, as defined by the manufacturer's warranty.

        Bottom line:  you probably can't legally do what you are proposing.

        a. user say:

        Hi Brandon,

        Thank you for posting in Microsoft Community.

        I understand your concern and frustration you have been through. I will be glad to assist you.

        By default, Windows provides the option to format a USB flash drive with FAT or FAT32 file systems only, but not with NTFS (New Technology File System.)

        The reason behind this is that there are some disadvantages of NTFS usage in this case. First of all, the speed of saving on the flash drive decreases (but the speed of reading data from the drive is the same), and you have to be more careful to always remove the USB flash drive safely.

        However, you can still format the Flash Drive in the NTFS format. Follow these steps for the same.

        1.      Insert the USB flash drive into the USB port.
        2.      Press Windows Key + R, type devmgmt.msc in the Search textbox and press  Enter.
        3.      Find Disk Drives and expand it.
        4.      Find the USB flash drive you want to format, and double-click it. It opens the  Properties dialog box.
        5.      Click Policies tab.
        6.      By default, the  Optimize for quick removal option selected, switch it to Optimize for performance and then pressOK.
        7.      Now open  Computer. Right click on the USB Flash Drive and select Format. The Format dialog box now appears.
        8.      Select NTFS in the File System field.
        9.      Press Start and wait while the process will be completed.
        10.   Now your USB Flash Drive is formatted with NTFS format.

         If you wish to format external hard drive in exFAT follow the steps below.
        1. Right click on start and select Command Prompt (Admin).
        2. Type format g: /FS:exFAT and then Enter to begin formatting the G drive as exFAT

        Hope it helps. Do let us know if your issue is resolved. For any further query, feel free to contact us.

        Thank you.
