adam vero say:

If I understand your problem description properly it sounds like you want three independent horizontal axes, which you can't do, but I just want to take a step back and see what you are trying to really achieve because it might be that you are trying to go about this the wrong way.

Ignoring the "data labels" for the moment (I'm not sure if you really mean data labels or the labels of categories on the axis anyway), are you trying to show 12 columns for Jan to Dec  and then have two lines superimposed on top of that

Do you want those lines to extend across the full width of the chart or do you want each line to only show up for part of the chart (eg line 1 from March to June, line 2 from July to December after you changed a target or something)?

Showing the lines across the whole width of the chart when they represent different months from the columns would be hugely misleading - the whole point of plotting things together on a chart is to draw conclusions or see patterns from where things correspond to one another. I'm going to assume you therefore want to achieve the second thing, to have two different lines for different parts of the year.

The simplest way to achieve this would be to include all twelve months for the data for the lines, but for the months which you do not want to be plotted don't have a number, nor a blank cell, but use the function =NA() to give a #NA() error result. These will never be plotted , thus leaving some gaps in your line (or at either side of it, in your example of March to June). These points will not exist, and therefore would also not show data labels if you did turn labels on.

All three of your series could then simply share a single range for their category labels.

Another way to alter the ranges used by your series is by selecting each series then looking at the coloured rectangles around the data areas and dragging the handles at the corners of the values area and the categpry labels area. By doing this (rather than using the select data dialog) I was able to produce something which sounds like what you are attempting, although as I said earlier, plotting things on a chart without a common horizontal axis is probably going to end up being misleading, so I hope this is not what you want.

joeu2004 say: Bill wrote:

Abosulute is not desired  so I used this modification  Address(False,False,,True) and miss the double comma ,, the i change it and it worked.  But for future understanding what does THAT DO?


In VBA, put the cursor on the word Address, then press F1 to look at the help page.

You can pass VBA parameters by position, like most procedural programming languages, or by name.

If you look at the help page, you will see that the 1st, 2nd and 4th arguments correspond to the parameters RowAbsolute, ColumnAbsolute and External.  These are booleans -- True or False.

By default, External is False.  Setting it to True returns the address with the file and worksheet name.  Excel ignores the file name when it is the same the cell where the formula is stored.

By default, RowAbsolute and ColumnAbsolute are True, which is why Address was returning absolute references.  Setting them both to False returns relative references.

I cannot imagine why it would matter to you, unless you intend to copy the formula down the column.  But if that's the case, you might as well allow the macro to do the work in one step.

I thought you might overlook the double comma, which takes the default for the 3rd parameter.  That is one reason why I provided the by-name alternative.  Also, some people prefer the self-documenting style.
