scottgem say:

I would use:

tblBorrower: BorrowerID (PK Autonumber), name, address and contact info

tblAccount: AccountID (PK Autonumber), BorrowerID (FK), AccountNumber, LenderID (FK), OriginalBalance, etc.

tblCollection: CollectionID (PK Autonumber), BorrowerID (FK), CollectionDate, CollectionAmount

tblPayment: PaymentID, PK Autonumber, CollectionID (FK), AccountID (FK), PaymentAmount

tblInterest: InterestID (PK Autonumber), AccountID (FK), InterestRate, InterestAmount, EffectiveDate

tluLender: LenderID (PK Autonumber) Lender, contact info.

Your customers are the lenders you are collecting for, that's why I would use the term Borrower for those you are collecting from. 

If the Borrower has multiple accounts, they may make one payment which could be spread among each account. That's why I have the Payments table as a separate table. Also you don't mention how and when interests accrues. That's why I have the separate interest table. So you can run an interest accrual.

Balances are calculated, not stored. You would calculate the Balance using a query that Sums all the payments and interest accruals and then adds the interest to the original balance and subtracts the payments. 

Finally, you would have a main form bound to tblBorrower and 3 subforms (on a Tab Control), 1 for Accounts, the other for payments and the third for Interest.
