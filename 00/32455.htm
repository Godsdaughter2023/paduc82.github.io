As for your question, there's no excellent answer.  YouTube has always gone to "true full screen" when you go full screen on a PC web browser.  So what you're seeing is the expected behavior (just not desired).

The only option I can think of is to use the "snap" function. Start your YouTube video and then drag the browser to the left side of the screen until your pointer arrow "bumps" the left edge, and when you let go it will snap to fill the entire left half of the screen.  Then drag your Autodesk program to the right side to do the same.

Once they are both snapped, their shared border between them will be sticky, making it easier to resize both at once.  The YouTube video will be scaled to stay at the proper size ratio (it won't become stretched out tall like silly putty or anything), so unfortunately for you in this case, you'll still be able to see things on the youtube page like comments under the video.

dax1792 say:

An Administrator account has two logon sessions. Normally they run in a session that only has standard user privileges. When they need administrator privileges, when installing a program, for example, you get a UAC prompt (depending on the level set) and switch into the session with administrator privileges. Mapped drives are only associated with the session they are created in. So, when there is a switch to the elevated session, the mapped drives are no longer visible.

 

Aaron Margosis demonstrates this about 53 minutes into this presentation.

http://channel9.msdn.com/Events/TechEd/NorthAmerica/2012/SIA311

You might want to back off to 48 minutes if you are not familiar with the concepts.

        dumbo knowall
        I'm assuming you're using hardware RAID...
        The RAID should be created and managed mostly by hardware and firmware - not the OS. If it's done  properly, the OS hardly knows the RAID exists, seeing a single drive rather than 2 separate ones.
         
        You don't need to use DISKPART - you can do most of what you need to do inside Win 7 - stretch/shrink/delete/create partitions are all available at last (and IIRC they are also available during the install) .
        That doesn't alter the fact that you can force an install of Win7 into a single partition, rather than having two partitions used by W7, by making sure that there's no unallocated space on the drive.
        Since it appears that you also want it to be the second OS on the system - sharing a RAID (which I consider to be a lethal combination, but it's your choice!), if you want to avoid cross-contamination of boot files, you'll need to hide the other OS during the install as well. That's not so easy to do<g> - and you'll then need to install a third-party boot manager.
        If your happy with the changes that Win7 will make to your other OS's files, then it's not a problem, and you can use the Windows Boot Manager.
         
        My personal preference would be to use at least three disks - one for OS's the others as RAID storage. There are very few advantages to having the OS on a RAID, and many disadvantages.
