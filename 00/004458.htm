john w. vinson say:

Perhaps you could step back a bit and tell us what real-life situation you want your database to model. Database tables don't use "words" as units, but rather characters; each character is (basically) one byte. So "An" is one word and two bytes; "Supercalifragilisticexpialidocious" is one word but 34 bytes.

As a rule, any table with 100 fields is very, very likely to be incorrectly designed. In fact, 20 fields is (in most cases) a very wide table which needs to me reexamined. Each Field (in the Codd-Date relational model, which has been working very well for some forty years) should be one distinct Attribute of whatever Entity (real-life person, thing or event) is modeled by the Record in the table; if your table design has lots of fields all of which are instances of one Property, you need to restructure your table!

bob jones aka: cybertaz say:

Well, technically, Master Pages never really existed :-) They were just one of the contrivances of the Publishing Layout View included only in Word 2011 & Word 2008. That view, itself, was nothing more than an elaborate veneer designed to make Word to appear to be a page layout program - which it is not.

The actuality is that Master Pages did little more than provide access to the Headers & Footers of the Word document. The use of multiple Master Pages also implemented Section Breaks & the properties of Sections. The mechanics of working with the Header/Footer 'layer' of the document & with Sections were manipulated behind the scenes. Although Pub Layout facilitated the use of some features, it severely restricted or obviated the use of many others.

Headers/Footers & Sections still can be handled in the traditional manner... It just takes a deeper insight into the features of the program, how those features are designed to work, & how they interact. Here are links to some of the Word 2016 for Mac Help articles pertinent to the use of Headers/Footers & Sections:

  • Add a header or footer
  • Add different page numbers or number formats to different sections
  • Add images to a header or footer in Word 2016 for Mac ...
  • Add section breaks in Word 2016 for Mac - Word for Mac

As a helpful learning tool you might open a copy of an existing Pub document, then switch to Print Layout View. Be sure to display the nonprinting characters [¶] in order to better see where Section Breaks are located.

Can you use conditional formatting to show duplicates over an entire - Microsoft Community

jessemorelos say:

Can you use conditional formatting to show duplicates over an entire workbook?

I have a Workbook consisting of 30 sheets. I would like to know if it is possible to use conditional formatting to highlight duplicates across worksheets?

Thank you, 

teylyn say:

Yes, it is possible but not very comfortable to maintain. 

You don't describe whether a duplicate is just one cell or a combination of several cells and if you only want to look in specific rows/columns or the whole sheet. These details would matter for the formula.

If you want to find a duplicate of a single cell in all sheets, you can perform a Countif() on each sheet and add the results. With 30 sheets, this will be a very long formula. 

The following formula checks if a value in A1 is present in four other sheets 

=COUNTIF(Sheet2!$1:$1048576,A1)+COUNTIF(Sheet3!$1:$1048576,A1)+COUNTIF(Sheet4!$1:$1048576,A1)+COUNTIF(Sheet5!$1:$1048576,A1)

This formula is applied to cells A1 to A3 in this screenshot. You can see that the value in cell A2 is not highlighted because it does not have any duplicates.

You can see how setting up and maintaining such a formula for 30 sheets is possible but not easy to maintain. 

Note that in Excel 2007 and earlier this approach will not work, since conditional formatting in these versions can only refer to cells on the same sheet. With Excel 2010 and later, though, it is possible to reference other sheets directly.
