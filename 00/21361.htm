scottgem say:

The 'new' dynamic fields are stored within the subform's table (tblResolutions), BUT - I also left the fields in the main form's (frmEmployeeRelations) table (tblEmployeeRelations) as well so I'd keep the historical data there. 

What you are referring to as "dynamic" fields are a misnomer. One of the main concepts of relational design is called Entity/Attribute. This is one of the harder concepts to understand.

Basically you have entities that have attributes. So one of your entities is employees, another is issues and a third is actions. Each of these entities has attributes. Employees have names, date of hire, department, supervisor, etc. as attributes. Issues have the employee, date, type of issue, etc. as attributes. Actions have the issue, actions taken and when taken, etc. I can't tell you you all the attributes of each entity, that's based on your workflow and business rules. But you have to determine which attributes belong to which entity. Where there is overlap, is generally where your key fields are. In a well designed database there will be very little overlap. Valid overlaps are key fields and time sensitive data. For example, the dept an employee works for is clearly an attribute of the employee. However, since employees may be able to change depts, it may also be an attribute of the issue. In a case like that, you usually populate the issue in the dept entity with the current dept from the employee entity to preserve the dept at the time the issue occurred. But there is another way to do this. You maintain a history of the employee. Therefore the employee history becomes a sub entity of the employee. Each change in department requires a new record with an effective date. So an employee worked in Shipping on 4/1/14, but transferred to Warehouse on 4/1/15. So, an issue that occurred on 7/1/14, can be determined to involve Shipping since that's the dept of record at the time of the issue. You need to decide which method works best for you.

This issue probably has nothing to do with differences between Word 2013 and Word 2016, but everything to do with the use of different active printers on the systems concerned. If you want to guarantee a consistent layout on all systems that might need to read whatever the document contains, distribute in the PDF format; otherwise, you'll need to ensure all parties are using the same active printer (and printer driver).

joeu2004 say: S2poon wrote:

I was told to use a product function to calculate the returns for an index, say S&P 500, for periods 1 year or less.  Such as 1mth, 3mth, 9th, YTD and 1 year.  For longer periods, I'm told to use a Geomean function.  ex. 2 years, 5 years and 7 years.

=PRODUCT(1+D88:D99)-1

=GEOMEAN(1+D76:D99)^12-1

Why is that?


It sounds like you have monthly rates of return, and:

1. For 1 year or less, you want to calculate the simple rate of return for the period, not annualized.

2. For longer periods, you want to calculate the annual compound average rate of return.

Suppose you have monthly rates of return r1, r2, ... rN.  The monthly return r1 was calculated by y1/y0 - 1, where y0 and y1 are the prices at times t0 and t1 (e.g. end of each month).

If you had the monthly prices y0, y1, ... yN, the for the period would be:


y1/y0 * y1/y2 * ... * yN/y[N-1] - 1, which can be simplified to yN/y0 - 1


But if you only have the rates of return r1, r2, ... rN, the calculation is:


(r1+1) * (r2+1) * ... * (rN+1) - 1, which cannot be simplified.


If monthly rates r1 to r12 are in D88:D99, the Excel calculation is =PRODUCT(1+D88:D99) - 1.

Note:  The PRODUCT formula must be array-entered (press


On the other hand, the monthly compound average rate of return (r) would be:


r = (yN/y0)^(1/N) - 1

or equivalently:

r = ( (r1+1) * (r2+1) * ... * (rN+1) )^(1/N) - 1


and the annual compound average rate of return would be (1+r)^12 - 1.


If monthly rates r1 to r24 are in D76:D99, the Excel calculation is =GEOMEAN(1+D76:D99)^12 - 1.

Note  The GEOMEAN formula must be array-entered (press


[EDIT] For the simple rate using PRODUCT, the frequency of rate data can be any subdivision of the period you want to calculate.  For the compound average rate using GEOMEAN, if the frequency is not monthly, the exponential compounding factor (12) must change accordingly, e.g. 365 for daily returns and 52 for weekly.  In any case, if the number of returns is large, PRODUCT and GEOMEAN might overflow.  Then, it is more reliable to make the following substitutions:  PRODUCT(1+D88:D99) = 10^SUM(LOG(1+D88:D99)), and GEOMEAN(1+D76:D99) = 10^AVERAGE(LOG(1+D76:D99)).
