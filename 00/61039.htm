vibroplex say:

The bars measure the latency, or delay in your connection, not the speed, and ping is not useful unless you use a utility which uses port 3074 for the testing.

 

Now, some NAT problems are subtle and don't appear in the Test Xbox Live Connection test. Most likely there's a port blocked... ports can be blocked in your router, modem, or at your ISP. Since the Xbox 360 uses different ports than PCs & Macs use, you can't see the effect of a port blockage except on the Xbox.

 

A sequential power cycle is the first step in clearing a port blockage:

1) turn off the Xbox then

2) unplug the power from the router (Some folks have a combination modem-router AKA 'gateway'. If so, skip step 3).

3) unplug the power from the modem.

4) Wait at least 30 secs then replug the modem (or gateway) power.

5) Wait 'til the light on the front of the modem (or gateway) which says ONLINE, READY, CABLE or DSL is on solid (not flashing), then plug in the router. If you have a gateway instead of a modem and router, then when that light says it's ready, skip step 6.

6) Wait until the WiFi light on the router is on; you may not use WiFi, but it's still a good indicator your router is finished booting up.

7) Turn on the Xbox.  For more background on Ports, see http://whatismyipaddress.com/port

If the problem persists, then send us the information we need from http://forums.xbox.com/xbox_forums/xbox_support/f/9/t/155724.aspx and we can work on this further.

obsessive power say:

Sorry but the stuff Vibroplex has posted won't be of much use to the OP.  The NAT status of a router whether it be Strict, Moderate or Open won't make a blind bit of difference with the connection speed during online play. 

The NAT only effects the ability to join games, it doesn't cause slow downs when in games.  If there is no problem joining games, then no ports need to changed.  Opening ports will not improve the performance of the connection.

The OP is probably having difficulty finding games in his home country likely due to a low up to take of Xbox consoles and the amount of people playing.  If a COD game can't find games locally it will widen the search, which means the OP could be joining games several hundred miles away.  This will cause latency, which translates to on screen lag.  This would be a problem even if their connection is super fast due to the distances involved.

This may be a silly question but I have to ask. Are you plugging into the HDMI port on the graphics card between the DVI and VGA connectors and not the one on the motherboard (if there is one)?

In Device Manager:

Expand the Monitors heading, if the Generic PnP monitor is there double click it to open the properties window and select the Events tab, what you should see is something like this:    Or do you see errors instead?

 

Expand Display adapters, what are the entry(s), Is there more than one? Open the Properties>Events window.

Did you go to the Asus support link and enter your specific model id and check to see if it is being supported for the Win 10 OS and if there are any software add-ons for increased Win 10 compatibility offered?

The best I can tell your graphics card is a GeForce GT 705 (OEM) type card. Were have you gotten the driver you are using from, Windows Update, your OEM, the NVidia site, the GeForce site, or other?

Note: When installing a new NVidia driver package always uninstall the old one first, I've seen many times were that the new one over installs on top of the old one creating conflicts and performance issues. With Windows 10 device drivers, especially graphics drivers, your best bet is to 1st: Let Windows Update install the driver(s) it likes. 2nd: If need be, go to the PC OEM site and use their supplied drivers. 3rd: The hardware device manufactures site.

Again, HDMI being used on computers has seemingly always been somewhat problematic and prone to causing headaches. My NVidia Quadro 4000 does not use HDMI ports it has 2 DP (DisplayPort) ports and 1 DVI port. I use an DP to HDMI adapter and have never had a problem (thankfully).
