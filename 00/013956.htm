rob koch say:

They're all scams, since Microsoft never makes such calls unless you had contacted them first to request it.

It wasn't a Microsoft popup, it was a popup that someone made look like it came from Microsoft by adding things like the Microsoft logo or text.  There's a huge difference and most consumers aren't capable of telling the difference, which is why these scams often work.

It's easy to open a popup through a browser if you're already using the PC to do something else that can also launch these.  It's more common for these popups to display when you're already browsing other web pages, especially those displaying advertisements, but since many apps are also capable of opening browser pages for various reasons including ads, these can just as easily be abused to display a popup.

If the item was displayed in a popup on a MAC, what would ever make you believe that Microsoft would be telling you about something on a device they don't even support, let alone doesn't have a Microsoft operating system?

You're buying into the fake messages here and shouldn't, since that's what the malicious actors use to trick people into doing foolish things.  It never makes sense to see security messages from Microsoft on an Apple device, so don't believe it.

The call is only related in that it also claimed to be from Microsoft and though it's possible they somehow got your phone number from some malware installed on your device, it's much more likely it was just a random "cold call" made by automated dialers running through every possible number in your particular area.  This type of  robocall is very common now, with some expecting these to reach half (50%) of all cell phone calls by late next year.

Rob
