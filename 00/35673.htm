An Event 7006 error is a less informative report. All anti-virus software includes features to protect the software from threats and this report is recording that the self-defence measures have been triggered by an attempt to write to a protected registry key. tryx3 say:

Kathy,

The only other thing that seems worth mentioning is password complexity.  I looked into the subject three years ago.  My own policy for managing passwords is derived from that investigation.  You are free to regard my precautions as excessive if you wish.

  • password length 18 non-dictionary-strings   - for websites / applications that accept passwords containing letters [both upper & lower case], numbers, common punctuation, at least 2 symbols
  • password length 19 non-dictionary-strings   - for websites / applications that accept passwords containing letters [both upper & lower case], numbers, common punctuation, without any symbols

These password types-lengths would have less than a 1/1,000,00 chance of being brute-force cracked in thirty years' time.

The use of non-dictionary-strings is very important but it is not possible to quantify the difference in their suspectability to being cracked.  Any string of letters that appears in a dictionary anywhere can be easier to crack than a completely random string of characters because hackers can apply different techniques to the problem.

The assumptions in my analysis were -

1  They do not deem me worth more than the 100 hours of dedicated effort that some of the password-cracking services offer as a standard service. 

2  Current hacker boasts of their capabilities are accurate

3  They have control of 1,000,000 - 2,000,000 PC-equivalent computing power [datacentres, botnets, ...]

4  Computing speed will continue to double every year for the next thirty years -  

Moore's "law" is the observation that, over the history of computing hardware, the number of transistors on integrated circuits doubles approximately every two years.  Intel executive David House subsequently predicted 18 months for a doubling in chip performance (being a combination of the effect of more transistors and their being faster). I assumed a doubling every 1 year purely for simplicity of calculation so all my results are looking on the bleak side of security.

- - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

  • I disconnect from the internet,
  • look the password up,
  • write the password down on a piece of paper,
  • take the USB stick out of the computer,
  • reconnect to the internet,
  • use the password,
  • put the piece of paper on the fire later on. 

The password for the spreadsheet containing all the other passwords is written down on a strip of paper that I keep on my other keyring inside one of those little cylinders people write their addresses on & attach to their pets' collars.

The password-protected spreadsheet is backed up to another USB stick that is hidden round the back of the boiler.

If I ever thought I had been hacked or if I lost either of the keyrings I would start working through changing all my passwords.

KERNEL_DATA_INPAGE_ERROR: This Stop message indicates that the requested page of kernel data from the paging file could not be read into memory. Method 1: Perform a check disk. This disk check detects bad sectors in hard disk. The backup could be failing if there are any bad sectors on the hard disk.

Follow these steps:

  1. Press Windows key + X, select Command prompt (Admin)

  2. At the command prompt, type:

 chkdsk [drive:] [/p] | [/r] (Example: C:\chkdsk \p \r)

 [drive:] Specifies the drive to check.

/p Check even if the drive is not flagged dirty, bad.

 /r Locates bad sectors and recovers readable information (implies /p).

Disclaimer: While performing chkdsk on the hard drive if any bad sectors are found on the hard drive when chkdsk tries to repair that sector if any data available on that might be lost.
