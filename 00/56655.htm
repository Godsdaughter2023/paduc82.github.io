Two things:

It's up to hardware manufacturers to choose whether or not they will support their hardware on a new operating system. Microsoft has no say in the matter. You can be very sure that Microsoft would love it if all hardware manufacturers supported Windows 10. But only the hardware manufacturer has the knowledge and the legal authority to write drivers for their hardware, and Microsoft can't force them. For the hardware manufacturers it's all about - you guessed it - the money. There's a substantial cost involved in writing and testing new drivers. Hardware manufacturers aren't going to make the investment if they don't see a return coming back to them.

Second, just because Dell says a computer isn't supported on Windows 10 doesn't mean it won't work on Windows 10. It only means that Dell hasn't tested the computer on Windows 10 and hasn't made any effort to make it compatible with Windows 10. You, my friend, have to do that work. And it's a lot of work. But if you're up for it, you can get good results.
