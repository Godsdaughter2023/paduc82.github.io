Hello,

To help you resolve your concern, kindly boot your computer into safe mode. Follow the "Get to Windows Startup Settings in the Windows Recovery Environment".

Afterwards, you may proceed to perform a clean boot. Here's how:

  1. Swipe in from the right edge of the screen, and then tap Search. Or, if you are using a mouse, point to the lower-right corner of the screen, and then click Search.
  2. Type msconfig in the search box, and then tap or click msconfig.
  3. On the Services tab of the System Configuration dialog box, tap or click to select the Hide all Microsoft services check box, and then tap or click Disable all.
  4. On the Startup tab of the System Configuration dialog box, tap or click Open Task Manager.
  5. On the Startup tab in Task Manager, for each startup item, select the item and then click Disable.
  6. Close Task Manager.
  7. On the Startup tab of the System Configuration dialog box, tap or click OK, and then restart the computer.

a. user say:

What you're thinking of doing - storing your data (documents, music, pictures and video) in its own partition separately from the C: drive - is an excellent idea.

Technically speaking, you want to 'partition' your hard drive, which means changing the existing partitions. This is perfectly safe and you won't lose anything IF you do it the right way.

There's no way to undo a mistake when you partition a hard drive, so you must plan carefully. The procedure has been the same since Windows 7 (and probably before that) so it's well-established.

Before I make changes to my computer I always like to search the web for information. Here you will find detailed information that will explain everything. Remember, there's no way to undo a mistake when you partition a hard drive.

I'll outline the procedure below, but I strongly suggest that you ask our good friend Google how to partition a hard drive in Windows 10. You can, if you wish, install third-party software to do this for you, but it isn't necessary and you'll feel more in control if you do it yourself:

  • Sign out of your user account.
  • Sign in to the user account with Administrator permissions.
  • Open the Disk Management snap-in.
  • Follow the directions to reduce the size of your C: drive. This leaves empty space where the C: drive used to be.
  • In the empty space, create a new partition.
  • Windows will automatically assign a drive letter to the new partition. You can change the letter if you like. It's always a good idea to give partitions names.

Read the details on the web and you'll do fine.
