zigzag3143 (ms -mvp) say:
To access People from Outlook.com, hover the cursor over the Outlook logo in the top left and click on the down-arrow that appears to the right of the Outlook logo.
Select People. 

Returning to your Inbox from People:
Hover the cursor over the People logo and click on the down-arrow that appears on the right of the People logo. Select Mail.  
You can also get to Calendar or Skydrive in the same manner.

Filtering your list:
Above the search box at the top of the Contact list on the left, is a filter option which says "All ". 
If you click on this you should see the groups you created in the past.  
Click on any of these to filter your list to just the contacts in that group.

Selecting one or many Contacts:
If you click on a contact name in the list, the details for that contact will appear on the right.  
Alternatively, if you click on the check box to the left of the contact picture, you can build up a list of selected contacts on the right by clicking on the check box to the left of multiple contacts.

Adding/Removing from Group membership:
Whether you select one or many contacts, you will see a top menu bar option for Groups.  
If you click on this, then it will display the list of groups with a check box checked for each group that the contact(s) is/are a member of. 

You can uncheck to remove or check to add, the selection of contact(s) to the chosen groups, and then click on Apply to apply the changes.  
Your selected contacts will then be updated to be added or removed from the groups you chose as appropriate.

Delete
When you select one or many contacts, you will see a top menu bar option for Delete.  
If you click on Delete and click Delete in the confirmation box, the selected contacts will be deleted.

Edit:
You can choose edit if you have clicked on a single contact (name or picture) in the list on the left to see their details on the right.  
When viewing their details, click on Edit in the top menu bar to edit or add details.

Blurry Screenshots - Microsoft Community

shannon-cole say:

Blurry Screenshots

Hi,

I had previously put screenshots of a website onto a word document for one of my subjects at school. To save space I made the screenshots smaller, and now when I make the screenshots larger again they are awful quality.

I'd rather not do all of that work again, how would I fix it?

jay freedman say:

I don't think you can fix it, but it depends partly on how much smaller you made the pictures.

A screenshot is necessarily a bitmap with a certain number of pixels. When you reduce it, the software throws away some of the pixels. You don't notice the loss of information or sharpness because the remaining pixels are closer together. When you later enlarge the reduced picture, those pixels move apart again and the space between them has to be filled with something. But there's no way for the software to guess what colors those new pixels should be, other than "cloning" the ones it has. That's what causes the blurriness.

If you didn't reduce the sizes too much, it may help to copy the screenshots into a graphics editor such as IrfanView and use the Sharpen command. It won't be perfect, but it may be acceptable.

Commingling Content Controls and Legacy Formfields - Microsoft Community

greg maxey say:

Commingling Content Controls and Legacy Formfields

Frequently in this forum and other Word user forums, I notice that several extremely knowledgeable Word contributors dissuade users from comingling Content Controls (CCs) and Legacy Formfields (FFs).  I see comments like "Content Controls were not designed to be used with formfields" and "Content Controls and formfields don't work well together", etc.

I certainly don't encourage comingling CCs and FFs and with the exception of users limited to Word 2007 (which lacks a CC checkbox), I see no reason to comingle them.  That said though, where is it documented that they weren't designed to be used together?  Personally, and since CCs have been left woefully unfinished for nearly a decade, I don't think Microsoft knows exactly what they were designed for. 

It is my experience, with the exception of one anomaly, that when they are used together and excepting the baggage that comes with any document protected for forms, they work quite well together.

That anomaly (i.e., bug) is this:
When content controls are comingled with legacy form field checkboxes, the content controls are "locked" after a legacy checkbox gets the focus.  This "locked" state is typically released automatically and is  usually transparent to the form user if your form is designed so users tab from the checkbox to the next content control or next non-checkbox formfield.  Otherwise, it can be resolved by clicking in some protected text preceding the locked content control or clicking in a non-checkbox formfield and then re-entering the content control.

I'm not trying to poke these experts in the eye.  I'm just curious as to where the sense that comingling the two somehow goes against the design or is in some way wrong or inappropriate.  If there are other examples to illustrate that they don't work well together, I would like to know what they are.

On other anomaly that springs to mind is that, once you click on or tab into a formfield, which is possible to do from a content control, you can't then tab to another content control - only to another formfield. Consequently, your example of "if your form is designed so users tab from the checkbox to the next content control" can't actually work in practice and the behaviour is thus not "usually transparent to the form user".

greg maxey say:

Here is another method for overcoming the obstacle of no checkbox content control in Word 2007.  It was suggested as a variation of my Add Interactive Checkboxes to a Document tips page: http://gregmaxey.mvps.org/word_tip_pages/add_interactive_toggle_objects_to_document.html

In Word 2007, insert a rich text content control.  With the cursor in the CC, insert a macrobutton field that displays a symbol:

Sub InsertInteractiveCheckbox()
Dim oFld As Word.Field
Dim oRng As Word.Range
  Set oFld = Selection.Fields.Add(Range:=Selection.Range, Type:=wdFieldEmpty, _
             Text:="MACROBUTTON ToggleCheckbox", PreserveFormatting:=False)
  Set oRng = oFld.Code
  oRng.Collapse wdCollapseEnd
  oRng.InsertSymbol (82), "WingDings 2"
lbl_Exit:
  Exit Sub
End Sub

Add the following procedures to a standard module in the document:

Option Explicit
Private m_lngOpt As Long

Sub AutoOpen()
  m_lngOpt = Application.Options.ButtonFieldClicks
  Application.Options.ButtonFieldClicks = 1
  m_bInLoop = False
lbl_Exit:
  Exit Sub
End Sub

Sub AutoClose()
  Application.Options.ButtonFieldClicks = m_lngOpt
lbl_Exit:
  Exit Sub
End Sub

Sub ToggleCheckbox()
Dim oFld As Word.Field
Dim oRngTarget As Word.Range
Dim i As Long
Dim StrFont As String
Dim strCharNum As Long
  Set oFld = Selection.Fields(1)
  i = 29
  Set oRngTarget = oFld.Code.Characters(i)
  strCharNum = AscW(oRngTarget.Text)
  If strCharNum = 163 Then
    oRngTarget.Font.Name = "Wingdings 2"
    oRngTarget.Text = ChrW(82)
  Else
    oRngTarget.Font.Name = "Wingdings 2"
    oRngTarget.Text = ChrW(163)
  End If
  Set oRngTarget = Nothing
  Set oFld = Nothing
lbl_Exit:
  Exit Sub
End Sub

While is requires a macro enabled document it seems to work really well.  The basic user may  never suspect the trickery required to give them the checkbox ;-)
