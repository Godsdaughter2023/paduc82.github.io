NtfsDisable8dot3NameCreation

HKLM\System\CurrentControlSet\Control\FileSystem\NtfsDisable8dot3NameCreation

(REG_DWORD)

 

 

The default is 0. This parameter determines whether NTFS generates a short name in the 8.3 (MS‑DOS®) naming convention for long file names and for file names that contain characters from the extended character set. If the value of this entry is 0, files can have two names: the name that the user specifies and the short name that NTFS generates. If the user-specified name follows the 8.3 naming convention, NTFS does not generate a short name.

Changing this value does not change the contents of a file, but it avoids the short-name attribute creation for the file, which also changes how NTFS displays and manages the file. For most file servers, the recommended setting is 1.

Notably they do not mention that the NtfsDisable8dot3NameCreation setting also affects name matching (e.g., DIR *.doc matching abc.docx); when set to 0 it causes this (IMO) inappropriate match.

a. user say:

Hi Alastair,

Thank you for posting your query in Microsoft Community.

When you are logged on to Windows, your computer can automatically connect to a preferred network (a network that you have previously connected to) that's in range. Sometimes, your computer will continuously switch between two preferred networks, which can cause temporary interruptions to your connection, or your computer might lose the connection entirely.

When you move around with a mobile computer within a certain area, and you're connected to the Internet or another wireless network, your computer continuously switches from one wireless router or access point to another to stay connected. Even when you're not moving, your computer might sometimes try to switch between routers or access points. This continuous switching can cause temporary interruptions to your connection, or you might lose the connection entirely. Here are some things you can try to stop the continuous switching.

Here are some things you can try to stop the continuous switching:

Move your computer closer to one of the networks. With 802.11b or 802.11g routers and access points, the maximum range is up to 150 feet (46 meters) indoors and 300 feet (92 meters) outdoors. With 802.11a routers and access points, the maximum range is 50 feet (15 meters) indoors and 100 feet (30 meters) outdoors. These ranges are in optimal conditions with no interference. Keep them in mind when deciding where to locate your computer.

Turn off automatic switching in one or both of the network profiles. Here's how to do it:

a. Press Windows key + X and click Control Panel

b. Click Network and Internet, click Network and Sharing Center, and then, in the left pane, click Manage wireless networks.

c. Right-click the network profile that you want to turn off automatic switching, and then click Properties.

d. Click the Connection tab, clear the Connect to a more preferred network if available check box, and then click OK.

This will turn off automatic switching.

You may also refer to: Wired and wireless network problems

Hope this information is helpful. Please feel free to reply in case you face any other issues with Windows in future.

This issue may occur if the Enable IEEE 802.1X authentication for this network check box is selected. To disable IEEE 802.1X authentication, follow these steps:

c. Right-click your wireless network connection, and then clicks Properties.

d. Click the Wireless Networks tab.

e. Under Preferred networks, click your wireless network, and then click Properties.

f. Click the Authentication tab, click to clear the Enable IEEE 802.1X authentication for this network check box, and then click OK two times.
