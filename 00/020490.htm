Venn Diagram in excel and word - Microsoft Community

pradiptochatterjee say:

Venn Diagram in excel and word

How can i set the radius of the circles in a Venn diagram in excel ?

I want to have two perfect circles, one inside another. The area of the inner circle (smaller circle) should be exactly 30 % of the outer circle (larger circle). How could I do it ?

All Help will be appreciated.

vijay a. verma say:

1. Excel does provide facility to create Venn Diagram but all circles will be equal in that case. If you need equal circles Venn Diagram, Insert>Smart Art>Relationship>Basic Venn and you can edit text and control no. of circles.

2. Your requirement is to have unequal circles. This you will have to do manually.

3. Insert an Oval Shape from Insert>Shapes while holding Shift Key. Holding Shift Key turns Oval into a Circle. Make it of desired size.

4. Right Click on Circle> Size and Properties. You will notice that both Height and Width are equal. This is your diameter.

5. Now, you will need to calculate the area of the circle.

6. Radius = Diameter/2, Assuming your radius in cell A1

7. Area of Circle = pi()*(A1^2)

Let's assume that Area is in cell B1.

8. Now, you need to calculate 30% of this

= B1*30%

Let's assume that this is in C1

9. Use following formula to calculate Diameter.

=SQRT(C1/PI())*2

10. Draw a new circle with this diameter i.e. you draw a circle and adjust its width by typing the number in either width or height which you got in step 9. You get width or height by Right Clicking on Circle> Size and Propertie

11. Adjust circles overlapping and make them group. Grouping will make sure that increase and reduction in size will be proportional to both circles.

scottgem say:
No you would not have a separate table for each survey. Asking that question shows you really need to do some research on normalization. This is a key set of design concepts for relational databases that you need to understand. There are many resources that explain it. For example, you do not define data by naming a field or table. You define data by the contents of a record. So you would not have a separate table. for each survey you would have a field that indicates what survey the answer is from. If you look at the suggested table I gave you, it includes SurveyID as a foreign key. 

The general structure I use for a survey is:

tblQuestions
QuestionID (PK Autonumber)
Question

tblRespondent
RespondentID (PK Autonumber)
Firstname
Lastname
other info about respondent

tblResponse
ResponseID (PK Autonumber)
RespondentID (FK)
ResponseDate

tblAnswers
AnswerID (PK Autonumber)
ResponseID (FK)
QuestionID (FK)
Answer

So tblAnswers list ALL your answers identifying an answer by the ResponseID and QuestionID. 

No, if you want the checker to be able to spot and fix errors as they are discovered, don't even bother with a second table. have the checker enter the data, then immediately check to see if the response matches. If it doesn't open a new form showing the two responses where the checker can correct the response if necessary.
