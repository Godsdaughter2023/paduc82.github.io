Using shared mailbox for leavers

We have decided to use a shared mailbox for this purpose. This mailbox has its primary SMTP address and the addresses of leavers will be added.

The question then was how to handle messages that come in to the mailbox. We thought that the simplest thing would be to set up automatic replies. However, we didn't like that mechanism for the following reason:

  1. *** Email address is removed for privacy *** sends to *** Email address is removed for privacy *** and gets the "OOO".
  2. Many months later *** Email address is removed for privacy *** send to *** Email address is removed for privacy ***, but gets no response.
 
We then looked at using the Inbox rule, "Have server reply using a specific message". We found information for setting this up for a shared mailbox and did some testing and it seemed to do what we want - we reply with a message that says the person is no longer with us and directs the sender to our website for more information.

During the testing I had the thought of what would happen if our response generated an auto-response (not OOO) from the sender. I realise that this may not be likely, but if it were to occur then wouldn't it result in each organisation flooding the other with messages? Is there any way to guard against such a scenario?

TIA.

adavid.messengeruser.com say:

Why the need for a response at all? It could lead to mail loops  if using an inbox rule or responses to a spoofed message that may get you put on a blocklist.

I think the best 2 options are:

1. Remove the addresses and let the NDR go back to the sender that its no longer valid.

2. Add the SMTP address of that ex-employee to the mailbox of whomever is now handling their tasks and let them respond accordingly.

I suppose you could also create a hub transport rule that replies with a rejection message. That rule would filter on messages sent to a member of a group you called "Ex Employees: or something similar and contain the SMTP addresses of the ones that are no longer valid. That would be less likely to lead to a mail loop.
