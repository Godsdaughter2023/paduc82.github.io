marcine lohman say:

I have found a solution that seems to work for me but it is only for Windows 7.  Our problem was with the Windows 7 Credential Vault.

 

Windows 7:
 1. Close Outlook.
 2. Click Start.
 3. In the search box enter "Credential". You will then see Credential Manager.
 4. Select Credential Manager.
 5. Under Generic Credentials, you will most likely see an entry that has Outlook in the name.
 6. Delete it. In fact, you can delete all the entries in Generic Credentials if you like.
 7. Close the Credential Manager window and open Microsoft Outlook.
 8. If it asks for a password, enter the password and check for it to remember the password.
 9. A new entry will be created in the credential vault with your latest login and password.
 10. Problem fixed.

 

The problem for us has been that the saved password in the credential vault file doesn't appear to work (especially if we've changed the password recently) so by deleting it, another credential will be created the next time you log into Outlook with your latest password.

 

I hope this helps.

Windows 10 - get rid of the hamburger menu - use app bars and ribbons - Microsoft Community

mihaicosmin say:

Windows 10 - get rid of the hamburger menu - use app bars and ribbons instead

Hello everyone,

Although I marked this specifically as a windows desktop problem, it applies to the entirety of Windows 10 devices (that have screens ofc).

Anyway, the big UI problem of this system is the hamburger menu. This is one of the trendy and quite frankly, extremely annoying UI designs that have slipped through the world like the black plague (I have used this comparison on purpose, because the hamburger menu is much like the black plague of UI designs).

Now, you would ask yourselves, what is wrong with this hamburger menu? Well - everything.

First of all, its very appearance.

It brings no indications whatsoever that it actually hides important stuff. In most windows applications, the hamburger menu hides stuff that is relevant to the user -> like the settings, or worse, navigation panels.

I've often found myself trying to figure out where the **** are all the options in my app. Nowhere to be found. Then I look, and I see the horrifying 3 line icon in the top left corner of the screen. Oh look, now all my navigation goes through there: one extra useless click! this makes a huge difference. Which brings us to the second problem.

Its positioning in the top left corner is awful

Out of all the corners on the screen, the left corner is the worst, especially on mobile devices. Most people on this planet are right handed. And phones don't tend to get any smaller. I often find myself uninstalling apps on sight when they ask me to stretch my fingers across the screen just because of a trendy yololololo design pattern.

Worse, it forces you to use the 2nd hand for your phone, which in some cases is not possible, or is simply not desirable.

The business world sticks to the tried and tested horizontal bars

Why? because they display the obvious information to the user all at once, and there is no necessity to hide anything. The users won't just roll their eyes across the screen, see the horrific hamburger icon and uninstall the app. They see all their options available to them, and they proceed from there. Do you remember why the ribbon UI was added to office instead of the plain old hidden menus on a gazzillion levels? because the users have an easier time doing what they came to the app to do instead of playing hide and seek with the app options.

The app bars and ribbons fix the problem

Not only do these UI elements do everything the hamburger button does (i.e hide stuff if the user so desires), but they are also pretty damn obvious on your screen and they also present all of your options all at once.

And of course, they are neutral when it comes to left hand or right hand users on mobile devices, and of course, neither of them forces you to do awkward finger gymnastics to get what you want.

People are ignoring the windows store on desktop because of the awkward navigation and waste of screen. b) Replace hidden menus and finger gymnastics with tried and tested patterns: Pivots, tabs, hubs, app bars on mobile

suzanne s. barnhill say:
Doug and Shane have given you the basics. You may also want to look at http://www.word.mvps.org/FAQs/Formatting/front_matter_2007.htm and http://sbarnhill.mvps.org/WordFAQs/HeaderFooter.htm

doug robbins - mvp office apps & services (word) say:

You need to insert a Next Page Section Break (Page Layout tab>Page Setup>Breaks>Section Breaks>Next Page) to separate the body of the document from first six pages, and then with the selection in the header of the second section of the document (the one that begins on page 7, the Link to Previous button in the Navigation section of the Header & Footer Tools section of the ribbon will have an orange background if the header is linked that in the previous Section (by default it will be).  Click on that  button to unlink the header in the second section from that in the first section and also check the Different Odd & Even Pages box in the Options section of the Header & Footer Tools tab of the Ribbon.

Then, by moving to an appropriate (Odd or Even) page, you can set up the Header as required for each.  Before setting up each Header, check to make sure that the Link to Previous button does not have an orange background. With your particular configuration (not having different Odd and Even in the first Section), it probably will not, but click on it if it does.

-- Hope this helps.
