marhatket say:

While it's true that the place Word saves is now called "Documents" and it is a collection of folders which is now called a "Document Library," this can confuse people so much that they set up alternate means of "making life simpler" for themselves. 

 

I think in your case a few tips may help. 

 

1.  When something "automatically saves" somewhere it's somewhere in Documents:  click start button, look on the right for "Documents" and click that to open your Documents Library the simplest way. 

 

2.  If you feel more comfortable with the Documents being on your desktop,  then Right Click it in the Start menu, Send To, Desktop (create shortcut). 

 

That's to prevent your feeling like you're wondering where in the world your document disappeared to.  Because sometimes, some programs will just do that to you and it's helpful to know what the computer thinks you should do. 

 

OK, so you don't have to take the computer's advice and keep things in Documents.  But I recommend you make folders on your desktop.  Make one called "Current Documents" (don't use My Documents, or Documents or Word Documents because all those have disadvantages like being confused with actual system folders, or being too narrow - I mean, you might save a Power Point presentation there too someday).  Make another that's called something like "Pictures and Movies" and maybe another like "Songs." 

 

You should understand that if you take this path, people who make backups of your computer will wonder why you did this and will continually lecture you about doing it "right" instead.  Just tell them you're a power user.  ;)  You'll have to remind people that there is a lot of data on your desktop that needs backup.  So...

 

As Palcouk has pointed out, you can then go ahead and change your default save location in Word and in other programs.  Obviously Word will be saving to your new Current Documents folder on your desktop. 

 

Don't ever feel ashamed or odd about this.  I've seen literally at least 20 executive level people do this because they had contract information they didn't want sync'ed or on the server in the corporate environment.  Power users and IT people also often defeat the usual usage of Windows.  So you're in good company.  Just realize you're making it harder on yourself, so you'll need to educate yourself more.  IT people will react with frustration if you mess it up and then expect them to fix it in an odd way.  Unless, of course, you're a vice president.  ;)
