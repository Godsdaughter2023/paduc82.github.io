SIMPLE BASIC DIAGNOSTICS

If there is no indication of power available when attempting to power up - it could simply mean that the power supply has failed.

If a PC does not boot - it is either a hardware error that prevents power on - or if it has power and attempts to boot but stops before getting the System running - there could be a number of reasons.

Simple diagnostic procedures have to be followed to find the cause.

Starting with checking all the cable connections - and using a known good system on a hard drive to boot from.

If the pc has power indicated by any lights showing at power on - and the BIOS can be accessed by using F8 (or the specific function key for the make of pc) - that indicates that at least the Motherboard and monitor are ok.

Note any error messages that are displayed.

Then try powering up the PC and putting ANY operating system CD / DVD in the slot and 'Hitting any key' to boot from it. If it boots to that media - there is little wrong with the hardware other than the hard drive itself.

It may have failed - or the operating system has been corrupted and cannot be booted up.

In some instances - a bad RAM stick can prevent a system from booting.

If there is more than one in the PC - they should be fitted ONE AT A TIME in ONE slot - and try to boot.

If a stick of RAM is faulty - it may only be able to hold a small amount of data - and will prevent a system from booting up if the faulty area is required to hold data.

Hope these basic diagnostics help you to find the cause of your problem.

rohn007 say:
There is no official MS way of "upgrading" from one 2007 bundle to another.

MS no longer sells Office 2007 (or 2010) so you will have to buy it from someone else, probably on the internet.  People are allowed to resell their Office license if they have a "Retail" / "FPP" type license.  For 2007 (and 2010) the easiest way to tell that is that they have the "boxed CD" version. 

Note: there are a few boxed CD versions that cannot be resold, ie ones labelled "NFR" / "Not for Resale" or "Academic".

When they resell it to you they have to uninstall it from all of their computers and give you all of the documentation, ie the Installation disk and CD box.

Sorry, there is no official way of finding out how many active Office installations you have on a license. MS expects you to keep track of it manually, "with a pencil and paper".

Yes, Home and Student allows activation on up to 3 machines at the same time. If Office is still installed on the unused machines, and they are usable (ie "not dead" or scrapped) then you cannot yet reuse those licenses.  If those machines are still usable you first have to uninstall Office.  Even though the uninstall process does not talk to the activation system, if you want to transfer the licenses to another machine you have to uninstall. That way, when you are using the old machine and the activation system triggers a "license validation" it won't show that you have it active on too many machines.

After you uninstall from the old machine, or confirm that the old machine is dead and totally unusable, that activation "count" is available for transfer to a new computer.  You can install, and activate on the new computer.  You can try an internet activation, but it will probably fail since the uninstall does not actually talk to the activation system, but it may work so it is worth trying.  That is OK. You will have to do a phone activation. When you phone in, don't reply to the initial prompts, they'll give you the same error.  Instead, wait a long time until the phone system errors out and gives you the option of talking to a live service rep. Explain that you have uninstalled from the old machine and are transferring the license to the new one. They should allow the activation to complete.
